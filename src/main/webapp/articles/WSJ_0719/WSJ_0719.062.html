Viewpoint: We Wuz Robbed] (That's Only My Opinion, Of Course)

  • Author: Michael Gartner
  • Date: 07/19/90

There's an old joke that goes something like this: A burly fellow walks into the doctor's office and says he feels terrible and wonders why.

"You're fat," says the doctor.

The fellow is outraged. "I want a second opinion," he says.

"OK," says the doctor, "you're ugly, too."

That was funny when you first heard it 10 years ago. Today, it isn't. At least it isn't to that little band of lawyers and newsmen who keep a close eye on the health of the First Amendment. To them, what was funny 10 years ago is frightening today.

For as of last month, a person has to keep his opinions to himself -- or risk being sued.

Historically in this country, there has been no such thing as a false opinion. Without fear, I could write an editorial that said you were the worst mayor in the history of my city. You could write a sports column that likened my tennis game to your aunt's butterfly chasing. A cartoonist could depict a politician as sinister and evil. A critic could compare a singer's performance to the baying of my otter hound.

And you and I and the cartoonist and the critic would be quite safe in saying what we said. The politician, the athlete, the performer -- or your aunt and my otter hound -- just had to grin and bear it. A person's opinions were all part of the uninhibited, robust and wide-open debate that characterizes free speech in this country.

At first, it was the common law that protected us and our opinions. Later, it was some state supreme courts. Finally, it was the Supreme Court itself. "Under the First Amendment there is no such thing as a false idea," the court said. "However pernicious an opinion may seem, we depend for its correction not on the conscience of judges and juries but on the competition of other ideas."

That was in 1974, and the only problem left was trying to determine what was an opinion and what was a fact. But courts developed guidelines for that, too, saying you had to look at the language, the context, the social environment, and the like. That was a problem, but not one that really threatened free speech. The rules were pretty clear, the lines well drawn.

It was in 1974, too, that the Maple Heights High School Mustangs and the Mentor High Cardinals squared off in an Ohio wrestling match that led to a melee of sorts. Michael Milkovich was the coach of the Maple Heights team, the home team, and Ted Diadiun was a sports writer for the News-Herald in Lorain. Mr. Diadiun thinks Mr. Milkovich ranted and raved and egged on the crowd.

Mr. Milkovich was called before a high school sports governing body, which put him on probation. He appealed to a court, which overturned the board's ruling. Mr. Diadiun was even less impressed with Mr. Milkovich's performance during the proceedings than he was with the performance at the wrestling meet. So Mr. Diadiun wrote a column -- it was labeled "TD Says" -- that said the sad lesson of the whole incident was this: "If you get in a jam, lie your way out." The headline on the column said:

Maple beat the law

with the `big lie'

Mr. Milkovich sued Mr. Diadiun and his newspaper, and thus began what the Supreme Court called "an odyssey of litigation spanning nearly 15 years."

The odyssey ended, sort of, June 21. The court unanimously said that it never really meant there was no such thing as a wrong opinion. What it really meant, the court said, was that there was no such thing as a wrong idea. If an opinion contains or implies a fact, the court said, the opinion has to be defensible in court.

How's that again?

"If a speaker says, `In my opinion John Jones is a liar,' he implies a knowledge of facts which lead to the conclusion that Jones told an untruth," the court said in explaining that opinions based on facts are litigable. "Even if the speaker states the facts upon which he bases his opinion, if those facts are either incorrect or incomplete, or if his assessment of them is erroneous, the statement may still imply a false assertion of fact. Simply couching such statements in terms of opinion does not dispel these implications; and the statement, `In my opinion Jones is a liar,' can cause as much damage to reputation as the statement, `Jones is a liar.'" Thus, Jones can sue and maybe win.

However, the court went on, "the statement, `In my opinion Mayor Jones shows his abysmal ignorance by accepting the teachings of Marx and Lenin' would not be actionable. . . . A statement of opinion relating to matters of public concern which does not contain a provably false factual connotation will receive full constitutional protection." So Jones can't win that one.

Huh?

What this means, apparently, is this: If I state my opinion clearly and succinctly, you can sue me. I can't get the suit dismissed on the ground that it was merely an opinion. We'll go to trial to determine whether my opinion was based on facts, which, indeed, is what Messrs. Milkovich and Diadiun may now have to do. If you can prove the facts were wrong or incomplete, you can win a lot of money from me.

What this will lead to, once it sinks in in newsrooms and law offices around the country, is mealy-mouthed editorials, wishy-washy sports columns, punch-pulling cartoons and namby-pamby reviews. It will lead to sentences like, "In my opinion Mayor Jones shows his abysmal ignorance by accepting the teachings of Marx and Lenin," and away from sentences like, "Mayor Jones is a dumb Red."

To be sure, this dumb decision -- excuse me, this decision, which appears to be based on a historical perspective that some might find misguided or troubling or based on abysmal ignorance -- won't topple democracy. But neither will it enhance that "free flow of ideas and opinions" that the court just two years ago said was "at the heart of the First Amendment."

"Permitting people to express their emotions as well as their thoughts is . . . a central purpose of the First Amendment," Robert Sack, a lawyer who represents this newspaper, among others, wrote in a friend-of-the-court brief that the big media companies (including my employer, NBC) entered on behalf of Mr. Diadiun.

Mr. Sack is old enough to have been a fan of the Brooklyn Dodgers, and he says that Mr. Diadiun's column brought back memories of Ebbets Field and the Dodger fans yelling "We wuz robbed."

"We wuz robbed," he says, "is not so different from what sports columnist Diadiun was saying. It cannot be put to a true/false test without endangering the ability of reporters and fans to get mad, to participate fully in the tumult and shouting of American spectator sports."

That was written before the Supreme Court ruled, of course. But the analogy is apt. For if there's one thing that's clear from the decision, it's this:

We wuz robbed.
