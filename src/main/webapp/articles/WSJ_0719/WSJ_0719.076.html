Economy: Greenspan Says Credit Policy To Stay Stable --- Fed Chief Tells Senate Panel Friday's Easing Isn't Sign Of Major Economy Shift

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 07/19/90

WASHINGTON -- Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan, testifying before the Senate Banking Committee, signaled that no further easing of credit is imminent. Mr. Greenspan labored to explain that the Fed eased credit "slightly" last Friday not because it sees the economy deteriorating but because it became convinced that the credit crunch "is beginning to have very real, unwelcome effects."

The distinction is important, Mr. Greenspan said. If the Fed were "looking at a more profound change . . . in either economic activity or inflationary pressures," Friday's action would have been the first of a series of moves, he said. "We were not looking to fundamentally change the underlying credit conditions in the country."

Pressed by Sen. Paul Sarbanes (D., Md.), Mr. Greenspan said that the economic odds are "perhaps a shade more toward recession than they were earlier in the year," but he attributed that almost entirely to the reluctance of banks to make business loans. "We have offset most of that with the action we have taken," he said.

Mr. Greenspan didn't slam the door completely on another move by the Fed if it sees evidence that the credit crunch is growing worse. He said the Fed "must remain alert to the possibility that an adjustment . . . might be needed," and he pledged "continued scrutiny" and "a flexible approach to policy making."

He also held out the prospect of additional Fed easing should Congress and the White House agree to "major, substantive, credible cuts in the budget deficit" of between $50 billion and $60 billion for next year. In apparent response to Bush administration concerns that the Fed might not move fast enough, Mr. Greenspan said, "We can decide that a policy adjustment is appropriate and implement it fully, all in the same morning if need be, and the effects of the change will show through interest rates . . . almost immediately."

The Fed had been holding short-term interest rates steady for more than six months. And as recently as a month ago, Mr. Greenspan said that he saw no "broad-based tightening" by lenders that was harming the national economy. But he changed his tune last week, telling the Banking Committee Thursday that the Fed was poised to ease because of "cumulating evidence" of a credit crunch. Yesterday, he confirmed that the Fed moved Friday to lower the short-term interest rate it controls by a quarter of a percentage point, to 8%.

Mr. Greenspan said it had become apparent "only in recent days" that Fed action was warranted. He cited surprisingly slow growth of the money supply as well as surveys and other indications that banks are tightening lending standards and increasing the spread between the rates they pay depositors and those they charge lenders. "If you wait until you get published numbers, you'll always be acting much too late," he said.

The Fed chairman said that for months he had welcomed growing conservatism among banks and other lenders as a prudent reaction to the excesses of the 1980s, but now he fears banks have gone too far. "It is difficult to discern the dividing line between lending standards that are still healthy and those that are so restrictive as to be inconsistent with the borrower's status and the best interests of the lender in the long run," he said. "In recent weeks, however, in my judgment, we may have slipped over the line."

Not only are banks reluctant to make real estate and merger-related loans, Mr. Greenspan said, but they are tightening lending standards for small and medium-sized companies as well as weaker big companies. "In some cases, loans have been simply unavailable," he said. Mr. Greenspan, always precise in his language, said he "wouldn't call this change technically `a credit crunch'" because it isn't a "contraction of lending on a major scale." But he conceded that to borrowers this distinction may be perceived as "a semantic nicety."

In its semiannual report to Congress, the Fed said that forecasts by members of the reserve board and presidents of district Federal Reserve banks anticipate that the economy will grow by about 1.5% to 2% between the fourth quarter of 1989 and the fourth quarter of 1990, after adjusting for inflation. The Fed policy makers now see consumer prices climbing between 4.5% and 5% in the same period; in February, their price forecast was between 4% and 4.5%.

For next year, the Fed policy makers see a pickup in growth and a slowdown in inflation. They expect the economy to grow between 1.75% and 2.5% after inflation and expect consumer prices to climb between 3.75% and 4.5%.

The Fed expects unemployment, currently at 5.2% of the work force, to climb to between 5.5% and 5.75% by the end of this year and to between 5.5% and 6% by the end of next year. Mr. Greenspan said the jobless rate has remained as low as it has because of "a surprising decline" in young people, particularly women, who are working or looking for work. He said this may be a statistical fluke, but he suggested that slower-than-anticipated growth in the labor force could be a constraint on economic growth.

As part of its long-run objective of reducing the inflation rate, the Fed also said that its goal is for the M2 measure of the money supply to grow between 2.5% and 6.5% next year, down a notch from the 3% to 7% range set for 1990, a range it reaffirmed recently. M2 includes currency, checking accounts and most savings accounts.

The target growth range for M3, which includes M2 plus large certificates of deposit and some other instruments, was lowered to between 1% and 5% for both 1990 and 1991; in February, the Fed set this range at 2.5% to 6.5%. Mr. Greenspan said this revision reflects the consolidation of the thrift industry rather than a policy change.
