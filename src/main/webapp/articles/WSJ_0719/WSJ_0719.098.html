Stotler Group Unit Is Said to Comply With Capital Rules

  • Author: Scott McMurray
  • Date: 07/19/90

CHICAGO -- The Chicago Board of Trade said it "believes" Stotler & Co., the futures brokerage unit of Stotler Group Inc., is in compliance with industry minimum capital requirements. The terse exchange announcement late yesterday averts a crisis for the old-line grain-trading firm whose chairman, Karsten "Cash" Mahlmann, is also Board of Trade chairman. But exchange members said the firm, while a survivor for now, will be a drastically smaller operation with an uncertain future if customer and investor concerns about its financial health aren't quickly assuaged.

Stotler officials didn't return calls for comment.

To slash costs, the company this week instituted sharp salary cuts for salaried employees earning more than $20,000 annually. An internal company memorandum from President and Chief Executive Officer Thomas Egan to the staff says salaried employees' base pay over $20,000 annually effectively would be halved, at least through Sept. 30. For example, the memo says employees earning $30,000 annually will have their pay cut to $25,000 a year. The memo said futures brokers' commissions weren't affected by the salary cuts.

Stotler, with more than 300 branches and affiliates scattered across the Midwest, has been transferring millions of dollars worth of customer accounts to other futures brokers this week in a frantic effort to reduce its regulatory capital requirements, exchange members said.

The Board of Trade last week determined that Stotler's capital levels didn't meet industry standards, and had given Stotler until the close of business today to shore up its capital position.

Neither the exchange nor Stotler said what caused the firm to slip below the minimum requirement. Exchange members said the problem may be tied to a rumored dispute with regulators over the counting of certain assets within Stotler Group and its subsidiaries, possibly about $11 million in commercial paper issued by Stotler as capital.

If Stotler hadn't met the minimum regulatory capital requirements -- capital on hand equal to 4% of segregated customer funds -- it could have been forced to only let customers trade to liquidate positions, or all of its customer accounts could have been transferred by regulators to other brokerage firms.

The exchange didn't say what amount of capital Stotler had to support its customer accounts as of late yesterday, nor did it reveal the amount of funds in the accounts. Exchange members, who asked to remain anonymous, estimated that Stotler's segregated customer funds peaked at between $250 million and $300 million a few weeks ago.

Earlier in the week Stotler confirmed that it was transferring customer accounts to other firms and said it was seeking a capital infusion to satisfy regulatory demands for capital. The company said that without a capital infusion, "Stotler Group may be unable to meet its debt obligations unless those obligations are refinanced or restructured."

The company also said that transferring accounts would reduce revenue and net income for Stotler Group. For the first quarter ended March 31, the company had net income of $370,000, or 17 cents a share, and commission revenue of $28.4 million.
