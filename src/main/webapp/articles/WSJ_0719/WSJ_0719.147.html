International: U.S. Offers Soviets Detailed Proposal For Technical Aid

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 07/19/90

PARIS -- The U.S. presented the Soviet Union a detailed proposal for extended U.S. technical aid to Moscow in several economic problem areas. The paper, handed to Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze by Secretary of State James Baker at a meeting here, offers no U.S. funds but promises U.S. expertise to help improve such areas as banking, housing, the tax system and distribution networks. The U.S. also is offering to help with market education.

The proposal is a follow-up to the annual economic summit last week in Houston, at which President Bush said direct economic aid was premature and, instead, stressed technical economic assistance.

According to an aide to Mr. Baker, the secretary of state emphasized U.S. help for changing the Soviet distribution system. The official said Mr. Baker told Mr. Shevardnadze that "It doesn't do the Soviets a lot of good if they are producing a lot more goods or acquiring a lot more goods if they can't distribute them."

The official conceded that President Bush made a similar offer to Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev at the Malta summit in December and that the technical advice projects offered then hadn't yet been carried out. But he added that Mr. Shevardnadze seemed anxious to accept the current offer and that he told Mr. Baker he might appoint a small staff to see that the technical assistance actually is delivered.

Separately, the State Department official said Mr. Shevardnadze didn't object to the U.S. policy of trying to build ties to the new non-Communist opposition in the Soviet Union.

"Shevardnadze said that was understandable, he had no problem with that," the official said.
