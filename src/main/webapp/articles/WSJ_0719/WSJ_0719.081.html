A Forum for Blacks In Leadership Roles

LOOKING FOR black role models? "SuccessGuide" is to publish the names and phone numbers of 44,500 black achievers in seven major markets, including New York, Chicago, Washington and Atlanta, next January.

George Fraser, a former Procter & Gamble marketing manager, launched the directory two years ago with a 5,000-name guide for the Cleveland area. The four-color 1990 Cleveland book includes names of executives, high school principals, visiting nurses and even caterers.

Mr. Fraser says his directories answer a need by ambitious African-Americans for information about other blacks who can help them succeed. This "intellectual capital," he says, is the black community's least-developed asset. Black magazines feature stories about blacks and black-owned businesses, but SuccessGuide aims "to identify role models not by tens, but by the thousands," says Mr. Fraser.

People listed must agree to accept phone calls from SuccessGuide users. At $10 each, some 12,500 of the 1990 Cleveland books have been sold on newsstands and by direct marketing to those listed in the directory. Advertising will account for 70% of the $2.5 million Mr. Fraser expects to gross this year, he says.

Sneaker Makers Face

Scrutiny From Push

BLACKS SHOULD shun some sneaker brands if their makers don't spend more with black businesses, says the Rev. Tyrone Crider, executive director of Operation Push.

Representatives of black-owned ad and media firms decided to press sneaker makers to procure more services from black companies at a meeting last month at the Chicago headquarters of Push, an activist group.

The athletic shoe market is driven by the preferences of minority youth, but black media received little of the $94 million reportedly spent by the top five brands on ads last year. Mr. Crider expects Push's board to approve plans to target a sneaker firm for negotiations at the organization's convention in Chicago next week. If negotiations fail, a boycott could be urged, he says.

Nike Inc., the domestic sales leader, has a good chance of being that target, he says.

Nike recently cited its absence from black media in response to another race-related controversy. The Beaverton, Ore., company had been under fire for marketing its expensive sneakers directly to poor black youth. A sportswriter had sparked widespread interest when he charged that black teenagers were killing for the sneakers. "We don't market by race," a spokeswoman says. "We market our product by sport."

Push has had a low national profile in recent years as founder Jesse Jackson focused on two presidential bids. In 1980, after a 40-day boycott organized by Push, Coca-Cola Co. agreed to buy some $30 million in products and services from black businesses and granted its first black-owned bottling franchise.

Minority Dealerships

Hurt in Auto Slump

WHEN DETROIT'S fortunes slide, minority car dealers can be the hardest hit.

Slow domestic auto sales are causing a shakeout among black dealers. At the end of 1989, General Motors Corp. had 104 black-owned franchises, most nurtured by a program to aid minority dealerships. At the end of June, the company had 90. Total 1989 sales for the top 10 Hispanic-owned dealerships ranked by Hispanic Business magazine fell 5% to $764 million.

Minority dealers tend to be new to the business, with less of a cushion built up to soften downturns. For example, 57 of the top 100 dealers ranked by Black Enterprise magazine were started since 1985. Another problem is that minority dealers -- with only a tiny share of Big Three dealerships -- have been nearly shut out of the healthy import-car business. Import leader Toyota, for example, has only seven black and seven Latino dealers out of 1,100. A Toyota spokeswoman says the company is always looking for qualified minority dealers, but doesn't include race in the selection criteria.

Gregory Baranco, a multi-franchise dealer in Atlanta and the nation's only black dealer of Honda's Acura luxury cars, says that black import-car buyers might think twice about their purchases if they knew the lack of minority participation in the market, "but these facts aren't printed in Consumer Reports."

Solar Energy Powers

Puerto Rican Quest

PUERTO RICO'S hopes for a place in the high-tech market are riding on three slim tires this week.

A solar-powered vehicle built by students from the University of Puerto Rico at Mayaguez is in a race from Florida to Michigan sponsored by General Motors. The team is up against 31 other schools in the 10-day "Sunrayce" contest for solar-powered cars that is to end today.

"The Shining Star of the Caribbean," as the University of Puerto Rico car is called, was conceived, designed, built and promoted by a team of students and faculty members. It is billed as Puerto Rico's first home-grown high-tech product.

The car has run near the rear of the pack, but win or lose, its backers hope to attract attention to Puerto Rico's technological potential. A new public agency with a mission to promote the island's high technology ventures pitched in $300,000 to get the project started a year ago.

The real goal, says Diego Loinaz, director of the agency, is to prove to the world that "we `browns' are not less at all."
