Buy-Out Firm Seeks Purchase Of IBM Lines

  • Author: Paul B. Carroll and George Anders
  • Date: 07/19/90

NEW YORK -- The leveraged buy-out firm Clayton & Dubilier is negotiating a possible $2.5 billion to $3 billion buy-out of International Business Machines Corp.'s typewriter, keyboard and other low-end operations, according to industry and financial executives. If completed, the acquisition would be the largest ever for the New York-based partnership. An IBM deal also would be consistent with some of Clayton & Dubilier's previous acquisitions, which included low-technology units shed by Harris Corp. and Unisys Corp.

The Wall Street Journal reported yesterday that IBM was looking at a $3 billion divestiture of its low-tech businesses. In a statement yesterday, IBM said it is "considering many options as it moves to sharpen its strategic focus," but the Armonk, N.Y., computer giant didn't comment directly on the newspaper story. IBM did say, though, that it "hasn't concluded any transaction."

IBM also said any action "would ensure that our employees are treated fairly, that service to our customers is uninterrupted and that the transaction is in the best economic interest of our company."

Financial executives also said Clayton & Dubilier may have lined up an equity partner to share in the acquisition. Clayton & Dubilier reportedly sounded out General Electric Capital Corp., a unit of General Electric Co., about buying some subordinated debt to help finance the transaction. GE Capital declined to comment.

Several bankers said Clayton & Dubilier's longtime lead bank, Manufacturers Hanover Corp., is likely to organize bank loans for an IBM deal. The bankers cautioned that they hadn't yet seen Clayton & Dubilier's exact financing terms. Officials at Manufacturers didn't return phone calls.

People familiar with Clayton & Dubilier said they expect the firm will strive to meet IBM's concerns. Recent Clayton & Dubilier acquisitions have let management and employees buy a stake of about 25% in the post-buy-out company. Clayton & Dubilier "has made an effort to put equity ownership in the broadest hands," an adviser to the buy-out firm said.

Clayton & Dubilier also has tried to assure IBM that its acquisitions generally don't involve the big asset sales and job cuts that have soured the public image of buy-outs. Instead, Clayton & Dubilier, led by its chairman, former McKinsey & Co. consultant Martin Dubilier, has portrayed itself as a team of hands-on operating specialists, with ideas on improving everything from a company's distribution system to its inventory control.

To date, Clayton & Dubilier's largest acquisition has been the $1.16 billion purchase of Uniroyal Inc. in 1985. Recently, however, Clayton & Dubilier has taken steps to increase its buying power. It raised a $1 billion equity fund last year from pension funds and other institutional investors, triple the size of its previous fund. At the time, Clayton & Dubilier said it wanted to "participate in transactions . . . above the $1 billion level."

Several bankers said Clayton & Dubilier's longtime lead bank, Manufacturers Hanover Corp., is likely to organize bank loans for an IBM deal. The bankers cautioned that they hadn't yet seen Clayton & Dubilier's exact financing terms. Financial executives also said Clayton & Dubilier is in talks with General Electric Capital Corp., a unit of General Electric Co., which is considering buying some subordinated debt to help finance the transaction. Officials at Manufacturers didn't return phone calls; General Electric Capital declined comment.

The indicated purchase price of $2.5 billion to $3 billion would appear to include other operations besides typewriters, keyboards and printers. Although IBM doesn't break out results by individual product lines, its world-wide sales of typewriters, keyboards and low-end printers are estimated at less than $2 billion. Given the relatively low margins on those businesses, any purchase price would presumably come to less than the annual revenue.

IBM spent $350 million in the early 1980s to modernize and automate its facility in Lexington, Ky., where more than 4,000 employees build the products that are up for sale. But it has apparently decided that it can't build the products as efficiently as competitors can.

IBM has been re-evaluating what businesses are central to its strategy and has been selling some marginal assets. With pressure from competitors unabating, IBM has aggressively cut costs, to the point of taking a $2.4 billion write-off in last year's fourth quarter to cover the costs of cutting 10,000 jobs and consolidating some plants.

In composite New York Stock Exchange trading yesterday, IBM shares closed at $119.875, down 87.5 cents.
