Board of National Intergroup Seeks To Appease Group

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 07/19/90

PITTSBURGH -- National Intergroup Inc., trying to defuse a heated proxy battle, said it would expand its board to 13 from 10 and give a seat to dissident shareholder Centaur Partners. But there's a catch. The offer is contingent on the company winning the proxy fight next week against Centaur. Moreover, the Centaur director would be named only to a one-year term.

National Intergroup directors voted the plan at a special meeting yesterday. One new director would be chosen from Centaur's slate and a second director would be selected from a list compiled with the advice of the company's institutional stockholders. The third seat would go to Robert King, president of FoxMeyer Drug, the company's wholesale drug-distribution unit. Mr. King has been tapped to succeed Howard M. Love, National Intergroup's chairman, when Mr. Love steps down early next year.

While Centaur's director would have only a one-year term, Mr. King would be a director for three years and the institutional representative for two years.

Centaur, in a statement, said it views National Intergroup's move as "a blatant, last-minute attempt to buy institutional support for their nominees."

Centaur rebuffed another board seat offer about a month ago, according to a National Intergroup executive. Centaur Partners, along with another dissident group, Walker Street Associates, owns 25% of National Intergroup and is seeking to elect three directors at the shareholder meeting on Wednesday.

This marks the second major move by National Intergroup to appease investors who have grown steadily impatient with the company's poor performance. Last month, the company said Mr. Love would step down as top executive and all operations, except FoxMeyer, would be sold.

While radical, those moves failed to appease Centaur. The group, urging a complete liquidation, noted that National Intergroup has had losses from continuing operations for seven years in a row. It also has questioned whether the company's proposed restructuring would result in shareholder dividends and why the board awarded Mr. Love a $300,000 bonus in 1990 and granted him options to buy 325,000 shares of stock at $16.80 each.

Yesterday, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, National Intergroup closed at $17.625, off 50 cents, on volume of 93,600 shares.

The two sides have been fighting since January. Centaur wanted to increase the size of the board to 15 and elect eight directors, effectively seizing control of the company. Although 54% of stockholders, including Centaur, backed the plan, it was defeated because an 80% majority is needed to amend bylaws.
