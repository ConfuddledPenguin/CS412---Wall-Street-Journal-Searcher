Credit Ratings: Credit Quality Declined At Record Pace in 1st Half

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/19/90

NEW YORK -- The credit quality of American companies fell at a record pace in the first half, as soft real-estate markets, weak junk-bond issuers and a sluggish economy undercut corporate creditworthiness, Moody's Investors Service Inc. said. Corporate downgrades led upgrades by a margin of 4.2 to 1 during the period. In 1989, downgrades led upgrades 2.5-1, and during the 1982 recession year they led 2.8-1, according to Moody's, a unit of Dun & Bradstreet Corp.

The credit-rating concern said there are signs that the quality of borrowers may start improving. Moody's said it's reviewing 41 companies for possible downgrade and 15 companies for upgrade. That ratio, 2.7-1, is below the 3-1 ratio of possible downgrades to possible upgrades on Moody's Watchlist at the start of the year.

"While both the economy and earnings growth may remain lackluster for the remainder of 1990, a recession that could put considerable stress on credit quality appears unlikely," said John Lonski, Moody's senior economist. "The frequency of downgrades to upgrades may thus have bottomed out, although there are few signs that any marked improvement in corporate creditworthiness is at hand," he said.

In the first six months of 1990, rating downgrades affected 217 companies and $235 billion of long-term debt, while ratings of 52 companies were raised, affecting $50.5 billion of debt.
