Homefed Reports $108.2 Million Loss For Second Quarter

  • Author: Charles McCoy
  • Date: 07/19/90

HomeFed Corp., stung by a surprisingly large increase in bad assets turned up by a special credit review, reported a second-quarter loss of $108.2 million, or $5.04 a share. San Diego-based HomeFed, which until recently had been considered fairly healthy, earned $28.1 million, or $1.30 a share, a year earlier.

HomeFed said it added $234 million to its reserve for loan losses in the quarter, increasing its reserves to $338 million at June 30, compared with $161 million at March 31. The swelling reserves reflected a leap in nonperforming assets, mainly bad loans and repossessed real estate, which totaled $708 million at June 30, or 3.38% of assets. HomeFed's nonperforming assets as of March 31 totaled $454 million, or 2.5% of assets.

HomeFed has been announcing surprise increases in bad assets on a monthly basis since April, when the thrift's nonperforming assets rose by $44 million. In May, HomeFed's bad assets jumped an additional $81 million, and last month it announced a special credit review it said was likely to turn up more bad assets. However, the size of the latest increase, $129 million, surprised even HomeFed management. "Sure it's a bit surprising," said Robert Adelizzi, president and chief executive officer. "The whole thing is a surprise, starting with April."

The bulk of HomeFed bad assets are outside of California and underscore how even relatively strong thrifts are being hammered by troubled real estate markets in Texas, Arizona and other states. They represent the apparent failure of HomeFed's strategy of expanding outside its California turf. "Normally, it's believed that geographic expansion reduces risk, but it didn't work for us," Mr. Adelizzi said. As a result, he said the thrift has halted almost all loan originations outside of California and Nevada.

Though most of the problems are in other states, some 37% of HomeFed's bad assets are in California, a level which raised the specter that the heretofore powerhouse California market may be beginning to sour. But Mr. Adelizzi said that market, while slowing, remains strong.

HomeFed's announcement came after the market closed. Still, rumors that the thrift might have bad news to report drilled its stock earlier in the day and reflected what management conceded was a loss of investor confidence. In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, HomeFed closed at $19, down $1.75.

HomeFed remains fairly well-capitalized, although the quarterly results trimmed its tangible capital to assets ratio to 3.68% from about over 5% as of early June.

Mr. Adelizzi said the special credit review that led to the big boost in reserves was HomeFed's idea and wasn't a response to any regulatory pressure. He said HomeFed feels comfortable with the current level of reserves but said the thrift couldn't guarantee that further additions might not be forthcoming.
