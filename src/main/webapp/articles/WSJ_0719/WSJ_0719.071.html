Giving a Hand -- John Neu Finds Where His Heart Is: It's in Homes for the Homeless

  • Author: John A. Conway
  • Date: 07/19/90

{From time to time in this column, The Wall Street Journal will report on efforts by people in business to help others in need.}

JOHN NEU, A MAJOR DEVELOPER of New Jersey real estate, is taking direct action to help the homeless -- he is giving them homes. Five of his prefabricated townhouses, installed two blocks from the New Jersey end of the Holland Tunnel on land donated by Newark's Roman Catholic Archdiocese, are now transitional housing for single parents and their children in Jersey City. In a second project, he will give away 25 more of these $50,000 three-bedroom, 1 1/2-bath prefabs to low-income families.

The developer's wife, Wendy, who is a former prison social worker, literally brought the problem home to her husband. In February 1989, she found a homeless woman on a street corner and took her to the couple's Hoboken home. During the woman's six-weeks' stay, John Neu learned firsthand the plight of the homeless.

Searching for a home for their new charge, the Neus became acquainted with the Newark Archdiocese's Catholic Community Services and Tom Catlaw, the church agency's director for Hudson County, which includes Jersey City. By this time, John Neu was determined to go further in helping the homeless. (He and his wife, incidentally, did eventually locate a place for their homeless guest.)

The archdiocese owned five empty lots in Jersey City, near one of its community centers. Mr. Neu had five unfinished prefab townhouses in a warehouse, repossessed from a failed customer. The combination was a natural. Mr. Catlaw and the archdiocese offered the five lots as a building site; Mr. Neu put the final touches on the prefabs and donated them. The church group raised most of the $115,000 needed for foundations, sidewalks, utilities connections and other site-preparation work.

In June, the archdiocese began using the new homes as transitional housing for families, most headed by single parents, already enrolled in programs for counseling, education and job training.

The townhouses represent the final phase of a three-part program for the homeless. This starts with emergency shelter for as long as 45 days, moving next into a transitional period of as long as six months, and finally, thanks to Mr. Neu, into a period of stabilization when families can count on a roof over their heads for as long as two years.

This process, Mr. Catlaw says, is designed to break a cycle of homelessness that finds many of the church agency's clients entering a second generation without a permanent home. They're "not necessarily on the street," he says, "but living with relatives or in shelters." After this new transitional stage, he says, "when children have been in the same school for two years," the families should be ready to move out on their own. The family heads, in turn, are expected to turn over part of whatever earnings they have to the archdiocese, which holds them in savings accounts against the day the families are ready to stand alone.

John Neu, in Mr. Catlaw's eyes, is "an incredible human being." He recalls that this past Christmas, the developer and his wife spent the day at one of the archdiocese's psychiatric centers, cooking and serving meals. Mr. Neu's company is Manhattan-based and he and his wife now live there as well, but his interests are New Jersey-oriented. The firm owns five million square feet of real estate there and a scrap business has an installation in Jersey City.

For Mr. Neu, this first gift is only the beginning. The next is designed to help the nearly homeless, families caught in that Catch-22 of too much income to qualify for housing subsidies and too little to afford a home.

Mr. Neu is now working with a new low-income project sponsored by another nonprofit Jersey City organization called Neighborhood Housing, which is rebuilding a 25-block area there. For this, he plans to buy and donate 25 additional prefab townhouses, which will be erected on land obtained from the city.
