Four Banks Post Lower 2nd-Quarter Net; Continental Reports a $56.7 Million Loss

  • Author: Jeff Bailey
  • Date: 07/19/90

Loan problems continue to dog the banking industry, as four big banks reported lower second-quarter profit and Continental Bank Corp. posted a $56.7 million loss. The depressed earnings at PNC Financial Corp., Shawmut National Corp., Fleet/Norstar Financial Group and First Interstate Bancorp are all the result of big portfolios of problem loans. Continental's troubled loans to highly leveraged borrowers, meanwhile, tripled during the quarter while income from corporate lending plunged 25%, reflecting the current doldrums in the commercial banking business.

"Where earnings go from here is a function of when we get out of this funk," said Robert Albertson, a bank stock analyst at Goldman Sachs & Co. in New York.

Continental's second-quarter loss also includes a $48 million pretax charge to cover the cost of cutting 900 jobs and retreating from some businesses, as well as some other one-time losses and a gain. But a spokeswoman said that, omitting such special items, Continental was essentially at "break-even" for the quarter.

The loss, which had been expected, was a steep slide from last's year second-quarter profit and illustrates the potential pitfalls of the Chicago-based company's strategy under Thomas C. Theobald of being a purely commercial bank. Continental's presence remains small in some commercial areas -- foreign exchange and bond trading, and equity investments -- that help sustain its competitors during periods of weak loan demand. And it has no consumer business.

Mr. Theobald said he remains committed to the strategy and blamed the "general slowdown in demand" for the poor quarter. The job and other cost cutting is expected to save $75 million a year, beginning next year, Continental has estimated.

Meanwhile, the financial bleeding from Continental's First Options of Chicago unit continued. For sale since last year, First Options' value was marked down another $9 million during the second quarter. That brings to well over a quarter billion dollars the losses from the options and futures clearing firm since Continental bought in it late 1986.

First Options had big losses during the 1987 stock market crash and its revenue has dwindled since.

Continental's nonperforming loans to highly leveraged borrowers, such as in leveraged buy-outs, tripled to $251 million at June 30 from $83 million at the beginning of the quarter. The bank wouldn't elaborate on the borrowers or their problems or say how leveraged loans are expected to perform in coming quarters.

While Continental's total nonperforming loans, at $673 million, are relatively low, it has a big highly leveraged portfolio, $2.9 billion at year-end 1989, in comparison to its shareholders equity of about $1.7 billion.

Continental's income from commercial lending and other corporate finance transactions fell 25% to $111 million in the second quarter, vs. $148 million a year ago. That reflects the smaller number of acquisitions and refinancings, as well as the fact that today's more conservative lending environment also makes deals less lucrative for banks. "The deals aren't yielding as much," a spokeswoman said.

PNC Financial Corp.

PNC Financial, based in Pittsburgh, as expected, said second-quarter profit fell 13% as domestic loans continued to deteriorate. It was the third consecutive quarter that PNC, known for years by its steady earnings growth, has seen profit fall.

The bank took a huge $168.3 million in net charge-offs, mostly foreign loans and domestic real estate loans. But new problem loans replaced the old ones charged off; since March nonperforming domestic loans have surged 16%, to $560 million.

"When you charge off that much and nonperformers don't go down, it shows how sticky the problem is," said Dennis F. Shea, analyst at Morgan Stanley & Co.

PNC increased its provisions for loan losses 88%, to $73.8 million, compared with the year-ago quarter. The jump follows an even bigger increase in provisions last quarter, but PNC said the moves are simply a sign of the times. "We have eliminated LCD debt and are aggressively writing down real estate values to dispose of these properties as quickly as possible," said Thomas H. O'Brien, PNC's chairman and chief exeuctive officer.

First Interstate Bancorp

First Interstate said profit would have been flat absent a special tax credit in the 1989 quarter. The company cited improved credit quality: nonperforming assets declined to $1.88 billion at June 30 from $2.24 billion a year earlier. Its provision for loan losses in the quarter declined to $117.7 million from $166.5 million.

Much of the credit turnaround reflects a nearly tripling of profit in operations at its Texas bank over the first quarter. "We continue to make steady progress in improving key areas of our business," said Edward Carson, chairman and chief executive officer.

Nonetheless, the Los Angeles-based banking concern still reported several sore spots in its interstate network. Its Arizona bank continued in the red. The company said its banks in California, Oregon, Washington and Nevada continue to "perform well," although profit from consumer and middle-market lending operations at those banks was essentially flat from the first-quarter level.

And profit at its other 17 banks, primarily in the Rocky Mountains, tumbled to $1.5 million from $16.8 million a year earlier, reflecting increased loan-loss reserves at its Denver bank.

Per-share net income fell more significantly because of a major stock offering in February.

Shawmut National Corp.

Shawmut National's results were hurt by the continuing slump in the New England economy, and its troubled real estate sector. Nonperforming loans were up, and earning assets were down.

Nonperforming loans and foreclosed property rose to $1.31 billion, or 7.72% of the total, from $1.16 billion, or 6.76%, as of March 31. Shawmut's reserves for possible loan losses were $748.3 million at the end of the second quarter compared with $287 million at the end of the year-ago period.

The latest second-quarter's profit included an extraordinary credit of $5.1 million from a tax loss carry-forward. Total assets were $26.6 billion at the end of the quarter, down 4.8% from the year-earlier period.

Fleet/Norstar Financial

Fleet/Norstar's nonperforming loans rose to $756 million from $653 million at the end of the first quarter.

Fleet's loan-loss provision for the second quarter was $90 million, nearly three times the year-ago provision. Its total loan-loss reserve rose to $618 million.

The second quarter neverthless was a rebound from a $98 million loss in the first quarter due to a big increase in the loan loss reserves. Fleet said its overall performance was aided by a 31% increase in net income of its financial services group to $31.2 million or more than half the bank holding company's net income.
