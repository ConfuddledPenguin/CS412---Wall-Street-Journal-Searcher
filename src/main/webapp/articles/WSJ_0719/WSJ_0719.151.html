Politics & Policy: S&L Cleanup Law's Measures Weakened By Court Rulings, Relaxed Regulations

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 07/19/90

WASHINGTON -- As political accusations over the savings-and-loan mess build, the tough measures in last year's S&L cleanup law intended to ward off a future disaster are quietly being undermined by court rulings and eased thrift regulations. The S&L cleanup law, passed last August, forced thrifts to abandon accounting rules that had allowed them to hide losses for years. The law also wiped out contracts between the government and buyers of S&Ls in which buyers believed they had special dispensation to continue using the accounting rules. But in isolated cases, both are being weakened.

"I think it's significant," says Karen Shaw of the Institute for Strategy Development, a Washington banking consultant. "Those are relatively unrelated events decided for different reasons, but they are happening."

In return for hitting up taxpayers for $130 billion -- not including hundreds of billions of dollars more in interest -- the S&L cleanup law racheted up capital standards. The capital represents the investment that thrift shareholders have at risk, and it acts as a cushion to protect the taxpayer-backed deposit insurance fund.

But already, four federal judges have blocked the Office of Thrift Supervision from seizing insolvent institutions, based on contracts that those thrifts had with the federal government. And recently, the thrift office delayed a provision of the S&L bailout law that limited a thrift's exposure to loss from any one borrower. The thrift office then delayed for review a proposed regulation to increase the amount of capital to be held for certain loans backed by the Department of Veterans Affairs.

The thrift office, which contends that capital standards haven't been eroded, said it was appealing the four cases. A spokesman said that the office has prevailed in four suits filed by other thrifts seeking to block federal takeover.

"The Office of Thrift Supervision believes that capital is king," Director Timothy Ryan said yesterday. "We will do nothing to erode the (cleanup law) capital standards, which we view as the most important provision of the statute."

The most recent court ruling was Monday, when federal Judge Thomas G. Hull in Tennessee issued a permanent injunction blocking regulators from seizing Franklin Federal Savings Bank of Morristown, Tenn., with $107 million in assets.

The court ruled that the thrift had a solid contract with the government in which the S&L was permitted to count certain losses as capital and therefore couldn't be seized as insolvent.

Some industry-watchers say the courts are just recognizing what they have contended for the past year -- that Congress, in a vengeful mood over the costs of the cleanup, went too far. They say that the law set standards too high and that the thrift office is inflexible. The Resolution Trust Corp., the new bailout agency, has so many insolvent thrifts to sell that, to cut its losses, it is trying to find buyers for failing S&Ls even before they are seized.

"You can sit in your ivory tower and dictate what should be true, but when you are out there in the trenches, you have to recognize reality," says Richard Kneipper, a Dallas thrift attorney.

Seven other court cases contesting capital rules are pending in federal courts across the country, and more are certain to be filed. Attorneys say that many of the suits are frivolous but that some thrifts make a compelling case that they were induced by the government to acquire failing S&Ls, based on the special capital provisions.

"The law was passed in a spirit of blunt-edged machismo," says Ms. Shaw. "The regulators are now in between a very hard rock and a very hard place."
