Chrysler Corp. Sees Negative Cash Flow In 1990 on Weak Sales

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/19/90

DETROIT -- Chrysler Corp. expects to end 1990 with negative cash flow for the second straight year because earnings from its sagging sales aren't covering its heavy spending for badly needed new products. Robert A. Lutz, president of the company's Chrysler Motors unit, said that the No. 3 U.S. auto maker will likely have "slightly negative cash flow this year," which will turn into break-even cash flow in 1991. He didn't give specific figures, but industry analysts said Chrysler could see its consolidated cash flow fall as much as $1 billion this year.

Chrysler managed to alleviate much of the pressure on its cash situation earlier this year. A sale and lease-back deal on its Auburn Hills, Mich., technical center -- which is still under construction -- raised $1.1 billion for this year. And the sale of its Gulfstream Aerospace unit added more than $800 million.

Chrysler common closed yesterday at $16, down 62.5 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

A big reason for Chrysler's negative cash flow this year is its plan to spend $3.1 billion on new products and other capital improvements. The spending is part of the company's goal of spending $15.6 billion through 1994 to revamp its vehicle lineup and improve facilities.

While spending has increased significantly, weak sales are driving down the company's earnings. Through the end of June, Chrysler's domestic new-car sales were off 15% from a year ago, while its domestic new-truck sales fell 13%.

Analysts are forecasting Chrysler will report second-quarter earnings of $138 million to $185 million, or 60 cents to 85 cents a share. In the 1989 second quarter, Chrysler had net income of $341 million, or $1.46 a share.
