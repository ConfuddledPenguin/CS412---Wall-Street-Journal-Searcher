Oil Spill Occurs At Exxon Dock Off New York City

  • Author: Allanna Sullivan
  • Date: 07/19/90

NEW YORK -- Two barges collided at an Exxon Corp. loading dock, spilling 40,000 gallons of heating oil into a busy shipping channel in New York Harbor. The accident occurred when one of the barges, which had finished loading at Exxon's Bayway refinery, was being maneuvered by two tug boats into the Arthur Kill shipping channel and rammed another barge that was unloading nearby. No Exxon personnel were involved.

Oil spewed from a gash at least 15 feet long as the vessel was returned to the dock. Half of the spilled oil escaped into the Arthur Kill before booms to contain it could be laid out.

The barges and tug boats are owned by Maritrans Partners L.P. of Philadelphia. Maritran Chairman Stephan Van Dyck, visiting the site of the accident yesterday, said his company is taking full responsibility for causing the accident and would assume the full cost of cleanup.

Regulators said that fines could run into hundreds of thousands of dollars.

The incident marks the 13th time oil has been dumped into the busy harbor this year. Three of the spills have been considered major, exceeding 100,000 gallons of oil. At least three have occurred at or near Exxon's Bayway refinery, including a massive spill of more than 500,000 gallons in January, when an underwater pipeline at the refinery ruptured.

The refinery was closed from late March until June for an inspection to see if there was anything inherently wrong with the facility or its operations. The results of that study identified some operating problems and established procedures to correct them.

Coast Guard Lt. Raymond Maher said he doubted that yesterday's collision would spark a re-examination of operations at the refinery.

Although only one of 10 storage tanks on the barge was ruptured, the entire cargo was unloaded at the dock. A Massachusetts company called Astro-Line owns the oil, which it had just purchased from Exxon.

The spill didn't appear to cause significant damage to the marine environment, according to a Coast Guard spokeswoman, and booms were put around sensitive areas to keep the oil out. "Because it's light oil, much of it will evaporate quickly," she said.

However, that evaporation may have caused an air-quality violation, according to Anthony McMahon, an official with New Jersey's Department of Environmental Protection. He explained that shortly after the accident, air-quality monitors near the refinery registered a jump in the ozone level.

Although regulators expressed frustration with yesterday's incident, they said the response was impressive. Shortly after the spill occurred, a cleanup crew of 170 was on the scene. Three Coast Guard boats showed up, and three skimmers were scooping up the spilled fuel. Five vacuum trucks along the shoreline were removing oil from the water as well. At some other oil spills, Mr. McMahon said, "it's taken up to 20 hours to get a skimmer on the scene."
