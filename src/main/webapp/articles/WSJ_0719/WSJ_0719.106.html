UAL Airline Unit's Non-Union Workers Accept Wage Concessions to Aid Buy-Out

  • Author: Judith Valente and Randall Smith
  • Date: 07/19/90

In a boost for the employee effort to buy UAL Corp., United Airlines' 26,000 non-union workers agreed to provide wage concessions to help finance the buy-out. Support from the non-union workers offers a sign of progress when the buy-out group's efforts to obtain bank financing appear to be moving slowly. Only 3 1/2 weeks remain until the Aug. 9 financing deadline.

For the moment, bank negotiations seem to be taking a back seat as the buy-out group, led by the unions representing United's pilots, machinists and flight attendants, concentrates on trying to obtain capital for the $4.38 billion transaction from various airline suppliers.

The group has contacted a number of companies to provide financing, including aircraft makers Boeing Co. and Airbus Industrie; engine makers General Electric Co., Rolls-Royce PLC and the Pratt & Whitney unit of United Technologies Corp.; and aircraft leasing companies and European airlines.

In an interview, Gerald Greenwald, the buy-out group's leader, confirmed that talks are continuing with several suppliers. He declined to comment further, saying the negotiations are "in a very delicate time."

The agreement with the non-union workers should help the financing effort. Banks had expressed concern about the prospect of labor discord at the airline if the non-union workers didn't participate. The accord with the non-union employees "eliminates a negative," said one person familiar with the effort.

The reservations agents, customer-service representatives and middle-level managers who make up the non-union group agreed to one-time wage concessions amounting to $45.9 million, or an average 5.9% pay cut. The group also gave up certain premium pay and rarely used medical benefits valued at an additional $4.6 million in cost savings.

In return, the non-union workers will receive a 14.26% stake in Chicago-based UAL, as well as some improvements in their pension plan. In all, UAL employees have agreed to provide $300 million in wage concessions for the first year and a total of $2 billion over five years if the buy-out succeeds.

The non-union workers agreed to support the buy-out after Mr. Greenwald assured them in a letter that their accord with the unions would become part of the actual agreement with UAL.

Just a month ago, the non-contract workers had threatened to upset the buy-out effort unless the unions met its conditions. In a statement yesterday, the group said it concluded that "the interest of all employees are best served by trying to work together." In an unusual move, UAL hired First Chicago Corp. and the law firm of Fried Frank Shriver Harris & Jacobson several months ago to advise the non-contract employees on the buy-out.

Mr. Greenwald disagreed with assessments that the financing effort is moving slowly. "This process is on track with my own timetable," he said.

The former Chrysler Corp. vice chairman said the unions are currently working to develop "very definitive {financing} proposals with some banks so that we can go with definitive proposals to a larger group of banks."

He added that the banks are reviewing large amounts of information so that they can "decide for themselves whether or not to make {financing} commitments."

Mr. Greenwald said he felt that the current financing deadline of Aug. 9 "has always been a little tight," suggesting the buy-out group may seek an extension. He said, however, there are currently no plans to ask for one.

Five major New York banks -- Citicorp, Chemical Banking Corp., Manufacturers Hanover Corp., Bankers Trust New York Corp. and Chase Manhattan Corp. -- agreed two weeks ago to assist in the financing effort. But the banks want the unions to obtain subordinated financing from other sources before they agree to commit bank funds.

The extra layer of junior financing would give the banks more comfort about the safety of their investment.

Separately, the buy-out group also declined to comment on whether United's pilots union had agreed to use $200 million from one of its pension funds as equity for the transaction. Pilot leaders concluded a three-day meeting yesterday but apparently arrived at no decision on the pension fund.
