Politics & Policy: Republicans Seek Guarantees From Democrats On Spending as Part of Deficit-Reduction Plan

  • Author: David Wessel
  • Date: 07/19/90

WASHINGTON -- When the subject of the budget process came up at the deficit talks recently, Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady was out of town, Budget Director Richard Darman came in late and Rep. Dan Rostenkowski (D., Ill.) slipped out early to go to a ball game. Clearly, "budget process" is a topic that bores even the most devoted of the budget negotiators. In two months of talks, they've devoted precisely one day to it.

But don't be fooled. Changing the process is critical to getting a budget accord because the White House has made it a top priority. Many congressional Republicans, convinced that past bipartisan accords have failed to limit spending, agree. The House Republican Whip, Newt Gingrich of Georgia, told fellow negotiators the other day that if he had to walk out of the room uttering the "T-word," they would have to utter the "C-word" -- control of spending.

What President Bush and the Republicans are looking for is some guarantee that any tax increase will go to reduce the deficit -- not to fuel more spending. That means pushing Democrats to establish in advance firm spending totals for defense and domestic programs for the next five years, and then finding a way to make that promise stick. "Whatever we come to agreement on would have to be enforceable on a multi-year basis," says Mr. Darman. "That has never been possible before."

In speeches, Mr. Bush has highlighted the need for a line-item veto -- a change favored by conservatives that would give the president the power to pluck wasteful porkbarrel projects out of massive spending bills.

But the line-item veto isn't considered a realistic solution. In private, Bush administration officials concede that Congress isn't about to surrender so much power to the president. Moreover, it isn't clear that the veto would be much help in cutting the deficit. "It's symbolic," says political scientist Thomas Mann of the Brookings Institution, a Washington think tank. "The president would find it extraordinarily difficult to do anything with the line-item veto that would add up to numbers that would make any difference as far as the deficit is concerned."

Efforts to change the budget process to enforce a bipartisan deficit-reduction accord are undeniably arcane. "The public doesn't understand the process, and you can't explain it to them," says Sen. Bennett Johnston (D., La.). But the process can be immensely important. The Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law has been driving tax and spending policy for the past five years. And because any change in the process tends to alter the balance of power in Congress, the resistance to altering the budget process is substantial.

Any agreement at the budget talks to raise taxes or save money on benefit programs like Medicare or Social Security will be written into law and won't require new enforcement mechanisms. But the spending levels for the Pentagon and for domestic programs ranging from space to sewers are less certain. Those spending levels are set annually and, as a result, the congressional appropriations committees can easily undo any agreements made by the summiteers.

Republicans want guarantees that that won't happen. "I want assurance that, but for absolutely unexpected events, the end result is achieved," says Sen. Pete Domenici of New Mexico, senior Republican on the Senate Budget Committee.

Participants in the talks say Mr. Darman has proposed fixing by law five years of spending totals for defense, overall domestic programs and foreign aid, a step that basically would prevent any changes to the summit understandings without the president's consent.

An alternative plan being circulated by the private Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget would alter the Gramm-Rudman law to target automatic spending cuts specifically at appropriations subcommittees that overspend their allocations. A subcommittee that overshot its target one year would be penalized the following year. The Gramm-Rudman deficit targets would be dropped in favor of the new spending ceilings.

Democrats are resistant to all these proposals. "I don't want to be a party to any mechanism that diminishes the constitutional authority of Congress to control spending bills," says Senate Budget Committee Chairman James Sasser (D., Tenn.). Some Democrats say they might go along with new parliamentary hurdles for spending bills that exceed summit-set totals, but the summit isn't likely to resolve the process issues until after it strikes a compromise on a package of spending cuts and tax increases.

Much of the disagreement isn't about how much the government spends in total, but what the money goes for. The administration, for instance, wants the summit to set a floor under defense spending to protect it from attempts to make deeper cuts in the future. Some Democrats complain that the world is changing too fast to lock in a five-year commitment now.

In the end, though, the issue is one of power rather than policy. Democratic and Republican members of appropriation committees cherish their role in divvying up the federal spending pie. Negotiators from both sides expect Sen. Robert Byrd (D., W.Va.) and Rep. Jamie Whitten (D., Miss.), chairmen of the appropriations committees, to be among the last obstacles to an agreement on writing rules to enforce a summit agreement.

Even convening the ad hoc congressional-White House summit steps on their toes. Indeed, Rep. Whitten occasionally rises from his chair at the summit talks -- the only participant who stands to speak -- and protests that the entire enterprise is improperly encroaching on his turf.
