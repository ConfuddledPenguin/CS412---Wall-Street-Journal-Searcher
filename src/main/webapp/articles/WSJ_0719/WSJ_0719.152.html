Marketing & Media: Gannett, New York Times Net Declined In 2nd Quarter, Reflecting Drop in Ads

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 07/19/90

NEW YORK -- Two major newspaper publishers reported lower earnings for the second quarter, reflecting a nationwide slump in advertising so persistent it is now hurting one of the industry's best performers. Gannett Co. and the New York Times Co., publishers of daily newspapers in major cities as well as smaller markets, had been expected to report lower earnings. Yet while the falling ad market has battered the industry for at least a year, Arlington, Va.-based Gannett's results were especially noteworthy: It was the first time since going public in 1967 that it reported a quarterly earnings drop from the comparable period a year earlier.

Gannett earnings were $105.3 million, or 66 cents per share, compared with $112.1 million, or 70 cents, a year earlier. Revenue fell 1% to $893.8 million from $904.2 million.

Gannett sold a radio station in Seattle, but it represented only a "minor" gain, the company said.

Gannett executives blamed "continuing restructuring in the retail industry and a deteriorating consumer economy" for stalling the revenue growth. Major department stores, some hit by bankruptcy reorganizations and consolidations, are traditionally among Gannett's largest advertisers.

Troubling for all newspapers is that classified advertising continues its fall, after years of robust growth in the 1980s. Gannett said classified volume at most of its 83 daily newspapers declined for cars, real estate and help wanted.

At Gannett's flagship, USA Today, the number of ad pages in the first six months fell 9% to 1,780. Gannett said, though, that ad rate increases and higher circulation resulted in a revenue gain of 3% for the first half. Gannett's outdoor advertising unit, the largest billboard company in North America, reported an increase in revenue but the broadcasting division, including radio and TV stations, reported a decline.

Peter Appert, analyst for C.J. Lawrence, Morgan Grenfell Inc., called Gannett's drop in revenue troubling. "Earnings will continue to be under pressure if revenues continue in this range," he said. "And there's nothing in the next quarter that says it will turn around."

For the six months, Gannett said net fell 4% to $180.3 million, or $1.13 a share, from $187.0 million, or $1.16 a share. Revenue for the first six months dropped 1% to $1.7 billion.

At the New York Times Co., still reeling from slumping ad linage at its flagship daily, the earnings drop wasn't as steep as some analysts had expected.

The company reported earnings of $26.8 million, or 35 cents a share, compared with $39.2 million, or 50 cents a share, a year earlier. Revenue for the quarter rose to $468.9 million from $460.3 million.

"We thought they might come in at about 27 cents, so it's better than we expected," said Jeffrey Russell, an analyst for Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co. "They are holding the margins as well as they can, considering the poor ad climate in New York."

New York Times executives said the earnings drop was largely a result of lower ad linage at the Times, brought on by a weak New York area economy. Earnings also suffered from the acquisition of McCall's magazine, and increased depreciation and interest expense related to completion of the Times's color printing plant in Edison, N.J. and a newly constructed facility for the Gwinnett (Ga.) Daily News.

About 60% of the loss in ad linage at the Times was in classified ads for help wanted, real estate and automotive.

For the six months, net income was $43.4 million, or 57 cents a share, compared with $70.7 million, or 90 cents a share, last year. Revenue for the period grew by 2% to $900.1 million.
