MCI 2nd Quarter Net Climbed 18%, But Revenue Data Cause Stock to Fall

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 07/19/90

NEW YORK -- The grueling long-distance phone battle is starting to wear on American Telephone & Telegraph Co.'s rivals as the war shifts from price-cutting to costlier salesmanship. Long-distance rates, which have fallen almost 45% since 1984, are stabilizing as evidenced by recent price boosts on certain consumer and business services by market leader American Telephone & Telegraph Co.

There also is a growing reluctance by MCI Communications Corp., AT&T's principal nemesis, to cut price at the expense of profits.

MCI yesterday posted an 18% gain in second-quarter earnings, but its stock was hammered after revenue growth fell below expectations. Net income was $177 million, or 67 cents a share, compared with $150 million, or 56 cents, the year before. Revenue rose 17% to $1.87 billion from $1.59 billion.

MCI's shares dropped $1.875 to $36.50 in national over-the-counter trading after trading as low as $36.125 during the day.

MCI said revenue growth was strong in the residential and big business sectors but slower in the general business sector.

Daniel Akerson, MCI's chief financial officer, said that during the second quarter, the company was preparing to roll out MCI Vision, a product that offers to small and medium-sized businesses some voice and data transmission services previously only available to big businesses. Also during the quarter, he said, MCI froze hiring in the spring to avoid sizable layoffs after MCI acquires Telecom USA. The acquisition is expected to win regulatory approval soon and could be completed this summer. Telecom USA's major strength is in the general business sector.

Mr. Akerson, who heads the effort to combine the two companies, said MCI still won market share in the general business segment but was "willing to accept a slower rate of growth to facilitate the integration" with Telecom USA. "We didn't have a product as competitive or as targeted as we have today," Mr. Akerson said, referring to MCI Vision. "Without the right weapon -- or product -- and a full team on the field, then you might hold for a quarter."

AT&T is expected to post lower second-quarter earnings today. Nonetheless, Sprint's and MCI's results could pose a sticky timing problem for AT&T. The company has been trying to convince the Federal Communications Commission that the industry is sufficiently competitive and that its classification as "dominant" carrier should be dropped in favor of looser regulation.

On Tuesday, Sprint, which has traditionally been the telephone industry's biggest price discounter, posted a $42 million second-quarter operating loss. This caused its parent United Telecommunications Inc.'s second-quarter net to drop 55% to $40.6 million, or 18 cents a share. Revenue rose 12% to $2.07 billion.

United Telecommunications' stock is still reeling from the Sprint debacle: Yesterday, its shares closed at $27.25, down $2.25 in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, after falling 24%, or $8.75, to close at $29.50 on Tuesday.

United was also the volume leader, with more than 11.8 million shares traded Tuesday and 10 million shares yesterday. MCI led in volume in over-the-counter trading yesterday with about 13 million shares traded.

Sprint and MCI are pushing to cut costs, but costs may be the least of their problems, said Jack B. Grubman, analyst at PaineWebber Inc. He said AT&T's size and market presence makes it easier for the company to cut costs while keeping marketing outlays high.

"In a starvation diet, the fat lady wins," said Mr. Grubman. "This is going to squeeze competitors to death."

Robert B. Morris III, analyst at Goldman Sachs & Co., noted that "Sprint has its eye on one ball -- cost-cutting -- while the industry has moved into an intensified mode of marketing and sales."

AT&T and MCI have moved aggressively on Sprint's bread-and-butter accounts: consumer and small business. To win these accounts over, AT&T and MCI have fueled their sales and marketing with new pricing promotions and advertisements about quality and service reliability aimed at consumer and business customers for domestic and international long-distance calls.

"Right now our goal is to stabilize our market share,"said Victor A. Pelson, senior vice president and group executive of communications services at AT&T. "We're on track with regard to residential and business customers."
