The Latest Crime Figures Indicate Staying at Home Isn't Much Safer

  • Author: Damon Darlin
  • Date: 07/19/90

SEOUL, South Korea -- Boy Scouts should "Be Prepared," but need they pack riot gear for the 17th World Scout Jamboree in Korea next year? A lot of U.S. moms and dads seem to think so. They believe Korea is just too dangerous, even though their Scouts have probably already survived subzero camping, undercooked food and rope-bridge crossings. The parents have seen coverage of bomb-throwing anti-U.S. student protesters and don't want their teen-agers earning merit badges in riot control.

So organizers of the giant camping trip, held roughly every four years, are having trouble persuading U.S. Scouts to camp in the shadow of Mount Sorak. During the Aug. 8-16, 1991, event, Scouts from European socialist countries may make their first appearance at a world jamboree. Scouts from 121 other countries will be there, with Japanese Scouts in record numbers. And some Girl Scouts will brave it.

But so far only about 2,000 U.S. Scouts have asked for applications. That compares with 3,000 -- at least -- that jamboree officials in Seoul were hoping to get, and the 7,000-strong contingent the U.S. sent to Japan in 1971. "There is still a perception of Korea that it is not a garden spot," says Terry R. Hanna, a prominent Oklahoma City attorney and Scout leader who is drumming up interest in the jamboree.

So supporters have made a video showing Korea at its best, sent letters to Scout councils around the nation and asked businessmen who have visited Korea to assure parents about its safety and hospitality. They point out that Korea isn't another Iran, where the 1979 world jamboree had to be canceled when Islamic revolutionaries overthrew the Shah. "It's not a life or death matter, but I want the Boy Scouts of America to be represented here," says Kim Kyu Young, secretary general of the Boy Scouts of Korea.

The U.S. news media are being blamed for the fears about safety, because isolated incidents have at times made it appear that the entire country was in flames. In reality, protests -- small and almost always contained on college campuses by riot police -- are dwindling, and no American has ever been attacked by Korean protesters.

In Korea, Doug Smith, a 19-year-old Eagle Scout from Portland, Ore., and volunteer worker for the jamboree, concedes that his parents initially were worried about his safety. "But they came out and visited and saw it was OK," he says.
