Technology: IBM to Join Ranks of Firms Developing Computers That Recognize Handwriting

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 07/19/90

International Business Machines Corp. gave a boost to the fledgling market for computers that recognize handwriting, announcing it plans to build electronic pads that will allow mobile workers uncomfortable with conventional computers to take handwritten notes. Big Blue said that rather than develop the basic software for such note-pad computers itself, it will rely on a tiny California company to supply it. The company, Go Corp., is attempting to establish its basic software as the standard for electronic note pads, which are expected by some observers to make up a major part of the computer industry by the mid-1990s.

IBM's decision to rely on Go marks a departure for the world's biggest computer maker. For the past decade, Big Blue has relied almost entirely on Microsoft Corp. to supply it with the basic operating software for its families of personal computers.

"I don't think this diminishes the role of Microsoft," said Richard Shaffer, editor of Technologic Computer Letter. "It just makes good business sense. IBM can't possibly want all of its computing future in the hands of one company."

Microsoft is developing its own software to take advantage of handwriting recognition, but IBM says it won't use such software for its electronic note pads because Microsoft aims to keep the software compatible with DOS, the standard operating system for many personal computers.

M.J. Quinlan, the IBM executive who crafted the licensing deal with Go, said he didn't think note-pad computers would reach their intended market of computer novices "by just extending existing software environments such as DOS." He said that handwriting devices required new operating software and that DOS wasn't acceptable because it was designed to be used with a keyboard.

Microsoft disagrees. "There's nothing about DOS that says it can't support {handwriting} input," said Jeff Raikes, who oversees the company's note-pad software project. Mr. Raikes added that he felt that in licensing Go's software "IBM is simply doing what they have always done -- investing in many different technologies." He said that such investments don't always result in IBM products and that he still believed Big Blue might embrace Microsoft's approach to the note-pad field.

IBM's endorsement figures to help Go in its efforts to persuade other computer makers to adopt its handwriting software. "IBM is the best possible strategic partner from those purposes," Mr. Shaffer said.

Jerry Kaplan, Go's chief executive, said the company hasn't signed any other licensing deals yet, but that it is in negotiations to do so with several other companies.

Not everyone is persuaded that a new operating software is needed for note-pad computers. Tandy Corp.'s Grid Systems Corp. subsidiary, which is the only U.S. company currently selling note-pad computers, relies on DOS for its basic software. "Right now, that's an easier way to go, for the vendor and the customer, because both are familiar with DOS," said Alan Lefkof, president of Grid.

However, Mr. Lefkof conceded that "maybe the Go and IBM {approach}, once we all see it, will be fabulous. Then we might license it."

Mr. Quinlan wouldn't say when IBM plans to introduce its first note-pad computers, but said that a team devoted to the product had been created in the company's Boca Raton, Fla., facility and that major customers were working with IBM on figuring out just how these new devices might best be used.

The technology underlying note-pad computers has been under development for years, and even today the means of recognizing handwriting is primitive. Grid's note pad, for instance, only recognizes neatly printed capital letters, forcing users to write in an unnatural fashion. Still, the company said it shipped 1,000 units last month.

IBM's interest in the market seems likely to heighten interest in it. Apple Computer Inc. and several start-up companies are considering entering the market, and at least two Japanese companies are selling such devices in Japan.
