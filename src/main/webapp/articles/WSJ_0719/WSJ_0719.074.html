REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Baker's Dilemma

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/19/90

U.S. Secretary of State James Baker emerged yesterday from a meeting in Paris with Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze with what's been greeted as a major policy shift. He announced that the U.S. would drop its recognition of the Cambodian resistance and start talking with Vietnam about how to solve Cambodia's miseries. He explained, "We want to talk to Vietnam because we think it has influence with the Cambodian government to create conditions for elections." Well, it's no secret that Indochina's political byways can be confusing. And clearly Mr. Baker has in mind a worthy goal: preventing the return of the Khmer Rouge, who conducted genocidal terrors from the time of the U.S. withdrawal from Indochina until the Vietnamese conquest of Cambodia. Our own sources in Thailand support the State Department's conclusion that right now the Khmer Rouge, who have been cultivating a village-level support network in the rural countryside, stand a good chance of again seizing power. The two non-communist Cambodian resistance groups, led by Prince Sihanouk and former Premier Son Sann, are far weaker and less well-organized. While they have a claim to Western support as the only conceivably democratic alternative, in fact they may well be doing little more than smoothing the way for the Khmer Rouge to make a hideous comeback in Phnom Penh.

But this is not a game that pits the bad Khmer Rouge against the good Vietnamese-backed regime of Premier Hun Sen, himself a renegade Khmer Rouge official. Both players are monstrous villains by the lights of civilized conduct. It is 12 years of Vietnamese occupation that have made the vicious Khmer Rouge, for all too many Cambodians, the only viable defenders of the Cambodian nation. And despite the announced pullout last fall, there have been reliable reports of the continued presence of Vietnamese troops.

In any event, Vietnam's Communists have never, anywhere, ever, done anything to create conditions for democracy -- not even in Vietnam, and certainly not in Laos or Cambodia. The idea that Vietnam is now going to help set up democracy in Cambodia is one of the strangest we've heard yet. Cambodia presents Mr. Baker with a choice of devils, but there's little point in avoiding one by leaping into the arms of the other. For the moment, Cambodia may be one of the world's impossible problems.

It could be, of course, that the biggest shift here is really an unstated U.S. step toward normalizing relations with Vietnam. This would be quite a coup for Hanoi, which has conceded nothing. Vietnam's Communists continue to run one of the world's most repressive states, beggaring their own people, bullying their neighbors and hosting a massive Soviet military base at Cam Ranh Bay. Does America really want to broadcast the message that these folks are now its chosen champions in Indochina?

When it comes to helping Cambodia, indeed, we have a radical thought. Granted that Vietnam sets the tenor for Indochina, wouldn't the best hope for democracy in the dominoes lie in holding real elections in Vietnam? Maybe Mr. Baker and Mr. Shevardnadze could make that their next project.
