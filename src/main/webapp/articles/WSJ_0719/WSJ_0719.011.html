Dividend News: Conrail Lifts Payout 14%, Posts 9.8% Drop In 2nd-Quarter Profit

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 07/19/90

PHILADELPHIA -- Consolidated Rail Corp. increased its quarterly dividend 14%, as a big reduction in shares outstanding improved its per-share net income. The freight railroad boosted the payout to 40 cents a share from 35 cents, payable Sept. 14 to stock of record Aug. 31.

Though Conrail posted a 9.8% decline in second-quarter net income to $74 million from $82 million a year earlier, earnings per share increased 35% to $1.61 from $1.19. Profit on a fully diluted basis was $1.46 a share in the latest period.

Revenue declined 2.3% to $861 million from $881 million.

The company said the number of common shares outstanding as of June 30 was 38% lower than that of a year earlier, accounting for the improvement in quarterly earnings per share.

Graeme Lidgerwood, an analyst at First Boston Corp., said: "Conrail is generating substantial cash flow in excess of its capital spending requirements. In addition, the company is paying dividends on fewer shares outstanding, which makes additional cash available to boost the dividend rate."

Conrail said it had an after-tax gain of about $9 million in the latest quarter, resulting from its previously reported sale of the Cockeysville, Md., industrial track to the Maryland Mass Transit Administration. But the company said the gain was more than offset by a $15 million reduction in net owing to the financing of the first quarter's $1.1 billion tender offer.

James Hagen, Conrail's chairman, president and chief executive officer, said that overall traffic levels in the second quarter were "essentially flat," and that the company continued to be subject to pricing pressure and an unfavorable traffic mix. For example, Conrail hauled relatively higher volumes of low-rated commodities, such as coal, and lower volumes of high-rated freight, such as autos.

In New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday, Conrail shares closed at $50.25, down 75 cents.
