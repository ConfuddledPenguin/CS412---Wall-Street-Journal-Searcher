Simon to Re-Enter Buy-Out Arena in Fund With Tokio Marine

  • Author: James A. White
  • Date: 07/19/90

NEW YORK -- Simon says buy-outs are back. After four years of sitting on the sidelines of the leveraged buy-out field that made him millions, William E. Simon is getting ready to re-enter the game. This time, he will do it with a money chest provided by a Japanese insurance company and what he expects will be a "club" of other big investors from the U.S., Asia and Europe.

Mr. Simon and partner Gerald L. Parsky said that Tokio Marine & Fire Insurance Co. of Tokyo has invested $100 million in a partnership formed to take over all of the Simon-Parsky investment projects, except those involving savings and loan associations.

In turn, the new partnership, WSGP Partners, hopes to raise a pool of at least an additional $300 million from a group of no more than 10 pension funds, insurers and other institutions world-wide. This fund will target medium-sized companies for friendly takeovers, including some overseas. It also will invest in real estate ventures.

The investment by Tokio Marine, the world's largest property and casualty insurer, reflects the continuing hunger of Japanese financial institutions for U.S. deal-making and money-management skills. Most big Japanese brokerage firms, insurers and commercial banks have formed links with U.S. investment firms, either by buying stakes or setting up joint ventures.

Tokio Marine itself paid $42 million last year for a 10% stake in Delaware Management Holdings Inc., a Philadelphia-based money management firm. Wilshire Associates of Santa Monica, Calif., served as investment adviser to Tokio Marine in taking both the Delaware Management and Simon-Parsky stakes.

For Mr. Simon, a former Treasury secretary, the new partnership ends an absence from the leveraged buy-out arena, when he and Mr. Parsky concentrated on S&L and real estate investments for several years. Mr. Simon helped spark the buy-out boom of the 1980s with his hugely profitable buy-out and resale of Gibson Greetings Cards Inc. in 1982-83. He dropped out of the field when he believed skyrocketing deal prices no longer made buy-outs attractive.

This may change, with the demise of the high-yield, below-investment-grade "junk" bond market and companies' running into trouble because of debt-heavy balance sheets, according to Messrs. Parsky and Simon. "We both believe that realistic prices are coming and that there will be opportunity for acquisitions from some of the overleveraged {buy-out} transactions" done by others, Mr. Parsky said.

Deals financed by the WSGP buy-out fund will probably involve as much as 30% equity, compared with as little as 5% or 10% in the heyday of the highly leveraged buy-outs. The fund itself might provide 10% of the equity for a particular investment, while fund investors would get the option to supply the remainder directly, as well as get first crack at financing the debt for the deal, Mr. Parsky said.

Tokio Marine declined to disclose terms of its investment pact with Messrs. Simon and Parsky. Mr. Parsky said the insurer received a minority stake and won't be involved in running the partnership, though the company will be active in helping set investment strategies.

At $300 million, the WSGP Partners buy-out fund would be tiny, compared with such behemoths as the $5.6 billion leveraged buy-out pool raised by Kohlberg Kravis & Roberts & Co. But the "investment club" approach for the new fund would greatly expand its buying power because participants could elect to invest more money on a deal-by-deal basis.

This added degree of control could appeal to big pension funds, which were criticized by politicians during the buy-out boom for getting locked into controversial takeovers.

Mr. Parsky said the fund will target companies and divisions with purchase prices of $300 million to $2 billion. He said the fund isn't aimed at investing in troubled companies but will be in the market for operations being divested by cash-strapped companies.

In real estate, the fund will shun downtown "trophy" buildings in favor of buying less-visible properties, perhaps from banks and thrifts paring their portfolios. About $800 million in commercial and residential properties, mostly in Southern California and assembled by Messrs. Parsky and Simon through earlier partnerships, won't be transferred to WSGP Partners. But all new real estate purchases will be made through the new partnership, Mr. Parsky said.

He said that while the buy-out fund will be WSGP's main focus, the partnership with Tokio Marine hopes to raise a series of investment funds. Another one aimed at attracting pension funds as investors will put money into companies owned or being taken over by minority executives.

The new buy-out fund won't invest in S&Ls, an area in which Messrs. Parsky and Simon remain active and for which they continue to try to attract pension funds as investors for a separate investment pool. Mr. Parsky said Tokio Marine "wasn't interested" in getting involved in S&L investments.

The Simon-Parsky group, as previously reported, last year made a proposal to the Los Angeles County Employees Retirement Association and other big pension systems to form an S&L acquisition fund. This plan stalled, but Mr. Parsky said he and Mr. Simon recently presented a new proposal to potential investors, including the Los Angeles County fund and the California Public Employees Retirement System.

The new plan involves setting up the proposed fund using Marina Del Rey, Calif.-based Western Federal Savings & Loan Association, owned by the Simon-Parsky group, as a vehicle to buy other thrifts. The initial plan called for the fund to start buying thrifts from scratch. Mr. Parsky said the pension funds and their consultants haven't responded to the revised proposal yet.
