Chicago Markets Alter Plans to Halt Trading On Sharp Price Swings

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/19/90

CHICAGO -- The Chicago Mercantile Exchange and the Chicago Board of Trade modified their "circuit breaker" trading halts that would be activated by sharp one-day swings in stock and stock-index futures prices. The revisions were made in accordance with recent recommendations from a New York Stock Exchange panel on market volatility, and have been submitted to the Commodity Futures Trading Commission for approval. The changes are conditional on their acceptance by the Big Board and other stock and stock options exchanges, the Chicago futures exchanges said.

Wild price swings in stock-index futures prices were blamed by many for worsening the slide in stock prices during the October 1987 stock market crash and last October's mini-crash. Futures industry officials generally reject that notion, but have acceded to circuit breakers and other restrictions to avoid more onerous oversight from federal regulators.

Trading would be halted for one to two hours in Standard & Poor's 500 stock-index futures trading at the Merc each time the S&P 500 contract moved up or down 12, 24, 36 and 44 index points. The Merc's current circuit breakers are triggered when the S&P 500 contract moves 12 and 20 points. A five-point price limit during the opening minutes of trading will remain in effect.

The Board of Trade revised circuit breakers for its Major Market Index of 20 blue-chip stocks to take effect at swings of 20, 40, 60 and 80 index points. The exchange's existing circuit breakers take effect at downward moves of 20, 30 and 50 index points, and as a result of an upward spike of 50 index points.

The stock-index future circuit breakers correspond with moves in the Dow Jones Industrial Average of 100, 200, 300 and 400 points.
