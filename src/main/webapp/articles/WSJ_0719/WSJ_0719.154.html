Foreign Exchange: Dollar Ends Little Changed as Dealers Are Confused by Greenspan Testimony

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 07/19/90

NEW YORK -- Confounded by the congressional testimony of U.S. Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan, foreign exchange dealers left the dollar about where they found it yesterday. After hectic, seesaw trading, the greenback was up strongly against the Swiss franc and marginally against other European currencies but was slightly lower against the Japanese yen.

At no point in his testimony before the Senate Banking Committee did Mr. Greenspan give a clear signal on the central issue of whether markets can expect lower U.S. interest rates.

The Fed chief confirmed that the recent quarter-point decline in the federal funds rate to 8% was the result of deliberate central bank action, but he said the move addressed a technical constriction rather than an economic stall.

Mr. Greenspan said the Fed wants to keep credit conditions "about where they are" and held out the possibility of further monetary easing to offset tightness in the market.

The central banker said the 0.5% rise in June consumer prices reported yesterday was "higher than we would like to see." He allowed that, for the U.S., the risk of recession is "a shade larger" than that of inflation but said the likelihood of a near-term recession "seems low."

Mr. Greenspan "is obviously dancing on the edge of a very teetery fence," said Robert A. Brusca, chief economist for Nikko Securities. "Doing one thing and saying something else," he said, allows the Fed chief to ease monetary policy without losing the market's confidence in him as an inflation-fighter.

Jesse Torres, chief foreign exchange trader in New York for Bank Brussels Lambert, concluded that Mr. Greenspan is "a bull on the dollar."

Valerie Walker, New York corporate foreign exchange manager for Chicago's Harris Trust & Savings Bank, said the Fed chairman "did a good job at balancing the Fed's conflicting signals" and added that "we got pretty much what the market expected."

Still, Mr. Brusca said, "It was a very, very confusing day. It always is when Greenspan speaks."

It was also a tumultuous day, as dealers and investors who had shorted, or oversold, the dollar ahead of the Greenspan question-and-answer session bought that money back when the resulting headlines proved ambiguous.

The dollar-buying wasn't strong enough to break the dollar out of its bounds. Several times, for example, the greenback was slapped back down just after exceeding a technical ceiling at 1.6490 marks. The last attempt to push the dollar out of its range against the German currency occurred about an hour before the close of global trading, when the U.S. unit hit its intraday high of 1.6495 marks.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.6480 marks, up from 1.6440 marks late Tuesday in New York. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 147.75 yen, just below 147.80 yen late Tuesday. Sterling was trading at $1.8155, down from $1.8165.

The dollar also ended in New York at 1.4165 Swiss francs, well above 1.4045 francs Tuesday. The U.S. unit's gains against the Swiss currency were abetted by the selling of francs against other nondollar currencies.

In Tokyo Thursday, the dollar was trading at 148.10 yen soon after the opening; it closed at 147.25 yen in Tokyo Wednesday. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6490 marks, up a bit from Tokyo's Wednesday close of 1.6436 marks.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $361.60 an ounce, down 40 cents. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.2 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Thursday, gold was quoted at $361.75 an ounce.
