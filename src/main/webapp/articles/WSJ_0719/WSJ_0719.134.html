World Markets: Tokyo Stock Prices Fall on Growing Expectations That Bank of Japan Will Push Up Interest Rates

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 07/19/90

Tokyo stocks finished lower as growing expectations for higher domestic interest rates increased a wait-and-see mood in the afternoon session, traders said. In London, shares ended near intraday lows in relatively active trading, pressured by sagging Wall Street share prices.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues, which had advanced for five consecutive trading days, fell back 124.17 points to 33048.11.

The index, a 150.55-point gainer Tuesday, fell as low as 32971 at midafternoon Wednesday.

In early trading in Tokyo Thursday, the Nikkei eased 67.77 points to 32980.34.

Wednesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 600 million shares, down slightly from 628 million shares Tuesday. Decliners outnumbered advancers 505-446, while 176 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section fell 9.53 points, or 0.40%, to 2397.78.

The second section index dropped 29.42 points, or 0.66%, to 4402.08. Volume in the section was estimated at 22 million shares, down from 23.8 million shares Tuesday.

Discouraging the market was speculation on the bond market that the Bank of Japan wants to push up both long-term and short-term interest rates.

"It was a blow to investors who were just ready to come out to the trading floor," said Shigeru Akiba, head of equity at UBS Phillips & Drew International.

After the federal fund rate reduction by the U.S. Federal Reserve Board Friday, expectation grew Monday that Japanese interest rates, if not falling, at least may not rise higher.

Such expectations were about to bring institutions off the sidelines, Mr. Akiba said. "But now they are unable to move" out of fear of higher interest rates, he said.

Kimitoshi Hasegawa, head of group pension management at Dai-Ichi Mutual Life, said continuing high interest rates despite the yen's strength lead to a consensus among investors that the overall funds invested on stocks won't increase. Therefore, strong gains for large-capital domestic demand-related shares are least expected.

Fund managers are more and more focused on small-capital shares with strong earnings predictions, he said.

Traders said that although the market lacked strength to push up stock prices, the sentiment wasn't too bearish. Buying orders constantly placed at lower levels prevented stocks from plunging.

Public spending-related shares advanced, helped by buying by the Big Four brokerage houses.

Nippon Hodo rose 50 yen to 3,830 yen (34 cents to $25.90). Taisei gained 10 to 1,330, while Ohbayashi was up 20 at 1,700.

Stocks that appeared to be targets of buying by some speculator groups rose strongly, traders said. Among these were Jujo Paper, which advanced 100 to 970, and Kurabo, which gained 80 to 1,150.

Pharmaceutical shares advanced, with Sankyo up 70 to 2,710 and Ono gaining 50 to 5,900.

High-technology shares finished generally lower as investors took profits after recent gains, traders said. Sony was down 80 at 8,900, while Kyocera fell 170 to 8,970 yen. Fanuc fell 50 to 7,500.

The London market opened higher but rapidly lost ground on expectations of a lower Wall Street because of an anticipated inflationary U.S. consumer price report for June. Those fears came true, with the data showing a rise of 0.5%, compared with the consensus forecast of 0.3%.

However, traders said some of London's losses could be ascribed to profit-taking after the recent rally. Overall, sentiment remains optimistic, with "people looking for buy ideas rather than sell ideas," one dealer said. "People are using bad news to pick up cheap stock."

Also affecting prices was sterling's new highs compared with the dollar and several European currencies. The rise put pressure on overseas earners.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-Share Index dropped 13 points to 2402. The intraday high of 2424.8 was reached in the midmorning. The Financial Times 30-Share Index closed off 12.3 points at 1900.6.

Volume was a reasonably robust 595.2 million issues, compared with 374.9 million Tuesday, even after taking into account the placing of 54 million British Gas shares.

These shares represent a line of reserved stock offered as a bonus to holders but not all taken up. British Gas was off a penny at 222 pence ($4.03).

British Steel's announcement that it had agreed in principle to acquire 40% of Spain's Aristrain for an undisclosed sum was unsurprising and therefore had little effect on the company's share price. The stock ended unchanged at 142 1/2 pence.

British Aerospace was down 5 at 550. However, dealers said the shares were hit by the falling market, as the news was largely discounted in the price because the government move was disclosed several weeks ago.

Among overseas earners to be hit by the pound's strength, ICI dropped 9 to #11.56, Glaxo shed 22 to 853, and B.A.T fell 8 to 963. Reuters dropped 17 to #12.65, and Wellcome finished at 580, off 13.

Elsewhere, share prices closed higher in Paris, Frankfurt and Milan, mixed in Brussels and Amsterdam, and lower in Zurich, Madrid and Stockholm. South African gold stocks closed lower.

Meanwhile, stocks ended lower in Sydney, Singapore, Manila and Taipei, mixed in Wellington, and higher in Hong Kong and Seoul.
