Technology: Lotus Predicts Net May Fall In 3rd Quarter

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 07/19/90

CAMBRIDGE, Mass. -- Lotus Development Corp. reported second-quarter net income more than doubled, as expected, but shocked analysts by saying third-quarter results may trail last year's. In a meeting with analysts that ended after the close of stock trading, Lotus said its forecasts are "a little bit more cautious" than analysts' predictions of 10% to 15% gains in the third quarter. Robert Schechter, chief financial officer, said in an interview, "We'll have a relatively tough time getting to" the year-earlier earnings level.

Lotus stock fell $2.875 to $31.25 in over-the-counter trading before Mr. Schechter made his announcement. Analysts predicted that the stock will fall further today. Rick Sherlund, an analyst with Goldman, Sachs & Co., said the third-quarter prediction "was a big surprise to people in the room." He added: "I would expect to reiterate my sell recommendation tomorrow."

Lotus's results have been strong for the past four quarters, in the wake of its introduction in June 1989 of upgraded versions of its best-selling 1-2-3 software program. However, analysts had cautioned that gains would be smaller in future quarters.

Second-quarter net rose to $23.5 million, or 54 cents a share, from $10.3 million, or 25 cents a share, a year earlier. Sales rose 33% to $175.2 million from $132.2 million.

Mr. Schechter said that Lotus found the market for spreadsheet programs is "getting more competitive." Both industry giant Microsoft Inc. and Borland International Ltd. have been selling their own spreadsheets vigorously, competing against Lotus's product.

Mr. Schechter said that in "our mainstream business, we don't see significant loss in share." However, he said the spreadsheet market has expanded, and in new market areas such as small business, "there has been a little bit of share loss, but not in a significant way."

He declined to provide market-share numbers. Most analysts estimate that Lotus holds between 60% and 70% of the spreadsheet market.

Robert Therrien, an analyst with PaineWebber Inc. who had previously predicted that Lotus would see an earnings decline in the third quarter, said "I'm not panicked," by the competition. "I think there's a reasonable probability that Lotus will get an injunction," stopping Borland from selling its spreadsheet. As reported, Lotus has filed suit against Borland charging copyright violation.

Mr. Schechter said that international sales rose 50%, accounting for 46% of Lotus's business in the quarter.

Mr. Schecter's caution about the forthcoming quarter reflects the continued strong competition and his assumption that Lotus won't see significant revenue from its next revision of 1-2-3 in the quarter. He also said he expects a seasonal sales slump in Europe this year, in contrast to last year, when revenue from upgraded versions offset the normal summer slowdown.

Mr. Schecter said that after the third-quarter slump, he expects fourth-quarter results to show improvement from the year-earlier figures. However, Mr. Sherlund of Goldman Sachs said, "It's very questionable whether the fourth quarter will be up."
