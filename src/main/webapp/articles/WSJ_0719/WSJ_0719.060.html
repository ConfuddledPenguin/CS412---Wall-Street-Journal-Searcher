Wednesday's Markets: Bonds Fall On Fears Of Unflation --- CPI Report Is Cited; Stocks Drop 18.07 As Dollar Is Mixed

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 07/19/90

NEW YORK -- Bond prices sagged in response to news that inflation is by no means whipped, helping drag stocks lower. Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices fell nearly one point, or about $10 for each $1,000 of face amount. Trading was frenzied in the morning but quieter in the afternoon.

Stocks also were hit by profit-taking and earnings disappointments. The Dow Jones Industrial Average backed away from its flirtation with 3000, dropping 18.07 to 2981.68. The dollar ended mixed, reflecting currency traders' less-certain interpretation of the inflation news.

The bond market was jarred early by news that the consumer price index for June rose 0.5%, compared with an expected increase of 0.3%. Later in the morning came comments from Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan, who said, among other things, that he is now slightly more concerned about the risk of recession than about the risk of rising inflation.

The remarks set bond investors to worrying that Mr. Greenspan is responding to political pressure to ease interest rates instead of sticking to his goal of reducing inflation. While bond investors like to see interest rates come down, which boosts bond prices commensurately, they fear creeping inflation even more because it erodes real returns on their investments.

Bond traders were puzzled that the CPI came in as high as it did and a few hours later Mr. Greenspan suggested inflation fell a notch on his priority list.

Though "the CPI report took a half-point {off bond prices} right away," said Stephen Slifer, economist at Shearson Lehman Hutton, traders also were concerned about a news report that House Republican budget conferees decided to oppose any tax increases as part of a deficit-reduction accord.

"That could be a monkey wrench in the budget talks," Mr. Slifer said. Hopes for further credit-easing moves by the Fed have been tied to a successful deficit-cutting effort.

But anything that prevents further Fed easing is good news for the dollar. Currency traders say falling U.S. interest rates are a greater immediate threat to the dollar's strength than inflation fears.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell in active trading, with 168.8 million shares changing hands on the New York Stock Exchange, where decliners swamped advancers, 1,030 to 483.

Bond prices fell. The yield on the Treasury's 30-year bond rose to 8.54%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.6480 marks and 147.75 yen, compared with 1.6440 marks and 147.80 yen Tuesday.
