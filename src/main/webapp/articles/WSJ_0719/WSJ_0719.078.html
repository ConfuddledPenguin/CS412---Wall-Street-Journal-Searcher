Dun & Bradstreet Reports 18% Drop In 2nd-Quarter Profit

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/19/90

NEW YORK -- Dun & Bradstreet Corp.'s second-quarter profit fell 18% as results reflected the negative impact of a controversy surrounding sales practices in the company's credit services business. Analysts said the results were in line with their expectations and the company's indications. Dun & Bradstreet has reiterated a forecast of 1990 per-share earnings of about $2.80, indicating an 11% decline from 1989's $3.14 a share.

In the quarter, the company said net income fell to $123 million or 67 cents a share, from $150.4 million or 80 cents a share a year ago. Revenue climbed 13% to $1.2 billion from $1.06 billion. However, adjusted for the acquisition last December of Management Science America, a software concern, and the divestiture of Thomas Cook Travel USA in April 1989, revenue rose 7%.

The business-information concern said the earnings decline was due in part to "a high single-digit decline" in revenue at U.S. Credit Services and a sharp rise in costs relating to the company's repositioning actions at that division.

Last year, many customers accused Dun & Bradstreet salespeople of misleading them to buy far more credit services than they needed. The company has conceded that, in some cases, customers were abused. In addition, without admitting any wrongdoing, the company reached an $18 million agreement to settle five major lawsuits that similarly alleged customers were deceived into overbuying services. As part of the settlement, the company agreed to modify its business practices in several important respects. And the resulting changes accounted for substantial cost increases in the credit services business.

Dun & Bradstreet's profit had continued to increase until the first quarter of this year, when the effects of the controversy began to be felt.

Yesterday, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Dunn & Bradstreet shares closed at $46.125, down 75 cents.
