U.S. Companies' Purchases Of Foreign Firms Decline

  • Author: null
  • Date: 07/19/90

NEW YORK -- U.S. companies' acquisitions of foreign concerns declined in the first half because of tighter credit conditions and fears about slower economic growth, according to a survey by KPMG, the accounting firm. The number of acquisitions declined to 124 from 213 in the 1989 first half, and the dollar total fell to $8.1 billion from $11.4 billion. The drop in dollars would have been even steeper except for Philip Morris Cos.' plan to buy Jacobs Suchard AG of Switzerland, an agreement that accounted for more than a third of the total in the 1990 first half.

The number of acquisitions by foreign companies in the U.S. also fell, to 338 from 392 in the 1989 half. The dollar total, however, continued to dwarf the value of U.S. purchases abroad, as it rose to $27.9 billion from $23.9 billion.

KPMG cited Swedish and Japanese companies as showing especially large increases in cross-border acquisitions. Swedish companies announced 71 transactions in the first half totaling $8.5 billion, well above the year-earlier 51 transactions totaling $700 million. KPMG said it expects Finland, Norway, Sweden and Switzerland to be active on the acquisition front in Europe to improve their position in advance of the formation of a highly unified European market in 1992. Japanese cross-border purchases increased to 114 from 70 and to $5.3 billion from $2.9 billion.
