International: Egyptian Refugees Say They Fled Brutality and Robbery in Kuwait

  • Author: Jane Mayer
  • Date: 08/22/90

NUWEIBA, Egypt -- Thousands of Egyptian migrant workers fleeing Kuwait struggled off overcrowded ferries at this port on the Gulf of Aqaba, bringing a few possessions and tales of what they left behind. In the 105-degree heat, the Egyptian refugees, some of whom have been trying to reach their homeland for more than a week, give accounts of pillaging and brutality by Iraqi soldiers in Kuwait.

Waddih Nagib said he tried to leave three times before making it to the Jordanian port of Aqaba, 40 miles northeast of Nuweiba at the northern end of the Gulf of Aqaba where the borders of Egypt, Israel and Jordan meet. He came with his two brothers and two friends, all in the front seat of a tiny truck.

In his first attempt a week ago, he and four Americans were turned back in the desert by Iraqi soldiers. Mr. Nagib said the soldiers took away the Americans.

"There was a lot of shooting, but I don't know what happened to them," he said.

The desert, according to Mr. Nagib and others, was studded with land mines. He and his companions said they saw rockets and missiles being deployed along the Persian Gulf in Kuwait by Iraqi troops. The editor of Jane's Soviet Intelligence Review in London said yesterday that Iraq has moved 36 missile launchers and 800 Soviet-built or Iraqi-modified ballistic missiles into Kuwait.

Mr. Nagib said Iraqi soldiers "robbed us of everything we had -- our money, a television set, a radio and a vacuum cleaner." He added, "We hate them. They have attacked everyone and robbed everyone."

Mahmoud Alweh, an Egyptian teller in Kuwait City's International Bank, left a week ago with his wife and two small sons in a Chevrolet.

"We lost everything," he said. "Our house, our money, 12 years' worth of savings. You could lose your mind."

On the morning after Iraq's Aug. 2 raid on Kuwait City, Mr. Alweh said he went to the supermarket for provisions: "It was incredibly crowded. People were buying everything they could get their hands on, whether they needed it or not."

When he got back to his house, he said, soldiers filled his street. "By nightime bombs were falling and there was shooting all night. My son was shaking. In the morning, the streets were filled with blood and bloody clothes, shoes, shirts and soldiers' caps."

The streets in Kuwait City were littered with "thousands" of cars, Mr. Alweh said, many of which had burned. Many cars were "just stolen," said Mr. Alweh.

The banker's wife said "they raped many." She said soldiers also stole jewelry. Mr. Alweh said soldiers robbed jewelry stores and electrical appliance stores.

"Where will I go now?" asked Mr. Alweh. "Anywhere would be better. We have been driving eight days. We have been hungry and thirsty."

Mohammed Hussein, an Egyptian air-conditioner salesman who left with his brother and two small daughters, said Kuwaiti soldiers and civilians offered scattered resistance. He said one Kuwaiti loaded his car with explosives and charged a hospital near the town of Shouera where Iraq's military had set up a command post.

Drinking water and orange soda at the Ali Baba restaurant here, Mr. Hussein complained of crowds and delays in Aqaba. He said he waited four days there before being able to catch a ferry to Egypt. Egyptian officials say 5,000 to 8,000 people are traveling from Aqaba to Nuweiba each day. They say as many as 20,000 Egyptians and 800 Sudanese are waiting in Aqaba. The Egyptian consulate provided free food to the refugees.

Jordanian officials have asked Iraq to try to slow the flow of refugees. A senior Israeli official said Jordan also asked Israel to open its border to refugees so they could travel to Egypt. Diplomatic sources in Washington told the Associated Press that Israel has offered to facilitate the return home of Egyptian civilians.

Mr. Hussein said refugees in Aqaba found their Kuwaiti currency almost worthless. "The Jordanians wouldn't take our money," he said. He also reported long lines at gasoline stations.
