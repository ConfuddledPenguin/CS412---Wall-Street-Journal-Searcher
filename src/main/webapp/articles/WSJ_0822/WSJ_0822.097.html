SEC and CFTC Bring Charges Against Stotler

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 08/22/90

WASHINGTON -- Securities and commodities regulators filed charges against Stotler & Co., adding to the troubles of the closed futures brokerage firm and its part-owner, former Chicago Board of Trade Chairman Karsten Mahlmann. In administrative proceedings, the Securities and Exchange Commission and the Commodity Futures Trading Commission charged the firm with failing to maintain sufficient capital since December. In addition, the CFTC charged Stotler with overstating the amount of capital held by the firm. The SEC also said the firm failed to file documents and kept outdated and improper records of capital levels and of positions in the government-securities markets.

The firm, which last month closed its doors because of a lack of capital, settled the CFTC charges, agreeing to have its license revoked and promising the agency it would cooperate with a continuing CFTC investigation, the commodities regulator said. But Stotler didn't settle the SEC charges, and the case is scheduled to be heard by an SEC administrative law judge. The SEC also regulates the commodities broker because the firm trades government bonds. Stotler officials couldn't be reached for comment.

CFTC enforcement director Dennis Klejna said the agencies knew of each other's charges, and that "as far as we're concerned this is an ongoing investigation." William Hegan, the SEC's deputy regional administrator in Chicago, said that the agency "regards net capital as a serious violation. It goes to the heart of the business."

The charges are the latest in a series of setbacks for Mr. Mahlmann, Stotler and its parent Stotler Group Inc. Mr. Mahlmann, who has said that he hasn't been involved in the firms' daily operations, hasn't been charged with any wrongdoing by either regulatory agency. But he recently resigned as head of the CBOT in the wake of charges against commodity pool operations run by a Stotler unit.

In those charges, the CFTC said the pool operator, Stotler Funds, violated anti-fraud laws by effectively making $5.5 million in loans from two pools to parent Stotler Group. That money represented customer funds that investors were told would be invested in commodities, the CFTC said. Mr. Mahlmann recently resigned as a director of Stotler Funds and chairman of Stotler Group.

Early this month, Stotler Group was forced into involuntary bankruptcy proceedings by the commodity funds it managed, which said that the group owed them more than $5 million.

More disciplinary steps against Stotler Group, its units and possibly its officers are expected in the next few months as details become known surrounding the collapse of what had been one of the country's biggest futures brokerage firms. Futures regulators have said they may also refer certain aspects of Stotler Group's collapse to the U.S. attorney for possible criminal prosecution.

Mr. Mahlmann, 52 years old, has asserted that he knew nothing of the loans made by the funds and hasn't been involved in the day-to-day management of Stotler Group for two years. But he became the first CBOT chairman in 130 years to resign from the nation's largest futures exchange.
