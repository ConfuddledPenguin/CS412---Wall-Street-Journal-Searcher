Loral Forecasts 7% Increase in Net For Its 2nd Quarter

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 08/22/90

Loral Corp. Chairman Bernard Schwartz, riding the winds of war, said the outlook for the defense electronics concern is bright and will only be improved by its recent acquisition of Ford Motor Co.'s aerospace unit. Speaking with investors during a conference call with Oppenheimer & Co., and then in a follow-up interview with this newspaper, Mr. Schwartz said he expects second-quarter profit to increase by about 7% from the same period last year, not including any expenses associated with the Ford Aerospace deal.

For the year-earlier second quarter ended Sept. 30, 1989, Loral had profit of $19.8 million, or 79 cents a share, on sales of $295.7 million.

For fiscal 1991, Mr. Schwartz added, he wouldn't take issue with projections by securities analysts that the company will earn about $85 million, or $3.45 a share, on revenue of $1.45 billion. In fiscal 1990, the company had net income of $78 million, or $3.11 a share, on sales of $1.27 billion. Backlog, he suggested, will climb slightly to more than $2.2 billion.

Loral's concentration in high-technology aircraft and ship warning systems, reconnaissance and surveillance gear; simulators and training devices; and intelligence and communications equipment "distinguishes us to some degree" from the defense contractors that are most affected by military budget cuts, Mr. Schwartz said.

"That's true today, and it will be true tomorrow as well," he asserted.

The Persian Gulf crisis has only enhanced the company's prospects. The Pentagon, according to Mr. Schwartz, already has stepped up orders for chaff, or tiny strips of metal foil dropped from aircraft that are used to confuse enemy radar. He said the situation in the Middle East makes it more likely that Loral will sell additional ALQ-178 radar warning receivers and jamming instruments to Turkey.

Should the U.S. sell more McDonnell Douglas Corp. F-15 jets to Saudi Arabia, as many in the industry believe is inevitable, that also would help Loral because the planes would be outfitted with Loral electronics. Observers believe Israel is also in the market for such components.

As tensions between the U.S. and Iraq continued to heighten yesterday, the share prices of a number of defense-electronics companies rose, even while the market sank broadly. E-Systems Inc. closed on the New York Stock Exchange at $27.125, up $1. Raytheon Co. finished on the Big Board at $62.50, up $1.125, while Loral finished at $29, up $1.

"What we have to see is if the fact that the Street has warmed up to the stock is really sustainable or just a trading spike," said Lior Bregman, the aerospace and defense analyst at Oppenheimer.

Loral has attracted a lot of interest in the investment community since it won the bid for Ford Aerospace last month. Mr. Schwartz said yesterday that if the Newport Beach, Calif., company's financial results were fully integrated into Loral's, it would ultimately lift sales on an annualized basis to $3.3 billion, backlog to $4 billion and profit to nearly $100 million. But it's not clear how much, if any, of Ford Aerospace's earnings actually will be consolidated on that basis because the merchant banking partnerships of Shearson Lehman Brothers Inc. will hold a 49% stake in the unit.

But whatever happens in terms of Loral's profit-and-loss statement, Mr. Schwartz maintained, Ford Aerospace was a good buy at $715 million. The company, which had $120 million in pretax profit last year, only has about $20 million in debt on its books that Loral will absorb. Even Ford Aerospace's one traditional money-losing arm, missile systems, should break even this year, Mr. Schwartz said.

"This is another example of Loral's acumen, luck, good fortune or whatever," the chairman declared.
