Marketing & Media: King Broadcasting Is Put Up for Sale By Major Holders --- Bullitt Sisters' Plan Is to Use Proceeds for Environment In the Pacific Northwest

  • Author: Charles McCoy
  • Date: 08/22/90

SEATTLE -- King Broadcasting Co. said majority shareholders put the closely held radio and television company up for sale, adding that proceeds will be used to help the environment. King owns six television stations in the Northwest and Hawaii, six radio stations in Seattle, Portland, Ore., and San Francisco, and 13 cable television systems in Washington, Idaho, California and Minnesota. Analysts placed the market value of the company at about $400 million. The company has annual revenue estimated at about $150 million.

Priscilla Bullitt Collins, board chairwoman, and her sister Harriet Stimson Bullitt, chairwoman of the executive committee, said they intend to concentrate on preserving the environment in the Northwest through projects funded by the family's Bullitt Foundation.

The two women said that, including stock owned by their late mother, they own 70% of King's shares. The women said that the company is financially healthy and that they chose to sell now partly because of their age. Both women are more than 65 years old.

"We prefer to sell at a time of our own choosing rather than wait until a sale would be required by our deaths," the sisters said in a statement.

They said they have retained Dillon, Read & Co. and Sterling Payot Co. to advise them on the sale.

The Bullitt family is one of the most prominent and wealthy in the Seattle area. The sisters' grandparents built a lumber and real estate empire and founded hospitals, schools and Seattle's symphony and yacht club. The sisters' mother, Dorothy, who died last year at the age of 97, founded King in 1946 by buying a radio station so she could fulfill her dream of broadcasting classical music. In 1948, she acquired a TV station that later became KING-TV, now King's flagship station.

Dorothy Bullitt also founded the Bullitt Foundation, which has long contributed to civic projects and to preserving the Pacific Northwest. The sisters said they planned to "build a program second to none" to protect the region's environment. They said they intend to set up or support programs to reduce air pollution, to preserve estuaries and wetland, and to protect wild salmon habitat, Puget Sound and the area's spectacular mountain and coastal scenery.

"Caring for these treasures is our purpose," the sisters said in the statement. ". . . Enhancing this foundation through the sale of the company will fulfill many of {Dorothy Bullitt's} and our dreams."
