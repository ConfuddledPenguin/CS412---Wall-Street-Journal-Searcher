Technology: Celgene Receives Patent for Process To Purify Drugs

  • Author: Jerry E. Bishop
  • Date: 08/22/90

WARREN, N.J. -- Celgene Corp. said it received a patent on an unusual technique for boosting the safety and efficacy of a broad category of drugs and chemicals. The little biotechnology company, formerly part of Celanese Corp., said it had developed processes for purifying an amine compound so that all its molecules are of the same shape, or "handedness." Amines are compounds that are basically derived from the ammonia molecule. They include the amino acids that compose all proteins as well as a wide variety of heart drugs, antibiotics, anti-depressants as well as farm chemicals.

Many amines come in two molecular shapes that, like the right and left hands, are mirror images of each other. The Celgene processes can ensure that all of a product's amine molecules are either the left-handed version or the right-handed version, whichever is desirable, the company said.

The handedness of an amine can affect the chemical's effectiveness and its safety, Sol J. Barer, Celgene vice president for technology, said. For example, the synthetic sweetener aspartame, sold as NutraSweet by a subsidiary of Monsanto Co., is composed only of the left-handed versions of two amino acids. Only the left-handed versions taste sweet; the right-handed versions of the same two amino acids taste bitter, a Nutra-Sweet spokesman confirmed.

Many medicines and chemicals are a mixture of the left-handed and right-handed forms of amines and this may account for some drugs having both beneficial as well as adverse effects, the company explained. A prevailing theory, for example, holds that thalidomide was both a highly effective sleeping pill and a drug that produced severe birth defects because it was a mixture of both the right-handed and left-handed versions of the same molecule, Mr. Barer noted.

Similarly, the potency of some medicines is diluted by the presence of the inactive version of the molecule. Potency can be boosted and the dosage of the drug reduced by purifying it to the active version of the molecule, the company said.

Celgene said its new patent covers the use of special bacterial enzymes to separate the left-handed versions of amines from the right-handed versions. The enzymes also can be used to produce only one version of an amine from a chemical raw material. Thus, Mr. Barer explained, once an undesirable version of an amine is culled out of the compound, it can be recycled into the more desirable version.

The company said it had identified more than 500 pharmaceutical compounds that are candidates for the new process.
