Wang Labs Wins Government Contract Valued at $841 Million Over Five Years

  • Author: John R. Wilke
  • Date: 08/22/90

Wang Laboratories Inc. won a government computer contract valued at up to $841 million over five years, the largest in the company's history. The contract to supply computer systems and service to the U.S. State Department and affiliated agencies represents a big boost for Wang, which has struggled to reverse huge losses and declining sales.

In American Stock Exchange trading, Wang's deeply depressed Class B common stock soared $1.125, to close at $4.50.

Analysts applauded the contract, but some cautioned that big government sales can be fickle sources of profit. "It's often very difficult to say whether all that money will actually be spent, and at what level of profit," said John Levinson of Goldman, Sachs & Co.

A Wang spokesman said the $841 million contract value is an estimate by the State Department of what it expects to spend. Like many federal procurements, however, it is formally considered an "indefinite delivery, indefinite quantity" purchase agreement.

Richard W. Miller, Wang's president, hailed the new business as a vote of confidence in the company's staying power. "A single contract, even one of this size, isn't enough to carry a company. But it's evidence of our customers' confidence in Wang," he said.

Since the former General Electric Co. executive joined Wang as its president, a year ago this week, the company has sold off assets to slash bank debt from $575 million to $12 million, and reduced the work force from 28,000 last year to about 19,500 today.

In an interview, Mr. Miller repeated that he expects the company to post a profit in the current year, ending June 30. He called the State Department contract "a real morale boost. This has been a rough year, but we're a lot stronger now and our financial problems are behind us."

For the year ended June 30, Wang reported a $715.9 million net loss, or $4.35 a share, including restructuring charges of $388.3 million and losses from discontinued operations of $87.3 million. Revenue, restated to reflect the sale of operations, declined 14% to $2.5 billion. A year earlier, Wang reported a net loss of $424.3 million, or $2.59 a share, including restructuring charges of $142 million and losses from discontinued operations of $100.1 million.

Under the contract, the Lowell, Mass.-based computer maker will supply its flagship VS line of minicomputers, personal computers and related equipment, software and training support over the next five years, with extension options for a total of 11 years. Included in the contract are such applications as word processing, image processing, electronic mail and messaging and database management.

Wang said one of the factors that worked in its favor in landing the contract was its experience in building "secure" computer systems that resist electronic eavesdropping. Wang historically has had a big presence in the so-called Tempest or secure system market.
