Everybody Sing Along: Bailing, Bailing, Over the Bounding Main

  • Author: Peter Pae
  • Date: 08/22/90

NEW YORK -- The Trump Princess is definitely not invited to this regatta. But there is sure to be a vessel in the tradition of the good ship "Warthog." It's Manhattan's Instant Boat Contest, commonly known by participants as "Glue It, Screw It and Row It." The contest is to take place this Saturday and Sunday at Pier 26 on the Hudson River.

The idea is to build a boat in a day using three sheets of plywood, four two-by-fours, three pounds of nails and lots of black glue made by Sika Corp., which originally sponsored the race to promote its adhesive. Builders must bring their own specified list of tools.

Todd Greeno, a former Navy figher pilot, and Don O'Donoghue, a bartender, built an eight-foot replica of the aircraft carrier USS Enterprise last year. It came complete with a flight deck and a battery-powered toy F-4 Phantom figher jet that circled the carrier as it was launched.

"We designed it on the back of a bar napkin," Mr. Greeno said of the carrier, which won first prize for best creative design but finished eighth in the sea trials. "This year's boat is going to be more streamlined."

The new sponsors of the contest are two nonprofit groups, the River Project, an oceanographic research organization, and B.O.A.T.S. (Boat Owners Association to Survive). The idea, the groups say, is to promote boating on the Hudson River and demonstrate that you don't have to be a millionaire to go boating.

The rules are simple: Contestants must build a boat in one day. On the next day, they must row or paddle it -- along shore -- from one pier to another and back, a distance of about 2,000 feet.

Steven Tiebout, a retired magazine writer, won for being the fastest boat builder last year. (The category has been eliminated.) His vessel was the aptly named Warthog. "I wanted to build a boat that would take the least amount of time, so I knew it wasn't going to be good looking," he says.

Of course, the boats don't always log much time on the water once the race is run. One entry has been converted into a flower planter, says Cathy Drew, director of the River Project. Another became a wading pool.

Judging will be based on creativity of design, functionality and results of the sea trials. But, sponsors warn, the judges' decision "should be expected to be arbitrary and capricious."
