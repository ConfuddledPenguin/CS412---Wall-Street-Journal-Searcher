Japan Denounces Curb On U.S. Log Shipments

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/22/90

TOKYO -- Japan's Foreign Ministry denounced a new U.S. law curtailing U.S. shipments of unprocessed logs, and warned that the measure raises broader questions about the reliability of the U.S. as a supplier of goods. In a sharply worded statement issued in response to President Bush's signing Monday of the 1990 Customs and Trade Act, the ministry called the act a betrayal of previous U.S. commitments to expand trade in forest products with Japan.

A provision that has caused the stir makes permanent a ban on the export of unprocessed logs taken from federal forests in the Western U.S. and restricts the export of logs taken from state lands in the area. While this was promoted as a conservation measure to protect owls, it would also protect some lumber mills in the Northwest that have been losing jobs to Japanese mills.

U.S. Secretary of Agriculture Clayton Yeutter, who is in Tokyo couldn't be reached for comment.

The ministry said that the move would raise uncertainties about the Japanese lumber industry's pricing and availability of certain kinds of lumber. The ministry expressed doubt about U.S. assertions regarding the move's environmental reasoning since export of processed forestry products wasn't curtailed and questioned the law's compatability with the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, or GATT.

The U.S. is the largest supplier of unprocessed timber to Japan, followed by Malaysia and the Soviet Union, officials said. Most of Japan's imports of forestry products from the U.S. are in the form of raw logs.
