USAir to Dismiss 1,500 Employees; More Cuts Expected

  • Author: Asra Q. Nomani and Francine Schwadel
  • Date: 08/22/90

ARLINGTON, Va. -- USAir Group Inc. said it is dismissing about 1,500 employees in another cutback at the airline holding company. USAir also lost a bitter union battle. The airline said yesterday that the fired "employees were hired in anticipation of an expansion program that has since been reduced in light of current economic projections."

A spokeswoman said the 1,500 employees, including pilots, flight attendants and customer service agents, represent about 3% of the company's work force. Most of the employees are considered "probationary," meaning they haven't completed a probationary period that the company said typically lasts six months.

The move follows an earlier cutback. Last week, the company confirmed that it is furloughing 211 pilots starting Jan. 1. The company yesterday indicated there is more to come, saying: "Additional reductions in personnel will be announced as determined."

Most U.S. carriers are anticipating that a downturn in the economy will hurt traffic growth over the next few years, but none have acted as swiftly as ailing USAir.

USAir has said it is going through a "detailed review" of its plans for the next 12 to 24 months. Already the company said it has dropped plans to start new service to certain cities, which it refuses to identify.

The airline is also expected to reduce its fleet. Industry analysts say the carrier will probably retire older planes earlier than planned and defer deilvery of options to buy planes. Earlier this summer, USAir postponed until 1995 delivery of a dozen Boeing Co. 737s that it had planned to receive in 1991 and 1992.

The company has been struggling since it merged its newly acquired Piedmont Aviation unit into USAir last August. In the first quarter, it suffered heavy losses amid widespread customer service problems in areas such as on-time performance and baggage delivery. Second-quarter operating income of $59 million fell far below year-ago performance of $201 million.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, USAir shares dropped 75 cents to close at $16.875.

Separately, the National Mediation Board ruled that USAir "tainted the laboratory conditions necessary" for a fair union representation election. The Teamsters union claimed that USAir had illegally interfered with the union's campaign to represent about 8,000 fleet service workers.

A USAir spokeswoman said, "We regret the decision." She declined to comment further.

The board, which governs labor-management relations in the airline and railroad industries, said the Teamsters could seek a new election. The Teamsters hailed the decision as a victory, and said it plans to wage "an all-out campaign" to represent USAir workers.
