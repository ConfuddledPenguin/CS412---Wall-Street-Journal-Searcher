Politics & Policy: Energy Agency Sees No Need to Use Petroleum Reserve Until Mid-September

  • Author: Rose Gutfeld
  • Date: 08/22/90

WASHINGTON -- Deputy Energy Secretary Henson Moore said his department doesn't expect an oil-supply disruption that would require use of the Strategic Petroleum Reserve until mid-September, at the earliest. Even then, a disruption -- and use of the reserve -- could be averted by an increased draw-down of commercial domestic stocks and higher production from such countries as Saudi Arabia and Venezuela, he said.

Some lawmakers and oil-industry officials have been urging the Bush administration to sell oil from the reserve to help calm oil markets and bring down prices. Bush administration officials, however, have said they don't want the government to get involved in influencing oil prices and fear that dipping into the reserves would take pressure off Saudi Arabia and other oil-producing countries to pump additional crude. However, an outbreak of fighting in the region could force the U.S. to tap the reserve.

In an interview, Mr. Moore said his view on the timing of any disruption is consistent with the views of officials from other countries in the 21-nation International Energy Agency and with the U.S. experience during the oil shock of 1973. "The level of stocks world-wide is high," he said. "The loss of crude really hasn't been felt yet. It's been handled by the drawdown in private stocks."

Since Iraq invaded Kuwait, the world oil markets have lost the two countries' production of about four million barrels a day. Mr. Moore said domestic stocks of crude oil and fuel oil remain higher than usual, while supplies of gasoline are lower than usual. The Strategic Petroleum Reserve holds about 590 million barrels of crude oil, which equals about 800 days of U.S. imports from Iraq and Kuwait.

In addition, Mr. Moore said the Energy Department is working to come up with additional ways of boosting domestic production, increasing conservation and encouraging industry to use fuels other than oil. Mr. Moore said the department is considering steps that would be longer-term and more controversial than its first package, though he wouldn't elaborate.

As part of the first plan, the administration announced that oil companies have agreed to boost production from the North Slope of Alaska; urged Americans to put more air in automobile tires and increase car-pooling; and said it would mediate a California dispute that is blocking production of 200 million to 300 million barrels of oil.

Mr. Moore said the department is working with the White House on ways to get the conservation message out through a public-information campaign.

The Bush administration does plan to renew its push for tax incentives to encourage domestic oil and gas production, including changes that would reduce the burden of the alternative minimum tax on producers. In the administration's budget proposal last January, the various tax changes aimed at the industry were estimated to cost $300 million in the fiscal year starting Oct. 1.

Mr. Moore was hopeful that the Mideast crisis might prompt environmentalists and other opponents of drilling in the Alaska National Wildlife Refuge to reconsider the situation.

The department doesn't plan to recommend a reversal of President Bush's decision to delay oil drilling in environmentally sensitive areas off the California and Florida coasts. "Even if we withdrew the decision tomorrow, there would still be two to five years of environmental work" and other preparation before drilling could start, he said, so it would be of little use in the current crisis.

Similarly, the department isn't planning to push the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission to speed up its consideration of a proposed Iroquois pipeline to bring Canadian natural gas to the Northeastern U.S.

Mr. Moore predicted that Congress will return from its August recess "flush with legislative ideas to solve the crisis." Among those will be a push to require auto makers to improve significantly the fuel economy of their fleets. While he said the administration would have to take a look at this and other proposals, he gave no indication that the White House will reverse its opposition to the measure, pushed by Sen. Richard Bryan (D., Nev.).
