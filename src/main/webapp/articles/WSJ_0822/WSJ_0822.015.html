AIG's Sliding Stock Price May Force It To Sweeten Bid for International Lease

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 08/22/90

American International Group Inc.'s stock price has fallen steadily for several days, raising the prospect that the insurer might have to sweeten its bid for International Lease Finance Corp. in order to salvage the $1.3 billion transaction. Since June 25, when the cash-and-stock agreement was announced, American International's stock has dropped about 12.5% from its closing price that day of $75.20 a share, adjusted for a recent 4-for-3 stock split. The shares closed yesterday at $65.75, down $1, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange.

Under terms of the agreement, if American International's stock is trading below $68.80 a share on Aug. 30, the day before shareholders are scheduled to vote on the acquisition, American International could be forced to issue more stock to maintain the tax-free status of the transaction. And if the stock drops below $66.40, which it did during yesterday's session, then either side could walk away from the agreement.

The stock's weakness reflects concerns about the acquisition, as well as the stock market's general decline.

Maurice Greenberg, American International's chairman and chief executive officer, said in a brief interview late yesterday, "We hope the {stock} price will rise. We hope the terms will go through {as they are} and we hope everyone will benefit" from this transaction.

He declined to speculate about whether terms might have to be changed.

Louis Gonda, International Lease Finance's executive vice president, said that "it's premature to discuss these alternatives because it's not the fundamentals of either company that's affecting the stock prices . . . We'll revisit the issue a little closer to the closing date. Both companies are as interested as ever to proceed with the merger."

Separately, International Lease Finance announced that third-quarter pretax operating profit will nearly double from the $17.3 million posted a year earlier. Total revenue for the period, which will end Aug. 31, is expected to climb almost 40% to $113 million from $81 million in the year-earlier period. The results will be reported officially in mid-September.

Steven Udvar-Hazy, president of the Beverly Hills, Calif.-based jet-leasing company, said in an interview that International Lease's strategy of building a portfolio of newer, fuel-efficient planes will ultimately prove to be sound, particularly as companies in the aviation industry continue to face rising energy costs in the wake of the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait.

International Lease Finance's stock fell $1.50 a share yesterday to close at $27.50 in national over-the-counter trading.

Herbert Goodfriend, an analyst with Prudential-Bache Securities in New York, said he believes both companies want the agreement to go through, but American International can't control the price of its stock in a generally declining market. "They might have to amend the terms," he said.

A collapse of the transaction actually could help American International's stock, said Udayan Ghose at Shearson Lehman in New York, because many investors have been lukewarm about the agreement from the start. He said they have feared the big multinational insurer might be buying the company at the top of a big growth cycle in the airline industry.

Along with other insurance stocks, American International shares have been hurt by concerns about the Persian Gulf crisis and attendant worries of rising inflation. Insurance stock generally underperform the market during inflationary times because their big bond portfolios suffer as bond prices fall and claims usually rise.

Terms of the agreement call for International Lease Finance holders to receive either $32.50 in cash or 50 cents in cash plus 0.3 common share of American International for each International Lease Finance share outstanding, subject to a maximum number of shares and certain other adjustments.

Leslie Gonda, International Lease Finance's chairman, Udvar-Hazy and Louis Gonda collectively control more than 50% of the company's 36 million shares outstanding. They have opted to take stakes in American International.
