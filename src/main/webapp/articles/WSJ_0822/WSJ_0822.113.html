Abreast of the Market: Industrials Fall 52.48, Reflecting Uncertainty in the Middle East

  • Author: Craig S. Smith
  • Date: 08/22/90

NEW YORK -- Stock prices fell sharply as investors grappled with the uncertainties of the economy and the Middle East. Traders say the selling was heavy by U.S. institutions as well as some European investors. "The market is seeking its equilibrium," says Jack Solomon, technical analyst at Bear Stearns. That's difficult to do, he adds, with so much doubt plaguing the market.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average swung broadly, triggering program-trading circuit breakers twice during the day. The index ended down 52.48 at 2603.96.

Only two of the 30 stocks in the industrial average posted gains: Union Carbide ended 3/8 higher to 16 3/8, while Allied-Signal ended 1/8 higher at 31 1/8.

The broader market also fell, as Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index lost 6.65 to 321.86, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index dropped 3.48 to 176.94 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index fell 5.98 to 298.02.

Declining stocks swamped gaining issues on the Big Board, with only 243 stocks rising while 1,441 stocks fell. Trading was heavy, with volume reaching 194,630,000 shares, up from Monday's 129,630,000 shares.

The New York Stock Exchange's "uptick" rule went into effect at 9:49 a.m. EDT after the industrial average dropped 50 points, but was lifted at 12:15 p.m. EDT, when the index recovered 25 points.

The rule was triggered again at 1:18 p.m. EDT, remaining in effect for the rest of the day.

Under the uptick rule, program trades in the stocks of the S&P 500 Index can only be executed at prices above or equal to the previous trade. The rule effectively stops index-arbitrage program selling in the stock market.

Kenneth P. Ducey, senior vice president of institutional trading at S.G. Warburg, says he expects the market to find firmer footing when the industrial average reaches the 2540 level. That's around where the market bottomed after the minicrash in October 1989 and again in January this year.

But Bear Stearns's Mr. Solomon says that if the Dow Jones average breaks below the 2540 level, "all of the stock that was bought in the past year, when the {average} was between 2500 and 3000, will technically be available for sale."

That could lead to a deluge of emotional, stop-loss selling, Mr. Solomon says.

But not everyone is shunning stocks these days. Mr. Solomon notes that he has seen an increase in stock buy-backs by firms who think their stocks are cheap.

Bear Stearns is one. The securities firm bought back 2.4% of its stock yesterday in a single 2.37 million-share block from an institutional investor. Bear Stearns paid $10 a share, a new 52-week low for the stock. The stock closed at 10 1/2, down 1/4, on volume of more than 2.6 million shares.

Fredric E. Russell, a Tulsa, Okla.-based money manager, says that despite the Mideast conflict, he's advising clients to continue to buy stock -- but only in those companies that can survive under the worst economic environments.

Mr. Russell looks for firms that dominate their markets, are not capital intensive and have little or no debt.

Such companies are likely to weather a recession, and may even pick up market share as more debt laden firms falter.

Selected energy issues continued to hold fractional gains.

Harken Energy rose 5/8 to 3, despite reporting a loss of 73 cents a share yesterday for its second quarter, down from profit of 7 cents a share after a gain a year earlier.

BP Prudhoe Bay Royalty Trust rose 1 to 31 3/4, while Plains Petroleum added 1 to 33 1/2. Oil-service company Baker Hughes added 3/8 to 32 1/4.

Some defense stocks were higher on the growing threat of war.

Raytheon added 1 1/8 to 62 1/2, and Loral gained 1 to 29. E-Systems gained 1 to 27 1/8.

Baltimore Bancorp gained 1 to 9. The bank repeatedly rejected offers from Al lied Irish Banks and its First Maryland Bancorp unit earlier this year. Allied Irish most recently offered $17 a share for the bank. Baltimore Bancorp said it hasn't been approached by Allied Irish or First Maryland again.

Casino stocks were among the worst hit groups in the market. Circus Circus Enter prises fell 2 1/8 to 45 5/8, while Golden Nugget dropped 2 1/8 to 22 5/8.

Baroid lost 2 1/2 to 11 3/4 after Merrill Lynch made negative comments about the company and downgraded its rating on the stock to neutral. On Monday, Baroid's board approved a plan to split the company in two. The company intends to spin off its petroleum services business and its metals businesses into two publicly traded companies.

ROC Taiwan Fund tumbled 1 1/4 to 7 1/2 after a sharp drop in share prices in Taiwan overnight.

Fluor dropped 2 1/2 to 33 3/8 on volume of more than 1.4 million. Investors may be concerned that Fluor's Saudi Arabian revenue might suffer from the Mideast conflict. In June, Fluor won a five-year contract award from Saudi Aramco, the state-owned Saudi Arabian oil company. The total value of the contract has not been disclosed. Fluor has said its business in Saudi Arabia has not been disrupted.

Salomon edged up 1/8 to 22 3/8. Despite the planned restructuring of its Philipp Brothers unit, the securities firm said it expects to report a profit for the third quarter.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index lost 3.95 to 327.34. Volume totaled 19,650,000 shares.

Compumat eased 1/8 to 3 1/8. JWP has agreed to acquire the company for $3.25 a share. JWP fell 1 1/2 to 19 1/2 in Big Board trading.
