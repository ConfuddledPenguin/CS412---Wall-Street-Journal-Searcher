Stock Markets Plunge as Gulf Tension Rises --- Tuesday's Markets: DJIA off 52.48; Bond Prices Sag

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 08/22/90

NEW YORK -- A sell-off in overseas stock markets sparked by Mideast war rumors spilled over into U.S. stocks. The Dow Jones Industrial Average plunged 50 points in the first half hour of trading and ended 52.48 points lower at 2603.96 in hectic trading.

Adding fuel to the decline early in the day was a sharp drop in bond prices, caused by rising oil prices and news reports that suggested hostilities were about to break out in the Mideast. Long-term Treasury bond prices later recovered to end just slightly lower. The dollar was mixed.

Ted Eggert, equity research manager at Yamaichi International, said the selling of stocks was indiscriminate. "They took them out across the board," he said, citing big declines in such stalwarts as Merck and Philip Morris. And though Boeing and PepsiCo had positive news, their stocks fell.

The stock market was poised for heavy selling even without the influence of big drops in European markets, said Paul Hennessey, head of stock trading at Boston Co. "The market looked better than it actually was" at the end of Monday's session, he said, referring to the modest rise in the Dow on thin volume with broader market indexes not performing as well.

Christopher Pedersen, director of trading at Twenty-First Securities, attributed the early, sharp slide to a 5.2% decline in the West German stock market. The sell-off was sparked by an unsubstantiated rumor that Iraq had shot down a U.S. war plane. Further heightening the market's fear that Mideast fighting was imminent were renewed reports that Iraq had moved missiles into southern Kuwait.

"The foreign markets created the down draft," Mr. Pedersen said. He added that Monday's rebound in the Dow industrials failed to deliver the gains that some traders had expected after the Dow resisted closing below 2640, a "support level," on Friday. Mr. Pedersen sees the Dow falling to around 2500 before the current slide ends.

Swings in oil prices contributed to big moves in the bond market. "Treasurys rode a roller coaster driven by the oil market," said Raymond Stone, managing director at Stone & Mccarthy Research Associates, Princeton, N.J. As oil prices rose early in the day, bonds fell, losing three quarters of a point, or $7.50 for each $1,000 face amount. But in the late morning, oil prices fell and bonds rebounded on an apparent indication that Iraq wanted to talk peace.

Though the perception of peace overtures faded as the day wore on, bonds didn't resume their decline because investors who had sold bonds before the peace news hit were busily buying them back.

The muddled interpretation of Mideast developments left many bond traders on the losing side for the day, said Roberta D. Olson, fixed-income portfolio manager at Bance One Asset Management, Columbus, Ohio. "Once again, it's a crap shoot," she said.

"A lot of the volatility in the stock and bond markets transferred into foreign exchange," said Robert White, manager of corporate foreign-exchange trading at First Interstate Bank, Los Angeles. The declines in stocks and bonds helped drag the dollar lower, but it later recovered and closed mixed against major currencies. Currencies of countries with high interest rates, such as Canada and Australia, continued attracting traders. But activity overall was very light, Mr. White said, because many players in Asia and Europe are on holiday.

In major market action:

Stock prices fell on volume of 194.6 million shares in trading on the New York Stock Exchange, where declining issues overwhelmed advancers by 1,441 to 243.

Bond prices fell slightly. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose to 8.94%.

The dollar was mixed. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.5645 marks, down from 1.5650 late Monday, and at 146.65 yen, up from 146.44 yen Monday.
