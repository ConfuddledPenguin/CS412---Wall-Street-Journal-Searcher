New Devices on Line To Slow Down Speeders

SPEED-DEMONS beware: The police are working up an arsenal of new weapons to nab you.

New radar-detector detectors have made their national debut along Virginia's Interstate 95. The devices, pioneered by Ontario police, pick up microwave signals emitted by radar detectors. Besides Virginia, fuzz-busters are illegal in Connecticut and the District of Columbia. A New York ban on detectors in big trucks took effect Sunday. And the federal government is considering a nationwide ban on detectors in trucks.

Meanwhile, a laser speed-tracking device is being perfected. More precise than conventional radar, it can't be sniffed out by radar detectors. Such new enforcement weapons raise hopes that the "era of the radar detector is coming to an end," says Chuck Hurley, a spokesman for the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety.

Another new technology is photo-radar, which snaps pictures of speeding cars' license plates. Officials in Paradise Valley, Ariz., where the system is being used, say that crashes have declined. The system is also being used in Pasadena, Calif. Photo-radar will be tested on Washington's Capital Beltway this fall, despite some grumbling about its Big Brother overtones.

The anti-speed offensive comes amid rising worries over a steadily accelerating populace. "Speeds are increasing every year," frets Clayton Hall, director of the Office of Enforcement and Emergency Services, National Highway Traffic Safety Administration. "Compliance has gone downhill."

Evidence mounts that speed kills: A new insurance institute study concludes that raising the speed limit to 65 miles per hour on rural interstate highways resulted in 33% more deaths last year than the average for 1982-1986, when the limit was 55 mph.

Air-Travel Taxes Head

For the Stratosphere

AS IF AIRLINE fuel surcharges aren't bad enough, Uncle Sam wants to attach an array of taxes and "user fees" to airline tickets.

Travelers on international flights already shell out $16 in U.S. departure taxes, customs and immigration-service fees and an additional $10 in airline-security surcharges.

Recently, the Senate approved a Bush administration proposal to slap an additional $1 to $3 on arriving international passengers to pay for inspections of plants and animals brought into the U.S. The House passed legislation to allow airports to impose long-banned departure taxes that could add up to $9 on round-trip international tickets and $12 on domestic ones. Domestic travelers face a proposed ticket-tax increase to 10% from 8%. Some of these fees appear doomed politically, but others may be adopted to ease the budget crisis.

The airlines gripe that passengers are being used as a cash cow and that the $7 billion aviation trust-fund surplus should be spent before new fees are imposed. But they are pushing a plan that probably would result in a new $3 surcharge on international tickets sold in the U.S. to help pay for higher compensation to victims of international air disasters. The plan would implement the Montreal Protocols, a pact pending before the Senate.

Pilots Are Encouraged

To Sleep on the Job

SO YOU'VE caught a glimpse of the captain snoozing in the cockpit. Relax, catnaps during long flights enhance safety.

The Federal Aviation Administration is using a new study to encourage United and Northwest airlines to institute "controlled napping" on trans-Pacific flights with three crew members. "Clearly it can be very effective," says Anthony Broderick, an FAA associate administrator.

The action is prompted by the National Aeronautics and Space Administration's Ames Research Center, which found that naps leave pilots refreshed and ready to juggle the tricky tasks involved in descent and landing.

Unplanned sleeping hasn't been blamed for any U.S. airline accident. But pilot drowsiness, caused by boredom and frequent time changes, appears to be a significant safety hazard, says Mark Rosekind, a scientist at NASA Ames.

Exhausted pilots, in reports to Ames's confidential air-safety hot line, have confessed to flying at the wrong altitude, landing on the wrong runway and slapping their faces during landings to avoid nodding off. There are even occasional reports of all three pilots falling asleep, says H. Clayton Foushee, a top FAA scientist.

The problem is so severe, experts say, that some flight crews currently take turns sleeping, even though they could be accused of recklessness under FAA rules.

Odds and Ends

MEMBERS of National Car Rental's Emerald Club, which offers free-rental coupons to frequent customers, may have a tough time redeeming their coupons in Manhattan until after Labor Day. The company, which has a disclaimer on the coupons, has imposed "capacity controls" for the area similar to those on airline frequent flyer programs. . . . The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration reports that some emergency workers have been alarmed by the dust emitted by air bags in crashes and have used elaborate rescue procedures for hazardous-materials accidents. "Such measures are unnecessary and can endanger crash victims if they lead to a delay in medical assistance," warns NHTSA chief Jerry Curry. The dust is cornstarch or talcum powder, used to keep the air bag's fabric from sticking to itself during deployment.
