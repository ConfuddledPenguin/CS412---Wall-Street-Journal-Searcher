Technology: Report Provides Support for the Critics Of Using Big Satellites to Study Climate

  • Author: Bob Davis
  • Date: 08/22/90

WASHINGTON -- A National Research Council study gave a boost to proponents of small satellites, who have criticized plans to spend as much as $50 billion over 25 years on a series of huge satellites to study the earth's climate. The National Aeronautics and Space Administration plans to launch two $3 billion satellites near the end of the decade to study the changing global environment. Each of the satellites, called Earth Observing Systems, or EOS-A and EOS-B, would be replaced at five-year intervals to get a 15-year environmental record of Earth.

Critics say the satellites are too expensive and too risky. If an EOS satellite should fail during launch or in orbit it could wreck climate research because, the critics contend, so many instruments are concentrated on a single satellite.

The National Space Council, headed by Vice President Dan Quayle, has said it shares the concerns of the critics and is considering whether to restructure the project. The White House turned to the National Research Council, a part of the National Academy of Sciences, to get an appraisal of EOS before deciding whether to make any changes.

The research council's report supports EOS generally, but says that a series of smaller satellites, which are scheduled for launch before EOS, should take precedence over the larger ones. Those satellites are intended to continue studies of the ocean and atmosphere. "If budget constraints arise, it would be more desirable to delay the launch of EOS spacecraft than to forgo or diminish the effectiveness" of the smaller satellites, the report said.

For the fiscal year beginning Oct. 1, the Bush administration is asking Congress for $235 million for the EOS program and $25 million for the smaller satellites.

NASA has proposed putting as many as 16 different instruments on each EOS-A and EOS-B. The report said NASA has demonstrated that its plan makes sense for EOS-A, but hasn't yet shown a need to put all the EOS-B instruments on one satellite. The space agency could carve EOS-B into a number of "smaller, independent satellites," the study said. EOS critics have urged such a move to simplify EOS design and to reduce the harm to the climate research program if one of the satellites should fail.

The study also warned that the biggest technical challenge to EOS lies in building a computer system on the ground capable of handling, storing and disseminating the vast amounts of data the satellites will relay from space. The ground-based computer system must be powerful enough to handle a Library of Congress worth of information every month or so.

"While the importance and challenge of {the computer system} are understood, it is not equally clear that the route to achieving such a system and the resources and advance planning required are fully appreciated," the report said.
