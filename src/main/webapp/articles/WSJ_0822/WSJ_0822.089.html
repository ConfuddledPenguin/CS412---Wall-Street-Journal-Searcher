BOG MINDING

Conservationists are wading in to try to save the bogs of Ireland.

Forestation, mechanical cutting of peat turf and drainage are fast eating away the age-old sanctuaries for plants, insects and birds. Peter Foss, of the Irish Peatland Conservation Council, said: "Industrial peat cutting leaves a vast brown desert. The whole thing is destroyed."

But Bord na Mona, Ireland's state-run turf board, which sells about four million tons of peat a year, rejects its critics: "Nobody else works the land of Ireland on a 50-year time scale like we do and supplies indigenous energy and employment in remote rural areas." Peat is used as fuel for homes and electricity generators and to improve garden soil.

Peatlands once covered more than 2.5 million acres, or 17% of the land surface of Ireland. "Today less than half of the original peatland area remains in a relatively intact condition," Mr. Foss said.

Tourist authorities have joined the fight with a brochure on the boglands that says: "Ireland without her bogs would be as unimaginable as Germany without its forests or Switzerland without snow."

LEBANON REFORMS CONSTITUTION

Lebanon's parliament approved constitutional reforms that give the country's Moslem majority more say in the Christian-dominated political system, helping to end one of the main Moslem grievances in the 15-year civil war that has claimed the lives of more than 130,000 people, wounded a quarter of a million and devastated much of the country and its infrastructure. Political sources said the move, although strongly opposed by Christian rightists, was a breakthrough in the political stalemate and presented a "real chance to end the civil war."

BIG LOAN FOR INDONESIA

Japanese trading houses agreed to lend $2.8 billion to Indonesia's state-run petroleum company for the construction of an oil refinery at Balongan, central Java. A joint-venture of six major Japanese trading houses, will obtain the money for the loan from a group of 21 Japanese banks led by Industrial Bank of Japan, said Mitsui & Co., which is heading the joint venture.

`TIGHT' AUSTRALIAN BUDGET

Australian Treasurer Paul Keating presented what he termed a "tight" federal budget designed to keep Australia on an even economic keel and enable it to dig out from under its massive foreign debt. He announced the government would post a budget surplus of 8.11 billion Australian dollars (US$6.56 billion) for the financial year ending June 30, 1991. The latest projection follows an A$8.04 billion surplus a year earlier.

SPIT AND POLISH PATROL

About 76,000 health inspectors are fanning out across Beijing to enforce higher fines against spitting and littering and to clean up the Chinese capital before next month's Asian Games. In one five-hour span near the city's main railway station, 230 people were fined, the China Daily said. Beijing is undergoing a frenzied facelift, repaving roads, painting fading facades and even hiding urban blight behind newly built walls. The games are expected to attract athletes from more than 30 countries.

SOUTH KOREAN ARMS ORDERS

South Korean manufacturers have been flooded with orders from Middle East nations for combat boots, uniforms and other military supplies, an official said. South Korean newspapers have reported that the U.S. has pressured South Korea to furnish military supplies to Saudi Arabia and other countries who are committed to an alliance against Iraq.

U.S.-VIETNAMESE RELATIONS

Two U.S. legislators said Vietnam's economic improvements and its willingness to hold direct talks with Washington justify U.S. efforts to normalize relations between the countries.

Sen. Richard Lugar (R., Ind.) and Rep. David Dreier (R., Calif.), who met Vietnamese leaders in Hanoi last weekend, told a news conference in Hong Kong that a recent U.S. policy shift had offered a new impetus to talks between high-ranking U.S. and Vietnamese officials.

In July, the U.S. withdrew recognition of the Khmer Rouge-dominated opponents to Cambodia's Vietnamese-backed government.

Sen. Lugar, a member of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee and one of the highest-ranking U.S. legislators to visit Vietnam since 1975, said he and Rep. Dreier, a member of the House Banking and Small Business committees, would tell Washington "that Vietnam has made considerable progress in moving toward a market economy, that there are clearly opportunities there for American businessmen, and that the U.S. should carefully consider whether this is an opportunity to progress (toward closer economic ties)."
