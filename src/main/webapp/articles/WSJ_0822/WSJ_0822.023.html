International: Yemen Leaves Oil Aboard Iraq Ships, Stalling U.N. Vote

  • Author: Geraldine Brooks
  • Date: 08/22/90

SANAA, Yemen -- By deciding not to unload Iraqi oil from ships arriving at its Aden port, this small country may have stalled the outbreak of a shooting war in the Persian Gulf. "The U.S. wanted to pick a fight on this," but Yemen was determined to avoid offering a pretext for conflict, says Yemen Foreign Minister Abdul Karim al-Iryani.

Yemen abstained in the United Nations Security Council on the resolution calling for sanctions against Iraq. Now it has shown that in practice it intends to observe sanctions.

"Everyone is taking it for granted that Yemen supports Iraq," Mr. al-Iryani says, when in fact the country's position opposes both the invasion of Kuwait and foreign intervention to reverse it. "We support Iraq in confronting this almost-crusade from the entire world. We feel that the problem had other solutions than going to war."

He says foreign forces "are coming to destroy Iraq to get it out of Kuwait. As Arabs, it is impossible to condone the destruction of Iraq and the killing of hundreds of times more Kuwaitis in the process than Iraq has done."

Yesterday, Yemen forestalled a U.S. push to have the Security Council approve the use of force to prevent sanctions-busting when it said it would refuse to accept a shipload of Iraqi crude. U.S. warships had fired warning shots at two ships and tracked one of them as it sailed on toward Aden. There had been a sense of urgency yesterday to the U.S. request that the U.N. Security Council approve military interdiction as the Iraqi tanker approached Yemen, but Yemen's statement that it would adhere to the sanctions cooled the atmosphere and no agreement was reached.

Yemen officials said that two ships carrying Iraqi crude oil had been allowed to dock in Aden, but said they wouldn't be allowed to unload. Yemen's stand is already proving costly for this poorest of Arabian lands. Kuwaiti officials in exile, seething over Yemen's abstentions in the U.N. and at Arab meetings in Cairo, have canceled $10 million in annual aid to Yemen's university and major hospital, Yemeni Foreign Ministry sources said.

Now, it seems that Yemen also will have to close its Aden oil refinery, which is supplied by Kuwaiti, Iraqi and some Soviet crude. It has a capacity of 165,000 barrels a day, but because it is about 33 years old, it generally runs at only 50% capacity.

"We weren't so lucky as the U.S.," says Mr. al-Iryani, noting that Iraqi crude bound for the U.S. before sanctions were imposed is permitted to unload. "Our ships were a little bit late." Yemen, he says, would like to be considered for compensation under U.N. provisions, "but we aren't blue-eyed boys right now with the U.S., so they probably will do very little to compensate us."

The foreign minister believes Yemen is suffering from a crisis mentality among Gulf countries and the U.S. that says "either you are with us or against us." In fact, he believes, "with war or without war, the U.S. alone can't solve this problem. Even if Iraq raises the white flag, the U.S. will need uncommitted Arabs to help her."
