NVR Posts Loss For Quarter, Cites Restructuring Costs

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 08/22/90

NVR Limited Partnership posted a $53.3 million, or $1.90-a-share, second-quarter loss, due mostly to the restructuring program the company began in recent months. The McLean, Va., company also said that it wouldn't make a second-quarter distribution to unit holders and it wasn't certain whether it would be able to make interest payments on its subordinated debt securities due later this year.

A year earlier, the company earned $9 million, or 34 cents a share.

The residential homebuilder and land developer said that restructuring provisions totaled $58.5 million. The company attributed about $12.2 million of the restructuring charges to costs it will incur to carry out its previously announced plans to phase out home building operations from its Florida and Midwestern markets. About $46.3 million is related to the company's write-down of certain land and real estate partnership interests to reflect more accurately their current market value.

NVR has been hurt by the slump in the real estate market and the drop in demand for new housing. In documents filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission Monday, the company said that as part of its restructuring, it will re-evaluate all of its markets and businesses. The company said "additional losses are likely" but said it couldn't estimate them at this time.

NVR expects that its banks will extend the company's working capital line of credit through Feb. 15. It's currently in place through next Wednesday. A $50 million mortgage warehouse credit line previously was extended through Dec. 31.

NVR cited its second-quarter loss and certain bank restrictions as the reason for omitting its second-quarter distribution. In the first quarter, it had paid unit holders one cent per unit, after distributing 67 cents per unit last year.

The company said its board will meet after year end to review taxable income and determine how much, if any, distribution would be appropriate for 1990.

The company said that its senior subordinated note holders were previously notified by the indenture trustee of a covenant default. NVR expects that the trustee will notify the bondholders of another covenant default arising from the previously announced nonpayment of certain debts by its limited recourse land development subsidiary, NVR Development, which is in the process of restructuring its debt.

NVR closed yesterday at 93.75 cents, off 6.25 cents, in American Stock Exchange composite trading.
