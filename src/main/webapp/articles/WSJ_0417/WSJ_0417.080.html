Marketing & Media: NBC Remains No. 1 for Prime Time During Latest Season; CBS Is Third

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 04/17/90

NEW YORK -- For the fifth consecutive year, NBC finished as the No. 1 rated network in prime time, although its audience was down 9% from last year and "The Cosby Show," the cornerstone of its success, was no longer the top-ranked program. In figures expected to be released today, National Broadcasting Co., a unit of General Electric Co., was rated No. 1 for the 30-week 1989-90 season with a 14.6 rating, followed by Capital Cities/ABC Inc.'s network with a 12.9 rating. CBS Inc.'s TV network was third for the third consecutive year with a 12.2 rating. A rating point equals 921,000 households with televisions.

Last year, NBC finished first with a rating of 16, ABC was second with 12.9 and CBS was last with 12.2.

Overall, the three major networks attracted 65% of the available audience, down 2% from last year. The networks continue to lose viewers to alternatives such as cable networks and News Corp.'s Fox Broadcasting Co., which has only eight hours of prime-time programming weekly and isn't a full-scale network.

ABC, which enjoyed prime-time supremacy in the late 1970's, showed significant improvement against NBC in the 1989-90 season, which ended Sunday. For instance, "Roseanne" was the top-rated show, unseating "The Cosby Show," albeit by two-tenths of a rating point.

ABC also posted crucial gains in two difficult time periods: Sundays at 8 p.m., with "America's Funniest Home Videos," which began in January and landed in fourth place, and Thursdays at 9 p.m., with its new dramatic series "Twin Peaks." Last week, in the first broadcast as a one-hour series, "Twin Peaks" gave ABC its best ratings in that time period in four years. It is up against NBC's popular "Cheers" in the first half-hour.

Network executives conceded yesterday that of the 23 series introduced last September, virtually none were considered a hit, although ABC said it is pleased with "Doogie Howser, M.D.," a half-hour show about a teen-age doctor. Each network had its own disappointment: CBS bombed with its highly touted "Famous Teddy Z," about a mailroom clerk-turned-Hollywood talent agent; NBC lost with "Nutt House," created by movie screenwriter and director Mel Brooks, and ABC failed with comedian Jackie Mason's "Chicken Soup."

CBS made inroads in the critical 8 p.m. time period on Mondays with the half-hour comedy "Major Dad" and on Tuesdays with the one-hour reality-based series "Rescue: 911."

As previously reported, NBC saw an erosion of 13% in every key demographic group that advertisers want to attract, including the highly sought-after group of women from 18 to 49 years old. In comparison, CBS's viewing groups were down an average of 7% and ABC was flat from year to year.

"We feel comfortable that we're poised for next year," said Alan Wurtzel, ABC's senior vice president of research. In a news conference last week, ABC Entertainment President Robert A. Iger declined to speculate on whether ABC would overtake NBC next season. ABC hasn't been No. 1 since the 1979-80 season when it tied with CBS.

"The lesson this year," said Bob Niles, vice president of research at NBC, "was dare to be different."

Such a philosophy led to success with ABC's "America's Funniest Home Videos" and so far with "Twin Peaks" and with Fox's "The Simpsons."

Mr. Niles said some series introduced by NBC late in the season, including "Carol & Company" starring Carol Burnett, show promise. In its first outing in its regular Saturday 10 p.m. time slot, the show won its time period.
