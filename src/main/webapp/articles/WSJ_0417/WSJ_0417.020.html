Letters to the Editor -- North Korea: Handle With Care

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/17/90

Your March 12 editorial "What's Up, Kim?" correctly asserted that the discovery by South Korea of yet another North Korean invasion tunnel under the Demilitarized Zone symbolizes the threatening nature of the dictatorship in Pyongyang and its "communist absolutism." However, it failed to mention that, despite continuing high tensions on the peninsula, some U.S. politicians and opinion makers are calling for significant cuts or even complete withdrawal of the 43,000-strong American troop presence in South Korea. The U.S. should take care to prevent the euphoria over improved East-West relations from distorting its perception of Korea. Pyongyang maintains a feverish military buildup and has about a million soldiers, one of the world's largest armies. The combined force strength of the U.S. and South Korea, on the other hand, is about 700,000. The North deploys about 65% of its military strength in forward areas near its border with the South. In short, a strong first-strike capability remains a high North Korean priority.

Despite new and flexible diplomatic initiatives recently advanced by Seoul and Washington, Pyongyang has refused to allow progress toward achieving even basic confidence-building measures such as South-North summit meetings and trade ties. The North remains uncommitted to good-faith negotiations and appears to approach inter-Korean relations with one outcome in mind: the eventual domination of the entire peninsula under the North's rule.

Since the end of the Korean War, the deterrence represented by America's strong political and military commitment to South Korea has worked. The litmus test for the U.S. should be a simple one: When the threat from the North has diminished sufficiently, it will be time to begin removing American troops from Korea. This approach not only sustains adequate deterrence but also allows South Korea to bargain with Pyongyang from a position of strength. Recent U.S. experience in dealing with the Soviet Union gives credence to this course of action.

DARYL M. PLUNK

Visiting Fellow

Asian Studies Center

Heritage Foundation

Washington
