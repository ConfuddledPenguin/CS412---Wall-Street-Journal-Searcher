Saudis Warn OPEC to Reduce Output In Order to Stop Decline in Oil Prices

  • Author: James Tanner
  • Date: 04/17/90

Saudi Arabia warned the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries to cut excess production to stop the fall in oil prices. In a strongly worded official statement, Hisham Nazer, the Saudi oil minister, called OPEC's need to discipline output through "adherence to production ceiling and quotas. . . an extremely crucial matter."

Mr. Nazer said the current situation, in which oil prices have fallen by $2 to $3 a barrel from the start of the month, not only hurts producers but also weakens chances of a price recovery in the year's second half. He said Saudi Arabia's government "is making necessary contacts with other members of OPEC to bring production back to the determined ceiling" of 22 million barrels a day.

The kingdom announced separately that Mr. Nazer will meet today in Jeddah, Saudi Arabia, with the oil ministers of Kuwait and the United Arab Emirates. Kuwait and the UAE are blamed for much of the two million barrels a day in overproduction that has pushed OPEC output to an eight-year high of 24 million barrels a day.

It was known that Saudi Arabia, the world's largest oil exporter, has been worried by the April price slide. But, in contrast to other times when over-quota output eroded prices, the kingdom had remained uncharacteristically quiet until yesterday.

Whether the Saudi moves will curb OPEC's overproduction or not, they already have had an impact on world oil markets. Many markets were closed yesterday in an extended Easter weekend. But prices rose, if only temporarily, in U.S. futures and spot markets after the Saudi announcements.

On the New York Mercantile Exchange, West Texas Intermediate crude for May delivery briefly broke above the psychologically-important level of $18 a barrel. It then fell back to settle at $17.85 a barrel, still up seven cents for the day. June WTI rose 16 cents to $18.67 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange, and other petroleum futures were higher for the day.

Not everyone gave the Saudis credit for the rally. "This was purely a technical bounce; the market clearly was oversold," said Shahrokh Nikkhah, senior vice president, futures investments, at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc.

Still, the Saudi announcements contributed further to a theory held by a number of industry officials that $15-a-barrel oil is the trigger for OPEC action when oil prices are falling. Once certain prices threaten to drop through that level, leading producers begin taking steps to stop the slide, the theory holds.

A premium crude such as West Texas Intermediate still is well above $15. But the average of the OPEC basket of seven crudes fell to around $15.30 late last week. This is the basket that OPEC uses to measure against its targeted minimum reference price of $18 a barrel. Significantly, too, spot prices of Dubai, an important Middle East crude produced in the United Arab Emirates, have neared $14 a barrel in recent days.

More people than the Saudi oil minister are alarmed by the declines, of course. Several other ministers -- including Sadek Boussena of Algeria, acting president of OPEC -- have suggested that an emergency ministerial meeting might be necessary if prices continued to fall.

But such a special meeting is doubtful because the ministers already are scheduled to hold their regular midyear conference May 25. It's understood Middle East producers don't want to see a special meeting of all the ministers held before then.

Meantime, most of the oil ministers are stepping up their contacts with each other, in informal face-to-face meetings such as the one that will take place in Saudi Arabia today, and over the telephone. The "telephone dialogues" are needed to reverse "the present downward trend of prices in the world market," said Ginandjar Kartasasmita, Indonesia's oil minister.
