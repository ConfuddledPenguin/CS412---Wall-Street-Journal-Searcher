Time to Place Big Bets on Small Stocks? --- But Glamour Issues Still in the Lead

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 04/17/90

NEW YORK -- Big blue-chip stocks outperformed smaller stocks again yesterday, continuing a trend that has prevailed for most of the 1980s. However, small stocks seem to be starting to catch up to big stocks, which would be good news for a market that lately has been led higher by relatively few stocks.

Yet, a number of investors expect big stocks to outpace smaller shares this year. They say big blue-chip shares typically do better in a year of economic uncertainty. Moreover, they expect foreign investors to keep buying the biggest U.S. stocks this year because the shares are easy to trade, and companies such as McDonald's Corp. and Coca-Cola Co. are known world-wide. Also, most of the largest stocks are in Standard & Poor's 500-stock index, attracting the buying power of more than $200 billion in investment money that attempts to duplicate the S&P 500.

Even from a fundamental standpoint, small stocks don't appear excessively inexpensive, says David Rajala, director of equity management at One Federal Asset Management, which typically invests in large stocks. "We are skeptical that small stocks are getting ready to outperform," he says.

The engine of the stock market over the past year has been big stocks. New York Stock Exchange stocks with a median market capitalization of $10 billion had a total return-price change plus dividends -- of 25.7% in the 12 months ended March 30, according to Dalton, Greiner, Hartman, Maher & Co., a new money management firm. The smallest stocks on the Big Board, with a median capitalization of $53 million, lost 10% on a total return basis for the year.

But more recently, small stocks have started to catch the attention of money managers. For this year, through last Thursday when trading ended for the week, median returns on the Big Board's smallest stocks were down 2.6% compared with a decline of 2.4% for the largest stocks.

Is the long cycle of small-stock underperformance over? "It appears that the deterioration of small stocks relative to large stocks has stopped," says James Awad, president of BMI Capital, a money management firm. He points out that in the first quarter the biggest stocks still led performance, but some average-sized stocks lagged the smallest shares.

More investors are thinking of companies in terms of earnings potential, rather than buy-out value, Mr. Awad says. And more investors are taking a closer look at younger, smaller companies whose earnings are growing at rapid rates, he says. This year, he says, "safety" may not mean owning stocks of big companies with predictable earnings, but rather stocks of companies, both large and small, which have strong franchises and innovative products that assure earnings strength in a sluggish economy.

"If the general thinking is that small caps are the place to be, the trend will be in place for several years until small caps get overvalued," Mr. Awad predicts. He says the last cycle of small stock outperformance lasted from the fourth quarter of 1977 to the third quarter of 1983. For 1983 alone, the bottom 20% of all publicly traded companies, based on market capitalization, gained 39.6% on a total return basis compared with 22.51% in the S&P 500.

Timothy G. Dalton, Jr., chairman and president of Dalton, Greiner, is banking on small stocks. Mr. Dalton says 45% of the $2 billion the firm has under management is invested in stocks with a capitalization of $2 billion or less. He says many of these small and medium-sized companies are manufacturing or technology concerns, which are two sectors he's bullish on.

"We see an opportunity to capture a double play -- good fundamentals and earnings growth," says Mr. Dalton. Smaller companies also tend to be less leveraged and therefore pay less in interest payments and more to the bottom line.

Kenneth J. Greiner, executive vice president of the firm, says consumption should begin to decrease as a percentage of gross national product, while capital expenditures -- from research and development to refurbishing factories -- should rise.
