Wheeling-Pittsburgh Gets Breakthrough In Chapter 11; Profit Fell in 1st Quarter

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 04/17/90

WHEELING, W.Va. -- Wheeling-Pittsburgh Steel Corp.'s shareholder group, headed by Goldman, Sachs & Co., has agreed to back a rival reorganization plan, marking a breakthrough in the steelmaker's five-year bankruptcy-law proceedings. Separately, Wheeling-Pittsburgh said first-quarter profit fell 22% to $35.6 million, or $6.48 a share, from $45.8 million, or $8.49 a share, the year earlier. Wheeling-Pittsburgh filed for protection against creditors under Chapter 11 of the federal Bankruptcy Code in April 1985.

In a filing with the Securities and Exchange Commission, Goldman Sachs, which owns 34% of Wheeling-Pittsburgh's common stock, said it agreed to drop its own proposal and accept a plan filed by the company and its unsecured creditors' committee. Now, the plan must be approved by official committees of unsecured creditors and stockholders.

One current stumbling block to the plan's success, however, is the lack of an accord with the United Steelworkers of America. The reorganization plan requires negotiation of a new labor pact that is acceptable to the board and the official committee of unsecured creditors.

Yesterday, steelworkers frustrated with lack of progress in Wheeling-Pittsburgh negotiations, picketed company headquarters in Wheeling. The union is seeking a contract similar to one approved by LTV Steel Co., whose parent LTV Corp. also is operating under Chapter 11 protection. However, Wheeling-Pittsburgh and creditors deem the LTV labor accord to be too rich.

The plan, which Goldman Sachs has agreed to recommend to other stockholders, calls for 1.5 million common shares of the reorganized company to be distributed between the two 6% and $5 preferred classes. It also calls for 1,740,000 shares of common stock of the reorganized company to be distributed to common shareholders, along with two million warrants for common stock, exercisable within five years of the plan's effective date. The reorganized company would have 20 million shares of common stock.

Goldman Sachs may also be required not to fund or propose another competing plan unless the current plan isn't consummated by Dec. 31.

Goldman Sachs, as chairman of the company's equity committee, submitted a rival plan on March 30. That plan called for Goldman Sachs to raise $300 million of new debt and equity capital for the new company, which would remain independent with no majority shareholders. At the time, Goldman said it was in the process of securing commitments for $100 million of Wheeling-Pittsburgh new common stock and $200 million of new senior debt.

The two rival plans were submitted following a February bankruptcy court order, directing each group to file a separate reorganization plan.

Wheeling-Pittsburgh attributed the fall in first-quarter net income to a 12.5% drop in shipments and a less-profitable product mix. The latest quarter includes gains from tax-loss carry-forwards of $6.9 million, or $1.35 a share. The year-ago period included tax-loss carry-forwards of $14.3 million, or $2.80 a share.

Sales dropped almost 11% to $256.8 million from $287.5 million.

The company said incoming orders and backlog, while lower than the year-ago period, increased during the first quarter from Dec. 31. Wheeling-Pittsburgh said it expects similar production levels and some improvements in shipping levels in the second quarter. However, pricing, which softened from 1989's fourth quarter, is expected to continue softening in the current quarter.
