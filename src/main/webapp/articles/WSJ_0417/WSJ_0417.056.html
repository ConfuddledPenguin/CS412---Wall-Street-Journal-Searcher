Enterprise: Science Parks Fall Victim to Overly High Expectations --- Project in Connecticut Attracts Start-Ups But Doesn't Create Enough Jobs

  • Author: Udayan Gupta
  • Date: 04/17/90

NEW HAVEN, Conn. -- The gloss is off New Haven's Science Park, which promised to help revive this aging city. Since the park opened in 1983, it has welcomed more than 170 start-ups. About 100 of those businesses survive, providing about 1,200 jobs. The park also has created a core of new technology businesses in a city long ravaged by economic decline and corporate flight. Nevertheless, the $50 million project is losing more than $1 million a year, and it's losing support.

The park's four sponsors -- the state of Connecticut, the city of New Haven, Yale University and Olin Corp., a chemical maker based in nearby Stamford -- provided the capital and land for the park. Most of the sponsors say they don't want to invest much more, because the park hasn't revitalized its inner-city neighborhood or provided jobs for large numbers of unemployed workers.

"The results the sponsors expected haven't materialized," says Benno Schmidt Jr., president of Yale. With the university hard pressed to finance its own needs, "we don't want to be seen as the only supporters of the park," he says.

William Ginsberg, chief executive officer of Science Park Development Corp., the not-for-profit developer of the park, says: "Achieving our ambitious, original goals is still very much within our reach." But he concedes that the existing commitments aren't enough. "We need more time from our lenders, more capital from the state and more scientific interaction with Yale to reach the goals we set out to achieve," he says.

The experience of New Haven's park exemplifies the predicament that many of the nation's 110 or so science and technology parks face today. Once touted as quick fixes for economically troubled communities, the parks have been slow in delivering the promised cures. Now, many of their backers -- state and local governments, universities and corporations -- are themselves in financial trouble.

The disillusionment threatens the growth -- and even existence -- of projects such as New Haven's Science Park. "The disillusionment is spreading," says Bruce Merrifield, a professor of management at the University of Pennsylvania's Wharton School.

Under pressure to reduce budgets, every level of government is cutting back support for these centers, which have been a vital source of small-business development by providing low-cost space and a plethora of inexpensive services. Disenchanted sponsors "are selectively starving the very centers of innovation we need to be competitive globally," according to Mr. Merrifield.

The Science Park here was aggressively promoted as the solution to local joblessness, says Douglas Rae, chief administrative officer for this city of 126,000. But most of the 1,200 new jobs have been filled by professionals and skilled high-technology workers, instead of by local workers, many of whom are minorities and unskilled. The park, formerly home to an Olin manufacturing complex with more than 10,000 industrial and research employees, "hasn't solved" the area's unemployment problem, Mr. Rae says.

Officials in Connecticut, which suffered a deficit of more than $220 million last year on a $7 billion budget, say the park can't play as large a role in its economic-development strategy as in the past. "It's worked well on the incubator side, but no one is entirely satisfied with its impact on New Haven's economy," says Stephen Heintz, Connecticut's commissioner of economic development. "We have to begin exploring a variety of development strategies for faster solutions."

If the major sponsors back away, "the ability of the park to deliver the services entrepreneurs need is sure to be affected," says George Leka, president of Jule Inc., a maker of biomedical products and a tenant of Science Park.

Development experts say that New Haven's Science Park is a victim of planners' inability to understand the nature of technology-based development. "Technology parks are a long-term building process," contends Tripp Peake, a managing partner of Science Park Associates, a business development consultant.

"Too many planners expected the parks to be instant cure-alls for economic ills," says Ian MacMillan, executive director of the University of Pennsylvania's Sol C. Snyder Entrepreneurial Center. "They got disillusioned when the parks didn't provide quick fixes."

Initially, New Haven's Science Park received extensive support. Olin contributed existing buildings and the park's 40-acre location. The state and the city provided over $7 million in grants and loans. Yale chipped in another $2.5 million.

From the very beginning, the park proved extremely successful in attracting and supporting small businesses and small units of big companies, including Eastman Kodak Co.'s International Biotechnologies unit. and supercomputer maker Visionary Systems Inc., a spinoff of ITT Corp.'s Advanced Technologies Center.

But the park has failed to attract big-business tenants that can employ large numbers of local workers and provide the fledgling space with some stability. It also has failed to persuade big Connecticut venture capital firms to finance the growth and expansion of the park's fast-growing tenants. Complains Mr. Leka of Jule: "We haven't gotten the support of local venture capitalists."

Mr. Ginsberg contends that the park has succeeded as an incubator. "We've been more successful than most other projects like this," he says. But to achieve its broader goals, it needs more capital and more support from its sponsors, he says.

The capital is needed to improve the infrastructure so the park can attract major companies. "We also need to work more closely with Yale to sell the park to the big technology concerns," he adds.
