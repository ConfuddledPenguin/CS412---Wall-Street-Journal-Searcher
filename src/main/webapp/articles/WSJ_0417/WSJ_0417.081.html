Politics & Policy: Banks Unlikely To Be Big Lenders To Eastern Europe

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 04/17/90

WASHINGTON -- Don't expect international commercial banks to take the lead in financing economic reconstruction in Eastern Europe, a bank-supported research group warned. The Institute of International Finance said banks will be "cautious" about making new long-term loans to governments and government-owned enterprises in Eastern Europe because they fear a repeat of the large loan losses they had in Latin America and other Third World nations.

In a report released yesterday, the institute said banks are likely to confine themselves for the most part to financing trade and specific development projects in Eastern Europe. Such loans generally are for short periods and are better-collateralized than "sovereign risk" loans, which are backed by borrowing governments.

The institute acknowledged that its assessment of Eastern Europe's prospects is partly intended to lower expectations of the region's capacity to be rapidly integrated with the West. "There's a grave danger that all this hype about Eastern Europe can give an impression of economic strength that isn't there," an institute spokesman said.

The report urged Western governments and multilateral financial institutions, including the newly created European Bank for Reconstruction and Development, to accept most of the burden of aiding Eastern Europe. "The banks aren't in the business of conducting foreign economic policy," said Horst Schulmann, the institute's managing director.

The report noted that countries in Eastern Europe are still poorly integrated into the world economy and have large debt burdens. At the end of last year, seven East and Central European countries collectively owed the West $116.8 billion; that doesn't count those countries' debts to the Soviet Union and its allies, which aren't convertible into other currencies.

The report said Hungary and Poland are moving ahead of their neighbors in adopting market-oriented policies, while Romania and Bulgaria are lagging far behind. It added that East Germany, Czechoslovakia and Hungary have the best chances of obtaining fresh financing because of their debt-service record, close ties with the West and the relative strength of their reform governments.

The institute suggested that East European countries need to attract more private investment before they can obtain substantial new bank lending. Banks won't step up their lending until they see a "better balance between debt and equity" in the region's capital supply, said Mr. Schulmann. By attracting more long-term capital, Eastern Europe can avoid a debt crisis such as occurred in many Latin American countries in the early 1980s, he asserted.

The report said East European countries can best help themselves by abandoning rigid price controls, releasing state enterprises to the private sector, liberalizing trade and investment rules and moving toward a system of freely convertible currencies. At the same time, it said, these countries can make their markets more attractive to foreign investors by establishing property rights, improving their judicial systems and promoting uniform accounting standards.

Mr. Schulmann added that Western banks will have more incentive to do business in the region if they are allowed to open branches and provide general banking services there. Currently, the opportunities for Western banks to provide such services are limited.
