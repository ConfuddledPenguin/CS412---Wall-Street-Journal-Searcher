Foreign Exchange: U.S. Currency Strengthens on Optimism About Trade Gap; Mark Touches 95 Yen

  • Author: Randolph Walerius Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 04/17/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar climbed against all major currencies yesterday on optimism about the U.S. trade results for February and with the additional boost of what was apparently a speculative move in early New York trading. The mark surged against its Japanese counterpart, finishing above the psychologically important 95-yen level in Tokyo trading. After bobbing above and below 95 yen in New York, the mark settled at 94.96 yen, above 94.90 yen late Friday in New York.

Dealers said the mark led the dollar's move up against the Japanese unit.

With trading in much of Europe, Hong Kong and Australia closed for the Easter holidays, activity was quiet around the globe. But dealers said the U.S. unit's advance yesterday, particularly against the yen, could be a sign of things to come.

In Tokyo, Katsumi Asako, chief corporate dealer with Standard Chartered Bank, said the view that Wednesday's U.S. trade report will be very good is so widespread that many participants are going long dollars before the report is released.

The deficit is seen narrowing to a seasonally adjusted $7.6 billion from January's $9.25 billion deficit, according to the average of estimates of 15 economists surveyed. But talk is circulating in the currency markets that the number could be as low as $4.5 billion to $5 billion, dealers said.

Standard Chartered's Mr. Asako said a big improvement in the U.S. merchandise trade deficit could probably overcome strong resistance to the dollar at 160.35 yen, near the U.S. unit's recent high set April 2 against the Japanese currency.

The U.S. unit got a strong boost in early New York dealing on what Alexis Egan, a corporate trader at Banque Indosuez in New York, said was probably some speculative trading by a few participants taking advantage of the illiquidity in the market.

The speculators bought dollars, pushing it to levels that triggered stop-loss buying of more dollars, she said. The stop-loss buying gave the U.S. currency a ride higher, where the speculators sold it to take profits, she said.

In late New York trading yesterday, the dollar was quoted at 1.6775 marks, up from 1.6724 marks late Thursday and 1.6720 marks late Friday, and at 159.30 yen, up from 158.18 yen late Thursday and from 158.70 yen late Friday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6300, down from $1.6415 Thursday and $1.6368 Friday.

Early Tuesday in Tokyo, the dollar was trading at 159.75 yen, up a bit from Monday's Tokyo close of 159.42 yen.

Banque Indosuez's Ms. Egan said the U.S. unit might also have strengthened because the Bank of Japan didn't raise the nation's discount rate. The discount rate is the central bank's base rate on loans to commercial banks. Such an action over the weekend had been rumored last week.

Roy Tillenburg, a trader at Connecticut National Bank in Hartford, said the market is going long dollars not only because of expectations for a better trade performance, but also because talks between the U.S. and Japan are expected to improve the trade picture further.

There is less risk in taking a position on expectations of a good trade number than on a bad one, Mr. Tillenburg said.

Tom Trettien, vice president for corporate foreign exchange at Credit Lyonnais in New York, said the dollar is poised to move higher. He said recent U.S. economic data suggest that interest rates won't be coming down.

By contrast, the dollar will be contained within narrow ranges against the mark, said Connecticut National's Mr. Tillenburg. The market has built the most inflationary currency exchange rate of one East German mark for one West German mark into current rates, but the mark could be hurt by other developments, he said, mentioning the strife between the Soviet Union and the republic of Lithuania as one potential reason to buy dollars for marks.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $375.20 an ounce, down 70 cents. Estimated volume was a light 1.6 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Tuesday, gold opened for trading at $374.05 an ounce.
