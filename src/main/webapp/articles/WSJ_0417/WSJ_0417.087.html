Panetta to Propose Cuts in Military Outlays, Tax Boost

  • Author: Jeffrey H. Birnbaum
  • Date: 04/17/90

WASHINGTON -- House Budget Committee Chairman Leon Panetta plans to propose cutting military spending by $11.5 billion and raising taxes by $13.9 billion to meet the Gramm-Rudman deficit target of $64 billion in fiscal 1991. According to congressional staffers, the panel is to begin drafting its budget resolution tomorrow and will try to complete its work on Thursday. Its starting point will be Rep. Panetta's recommendations, which are being floated privately on Capitol Hill but won't be officially released until the drafting session.

The panel's task is to come up with a plan that would reduce the projected fiscal 1991 deficit of $100.5 billion to no more than $64 billion. Among other cuts, Mr. Panetta is expected to suggest trimming projected entitlement programs such as Medicare by about $4 billion, to cut nonmilitary discretionary programs by about $1 billion and to save about $1.4 billion in interest payments on the national debt, aides said.

The Panetta plan won't just cut, however. It also includes $5 billion to $6 billion in "initiatives," meaning spending increases, for such projects as AIDS research, education, space exploration and ground transportation.

The most contentious number is the reduction in projected military spending. Aides said the California Democrat intends to suggest trimming military outlays by $11.5 billion from the $306.9 billion projected for the year starting Oct. 1. These reductions are smaller than committee liberals were hoping for, close to the moderates' wishes, but far larger than the roughly $3 billion in cuts that President Bush suggested.

Mr. Panetta may try to placate the liberals on his panel by recommending deeper cuts in military spending authority, which is a separate fiscal category from outlays.

Reducing outlays -- the actual cash spent in a given year -- is aimed at avoiding across-the-board spending cuts this fall if the Gramm-Rudman deficit target isn't met. Cuts in spending authority, akin to the balance in the Pentagon's checkbook, would yield lower outlays in future years.

In the Senate, Budget Committee Democrats have had trouble agreeing on a defense number. Committee Chairman James Sasser (D., Tenn.) recently proposed a $15.2 billion reduction in outlays for fiscal 1991. But some other committee Democrats aren't willing to go that low. Some are still waiting for cues from Sen. Sam Nunn (D., Ga.), chairman of the Armed Services Committee.

The $13.9 billion in tax increases is the amount that Mr. Panetta contends President Bush has recommended in his own budget. The president himself lists that number as closer to $9 billion. In addition, the Panetta plan is likely to include about $4.5 billion in fees and collections that he doesn't classify as tax increases although Republican critics surely will.

In any case, the Panetta plan isn't expected to suggest any specific ways to raise taxes and will leave that decision to the House Ways and Means Committee.

Congress's budget resolution is the nonbinding blueprint for fiscal legislation. The president doesn't sign the measure, but his position on it is often pivotal to the success of the legislation that comes along later to carry out its instructions. Few if any Republicans are likely to back Mr. Panetta's budget resolution, which can serve, at best, only as a starting point for negotiations with the White House.
