AQUINO'S GUARDS

British army counterterrorism experts are helping to train Philippine President Corazon Aquino's palace guard to protect her against a possible fresh coup attempt.

Mrs. Aquino told a press conference the small team of British military personnel has been brought in to train the Presidential Security Group, an elite force of 3,000 men that guards her.

The president, who has survived six coup attempts in the past four years, said the training was being provided free as part of British assistance to the Philippines. She rejected as untrue allegations that British mercenaries were involved.

The British Embassy, while confirming the presence of a team of army trainers, declined to specify their exact role or how many there were. But Philippine military sources said the team was made up of about a dozen experts in anti-terrorism techniques, and that they arrived late last month. Anti-Aquino army rebels threaten to attack the British officers if they aren't withdrawn.

JAPANESE ACQUISITIONS ABROAD

Japanese firms purchased 448 foreign companies in the fiscal year ended March 31, up from 334 in fiscal 1988, according to a survey by Yamaichi Securities Co. Foreign firms acquired only 14 Japanese companies in fiscal 1989, Yamaichi reported.

MEXICAN TRADE

Mexico's new open door trade policy forced 50,000 small and medium-size businesses to turn to exporting or shut down in 1989, according to a study by Mexico City's Metropolitan University. The industries most affected are clothing, footwear, plastics, electronics, toys, foods and beverages, the study says. Last year, President Carlos Salinas de Gortari's government approved new foreign trade rules lowering duties and lifting restrictions on imports, with the aim of making Mexican companies more competitive at home and abroad.

FORTRESS EUROPE?

The U.S. International Trade Commission says U.S. business still worries that greater unity among West Europeans will lead to a "Fortress Europe" designed to protect their companies against U.S. goods and services. American interests that could be hurt, according to the ITC, range from the sale of insurance to the showing of U.S. television shows.

JAPANESE TANKER ORDER

Hitachi Zosen Corp., a leading Japanese shipbuilding firm, received an order for a crude-oil tanker valued at approximately $82.3 million. According to the Japan Ship Exporters Association, that's the highest price paid for a Japanese tanker since 1973, when the world shipbuilding industry contracted because of soaring oil prices. Hitachi said it will build the 280,000-grossmetric-ton oil carrier for Britain-based Lykiardopuro & Co., and deliver the ship in early 1993.

CRIME IN POLAND

Crime is soaring in post-Communist Poland at a time when police are short of manpower, money and public trust.

The number of crimes jumped 80% in 1989, to more than 500,000. Nearly a third were property crimes, traceable in part to the worsening economic situation.

Muggings increased by 26%. The number of reported rapes rose, while murders remained steady at about 500 a year.

But the police force is shrinking as crime grows. Col. Wojciech Garstka, spokesman for the Interior Ministry, says Warsaw alone has 3,200 vacancies. Low pay and public distrust account for the failure to attract new recruits, despite the surge in unemployment accompanying economic reform, says Col. Garstka.
