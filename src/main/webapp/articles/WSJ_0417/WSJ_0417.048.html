Marketing: Citicorp Deals AT&T A Wild Card

  • Author: John J. Keller and Robert Guenther
  • Date: 04/17/90

NEW YORK -- American Telephone & Telegraph Co.'s entry into the credit-card business has cost it a hefty chunk of Citicorp's telecommunications business. Citicorp transferred $30 million in telecommunications services now provided to its Citibank Card Products Group by AT&T to MCI Communications Corp., the nation's No. 2 long-distance communications carrier. The pact, which calls for MCI to provide inbound and outbound voice services, directory assistance, private data line services and MCI calling cards for Citibank employees, expands an $80 million MCI contract announced in November.

Citibank's decision is a clear sign that the nation's biggest bank issuer of credit cards feels threatened by AT&T's entry into one of its most profitable businesses.

Landing more Citicorp business is a coup for MCI, which has won some lucrative deals with financial services companies in the last year, including Merrill Lynch & Co., where it provides all of Merrill's voice and data communications services. That account is worth roughly $40 million annually. Other recent MCI additions include Aetna and Bear Stearns.

James Bailey, Citibank group executive for U.S. credit cards, said: "We've been studying communications carriers for some time. We've been trying to balance our business between AT&T and MCI. The fact that AT&T has decided to become a competitor of ours maybe accelerated that decision a little bit."

Victor Pelson, AT&T's president of communications services, said that although some of AT&T's big financial services customers are "concerned about the competitive threat" posed by AT&T's new bank card, "the general reaction among our big customers has been one of understanding. We recognize that in some areas we will be customers, suppliers and sometimes maybe even competitors."

Said Nathan Kantor, president of MCI's Northeast Division: "MCI, which doesn't own its own bank card, is starting to be seen as much more of a strategic partner for these banks."

Mr. Bailey said Citicorp has no intention of eliminating all its business with AT&T. "AT&T will continue to be a supplier to us in the card business." He added that AT&T and MCI will have "roughly equal" amounts of the credit card group's communications business.

Nevertheless, other Citicorp executives said that in moving some of its business the bank wanted to make a point of its displeasure with the "ungentlemanly" way AT&T has recruited some high level executives away from its main credit-card unit, Citibank (South Dakota).

"We wanted a particular group of managers who are expert in this {credit card} business, and they didn't exist at AT&T," said Mr. Pelson. "But our hiring practices have been aboveboard. We didn't do anything that wasn't of the highest ethical standards."

Citicorp's decision to switch vendors parallels its dealings with other competitor credit card companies. For instance, Citicorp units don't sell the travelers checks of American Express Co., nor do Citicorp executives use that company's green card for business expenses.

AT&T introduced its AT&T Universal bank card last month. Units of Synovus Financial Corp., a Columbus, Ga., bank holding company, will issue the card and handle processing and billing.
