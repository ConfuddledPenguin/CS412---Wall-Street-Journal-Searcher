REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Farm Follies

  • Author: null
  • Date: 04/17/90

Congressional liberals continue to demand tax increases, claiming there's nowhere to cut spending except the Pentagon. To test the claim, take just one area, say farm subsidies. Here spending cuts would perform the neat hat trick of reducing the deficit without higher taxes while lowering food prices for the poor. So what does Congress do? The House agriculture subcommittees are in a feeding frenzy to see who can bust the budget the most. They've so far raised the projected five-year cost of the pending farm bill by $42 billion over President Bush's request, up to a total of $180 billion.

That's bad enough, but history teaches that projected costs of these five-year plans never add up quite right. In 1981, the Agriculture Department said the farm bill would cost $12 billion over four years. It cost $60 billion. In 1985, Agriculture said the current farm bill would cost $52 billion over five years. It has cost $110 billion -- so far.

One reason the farm lobby can make such massive raids on the Treasury is that few people realize the full costs. Farmers tell us that if people knew by how much farm subsidies raise prices, there would be uprisings at supermarket checkout lines. Another reason there is no revolt is that most Americans have come to more or less accept several myths propounded by the farm lobby:

Without subsidies, the family farm would disappear.

Current subsidy programs compel farmers to increase their yields rather than control costs. This gives an advantage to large, wealthy farmers who can afford the huge combines that allow them to outproduce small farmers. Dennis Avery, a former government agriculture analyst, says, "Farm programs have led to an overcapitalization of agriculture with less actual employment than what it would otherwise have." As it is, the big growth in farm employment is at the Agriculture Department, which now has one bureaucrat for every six full-time farmers.

Farm programs are designed to help the family farmer.

In fact, most subsidies flow to large corporate farms or part-time "gentleman" farmers. The USDA counts anyone who sells more than $1,000 in commodities in a year as a farmer. The number of full-time farmers has gone up since 1980, and their average net worth is now more than $1 million. Since 1985, the USDA has spent more than $1 million on each full-time rice and sugar grower.

Subsidies are available to most farmers, and are universally supported by those who receive them.

Farm subsidies help only selected growers. Almost half of U.S. farmers produce meat, vegetables or specialty crops that receive no federal price supports. The bankruptcy rate of these farmers is below that of farmers who receive federal help.

Farm programs are now so generous that even the most self-reliant farmers have become yoked to them. A 1988 poll in Iowa found more than one out of four farmers who received subsidies were in favor of ending all support programs within five years. GOP Rep. Dick Armey and Democratic Rep. Charles Schumer have a plan to weed out wealthy recipients of farm subsidies. They would stop payments of any kind to farmers who earn $100,000 or more a year. Shifting farm programs from price supports to welfare payments aimed at needy farmers could cut the cost of farm subsidies by two-thirds.

So far, this sensible idea is going nowhere in Congress. We note that several well-off Members of Congress themselves receive federal farm subsidies. Since 1984, Rep. Charles Stenholm (D., Texas) has collected between $5,000 and $15,000 a year for producing cotton for which he helped legislate subsidies on the Cotton, Rice and Sugar Subcommittee. If executive-branch employees netted such a windfall they would face immediate investigation by a special prosecutor. Congress has exempted itself from the conflict-of-interest provisions of the Ethics in Government Act.

The subsidy lobby's basic calculation is that President Bush would't dare veto a farm bill, no matter how expensive, if it's passed just before the November elections. The best bet for containing the subsidies may be the next round of talks on the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, due to end in December. Negotiators hope to agree on a world-wide reduction of farm subsidies, and presumably Congress would have to write a cheaper farm bill.

As of now, though, Members of Congress seem bent on continuing their annual farm pig-out, all the while screaming at the President and the electorate for not raising taxes.
