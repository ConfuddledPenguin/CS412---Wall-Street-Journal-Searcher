Bank of New England Expected to Form Entity for Troubled Real Estate Assets

  • Author: Christopher J. Chipello
  • Date: 04/17/90

BOSTON -- Bank of New England Corp. will set up a new entity to take over much of its troubled real estate assets, according to banking industry officials. While details of the plan remain sketchy, the officials said it could resemble so-called good bank/bad bank programs set up in recent years by Mellon Bank Corp. and other banking companies. Bank of New England's new entity is likely to take on at least $1 billion of the roughly $3.2 billion in assets that were classified as nonperforming or as potential problems at Dec. 31.

Steven Wheeler will be named to head Bank of New England's program, the officials said. Mr. Wheeler, 43 years old, is former co-head of Morgan Stanley & Co.'s Morgan Stanley Realty unit. For about the past year he has run a financial consulting firm in Boston specializing in restructuring of real estate loans.

A Bank of New England spokesman declined to comment on the reported plan. But an announcement could come this week, when Bank of New England Chairman Lawrence Fish is expected to outline the company's strategy for recovering from the $1.1 billion loss it suffered last year and for working off its troubled loans.

The officials said the final shape of the new entity remains to be determined. It could simply be an administrative division within the existing bank, devoted to handling bad assets. But the officials said Bank of New England hopes to be able to raise funds from outside investors to set up a separately capitalized unit akin to Mellon's Grant Street National Bank.

By setting up such a "bad bank" -- also known, within the industry, as a "collecting bank" -- Bank of New England would be able to remove the troubled assets from its balance sheet. That would improve the financial health of the original bank, and presumably enable it to attract deposits at lower rates.

But that approach would depend on how much investor appetite there is for the assets -- mainly in New England commercial real estate -- that the entity would have to offer. With hundreds of billions of dollars in real estate assets from insolvent thrift institutions expected to be sold by the U.S. government in the next few years, there will be plenty of competition for investors' attention.

To be sure, the thrift industry properties are concentrated primarily in the Southwest. But Bank of New England will also have to deal with investor worries about the clouded outlook for the New England economy.

William Bahrenburg Jr., chairman of Morgan Stanley Realty and a former colleague of Mr. Wheeler, said Mr. Wheeler's broad background in real estate and finance is likely to come in useful in his new post. Mr. Wheeler has worked in Morgan Stanley's securities and corporate finance divisions. According to Mr. Bahrenburg, Mr. Wheeler "was very much involved" in several "significant corporate restructurings," including one at May Department Stores Co. in 1988.

Mr. Wheeler was president of First Winthrop Corp., a Boston-based real estate syndication and investment firm, during the mid-1980s. Mr. Wheeler and Mr. Fish, who was then an executive with Bank of Boston Corp., became acquainted during that period and got to be "squash buddies," Mr. Bahrenburg said.

Mr. Wheeler's operation at Bank of New England will work in tandem with the bank's controlled loan division, which is in charge of problem loans. Officials at Bank of New England have indicated that division is being expanded, despite sharp cutbacks in staffing elsewhere in the company. Richard Stover, a former officer of Mellon Bank's Grant Street unit who joined Bank of New England late last year, is expected to head the expanded division.

Whatever plans Mr. Wheeler and Mr. Fish develop for separating the bad assets from the good will have to be approved by federal regulators, who are closely monitoring Bank of New England's operations. Thomas Cimeno, senior vice president for bank supervision at the Federal Reserve Bank of Boston, acknowledged that Bank of New England has discussed the general concept of segregating bad assets. But he said the agency has yet to take a formal position on any plan by the bank.

Bank of New England currently has about $25 billion in total assets, down from more than $31 billion before its massive loan problems became apparent late last year.

In composite trading yesterday on the New York Stock Exchange, Bank of New England closed at $3.75, down 12.5 cents.
