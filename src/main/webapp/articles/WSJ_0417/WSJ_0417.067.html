Manufacturers Hanover, Chase Post Lower Net --- Operations at J.P. Morgan Anemic in 1st Quarter; New Unit Aids Banc One

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 04/17/90

Four major banks reported generally weak earnings, reflecting varying factors, including lower borrowing levels for corporate takeovers, mounting bad loans and the absence of interest payments from Brazil. Chase Manhattan Corp., the parent of the nation's second-largest bank, posted the biggest earnings drop yesterday -- a decline of 67% -- chiefly because of a $75 million increase in the provision for possible credit losses on commercial real estate loans.

An accounting change at J.P. Morgan & Co. more than doubled quarterly profit, masking continued anemic operating earnings. Without the change, profit would have been off 6%.

At Manufacturers Hanover Corp., a big drop in loan syndication fees depressed earnings 7% from the year-earlier level.

And Banc One Corp.'s 17% rise resulted partly from its acquisitions of two Texas banks. Excluding the Texas acquisitions and certain one-time items, after-tax profit was up 4.7%.

All common stock prices except Banc One's sagged yesterday in New York Stock Exchange composite trading on the earnings news. Chase fell $1.625 a share to close at $27.75; Morgan declined 75 cents to close at $34.75; and Manufacturers' stock fell 62.5 cents to close at $33.875. Banc One stock edged up 12.5 cents to close at $29.125 a share.

Chase Manhattan

Today's tougher regulatory environment for banks was reflected in Chase's decision to increase its provision for possible loan losses to $225 million, compared with $130 million in last year's fourth quarter.

James J. McDermott, research director at Keefe, Bruyette & Woods Inc., said: "Here we see the handiwork of the regulatory examination process. Banks are anticipating rigorous examinations and are adding to their provisions."

In a statement, Chase said that future provisions will continue at high levels if the domestic commercial real estate market deteriorates further.

Despite the higher provision, Chase's nonperforming assets remained below the levels of a year earlier on both a relative and absolute basis. Total nonaccruing loans stood at $4.19 billion, or 3.82% of total assets, compared with $4.2 billion, or 4.08%, a year earlier.

Net charge-offs of bad loans for the quarter totaled $325 million, more than double the $159 million charged in the same period a year earlier. Commercial real estate charge-offs were $15 million, up from only $1 million a year earlier. Charge-offs of domestic consumer loans rose by $23 million, to $83 million, largely because of portfolio growth.

Net interest income totaled $814 million for the quarter, down 1.8% from $829 million a year earlier. The 1989 interest figure includes $61 million in Brazilian payments, compared with none in the latest quarter. Non-interest income for the period rose 11.4% to $468 million, as consumer banking fees, trust fees and securities trading posted gains from last year's period.

J.P. Morgan

While Morgan showed none of the loan-quality problems bedeviling other banks, it is feeling the slowing pace of the general economy and corporate finance activity.

Excluding the effect of an accounting change for contracts hedging interest-rate, currency and commodity risks, Morgan's first-quarter results showed a drop in earnings to $169 million from $180 million a year earlier. Part of that decline reflects last year's receipt of $27 million in Brazilian interest payments; the latest quarter included no Brazilian payments.

Morgan's "asset quality remains impeccable," said Mr. McDermott of Keefe Bruyette. "For them, revenue generation is a key issue, and expenses continue to be high."

Net interest income fell 11% to $266 million from $299 million, as Brazil's de facto debt moratorium and tighter margins squeezed Morgan's net yield on interestearning assets to 1.43% from 1.63% a year earlier.

More than making up for that reduction were strong foreign-exchange and securities-trading results, which both more than doubled to $73 million and $140 million respectively. The strong gains in part reflected the U.S. dollar's rise during the first quarter.

But one of Morgan's mainstays -- fees from advising corporations on financial matters -- was hurt during the period by ebbing takeover activity. Those fees fell 30% to $62 million in the latest period.

Operating expenses climbed 11% from a year earlier, but dropped 9% from the fourth quarter, when costs related to its move to new headquarters were especially high.

Manufacturers Hanover

Two factors -- sharply lower fees from arranging loans for leveraged buy-outs of corporations and the lack of interest payments by Brazil and Argentina -- detracted from an otherwise improving picture at Manufacturers.

Chairman John F. McGillicuddy said, "Although earnings were down, they were nevertheless achieved in a difficult environment, including a slowdown in corporate finance activities."

Manufacturers is one the leading arrangers of senior bank debt for leveraged buy-outs, and the slowdown in that activity has hurt the bank's results. The latest quarter included only $11 million in loan syndication fees, down 70% from $37 million a year ago.

Similarly, the absence of interest payments by certain Latin American borrowers reduced interest income by $18 million.

Despite those factors, Manufacturers was able to post gains in both the interest and non-interest categories of income. Net interest income was $378 million, up slightly from $373 million a year earlier. And non-interest revenue also rose a bit, to $326 million from $319 million.

Reflecting improved credit quality, Manufacturers' total nonperforming assets and net charge-offs, excluding those of loans to developing countries, dropped during the period.

Banc One

The Columbus, Ohio, bank holding company attributed its record results to the inclusion of $10.7 million in its net income by the newly created Bank One unit of Texas. That unit is made up of the former Deposit Insurance Bridge Bank of Dallas, whose results were included for the full quarter, as well as Bright Bank Savings Association of Dallas, whose results were included from Feb. 2.

As of March 31, Banc One said its nonperforming assets were 1.5% of total loans, or $277 million, an increase of 7% over its year-end total of $259 million.

Banc One noted that its assets excluding Bank One of Texas were $27.2 billion at the quarter's end. The Texas unit had assets of $14 billion, including $2.4 billion attributable to certain provisions stemming from its purchase of the once troubled Texas lenders.
