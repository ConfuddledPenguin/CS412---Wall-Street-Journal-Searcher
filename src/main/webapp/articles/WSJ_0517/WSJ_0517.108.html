Sotheby's Art Auction Is Well-Received As New Sector of the Market Takes Off

  • Author: Alexandra Peers
  • Date: 05/17/90

NEW YORK -- As the impressionist art market shows signs of cooling from its torrid pace of appreciation, another sector of the art market may be taking off. Sotheby's Holdings Inc. held a well-received sale here last night of the art collection of Lydia Winston Malbin, which consisted mostly of Italian futurist paintings. Moreover, the auction house had its own money on the line.

Sotheby's had estimated that the artworks for sale would fetch $56.5 million to $74.8 million. The final total for the 91 artworks sold was $74.3 million, with many of the lots selling above their highest presale estimate.

While the sale was technically not part of the spring series of impressionist and modern art auctions that sets the tone for the overall art market, it was notable because Sotheby's had gone out on a limb to guarantee the entire collection.

Sotheby's declined to disclose the amount of its guarantee, but dealers reportedly put the figure at upward of $70 million. That includes the artworks sold last night and a handful of others, notably a Claude Monet painting estimated at $6 million, which are up for bid at separate Sotheby's auctions.

Bidding was active and broad-based from a variety of mostly European dealers and collectors. Buyers said demand was brisk because futurist works rarely come up for auction.

Under a guarantee arrangement, the estate receives the amount of the guarantee, regardless of what the bids for the artworks total, essentially making Sotheby's, not the estate, the primary seller. Guarantees have been much criticized because of the conflict-of-interest questions they raise, but they have grown increasingly common as competition for collections has intensified among the three fine-art auction houses here, Sotheby's, Christie's International PLC and Habsburg, Feldman Inc.

At the Sotheby's sale, among the top-selling artworks was Piet Mondrian's "Composition in Black and White with Blue Square." The stark geometric canvas sold for $6.8 million. It had been estimated to sell for between $5 million to $7 million. Overall the handful of impressionist and modern paintings in the Malbin collection, such as paintings by Matisse and Miro, sold within their estimates.

Sotheby's stock, which is traded on the New York Stock Exchange, closed $1.25 higher at $18.625 yesterday. The stock rose primarily on news of the stunning, world-record price set for a Vincent van Gogh at Christie's Tuesday evening. Kobayashi Gallery of Tokyo paid $82.5 million for "Portrait of Dr. Gachet," almost double the previous record for an artwork. Christie's stock also rallied in London Stock Exchange trading, closing at #3.37 ($5.66), up 10 pence.

At Tuesday night's impressionist painting sale at Christie's, the total for the artworks came to about $269 million, in the midrange of the company's published presale expectations. Overall, 24 of the 81 works Christie's auctioned didn't sell, and of those that did, 31% sold below their presale price estimates. Moreover at a daytime sale of second-tier and less-expensive impressionist art yesterday, Christie's reported that one-fourth of the artworks were unsold.

On balance, the results weren't as impressive as those reached in recent years, showing a slowing in the rate of appreciation for impressionist art, but they were better than the doleful returns fetched for contemporary art at Sotheby's and Christie's last week.

Christopher Burge, president of Christie's America, noted that, while some pieces sold below estimates on Tuesday, "they sold," and the numbers represented a "solidifying of the market."

The sales so far have been a prelude to Sotheby's huge auction of impressionist and modern paintings tonight. The centerpiece of that sale is Pierre-Auguste Renoir's "Au Moulin de la Galette," an ebullient, colorful depiction of a Parisian dance hall. The estimate for the painting is $40 million to $50 million, but given the price fetched by the more somber van Gogh, some dealers are now expecting it to break the world record. Reportedly, the well-endowed Getty Museum of Los Angeles is expected to be among the bidders.
