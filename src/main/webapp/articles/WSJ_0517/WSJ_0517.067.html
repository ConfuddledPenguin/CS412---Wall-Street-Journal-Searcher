Politics & Policy: Lawmakers Seek to Pass 1990 Farm Bill Before Summit Brings Any Subsidy Cuts

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 05/17/90

WASHINGTON -- Farm policy makers in Congress are in a rush to pass a politically palatable 1990 farm bill, putting off until later any deep crop-subsidy cuts that may come out of the budget summit. Such a legislative strategy would let Congress reauthorize the government's expiring farm programs for another five years and avoid the pain of slashing agricultural subsidies until the fall or, conceivably, in a lame-duck session after the November elections.

Both the House and Senate appear inclined to hold the line on farm spending by adopting a $53 billion proposal, roughly what would be spent over the next five years if the 1985 farm law were extended. At the same time, House and Senate Democrats are contemplating various plans to boost farm income, including tying subsidy payments to farm-production costs or the consumer price index. But they would find savings elsewhere in the farm budget to offset such subsidy increases.

The current thinking is that if the White House and congressional negotiators reach a budget agreement, farm lawmakers can always go back in September or December and make whatever cuts are required of agriculture. With the entire House and one-third of the Senate, including nine Agriculture Committee members, up for re-election, this approach has considerable bipartisan appeal.

"Politically, you always look for the course of least resistance," said Rep. Kika de la Garza, chairman of the House Agriculture Commitee. "My estimation is that they can't hit us with any more cuts. {But} if we need to do more, we will."

At this point, the consensus is that agriculture won't have to take more than a $1.5 billion cut in fiscal 1991 -- about what the Bush administration has recommended. "There's a strong case for freezing {commodity} target prices where they're at," said Rep. Leon Panetta (D., Calif.). "Frankly, from the view of the House Budget Committee, that wouldn't be all that bad."

The administration proposes cutting farm-program spending $19.2 billion over the next five years, while the Senate budget resolution spares farmers from any cuts. The House budget resolution calls for cuts totaling $8.1 billion, including $800 million in fiscal 1991. But it insists that these cuts be contingent on a summit agreement regarding taxes or other revenue-raising measures.

"In political and procedural terms, we won't be forcing spending cuts until we have a revenue deal," said a Budget Committee staff member. "That could be very much later this year."

Earlier this month, Agriculture Secretary Clayton Yeutter denounced several House Agriculture subcommittees for exceeding the so-called spending baseline of $53 billion by $14.3 billion. "Maybe these big, high numbers make good campaign material back home, but the day of reckoning is coming," Mr. Yeutter said.

Mr. de la Garza (D., Texas), in turn, cautioned his colleagues: "We have promised policies we don't have money for." He now has a contingent of subcommittee chairmen and ranking Republicans working to bring the budget numbers down.

Taking a bloated farm bill to the House floor would be "a big mistake," said Rep. Edward Madigan, ranking Republican on the House Agriculture Committee. "It would be just inviting a lot of trouble that the committee wouldn't be able to handle."

For one thing, a coalition of conservative Republicans and big-city Democrats, led by Rep. Richard Armey (R., Texas) and Rep. Charles Schumer (D., N.Y.), is preparing to pounce on the bill at the first opportunity. One of the coalition's main objectives is to prohibit farmers earning $100,000 a year or more from receiving farm subsidies. Some members also have targeted certain programs, such as peanuts and sugar, as being anti-consumer.

"I think their presence dictates to the committee an extra degree of fiscal responsibility," said Mr. Madigan of Illinois.
