Economy: April's 0.2% Rise in Consumer Prices Reflects Slowing in Rate of Inflation

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 05/17/90

WASHINGTON -- Inflation at the retail level is slowing from its fiery pace at the start of the year, following the path of wholesale prices, new government figures indicate. The Labor Department said its consumer price index rose 0.2% in April, the mildest increase since September and far below the rises of 0.5% in March and February and 1.1% in January.

The new price figures, which came a few days after the Labor Department said producer prices declined in April, supported the predictions of many analysts that the inflation surge at the start of the year would be temporary.

The price slowdown comes amid signs of continuing slack in the economy. The Commerce Department said housing starts fell 5.8% in April, following drops of 11.2% and 5.1% in March and February. Earlier government reports for April showed declines in industrial output, retail sales and private employment.

Many economists said the price figures give little indication that real progress is being made in reducing the country's core inflation rate.

"The real changes have been in perception and market sentiments," said Robert Falconer, senior vice president of Aubrey G. Lanston & Co. in New York. "Underlying inflation is not getting any better or any worse."

Excluding food and energy prices, whose wide and frequent swings tend to distort the overall inflation picture, consumer prices also rose 0.2% in April. Food and energy prices, along with a surge in clothing prices, were largely responsible for the first quarter's swift inflation rate, which was the fastest pace in seven years.

The latest reading brought the increase in consumer prices over the preceding 12 months down to 4.7%, where inflation has been hovering during the past few years. Excluding food and energy, the rate was 4.8%.

"Obviously the figure was good news," said Richard D. Rippe, chief economist for Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. in New York. "But I don't think one month of a two-tenths CPI reading is going to erase inflation concerns."

Still, given the moderation in both producer and consumer prices -- along with new evidence of weakness in the economy -- it seems likely that the Federal Reserve will leave interest rates alone for a while. Before this month's string of reports, speculation had grown that the Fed was about to push up interest rates to battle price pressures.

After the inflation report came out yesterday, John LaWare, a Fed governor, said: "It sounds pretty good."

Most of the moderation in consumer prices last month came in categories that posted sharp rises in earlier months. Energy prices, which soared in January in response to a surge in demand during a late December cold wave, fell for the third-consecutive month. They dropped 0.4%. Food prices, which shot up after the cold snap damaged crops in the South, fell 0.2%.

Clothing prices grew only 0.1% after leaping 3.3% in February and 1.6% in March. The earlier jumps had puzzled analysts, but the Labor Department said they partly reflected earlier-than-usual introduction of spring-fashion lines, which escaped the department's formula for adjusting for regular seasonal price changes. Housing costs inched up 0.1% after rising 0.5% in March.

In some other areas inflation remained stubborn. Medical-care costs increased 0.8% for the third month in a row, bringing their rise over the past 12 months to 8.9%. Entertainment costs grew 0.4% after rising 0.6% the month before.

Richard Peach, deputy chief economist at the Mortgage Bankers Association, blamed the miserable performance in housing construction on rising mortgage rates. Work began on houses and apartments at a 1,245,000 annual rate in April, the lowest level since October 1982, when the country was in a recession.

At the end of April the interest rate on 30-year fixed-rate mortgages averaged around 11.1% compared with about 10% at the start of the year, according to the bankers association.

"Builders are scanning the environment and seeing sales falling, inventories rising and interest rates on a dramatic upward trend for four months," said Mr. Peach. "I suspect that really shook their confidence in going out and starting new units."

Starts of single-family homes, the core of the housing market, dropped 7.5% after plummeting 12.5% in March. Building permits tumbled 8.7%, to an annual rate of 1,125,000.

But mortgage rates have been edging down in May, leading housing economists to hope for improvement in both home building and sales during the month.

The numbers in both reports were adjusted for seasonal fluctuations.
