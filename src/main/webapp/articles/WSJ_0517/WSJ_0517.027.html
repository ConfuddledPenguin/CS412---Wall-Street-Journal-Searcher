SUPPORT FOR THE ECU

A survey of European companies and banks found broad support for elevating the European currency unit, or ECU, to become the main currency for commerce within the European Community.

The study, sponsored by the Paris-based Association for the Monetary Union of Europe, urged that in 1997 the ECU be declared the sole currency in the EC, replacing the currencies of the 12 member states. The study, covering 209 European companies and 47 banks, was conducted by Ernst & Young Management Consultants and the British-based National Institute of Economic and Social Research.

The ECU is a basket currency made up of weighted values of 12 currencies. Governments and international institutions have occasionally issued ECU-denominated bonds, and certain transactions are accounted for in the currency. But it hasn't gained wide commercial use.

Most of the companies surveyed expected significant savings to result from a single currency. Companies could eliminate the costs of hedging against currency fluctuations and would have clearer information about prices and costs throughout the EC, the study pointed out.

A STRIKE IN PERU

A strike this week by 12,000 workers of Peru's biggest mining concern has cost at least $2.8 million, the government-owned company said. The strike that began Monday at Central Peru Mining Co., or Centromin, is "causing grave and perhaps irreversible damage to {the company} and is endangering its further operations," a company statement said. The workers are demanding a 137% increase in their wages, which inflation has shrunk to about $5 per day; Centromin is offering a 90% increase.

POLAROID IN MOSCOW

Polaroid Corp. opened a new retail shop in Moscow that will sell the company's cameras to Soviet citizens for rubles. The cameras, assembled at a Soviet plant from Polaroid parts, will sell for 250 rubles, about $405, with instant film going for 30 rubles, a company spokesman said. The new shop is a joint venture formed by Polaroid and the Soviet Ministry of Atomic Energy and Industries. Sales were expected to be about 10,000 cameras in the first year, a Polaroid official said.

SWITZERLAND AND THE IMF

Switzerland said it will submit an application for membership in the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank. It said the decision to apply was made after Swiss finance and monetary officials held exploratory discussions with their counterparts in the Group of Ten industrial countries, of which Switzerland is a member. The questions of what Switzerland's capital quota would be in the IMF and if it would obtain a seat on the body's executive board remain open.

THE CIA ON EASTERN EUROPE

Many Eastern European economies will weaken before they gain strength, the U.S. Central Intelligence Agency said in a report. The CIA predicted it would take years before East Europeans' living standards rebounded from the damage inflicted by longtime communist rule. "The region's economic situation is deteriorating, and there is a prospect for deep recession," said the report.

KIRIN PROJECT IN CHINA

Kirin Brewery Co., Japan's top beer brewer, said that it has reached an agreement with another Japanese company and Chinese interests to establish a joint agribusiness venture in China. Kirin officials said the new company, to be called Qingdao International Seeds Co., will be established in Qingdao sometime this summer and will be owned 45% by Kirin, 5% by Tokita Seeds Co. of Japan and 50% by Chinese interests. The new company will grow vegetables and flowers and market them in and outside China.

FRENCH FIRE PREVENTION

Faced with a replay of last year's drought, France is pouring a record 780 million francs ($141 million) into fighting and preventing forest fires.

The campaign has begun two months ahead of schedule because water reserves are less than half those of an average year, and 33,300 acres of forest have already burned this year, though the official fire season hasn't begun.

Half the money targets airborne protection from spotter and water-bombing planes and helicopters. The rest covers preventive measures such as clearing highly flammable undergrowth.

Though efforts focus on Southern France, the drought isn't limited to the sunny Mediterranean. Normally soggy Lower Normandy is restricting watering of gardens and golf courses, and washing cars is temporarily banned.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

Australia's state-owned communications company OTC is developing a computerized speech simulator with an Australian accent. An OTC spokesman said current voice-reproduction systems were generally of poor quality and tended to have strong American accents. . . . Ireland's Family Planning Association was fined $600 for selling condoms at a Virgin Records store in Dublin. Under Irish law, condoms can be sold only in drugstores and family-planning clinics. Richard Branson, Virgin Records' British chief, said the Irish government seemed "to be treating their citizens with the same disregard as Ceausescu did in Romania."
