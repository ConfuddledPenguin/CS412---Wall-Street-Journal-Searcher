Media: Maxwell Nabs Three Supermarket Tabs

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 05/17/90

NEW YORK -- "Hungry Alien Gobbles Tab Trio]" That headline might be a fittingly sensationalized banner in one of the newest properties of British media mogul Robert Maxwell, who announced plans to acquire three of the largest-selling tabloid papers in the freaks-and-geeks field -- the Globe, the National Examiner and the Sun -- from Globe International Publishing Inc. of Montreal. Publishing executives involved in the transaction say the price topped $100 million.

The acquisition thrusts Mr. Maxwell and his closely held Mirror Group Newspapers Inc. into an escalating war of supermarket tabloids. He plans to acquire the three weakest competitors in the field, aiming to spruce them up and go head to head with the granddaddy of sensation, the National Enquirer, and its sisters, the Star and Weekly World News, all owned by Macfadden Holdings Inc. of New York.

The Globe acquisition gives Mr. Maxwell, the rotund and hyper-competitive chairman of Maxwell Communication Corp., a spot on U.S. magazine racks, a market he has fought to enter before. In April 1989 he lost out to Macfadden in the bidding for the National Enquirer, the tabloid leader, and the Weekly World News, which went for $412.5 million combined.

But not all supermarket tabloids are the same. What Mr. Maxwell has scooped up are the racier, raunchier and riskier of the tabs. "Jesus Is Already Back On Earth" screams a National Examiner headline this week. "Boy, 5, Gives Birth" informs this week's Sun, whose cover also features a large photo of a badly deformed baby.

Times have been tough for the tabloid field recently, but the Globe papers have lost circulation at a far higher rate than their three stronger rivals. The National Examiner circulation dipped 20.2% in the second half of 1989; the Globe lost 12.9% in the same time; the Sun's figures weren't audited for that period. Meanwhile, Macfadden's Enquirer lost 4.3% in the same period while the Star lost 2.6%.

Maxwell executives readily agree the sassy publications need some help.

The Globe tabloids "aren't the fat cats in the industry," concedes Ernest Burrington, deputy chairman and managing director of Mirror Group. "We realize these newspapers need to be strengthened." For now, Mr. Burrington plans no major staff changes at the tabloids, all three of which are based in Boca Raton, Fla., just down the road from the National Enquirer, in Lantana.

But the quality of the publications, which are profitable, may matter less than the national distribution system they use -- the true object of Mr. Maxwell's desire, in the view of some industry experts.

Mr. Maxwell bought not only the tabloids but also Globe Marketing Services, with a 500-person field force that distributes magazines and newspapers in supermarkets and convenience stores nationwide and negotiates for premium display space. Mr. Maxwell could use the operation to distribute other companies' publications for a fee and, more likely, to distribute publications he wants to start up or acquire.

"The field force is a great asset because competition for space at checkouts is so fierce," says Robert A. Cohen, head of Robert Cohen Associates, a New York publishing consulting firm. "I don't know if anyone really wants these magazines for what they are. What he's done is like buying landing slots at an airport."

Mr. Maxwell also seems eager to expand elsewhere. Yesterday he announced his intention to acquire a stake in Berliner Verlag, an East German publishing company whose properties include the 420,000-circulation Berliner Zeitung and the 1.5-million-circulation magazine FF.

Mr. Maxwell already owns several daily U.K. tabloids that are somewhat less raunchy than rival Rupert Murdoch's tabloid, the Sun, which regularly runs pictures of bare-breasted models on Page Three. In photographs in Mr. Maxwell's Daily Mirror, the women are less scantily clad.

In the U.S., Mr. Maxwell's tabloid archrival, the National Enquirer, is itching for a fight.

It could be an expensive fight for both publishers. Iain Calder, editor of the National Enquirer, said he looks forward to competing with the Maxwell-owned tabloids. But the Scottish-born editor boasts the largest editorial budget -- $16 million annually -- and thus can outbid other tabloids for "exclusive" stories. The Enquirer had offered $1 million to Marla Maples, alleged girlfriend of billionaire Donald Trump, but she turned it down. (Paying for stories is frowned on at most major newspapers.)

Globe executives said their tabloids operate with editorial budgets "significantly less" than that of the Enquirer. The Canadian publisher decided to sell the tabloids only within the last week, after choosing to focus on its consumer magazines, such as Bridal Guide. The company is owned by Michael Rosenbloom, who is 62 years old and has run the company for 20 years.

Peter Rubenovitch, chief financial officer of Globe International, says now is the time to sell the tabloids because "the prices in the market have been so buoyant." In March, Rupert Murdoch's News Corp. agreed to sell another scandal sheet -- the Star -- to Macfadden Holdings for $400 million in cash and stock.

Industry experts say the falling circulation of supermarket tabloids is due largely to overcrowded checkout shelves as well as the competition for mass audiences from the growing number of gossip sheets and magazines such as Time Warner Inc.'s People.

That doesn't mean Globe executives won't miss the tabloid business, despite its troubles. "I am personally going to miss it a lot," says Mr. Heine. "It's the most exciting part of the publishing business."
