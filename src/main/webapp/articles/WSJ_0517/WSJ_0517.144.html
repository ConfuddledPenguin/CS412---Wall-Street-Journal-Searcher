But If Christmas Is Moved, Santa May Put Coal in Their Stockings

  • Author: Keith L. Alexander
  • Date: 05/17/90

PITTSBURGH -- The Fourth of July is a long way off, but the fireworks have already started here. Declaring its own independence from the federal calendar, the city of Pittsburgh will hold its celebration of Independence Day on July 3 this year, even though the Fourth remains a holiday. Despite tradition, city officials figure it's a way to save some money.

But the decision has lit a fire under some residents, who consider it dumb and, if nothing else, un-American.

Not so, counters Pittsburgh Mayor Sophie Masloff, who made the change through her authority as chief civic permit giver. Through an aide, the 72-year-old mayor insists the change shouldn't be seen as a litmus test of her patriotism. It's a prudent move, she says, because it will save about $23,000 in holiday overtime for police officers, fire fighters, ambulance workers and city park employees.

As for people who have complained, city officials say, they should consider themselves fortunate for having even a July 3 celebration. It was "either canceling the Fourth of July celebration or compromising," says Albert Neri, the mayor's press secretary.

Still, the mayor's rationale hasn't cooled the fire of local business owners. They say the switch will cost them thousands of dollars because people won't be coming downtown on the Fourth.

"Anytime they change the celebration of a holiday two months ahead is asinine. If you're going to make a change, make it for 1991, don't make it for the same year," says Sonnie Miller, co-owner of Love Tourists, a local tour-guide operator. Now, Ms. Miller says, her company is going to have to notify 181 people who booked a dinner cruise on the Fourth expecting to sail down the Allegheny River with fireworks bursting overhead.

The city's police are also hopping mad. They charge that the mayor is merely being vindictive. She's sour, they say, because city employees won an arbitration case in February that more than doubles their overtime pay on holidays.

Although the city will sidestep holiday overtime pay, the police department may have the last laugh. Patrick McNamarah, president of the Fraternal Order of Police, says the department normally has more officers off on Tuesdays than Wednesdays. So the city will have to pay some overtime to bring in extra officers on July 3, a Tuesday.
