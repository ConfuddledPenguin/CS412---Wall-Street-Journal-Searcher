Who's News: Stride Rite's Jackson Again Resigns As President, Will Head Fisher-Price

  • Author: Diane Tracy
  • Date: 05/17/90

CAMBRIDGE, Mass. -- Stride Rite Corp. isn't likely to offer Ronald J. Jackson a third shot at filling the boss's shoes. Mr. Jackson, who has been president and chief executive officer of the footwear retailer for just six months, resigned to become president, chief executive officer and chairman of Quaker Oats Co.'s Fisher-Price toy business, which is scheduled to be spun off early next year.

It's not the first time that Mr. Jackson, 46 years old, has left Stride Rite for top posts in the toy business. In 1985, after serving as the company's president and chief operating officer for only five months, Mr. Jackson left to become president and chief executive of Kenner Parker Toys.

When he rejoined Stride Rite last November, Mr. Jackson said he planned to stay longer than he did the first time around, and this time he did, albeit only by about a month.

"There were a lot of questions at the time of whether Ron would do the same thing again. He reassured the board that would not be happening," said Stride Rite's executive vice president and treasurer, John J. Phelan. He added, "In the words of that famous philosopher Yogi Berra, `It's deja vu all over again.'"

Mr. Jackson couldn't immediately be reached for comment yesterday.

Stride Rite Chairman Arnold Hiatt will assume the responsibilities of chief executive on a temporary basis while the company searches for a replacement. "Arnold told me that in all of his corporate years, this has been one of the most devastating things he's experienced," Mr. Phelan said.

Mr. Phelan said the Fisher-Price offer is "a wonderful opportunity" for Mr. Jackson, noting that he'll get the chairman's post as well. "How often does something like that happen?"

In a statement, Mr. Jackson said leaving Stride Rite was an "extremely difficult decision," and that the Fisher-Price offer was "too good to turn down."

Quaker last month said it planned to spin off troubled Fisher-Price to shareholders in early 1991 in order to concentrate on its other businesses. Under the plan, Fisher-Price would operate as an independent, publicly traded company.

But some Wall Street analysts now suggest -- and Quaker won't dismiss the possibility -- that Quaker may decide instead to sell the beleaguered toy business, should it receive an adequate offer.

"There have been some additional inquiries since our announcement," a Quaker spokesman said, adding that while a sale was possible, "it's our feeling that a spin-off approach still offers the most shareholder value and we are proceeding with that as quickly as we can."

Quaker also announced yesterday that E. Robert Kinney, a former chairman and chief executive of General Mills Inc. and former chairman of Kenner Parker, which General Mills once owned, would serve on Fisher-Price's board.
