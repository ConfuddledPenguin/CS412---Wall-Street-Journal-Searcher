Heard on the Street: Stripped-Down Beneficial Profits by Lending To the Little Guy and Shunning the Big Deals

  • Author: David B. Hilder
  • Date: 05/17/90

NEW YORK -- Looking for a financial services stock untainted by commercial real estate loans, leveraged buy-out loans, foreign loans or junk bonds? Beneficial may be a good way to bet on the old-fashioned middle American borrower. After shedding unrelated businesses such as the Spiegel mail-order catalog, Western Auto Supply and a loss-plagued reinsurance unit, Beneficial is focused on consumer lending. About 63% of its loans are second mortgages; most of the rest of its assets are unsecured personal loans.

Those loans have helped Beneficial generate consistently increasing earnings. Several analysts expect earnings to increase about 12% this year and next; the company is widely expected to boost its dividend 9% to a $2.40 annual rate at its board meeting next Wednesday. Yet Beneficial shares, at 52 3/4, are selling for about 10 times trailing earnings, while the overall market multiple is 16 times.

Noting Beneficial's 13 consecutive quarters of increasing earnings, analyst Sy Jacobs of Mabon Nugent says, "In an environment where the Standard & Poor's 500 earnings have become unpredictable and disappointing, that kind of record deserves something close to a market multiple."

Others argue that Beneficial offers a chance to invest in consumer lending unburdened by other businesses. Of Beneficial's main competitors, Commercial Credit is owned by Primerica, which has big securities and life insurance units; and Household International has more than $2 billion of such unpopular assets as LBO loans, commercial real estate loans and junk bonds.

"My theory is that Beneficial is really the only pure play left in the consumer finance business," says Lacy M. Shockley, a regional bank analyst with Smith Barney, a Primerica unit.

The major risk for Beneficial is one that the stock market views as unlikely: A recession or depression in which unemployment soars and middle-income borrowers stop paying their debts.

Over the past decade, Beneficial has increased the share of its lending that is secured by homes, but it has remained conservative in the amounts it will lend. Beneficial won't allow total debt on a borrower's house to exceed 75% of its appraised value. The amount of Beneficial's average second-mortgage loan is $25,000; its average unsecured loan is $2,200.

Most Beneficial customers could get lower rates or bigger loan amounts from banks or thrifts, but the company has thrived by developing a network of offices staffed by aggressive, service-oriented salespeople.

One new program that is providing significant earnings growth is tax-refund lending, offered through H&R Block. For a flat fee of $35, Beneficial will send a taxpayer a check for his expected refund within a day or two after H&R Block makes an electronic tax return filing for the customer. Last year, Beneficial made about 550,000 of the so-called "refund anticipation loans"; this year, the number doubled to 1.1 million. Gregory Whyte, a Shearson Lehman Hutton analyst, expects the program to contribute 65 to 70 cents a share to earnings, up from 30 to 35 cents a share last year.

Andrew C. Halvorsen, Beneficial's chief financial officer, won't predict a dividend increase but says the issue "will be a topic of discussion at the May 23 board meeting. We realize that dividends are important to our shareholders." He also assures investors with long memories that Beneficial doesn't have any remaining liability for its former American Centennial reinsurance unit. The unit, which ate up $460 million of Beneficial's capital in 1986, was sold for $10 million in cash and $98 million in notes, and its new owners have paid Beneficial $15 million in principal and interest so far. Mr. Halvorsen said American Centennial soon will begin making modest payments on a $60 million note that matures in 1997 and that Beneficial carries on its books at zero.

One drag on earnings is the Harbour Island real estate development near Tampa, Fla., that will produce pretax losses of about $21 million this year, which Mr. Halvorsen hopes to reduce. A potential real estate gain literally surrounds Beneficial's headquarters in Peapack, N.J., where the company hopes to sell 600 acres of a 700-acre tract, now on the books at only $11 million.

Ms. Shockley of Smith Barney believes that within five years, Beneficial will be acquired by a diversified financial services company or a big regional bank. "I would not be surprised to see a price somewhere near $100 a share," she says.

But beware. Beneficial's management put the company up for sale in 1986, and some analysts estimated it could fetch a price of $85 a share. But the best bid was only $75 a share, which Beneficial rejected, and the stock since then hasn't approached its takeover-induced high of 78 5/8.
