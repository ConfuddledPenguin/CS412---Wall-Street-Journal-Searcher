Technology & Health: Hewlett Net Fell 8% in Quarter; Stock Slips $1.875

  • Author: G. Pascal Zachary
  • Date: 05/17/90

PALO ALTO, Calif. -- Hewlett-Packard Co. reported an 8% decline in net income for its fiscal second quarter ended April 30. Though the results were squarely in line with analysts' expectations, Hewlett-Packard's stock skidded on the news, closing down $1.875 at $46.125 in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange. Analysts said management's cautious forecast of future business and the disclosure that the company was losing share in the market for computer workstations sent the stock tumbling.

The slip in workstations market share is particularly worrisome since Hewlett-Packard recently was vying with Sun Microsystems Inc. for the top spot in the segment. Last year, Hewlett-Packard bought Apollo Computer Inc., its biggest acquisition ever, with an eye toward commanding a sizable share of the workstation business, which is growing rapidly.

"From their comments, it's clear that Sun is gaining share and IBM's entry {into the workstation business} is having an impact on HP," said Steven Milunovich, an analyst at First Boston Corp., referring to comments made by Hewlett-Packard executives yesterday during a conference call.

Orders for Hewlett-Packard's personal computers also have slowed and are flat compared with last year, Mr. Milunovich said.

Net income for the quarter totaled $186 million, compared with $203 million in the year-ago period. The company had per-share earnings of 78 cents, down 9% from 86 cents in the year-ago quarter.

Revenue for the quarter was $3.3 billion, an increase of 16% from the year-ago quarter.

Hewlett-Packard, which makes medical equipment, instruments and other electronic goods as well as computers, blamed its lower earnings on a continuing shift in its business that has made the company more reliant on sales of lower-margin goods such as personal computers and printers. The company said that, in response, it was moving to reduce operating costs.

For instance, in the second quarter the company said it cut its world-wide work force of 93,000 by 1,000, mainly through an early retirement program.

The situation facing Hewlett-Packard is similar to that facing International Business Machines Corp., Digital Equipment Corp. and other makers of minicomputers. As prices for computers decline sharply and minicomputers are replaced by smaller, far less expensive machines, these firms are stuck with large, costly direct sales forces that aren't any longer efficient.

While analysts said Hewlett-Packard is trying to cut costs, they said the company isn't moving quickly enough. They also don't expect any drastic moves any time soon.

"Progress is slow," said Peter Rogers, an analyst at Robertson Stephens & Co. "This is another discouraging quarter."

In a statement, John Young, Hewlett-Packard's chief executive officer, expressed caution about the outlook for the second half of the company's fiscal year. He called the company's results so far this year "mixed" and said that "slower economic growth continues to be a concern in many of the countries we serve."

"My concern is there's a good chance you'll have a sharp slowdown in {the company's} revenues growth in the second half of year," said Mr. Milunovich. However, he said he wouldn't cut his 1990 earnings estimate of $3.40 a share.
