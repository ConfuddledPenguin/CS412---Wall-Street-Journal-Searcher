Northrop Counts On Strong Rebound In '90 Profitability

  • Author: Roy J. Harris Jr.
  • Date: 05/17/90

LOS ANGELES -- Northrop Corp. said it is counting on a strong rebound in 1990 profitability, although Wall Street reacted skeptically to indications from its chief executive officer that the defense contractor is seeking to earn as least $163 million this year. In a news conference after Northrop's annual meeting, Kent Kresa, president and chief executive, said he hopes the company would achieve better than a 10% return on stockholder equity this year, excluding the $67 million gain the company recorded in the first quarter from sale of its headquarter complex here.

Based on first-quarter shareholder equity of $960.7 million, that would indicate at least $96 million, with total net income approximating at least $163 million. In 1989, Northrop had a net loss of $80.5 million, or $1.71 a share, although in the 1990 first quarter, net income surged to $96 million, or $2.04 a share, including the gain from the headquarter sale.

Northrop traditionally doesn't forecast corporate earnings.

In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Northrop shares closed at $17.50, up 12.5 cents.

Mr. Kresa also said the company plans a reduction in employment of about 3,000, or 7%, this year from a current work force of 41,000. Employment was cut by about 2,500 last year.

The reduction in employment reflects attempts to reduce overhead expenses at Northrop, which is the prime contractor for the Pentagon's B-2 Stealth bomber and for the YF-23 Advanced Tactical Fighter, one of two competing designs for the next generation of Air Force fighters.

Mr. Kresa said better inventory management and the efforts to reduce overhead will facilitate a planned major reduction of Northrop's current debt of $1 billion. He said the company's "financial projections show that we will eliminate our present debt by the mid-1990s."

The Defense Department's proposal to cut the number of B-2s to be acquired to 75 from 132 will lead Northrop to "reduce the size of our B-2 operations accordingly," the executive said. He said about 500 of the employee cuts could be directly associated with the Stealth bomber. As a way of "capitalizing on the reduced requirements for B-2 manufacturing," he said, Northrop would carry out full-scale development work on the ATF at the same Pico Rivera, Calif., facility where work on the B-2 is in progress. He said the Air Force would benefit from having the fighter developed at a leading aircraft-development facility.

The company also will continue to sell assets that "don't match the business environment we see ahead of us, or where we feel better use can be made of the assets," he said. Northrop's longer-term goal is for "returning over the next few years to at least a 20% return on equity," Mr. Kresa told shareholders.

David Smith, who follows aerospace concerns for the securities firm of Raymond, James & Associates in St. Petersburg, Fla., said the investment community "didn't even blink" at Mr. Kresa's hopes for earnings. That suggests, according to Mr. Smith, that "the market is saying I don't believe you." Mr. Smith, like other analysts, however, hasn't forecast earnings for the company because of a history of unpredictability that includes numerous write-downs from weapons programs.

Mr. Smith also suggested that the efforts to pay down debt, tighten operations and purchase shares of Northrop stock for the company's retirement plan could be part of a "scenario that could involve taking the company private." Mr. Kresa also said at the meeting that 1.2 million additional shares of Northrop stock had been purchased for the retirement plan, putting a significant stake in employee hands.

A Northrop spokesman said the firm doesn't have any plans to go private.
