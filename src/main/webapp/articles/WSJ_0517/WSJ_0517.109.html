Credit Markets: Interest Rates Finish the Day Little Changed Despite Encouraging Data on Consumer Prices

  • Author: Tom Herman and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 05/17/90

NEW YORK -- Most interest rates ended little changed despite further signs that inflation pressures have eased from their torrid pace earlier this year. A government report yesterday morning showing that consumer prices rose only 0.2% last month sparked a brief bond-market rally. That was the mildest increase since September and was slightly less than the 0.3% rise many analysts expected.

But the rally didn't last long, partly because of growing concern about the nation's huge budget deficit. Several money managers and bond traders are worried about the lack of progress so far in budget talks between Bush administration officials and congressional leaders.

Prices of actively traded Treasury notes and bonds ended the day unchanged to slightly lower. Long-term government bonds fell about 1/8 point, or about $1.25 for each $1,000 face amount. Short-term Treasury bill rates were flat.

Washington's growing budget woes are "not a positive for the bond markets," said William Brachfeld, executive vice president and head of fixed-income securities trading at Daiwa Securities America Inc. He said several Daiwa clients here and in Japan "are increasingly concerned" about the failure so far of the White House and congressional leaders to agree on a big deficit-reduction package.

The budget talks are being watched closely because an accord could mean less government borrowing and might even encourage the Federal Reserve to reduce interest rates. The Fed's policy-making arm, the Federal Open Market Committee, met Tuesday to review strategy.

Until recently, many analysts had expected the Fed to raise short-term rates in an effort to fight an inflation problem that appeared to be growing. But those worries evaporated quickly following a string of government reports showing unexpectedly weak business activity and less inflation than generally feared. Now, the consensus is that the Fed will hold short-term rates steady in coming weeks.

However, the Treasury's borrowing needs remain huge, and investors received a fresh reminder of this yesterday.

The Treasury said it will sell $10.8 billion of new two-year notes Wednesday, followed Thursday by the sale of $8.5 billion of notes due in five years and two months. The size of these two sales was "bigger than expected," said Kevin Flanagan, money-market economist at Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. "It eclipsed even our gloomy expectations."

Mr. Flanagan and other analysts worry that the Treasury's borrowing needs could be exacerbated by the thrift bailout and a growing federal deficit. "Things don't look that rosy for next year" for the budget deficit, Mr. Flanagan said.

Late yesterday, the new two-year notes were quoted in when-issued trading at 8.55%, while the new five-year, two-month notes were trading at about 8.60%.

Fed officials probably won't make any credit policy changes at least until early next month, after the release of May employment statistics, several analysts said. The April employment report, released earlier this month, showed much smaller employment gains than expected.

Robert Brusca, chief economist at Nikko Securities Co. International Inc., said the bond market appears to be "on hold" until the May jobs report. Until then, Treasury bond yields are likely to hover between 8.55% and 8.75%, he said.

Corporate Issues

Lured by lower borrowing costs, two more large corporations tapped the bond market.

Sears, Roebuck & Co., the Chicago-based retailer, was the biggest borrower in the corporate debt market with a $400 million offering of new three-year notes. The non-callable notes were priced to yield 9.387%. Underwriters led by Goldman, Sachs & Co. boosted the size of the issue from $300 million in response to strong investor demand.

Desmond Wong, corporate finance director at Sears, said the company decided to tap the market after the consumer price report showed an unexpectedly small increase in inflation last month. "In our case, we just saw the opportunity when the consumer price index was announced and the market was stable," he said. "We are getting a coupon of 9.35%, and for a three-year, we think that's a good level for us."

Separately, Maytag Corp. sold $200 million of 12-year debentures. Thomas Ringgenberg, treasurer at Maytag, said the appliance maker had been seeking to tap the market for some time. However, "it was really last Friday's PPI {producer price index} number that caused {yields on} Treasurys to come down within our range" and prompted Maytag to issue debt, he said. Maytag's 12-year debentures were priced to yield 9.814%.

Nevertheless, many other corporate treasurers are waiting on the sidelines for lower rates before they tap the market.

"It's going to take a long {Treasury} bond range of between 8 3/8% and 8 1/2% to bring a lot of corporate treasurers in," said James Quigley of Merrill Lynch Capital Markets. The Treasury's 30-year bonds yielded about 8 5/8% as of late yesterday.

In the investment-grade corporate debt market, corporate issues closed between 1/8 and 1/4 point lower. In the junk bond market, high-yield issues, or bonds with below-investment-grade ratings, ended the day mixed.

Among the junk bonds that advanced were issues of Owens-Illinois Inc. as investors were encouraged by the company's first-quarter earnings report. But among the big losers in the market were junk bonds of Burlington Holdings Inc., which slipped about 1/2 point on average.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities fell 1/32 to 5/32 point, but most issues held up better than Treasurys despite talk that the Resolution Trust Corp. sold as much as $1 billion of mortgage issues.

The RTC, the government conservator for failed thrifts, has stepped up its sales of mortgage securities in recent weeks as it seeks to resolve the situations of 141 thrifts by the end of the quarter.

While the RTC was a rumored seller, there also was strong demand for mortgage securities.

Government National Mortgage Association 9% securities for June delivery ended at 95 18/32, down 2/32; 9 1/2% securities were at 97 30/32, down 3/32; 10% securities were at 100 13/32, down 1/32; and Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9 1/2% securities were at 97 1/2, down 5/32.

The Ginnie Mae 10% issue was supported by the issuance of a $500 million real estate mortgage investment conduit backed by 10% securities via Salomon Brothers Inc.

The Ginnie Mae 9% issue was yielding 9.78% to a 12-year average life assumption, as the spread above the Treasury 10-year note narrowed 0.01 percentage point to 1.12.

Treasury Securities

After a brief early-morning rally, Treasury bond prices reversed direction, finishing the day with slight declines.

The government's benchmark 30-year bond initially rose to 101 19/32, a gain of about 1/4 point from Tuesday. But by late in the afternoon, the price had fallen to 101 6/32, to yield 8.63%.

The latest 10-year notes fell to 101 9/32 from 101 14/32. Earlier yesterday, the issue traded as high as 101 19/32. The yield rose to 8.66% from 8.64%.

However, prices of two-year Treasury notes inched higher, helped by rumors that an institution had purchased a large amount of the latest two-year issue and sold a similar-sized block of long-term bonds. The two-year notes rose 1/32 point to 100 19/32 to yield 8.51%.

Municipals

Tax-exempt issues ended mostly unchanged to off 1/8 point with some issues trading down 1/4 in a moderately active session.

In new-issue activity, $266 million in debt was priced, following more than $1 billion on Tuesday.

In related activity, Moody's Investors Service Inc. confirmed its single-A-1 rating on Nassau County's general obligation bonds, citing the New York county's "strong, diverse and affluent economic base" that provides "fundamental credit strength." The confirmation came ahead of the sale today of $85.1 million of various purpose bonds via competitive bid.

Among seasoned bonds, New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 was unchanged at 98 bid to yield 7.36%.

Foreign Bonds

Snapping a three-day rally, Japanese government bond prices fell as the yen sank against the dollar.

Japan's benchmark bond due in 1999 was quoted in mid-afternoon at a price of 87.79, down 0.34, to yield 7%.

In other major markets, West German bond prices drifted lower as traders braced for a heavy supply of new borrowing. The government is creating a 115 billion mark ($69.8 billion) special German Unity Fund, which will issue large amounts of debt to raise money to rebuild East Germany's economy. West Germany's 7 3/4% bonds of 2000 fell 0.15 point to 93.95 to yield 8.69%.

In London, British government bond prices seesawed throughout the day, ending very close to where they were the previous day. Britain's benchmark 11 3/4% bonds due 2003/2007 declined 1/8 point to 98 7/32 to yield 12%. The 12% bonds due 1995 rose 1/32 point to 96 27/32 to yield 12.92%, while the 11 3/4% issue due 1991 was unchanged at 98 6/32 to yield 14.75%.
