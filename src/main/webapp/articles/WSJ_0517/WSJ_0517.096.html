Politics & Policy: Rep. Waxman Says Health Official Urged Not to Speak

  • Author: Kenneth H. Bacon
  • Date: 05/17/90

WASHINGTON -- U.S. Rep. Henry Waxman charged that the Bush administration "silenced" a top health official by preventing him from testifying on U.S. efforts to promote tobacco exports. In a letter to Health and Human Services Secretary Louis Sullivan, the California Democrat alleged that James Mason, assistant secretary for health, was "instructed not to present testimony" at a congressional hearing on cigarette exports today. James Brown, a spokesman for Dr. Mason, called the charge "nonsense."

The flap highlights the awkward position the administration finds itself in over the promotion of smoking. Dr. Sullivan and Dr. Mason strongly oppose smoking, and Dr. Sullivan has sharply attacked cigarette advertisements aimed specifically at women and blacks. But another branch of the administration, the U.S. trade representative, has been fighting to tear down foreign barriers to U.S. cigarette exports. Despite his outspoken opposition to smoking, Dr. Sullivan has refrained from attacking the export promotion policy.

Separately, the Senate Labor and Human Resources Committee voted for an anti-smoking bill after deleting a provision that the tobacco industry complained would interfere with nationwide advertising campaigns.

As initially introduced by panel Chairman Sen. Edward Kennedy (D., Mass.), the bill would have repealed a federal preemption of state advertising regulations. This would have freed states to set their own standards, making nationwide advertising campaign impossible. Sen. Kennedy said he removed the provision to avoid a jurisdictional controversy that could have bottled up the bill. Remaining provisions of the legislation would impose new regulations over certain cigarette additives and finance new federal anti-smoking campaigns.

The cigarette export issue pits health concerns against export promotion. Congressional smoking opponents have launched a spirited campaign to stop the administration from expanding markets for U.S. tobacco companies by threatening trade reprisals against Thailand and other countries that limit tobacco imports.

Dr. Mason had been scheduled to testify today at a hearing where a number of witnesses, including the head of the American Cancer Society, are planning to attack the export-promotion policy. But he withdrew on Tuesday, provoking Rep. Waxman, chairman of the health panel of the House Commerce Committee to suggest that "the administration has refused to permit Dr. Mason's appearance before us."

HHS officials rejected this interpretation, saying that Dr. Mason decided not to testify after learning from the department's legislative staff that the hearing was on trade practices and that an official from the trade representative's office was going to testify on behalf of the administration. "I can assure you that the secretary didn't tell Dr. Sullivan not to go to that hearing," Mr. Brown said. "And I can assure you that Dr. Sullivan and Dr. Mason will continue to speak out about smoking and health."

Controversy over smoking is likely to heat up if, as expected, a proposal to increase the cigarette excise tax emerges during the budget summit as one of the measures to lower the deficit.
