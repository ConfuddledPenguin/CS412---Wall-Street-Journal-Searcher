Nazi Hypothermia Study Is Useless, Surgeon Says

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/17/90

Results from the infamous Nazi medical experiments at Dachau to see how much cold the human body could withstand are scientifically useless, a Harvard Medical School surgeon said. The surgeon, Robert L. Berger, reported that he had examined the surviving Nazi report on the experiments in which Dachau prisoners were immersed in tanks of ice water under various conditions. It is believed that as many as 300 male prisoners were subjected to the experiments, of whom 80 to 90 died.

In an article in this week's New England Journal of Medicine, Dr. Berger said he undertook the examination because of a long-simmering debate over whether scientists should use the results of the experiments despite their atrocious nature. Some physiologists claim the results might help advance research on the body's response to cooling -- hypothermia -- and thus save lives. But others, like Arnold Relman, editor of the New England Journal of Medicine, advocate a total ban on using the Nazi results because the experiments were "a gross violation of human standards."

Dr. Berger wrote that the 56-page report to SS Reichsfuhrer Heinrich Himmler on the results of the Dachau experiments was "riddled with inconsistencies" and contained evidence of data falsification and fabrication. "On analysis, the Dachau hypothermia study has all the ingredients of a scientific fraud, and rejection of the data on purely scientific grounds is inevitable. They cannot advance science or save human lives," the surgeon wrote.
