OTC Focus: Market Gains in Contrast to Big Board; Bio-Medicus Surges 3 3/8 on Buy-Out Offer

  • Author: Anne Newman
  • Date: 05/17/90

Over-the-counter stocks upstaged exchange-listed issues and posted a slight gain. The Nasdaq Composite Index rose .43 to 442.93, a 0.1% advance, compared with a 0.1% decline in the Dow Jones Industrial Average. Advancing shares reclaimed their lead over declining stocks, 993 to 960, but volume slipped to 152.5 million from 162.3 million.

Portfolio manager Stephen Lieber, co-chairman of the $700 million Evergreen Fund, says the strength of secondary issues doesn't surprise him.

"We're beginning to see some evidence of a shifting phase," Mr. Lieber says. Portfolio managers who were reluctant to commit their cash in the first quarter are now buying shares of specialty companies that have reported healthy earnings, he says.

"I think the evidence shows that in any period of declining corporate profits, there tends to be proportionately more interest in those companies that show rising earnings trends," he adds.

At the same time, more aggressive money managers who traditionally buy fast-growing, smaller companies "are rather quiet" after actively trading OTC stocks in the first quarter, Mr. Lieber says. He also suggests that in the near term the market in general will reflect the shifts in federal budget policy and interestrate expectations.

Strong earnings aren't confined to any particular OTC sector, according to Mr. Lieber, but range from some of the healthier regional banks to technology shares and even some speciality retailers. Among some shares favored by the portfolio manager are: international advertising agency Omnicom Group, which yesterday gained 1/2 to 25 3/8; specialty retailer Medicine Shoppes International, which yesterday dipped 1/4 to 21, and software designer and marketer Borland International, which advanced 3/8 to 16 7/8.

Peter Coolidge, senior OTC trader at Kidder Peabody, said he was impressed with the market's action yesterday. After the Nasdaq Composite Index drifted to a low of 440.58 at noon, it held firm and then recovered, Mr. Coolidge noted. He called this performance a sign that the rally may continue.

Mr. Coolidge pointed to two other signs that may bode well for the market: an increase in initial public offerings and recent announcements of mergers or proposed buy-outs, calling the latter "a phenomenon that's creeping back into the market."

Three of the day's top percentage gainers -- Bio-Medicus, Unilab and Silicon Valley Group -- were sparked by news of buy-outs or potential takeover threats.

Bio-Medicus surged 3 3/8 to 20 on 1.2 million shares after Medtronic, a Minneapolis medical-technology concern, unexpectedly offered Tuesday to buy the Eden Prairie, Minn., health-services company in a tax-free exchange of stock. Medtronic offered one share of its common stock for each 3.3 shares of Bio-Medicus common. Based on Medtronic's May 11 closing price of $71.375, the offer is valued at $21.74 a share. Medtronic currently holds about 5.5% of Bio-Medicus's outstanding shares.

Bio-Medicus said it will consider the offer at a regular board meeting today.

Separately, Bio-Medicus reported net income for the fourth quarter ended March 31 of 22 cents a share, up from five cents a year earlier.

Unilab rose 1 5/16 to 6 after MetPath Inc., a unit of Corning Inc., said it raised its stake in the Norcross, Ga., provider of laboratory testing services to 26.25% from about 20.9%. Metpath said it may consider seeking control of the company.

Silicon Valley Group gained 1 5/8 to 9 3/4 after Perkin-Elmer said late Tuesday that it sold a two-third interest in its semiconductor-equipment business to the San Jose, Calif., maker of electronic equipment. The two businesses will form a new company, SVG Lithography Systems Inc., involved in optical lithography technology used to produce semiconductors. International Business Machines is planning to invest in the new business's research and development; Sematech, a consortium of U.S. industries and the government, has entered into a joint development with the new company.

Positive earnings continued to attract investors to selective OTC shares. Applied Materials gained 2 1/4 to 34 3/4 after the Santa Clara, Calif., maker of water fabrication equipment reported late Tuesday that net income in the second quarter ended April 29 fell to 62 cents a share from 83 cents a year earlier, but was still higher than the average of analysts' estimates. Scitex rallied 1 1/8 to 33 1/4 after the Israeli maker of imaging and computer graphics reported late yesterday net income for the first quarter ended March 31 of 78 cents a share, up from 35 cents a year earlier.

Coda Energy traded as high as 2 5/8 before closing unchanged at 2 3/8. The Dallas oil and gas drilling company reported income from continuing operations for the third quarter ended March 31 tripled to three cents a share from year-earlier results.

Analysts' recommendations sparked interest in Duty Free International, up 3 1/4 at 29 1/4, and Optical Radiation, up 1 3/4 at 34 1/2, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Shearson Lehman Hutton retail analyst Stacy Ruchlamer repeated her "buy" opinion on Duty Free and raised her earnings fiscal 1991 first-quarter and full year earnings estimates for the Ridgefield, Conn., operator of duty-free retail stores, DJPIR said. Duty Free's chief financial officer tied the rise to the analyst's comments.

Similarly, Montgomery Securities analyst Leonard S. Yaffe emphasized his purchase opinion on Optical Radiation and raised his 1991 and 1992 earnings estimates for the Azusa, Calif., maker of optical equipment, according to DJPIR.

Homestyle Buffet gained 1 to 10 3/4 after a secondary offering of 1.45 million shares of the Oldsmar, Fla., restaurant concern was priced Tuesday at $9.50 a share.

Among the day's biggest percentage losers was Action Savings Bank, off 1 5/8 at 2 1/4. The Somers Point, N.J., thrift bank said Tuesday after the market closed that an ongoing examination by the Office of Thrift Supervision will result in as yet unspecified increases in its non-performing assets and allowances for loan losses.

Common and preferred shares of First Executive lost ground after the troubled thrift reported that it paid more than $1.33 billion in the first quarter to people surrendering insurance policies and annuities. First Executive common shares fell 1/4 to 2 5/8; the series E preferred shares dropped 1 3/8 to 5 3/8; series F preferred shares tumbled 2 1/8 to 9 3/8, and series G lost 5/8 to 4 3/4.

Concord Camera fell 1/4 to 3 3/8 after the Avenel, N.J., maker and distributor of cameras reported a loss of 24 cents a share for the third quarter ended March 31, compared with net income of 17 cents a share a year before. The company, which a week ago couldn't explain a jump in its stock to 3 7/8, attributed the quarterly loss to price competition, continuing retail weakness and the financial difficulties of one of its major customers, Ames Department Stores.
