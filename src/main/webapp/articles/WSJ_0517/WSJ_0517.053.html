Marketing & Media: Big Coke Promotion Jolted as Boy Finds Strange Fluid in Can --- `MagiCan' in Massachusetts Is Turned Over to Police; Firm Looks Into Problem

  • Author: Michael J. McCarthy
  • Date: 05/17/90

ATLANTA -- Coca-Cola Co.'s $100 million plan to surprise consumers this summer has turned up some nasty surprises for the company instead. In what Coke calls its biggest promotion ever, the soft-drink giant has begun distributing 750,000 "MagiCans" -- seemingly ordinary 12-ounce cans of Coca-Cola Classic that actually contain millions of dollars in cash and prizes.

When a buyer pops the top, a device rises through the opening in the can and displays a curled-up legal tender -- anywhere from $1 to $100 -- or a scroll of paper, redeemable for prizes. To give the cans the feel of the real product, Coke has partially filled them with water.

But last week, an 11-year-old boy in North Andover, Mass., opened a so-called MagiCan and drank a clear liquid that his mother described as "tasting and smelling like a cleaning solution." Noting that she knew nothing about the promotion, the mother, Robin Gendron, said she turned the can over to local police, asking them if they could determine what was in it.

Upon inspecting the can, the police found the prize-delivery mechanism and a $5 bill, and sent the liquid to a laboratory for analysis, said Mrs. Gendron. The police spokesman couldn't be reached.

What happened in Massachusetts, a Coca-Cola spokesman said yesterday, was "an isolated incident."

Concerned Coca-Cola marketing officials have huddled for the past two days to discuss how serious a problem they face. The cans are the heart of a $100 million summer promotion that features commercials with New Kids on the Block, a popular vocal group, and giveaway promotions at fountain outlets.

The Coca-Cola spokesman confirmed that there have been malfunctions in other cans, but said that there were "fewer than 10." He said the company is looking into the problem.

The early reading, according to one person familiar with the discussions, is that Coke believes the can problems are more of an isolated nuisance than a widespread nightmare.

Ever willing to jump on its main rival's mishaps, Pepsi turned up Tuesday on the doorstep of the Massachusetts youth. A local Pepsi sales manager, performing what the company described as a "good-will" visit, offered the boy a couple of cases of Pepsi, as well as several Pepsi hats and towels.

Mrs. Gendron said she has mixed feelings about the affair. Her son, she said, "was thrilled to have the $5, as any 11-year-old would be." She added, however, "Knowingly putting something in a can that says Coke -- but isn't -- doesn't make a lot of sense to me."
