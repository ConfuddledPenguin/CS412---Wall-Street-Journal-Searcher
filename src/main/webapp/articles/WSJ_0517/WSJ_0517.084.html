Technology: Tests May Fail to Detect AIDS Virus in Some Cases

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/17/90

Some individuals infected with the AIDS virus may not be detected by current testing procedures, according to a new study. Researchers at Emory University and the Centers for Disease Control, both in Atlanta, examined a group of 165 individuals at high risk of having the virus associated with acquired immune deficiency syndrome. While no one in the group had tested positive for AIDS using conventional examinations, a new test found that 30 individuals -- or 18% of the group -- were, in fact, infected.

The report, which appears in the current issue of the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, doesn't mean that current testing procedures are inadequate or should be replaced, says A. Ahmed-Ansari, a professor at Emory and one of the authors of the study. Rather, he says, the new test provides a "window for early detection {of the virus} in high-risk groups," such as drug abusers.

Current tests for AIDS involve examining serum samples for the presence of antibodies against the human immunodeficiency virus. The new test involves stimulating purified blood cells to induce the release of such antibodies.
