Bush, Congress Fear the Effect Of Deficit Cuts --- As Forecasts of Budget Gap Widen, Concern Grows About Jolt to Economy

  • Author: David Wessel and Michel McQueen
  • Date: 05/17/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration's budget-deficit projections have grown so large that concern exists in the White House and Congress that meeting deficit targets may do more harm than good to the economy. New administration forecasts show that to avoid across-the-board spending cuts this fall, the government may need to find as much as $64 billion in spending cuts and tax increases -- even if the added borrowing needed to clean up the thrift industry is excluded. If all the thrift costs are included, as the law currently requires, they would need as much as $114 billion.

At his first news conference since the start of budget talks with Congress, President Bush said that paring the deficit by more than 1% of the gross national product -- about $55 billion -- would "risk . . . slowing down the economy further."

Mr. Bush said that he won't give details of his position on budget issues until an agreement has been reached and that he was concerned about "frightening" the financial markets by exaggerating the extent of the budget problem.

The president dismissed calls by some Democrats that he give a televised address or otherwise lead a broad public discussion of the budget deficit, presumably to prepare the public for the possibility of tax increases or other measures.

Another alternative mentioned with increasing frequency on Capitol Hill would be to alter the targets under the Gramm-Rudman law to require less deficit reduction this year. Both administration and congressional negotiators already seem prepared to alter the law so they don't have to count the cost of the thrift bailout.

Cutting the deficit in one year by the amount that may be required to meet the Gramm-Rudman target might be neither politically possible nor economically wise, some congressmen say. "You may not be able to save that much without harming the economy in some way," House Budget Committee Chairman Leon Panetta (D., Calif.) warned.

Both White House Budget Director Richard Darman and Robert Reischauer, director of the Congressional Budget Office, are to discuss this issue with budget negotiators this afternoon.

A major economic rationale for trimming the federal deficit is to curb the government's appetite for debt, thus freeing up money for private investment and bringing down interest rates. This, in turn, would help keep the economy growing. But the newly expressed fear is that such a beneficial effect might be offset by a sharp cutback in government spending and an increase in taxes. The resulting reduction in overall demand could slow the economy down.

"I don't think anyone can say for sure where that line can be drawn," Mr. Reischauer said.

Some members of Congress say worrying about cutting too much from the deficit is ridiculous. They say the greater risk is that this year's deficit-reduction package, like those in the past, will be swollen with accounting gimmicks that have little economic impact.

In the past several years, Congress never agreed on more than $25 billion in real, lasting reductions of the deficit, according to the Congressional Budget Office.

At his news conference, Mr. Bush defended his reticence, saying he doesn't want to "go out there and do something that might inadvertently suggest crisis and frighten markets."

But Democrats continued to press him. "The American people must hear from the president about the length and breadth of this problem, and they must also see a proposal from him," said Senate Finance Committee Chairman Lloyd Bentsen (D., Texas).

Mr. Bush said that while he feels an obligation to be candid with lawmakers, he sees no need to involve the public in the discussions. Nor, he said, will he put his own proposals out first, as some members of Congress have suggested.

"I want to go out when we have a bipartisan answer to the problem, (and) say, `Here's what the problem is and here's what the answer (is).' And if I go out there today, we don't have a bipartisan answer, and that's the only way this problem's going to be solved," Mr. Bush said.

Mr. Darman now figures the deficit for the year beginning Oct. 1, without any policy changes, will total between $123 billion and $138 billion, congressional staffers said. Mr. Darman says thrift borrowing could add as much as $50 billion to that total. The range is slightly different than the one reported by lawmakers immediately after the first budget "summit" meeting Tuesday. Under the Gramm-Rudman law, the deficit for the year can't exceed $74 billion.
