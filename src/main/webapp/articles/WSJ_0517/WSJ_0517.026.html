Taiwan Dollar Stays Volatile After Losing Central-Bank Support

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/17/90

TAIPEI, Taiwan -- Trading in the New Taiwan dollar continued to be volatile Wednesday, one day after Taiwan's central bank withdrew support for the unit. Analysts said the currency is likely to remain shaky for several weeks. After a roller-coaster day of trading, the currency closed in Taipei at NT$27.35 to one U.S. dollar, near its intraday low but stronger than its close of NT$27.50 to the U.S. dollar on Tuesday, when the Taiwanese currency plunged approximately 4%, its sharpest fall since 1981.

As a drop in stock prices and expectations of slower economic growth increased pressure against the New Taiwan dollar, the central bank had spent about US$6 billion to support the currency this year. But in the final minutes of trading Tuesday, the central bank pulled its support. Some dealers said the bank joined speculators and started selling New Taiwan dollars.

Several dealers said there was no apparent intervention Wednesday, but one report said the central bank sold approximately US$40 million.

Several dealers said the central bank's main goal is to push out speculators because of concern they might shift their attention to the foreign-exchange market from the declining stock market. On Wednesday, share prices on the local stock exchange plunged 6% in response to the New Taiwan dollar's tumble.

Officials of the central bank have been unavailable for comment since Tuesday. On Sunday, the bank's governor, Samuel Shieh, denied rumors that the currency was about to be devalued.

The U.S. has long urged Taiwan to let market forces, rather than manipulation, rule foreign-exchange rates. In April, the U.S. Treasury gave Taiwan improved marks for allowing the rate to more closely follow the market. That is just what Taiwan's central bank did Tuesday.

Dealers said the New Taiwan dollar might rally a bit in the next few days but is likely to consolidate around a rate of NT$27 to the U.S. dollar for a few months while people holding the currency evaluate changes in the country's economy.
