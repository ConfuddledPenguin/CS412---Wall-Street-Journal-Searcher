Bush Push for Aid To Nicaragua Stirs Pork-Barrel Polka

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 05/17/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush stepped up pressure on Congress to expedite aid to Nicaragua, as House and Senate negotiators loaded the legislation with bids for new domestic appropriations. A revised fiscal 1990 spending bill, introduced by House Appropriations Committee Chairman Jamie Whitten, incorporates tens of millions of dollars in new initiatives that are sure to invite criticism from an already irritated White House.

The proposals run the gamut from at least $68 million to address flooding problems in the South to smaller but controversial provisions challenging the authority of Interior Secretary Manuel Lujan and earmarking defense funds for favored projects in the home states of influential members.

Mr. Whitten appears bent on reasserting the House's influence on what has grown into a bill of more than $3.3 billion in the Senate, but as lawmakers pile on, the Mississippi Democrat and his committee colleagues expose themselves to reproach.

In a private meeting Tuesday, the president personally asked the chairman to expedite the bill, and yesterday, the White House and GOP allies took a more public route, warning that the continued delays were endangering the new Chamorro government by withholding aid.

The bill promises $300 million in economic assistance for Nicaragua, as part of a larger foreign-aid package that includes $420 million for Panama. Mr. Bush reminded Congress that he initially asked for approval of both items last month. But the importance of the bill has made it an attractive vehicle for lawmakers and the president's own cabinet members, who have lobbied behind the scenes for supplemental funds to carry their departments through the fiscal year ending Sept. 30.

Mr. Bush accused appropriators of having tripled the cost of his initial request, but the largest single item of more than $1 billion is to cover what the Agriculture Department estimates is a shortfall in food-stamp funding. Other major items include $110 million for the current census and almost $196 million for child vaccine and preschool education programs -- much of which is backed by the administration. And separately, the White House has indulged in its own brand of pork-barrel politics to buy leverage for itself in the Senate.

Still more Pentagon cuts are expected to be needed to offset added costs, and the military accounts are the focus of some of the most intensive bargaining. The revised House bill rescinds $238 million for a Louisiana explosives plant and anticipates the closing of a second Mississippi ammunition facility. But the measure also inserts language sought by Mr. Whitten for a Pentagon-supported academic center in his home state and includes new provisions for defense subcommittee chairman, Rep. John Murtha (D., Pa.), to transfer military land and earmark at least $5 million in funds for research facilities in Pennsylvania.

The negotiations are to continue this morning. But even if an agreement is reached on the spending bill, the administration still needs legislative waivers and authorizations before the Central American funds can be distributed. The president sought to focus attention on Nicaragua's plight and the delayed appropriations process, but his greater political problem lies with the accompanying authorizing bill and amendments seeking to impose conditions on military aid to El Salvador.

Efforts to separate the two issues have so far failed, and as peace talks began this week on the Salvadoran war, Sen. Nancy Kassebaum (R., Kan.) has joined Democratic efforts to put pressure on the El Salvador government to negotiate. The first test will come in the House, and though Speaker Thomas Foley (D., Wash.) has signaled a willingness to move toward the administration, there is pressure to reach some resolution before the Nicaragua and Panama money is released.

Amid the extraordinary mix of accounts in the supplemental bill, one policy priority in the negotiations has been the popular Head Start program to assist poor, preschool-age children. The House-Senate conference tentatively agreed last night to add $165.7 million for this fiscal year, an 11% increase that would bring the program up to its fully authorized level of $1.55 billion.

The action came as the House separately approved a $2.4 billion Head Start authorization for the fiscal year beginning next Oct. 1, but few expect spending to reach that level, given the pressure of deficit-reduction negotiations.
