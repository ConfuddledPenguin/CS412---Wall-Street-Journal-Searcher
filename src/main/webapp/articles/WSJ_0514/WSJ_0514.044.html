Whittle Will Add TV To Its Services For Doctors' Offices --- One-Hour Videodisk Show To Be by January 1991 In 20,000 Waiting Rooms

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 05/14/90

Whittle Communications L.P., which pioneered magazines made especially for doctors' waiting rooms, will now put television there. Whittle, the Knoxville, Tenn., media company 50% owned by Time Warner Inc., said it will introduce Special Reports Television to about 20,000 family doctors' offices by January 1991. The new TV system will be coupled with Whittle's existing Special Reports magazines, which are currently found in about 19,000 medical reception areas.

While media companies are trying various new ways to deliver traditional media, Whittle's Special Reports TV is considered one of the most ambitious plans to target a particular audience -- in this case, mostly women -- outside of the home with television. Like TV networks, Whittle said it will use A.C. Nielsen & Co. to measure viewers of its new TV program.

Alan Greenberg, vice chairman of Whittle, said advertisers, for a varying range of prices, will be offered both time on the TV program and pages in Special Reports magazines. Whittle claims that doctors are converting to the new Special Reports TV at a rate of 80%.

Special Reports Television will include original programming, described by Whittle executives as "news and entertainment for the family." The one-hour program will be delivered to doctors' offices on videodisk and replaced every two weeks. The videodisks will be shown via a player held in a single oak cabinet that also holds display shelves for Special Reports magazines.

The new media system from Whittle comes after criticism from some advertisers and advertising agencies that Special Reports magazines, introduced two years ago, were overpriced and not read by as large an audience as Whittle originally promised. Whittle in the past has disputed that.

Mr. Greenberg said Whittle is guaranteeing the TV system will be in 20,000 offices and seen by 44 million people annually, of which two-thirds will be women with children. Should the TV system fall short of audience promises, Mr. Greenberg said, "we'll write checks, we'll give money back" to the advertisers.

Under the original Special Reports, an advertiser would have to commit to spending $2.6 million over a two-year period. Under the new TV program, advertisers could spend as little as $1 million during 12 month period, Mr. Greenberg said. The original Special Reports magazines had annual revenue of about $40 million; Special Reports Television, it's estimated, will generate revenue of $80 million a year.
