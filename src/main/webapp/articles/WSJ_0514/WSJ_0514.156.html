Foreign Exchange: Shaky Dollar May Be Hammered Down By More Heavy Sales, Many Traders Say

  • Author: Candace Cumberbatch Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/14/90

NEW YORK -- The already-shaky dollar may be hammered down by more heavy sales in the near term, many traders say. Because of the yen's sudden revitalization and renewed speculation that U.S. interest rates are headed lower, "Market sentiment has changed. The dollar should be the weakest" currency rather than one of the strongest, says Shigeru Tokunaga, foreign exchange manager at Fuji Bank Ltd., New York.

Late Friday here, the dollar was quoted at 152.65 yen, having dropped steeply from 156.70 at Thursday's close. Against the mark, however, the dollar was quoted at 1.6478 late Friday, up from 1.6285.

In trading in Tokyo Monday, the U.S. currency opened at 152.45 yen, down from Friday's Tokyo close of 154.15 yen.

The dollar's 2.6% plunge against the yen Friday -- one of the severest setbacks it has undergone in the past 16 months while strengthening more than 25% against the Japanese currency -- was a pivotal development for a number of reasons.

The drop marked the dollar's breakout from its recent trading range of 156 to 160 yen and has left the dollar dangerously close to an important psychological support at 150 yen, traders say.

Moreover, there is evidence that Japanese investors at life insurance companies, trust funds and other institutions are getting nervous about holding dollars.

While the dollar-yen is one of the most actively traded exchange rates in global dealings, powerful Japanese institutions sometimes have a big influence on the dollar's level. The dollar's arrival at a 39-month peak of 160.30 yen in early April was attributed largely to long-term position adjustments by institutional investors who hadn't anticipated such a strong rise this year.

Much of the dollar's losses against the yen were registered in Tokyo Friday, where there was a mad rush to dump the currency. Tokyo foreign exchange brokers reported a total spot transaction turnover of $21.58 billion, the highest ever seen in Japan.

In Tokyo, the dollar closed at 154.15 yen, the lowest finish since 153.65 on March 20.

Institutional investors were spotted selling the dollar aggressively after its support around 156 yen crumbled, apparently moving to limit losses on positions in the currency options market.

Some institutions previously had written options to sell dollars at levels of 157 yen and higher as part of a strategy that would have succeeded if the dollar had traded as anticipated in a range of 156 to 160 yen; they were caught off guard when the floor fell out from under the dollar Friday, market participants say.

If an institution had to buy dollars at, say, 154 yen to fulfill an option being exercised at 157 yen, it would incur losses of three yen less the premium gained from the option sale.

Options to sell dollars, called puts, gives the owner the right, but not the obligation, to sell the dollar at a specified level in a set time frame. If the dollar falls below the pinpointed price before the expiration, the put holder may reap profits from the exercise of the contract.

While it is hard to predict exactly what tactics Japanese institutions and other market participants will use in the near future, more dollar losses against the yen may be in store, traders say.

"We'll probably continue to see dollar selling on rallies," says Linda McLaughlin, a trader at Chemical Bank. She sees resistance at 154.50 yen as a roadblock that will probably make it hard for the dollar to rebound in the short run.

It is too soon to say that the yen has bottomed for sure after its lengthy slump, but the mood about it has clearly changed sharply. Only a few months ago the yen was caught in a downward spiral because of the hard times that Japan's stock and bond markets went through and Japan's relatively low interest rates.

And while the situation has turned against the dollar, the U.S. currency may show some buoyancy if the yen remains weak against the mark and the Swiss franc in cross-trading, traders say.

The yen underwent strong upward corrections against those Western European currencies Friday, but the gains may be short-lived.

Late Friday in New York, the mark was quoted at 92.639 yen and the Swiss franc traded at 109.075 yen, down sharply from 96.224 and 112.896, respectively, at Thursday's close.

Eventually, the dollar may fall swiftly against all major foreign currencies if the Federal Reserve loosens its monetary policy, traders say.

Speculation about Fed easing continues to grow because of more evidence of slower growth and lessening inflation.

In April, retail sales fell 0.6%, the sharpest decrease since October 1989, after falling 0.2% in March. The producer price index declined 0.3% last month after dropping 0.2% in March.

Those data follow news about last month's unexpectedly weak employment data.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York Friday, gold for current delivery settled at $368.80 an ounce, down $1.30. Estimated volume was a moderate 3.2 million ounces.

In Hong Kong Monday, gold was quoted at $367 an ounce.
