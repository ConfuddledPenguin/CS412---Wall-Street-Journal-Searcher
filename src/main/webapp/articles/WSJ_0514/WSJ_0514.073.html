Bankers See Real-Estate Credit Pinch, Say Session With Regulators Was Useful

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 05/14/90

HOT SPRINGS, Va. -- Leaders of some of the nation's largest banks said they see clear signs of a credit pinch in real estate and suggested that last week's meeting between bankers and the top three banking regulators could help prevent a broader pullback in lending. Joining top corporate executives from a variety of industries for a weekend conference in this mountain resort, bankers generally welcomed last Thursday's unusual private meeting.

"It will be useful," said John Reed, chairman of Citicorp. "To have these three gentlemen meeting with the bankers can only be good."

In the meeting with officials of the American Bankers Association, Federal Reserve Board Chairman Alan Greenspan, along with William Seidman, chairman of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp., and Robert Clarke, comptroller of the currency, tried to allay fears that new, tougher supervision by banking regulators would punish prudent lending. Though the regulators denied that the session was prompted by concern that a credit crunch threatened the economy, they urged banks not to overreact to regulators' increased scrutiny of lending practices.

Asked if the message was received by bankers, Lewis Preston, chairman of J.P. Morgan & Co., said: "I'm sure it would be."

In reaction to huge losses on real-estate loans, particularly in the Southwest, banking regulators have stepped up their policing of lending in recent years. But many bankers believe that regulators have overreacted, going so far, for instance, as to require some banks to increase reserves even for loans classified as performing.

While several banking leaders here said the stricter supervision was warranted, given the fiasco in their sister savings-and-loan industry, they expressed concern that regulators could have gone too far.

"I think they had to tighten down," said Mr. Reed of Citicorp. "On the other hand, there is no question that they have sent a ripple through the market that is causing people to withdraw. . . . There is clearly a significant withdrawal of capital from the real estate market."

"I don't know whether they're being overzealous or not, but one wants to be certain that you don't spook an industry," said Willard C. Butcher, chairman of Chase Manhattan Bank.

He added: "I think the concern by banking regulators is healthy, but you're always walking a narrow plank between being part of the solution and part of the problem."

White House Budget Director Richard Darman, interviewed yesterday on ABC-TV's "This Week With David Brinkley," also said that bankers should adopt a balanced approach. "There's anecdotal evidence that -- especially in New England -- there has been perhaps a little overcorrection, but I don't want to see it swing all the way back in the other direction," he said.

To date, the bankers said they saw no evidence of a pullback in lending beyond the troubled real-estate industry. But, Mr. Preston of J.P. Morgan said, "I think this is an issue in terms of attitudes."

Many of the business leaders attending the weekend meeting of the Business Council, an association of chief executives from the country's largest corporations, also heralded President Bush's indication that he will discuss taxes at the budget negotiations set to begin tomorrow.

While many conservatives are upset at what they see as a retreat by Mr. Bush from his "read-my-lips" campaign pledge of no new taxes, several corporate executives here indicated that any serious deficit-reduction package would have to include some new taxes.

"It is unrealistic to believe you can get a substantial {deficit reduction} without some new taxes," said Chase Manhattan's Mr. Butcher.

Nevertheless, most echoed the attitude of Edmund Pratt Jr., chairman of Pfizer Inc., the pharmaceutical and health-care company, who declared: "If there are new taxes, they ought to be used only to reduce the budget deficit" and not spent on new programs.

The executives also said government officials should stay away from excise taxes that can harm the competitiveness of American industries.

"The only kind of tax we should raise is the personal or corporate income tax," said Andrew Sigler, chairman of Champion International Corp., a paper and wood-products company. "I think I'd prefer to see it on the personal side."
