REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): ABC's Documentary

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/14/90

ABC's Cambodia documentary, "Peter Jennings Reporting: From the Killing Fields" deserves some comment of its own, insofar as it is the centerpiece of this controversy. It's our experience that Mr. Jennings's daily instinct is to play the news straight. But TV journalism -- with its mix of images, sound and facts -- is an unwieldy instrument, and too often, large efforts, such as this documentary, go awry.

Why, we wonder (not for the first time) is there so little room for sublety in TV news? Why is it necessary for TV journalism to dwell forever in a world of melodramas inhabited only by the innocent and those who scheme against the innocent?

The ABC documentary displayed many of the techniques TV uses repeatedly to make sure viewers Get The Message.

The most familiar is focusing in tight close-up on the face of one's opponent during an interrogation, as Mr. Jennings did repeatedly with a State Department official. From this Joe Isuzu interview, ABC's cameras cut to pictures of desperately ill Cambodian children (with an obligatory close-up of a fly on a little limb) who presumably would live happily ever after except for U.S. intransigence.

We get old footage of the Nixon administration's Cambodian incursion, bombs cascading down, set against footage of Kent State going up in smoke, without the least awareness that the incursion was intended to keep the Khmer Rouge from prevailing. Throughout, there is withering skepticism about U.S. explanations and motives set against bland acceptance of false Vietnamese claims to have withdrawn from Cambodia.

In his letter to us defending the program, Mr. Jennings asks why (except presumably to give them arms) is a U.S. Special Forces colonel in Khmer Rouge refugee camps? Simply, common sense will tell you, to find out what they're up to.

This tendentious character in what purports to be objective reporting invites suspicion of political motives. The political stance of the program's producer is surely relevant, and Mr. Jennings and columnist Anthony Lewis smear Stephen Morris with their charge of McCarthyism. Yet in the end, we think that politics is less of an explanation than the nature of television, which to tell the news needs black hats and white hats.
