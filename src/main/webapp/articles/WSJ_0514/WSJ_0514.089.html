Candy Maker Loses Bid for Exemption From Sugar Quotas

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 05/14/90

CHICAGO -- Brach's Candy Co. said the Commerce Department rejected its request to have the company's Chicago plant designated a Foreign Trading Zone. The refusal by the department's Foreign Trade Zones Board means that the candy manufacturer -- owned by Switzerland's Jacobs Suchard AG -- won't be allowed to import sugar at world prices, but must instead continue to buy the essential ingredient under import quotas that boost its annual costs by about $20 million.

The petition had become a rallying point for critics of the import quotas, which keep domestic sugar prices high by restricting foreign imports. Such quotas, they contend, represent a subsidy for U.S. growers at the expense of U.S. consumers.

A successful bid by the candy company for an exemption might have sparked similar requests for relief by other companies.

While it eliminated the last possibility of success for the Brach's request, the turndown didn't come as a surprise; late last month, a Commerce Department examiner recommended that the petition be denied.

Brach's had threatened that if it didn't get the relief it sought it might close the aging Chicago plant -- an island of high-paying, unionized production jobs in the midst of the city's blighted West Side -- and shift the about 3,400 jobs to either Mexico or Canada.

Late Friday, the company said in a release that it is "disappointed" with the ruling. "At this time, we haven't determined what our next steps will be regarding the plant," Brach's said.

Had its trade-zone request been approved, Brach's said, the company would have been allowed to purchase 100,000 tons of sugar at world prices, currently 10 cents a pound cheaper than the price in the U.S.

Brach's has spent heavily on modernization at its 66-year-old Chicago plant, and needs to spend much more. The company has claimed that the only way to justify the further investment required to bring the plant up to standard is through access to the cheaper sugar.

Suchard paid $730 million in 1987 to acquire Brach's from American Home Products Corp., but the acquisition has proven a major disappointment: Brach's, which had pre-tax 1986 income of $75 million, recorded an operating loss of $50 million in 1989.

The Swiss concern replaced Brach's former management, but has since repeatedly reshuffled its own Brach's management team. Analysts say a rapid post-acquisition restructuring, including changes in its sales operation and what some industry experts think was an ill-considered streamlining of Brach's product line, helped giant competitors Mars Inc. and Hershey Foods Co. take market share away from Brach's.
