Economy: Auto Makers To Lift Output In Third Period --- Car Production to Rise 11%; Stronger Buyer Demand, Fears of Strike Are Cited

  • Author: Gregory A. Patterson
  • Date: 05/14/90

DETROIT -- U.S. auto makers are planning sizable production increases for the third quarter, reflecting forecasts of stronger buyer demand and fears of a labor strike in September. Individuals in the industry say U.S. auto makers are planning to build more than 1.5 million cars in the third quarter, up 11% from last year. The individuals say the auto makers also intend to increase truck production by 6%, to 887,009 vehicles, during that period.

But the 10 companies that build cars in the U.S. aren't throwing caution to the wind. On Friday, Ford Motor Co. said it will close its Chicago auto-assembly plant for one week beginning today because of a shortage of dealer orders.

Although inventories of the Ford Taurus and Mercury Sable models built there are slightly below normal, Ford said it intends to be cautious regarding vehicle inventories in light of sluggish spring sales. In all, the company has cut second-quarter car production by 25,000 vehicles during the past month.

Overall, auto makers are planning to build nearly 1.8 million cars in the second quarter, down 9% from a year ago. Production of trucks -- including vans -- is running about even with last year.

Although second-quarter production trails that of last year, it is far better than the first quarter, when auto makers built 21% fewer cars and trucks than in 1989. Analysts say the more ambitious production plans for the second and third quarters, combined with lower consumer incentives, will help the Big Three auto makers record profits better than in their dismal first quarter.

"Despite a horrible first quarter of earnings, the outlook of the Big Three is fairly positive for the rest of the year," says John Casesa, an analyst with Wertheim Schroder & Co.

Lower inventories have helped auto makers reduce the cash back and financing incentives they offer to buyers. General Motors Corp. and Ford have reduced their incentives by an average of about $200 a vehicle during the past month, according to Scott Merlis, an analyst with Morgan Stanley & Co. Mr. Merlis says the auto makers can afford to reduce incentives because inventory levels are modest. Ford, for example, has a 61-day supply of cars and a 63-day supply of trucks, both of which are normal.

Some car dealers say production cuts might have gone too far. Steven Fish, general manager of Freeway Dodge in Bloomington, Minn., said he has a pretty good stock of trucks and vans but is low on Dodge Daytonas, Shadows, and Spirits. "I'm supposed to have 20 or 30 of each but I only have two or three of each," he said, noting that Chrysler Corp. has been heavily advertising these models. "I could have sold more if I had more."

While this accounts for part of the increase in third-quarter production plans, there is another reason as well. Auto makers may be stepping up production to hedge against a possible strike in September, when their labor contracts with the United Auto Workers union expires.

"You have to take into consideration dealing with the UAW this fall," says Edward Sullivan, an analyst with WEFA Group, in Bala Cynwyd, Pa. The big union represents some 450,000 workers, who could cripple production by any of the companies they would choose to strike.

Currently, seven of the nine U.S. auto makers who were building cars a year ago are planning production boosts in the third quarter. Chrysler's production will be lower, largely because it stopped building its Dodge Omni and Plymouth Horizon subcompact cars last fall.

Nissan Motor Corp. will build fewer cars, but is delegating more of its capacity to trucks in the third quarter. Subaru-Isuzu Automotive Inc. wasn't building vehicles a year ago.
