Economy -- Tracking the Economy: April Consumer Prices Expected to Slow But Not Enough for 1st-Quarter Damage

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 05/14/90

NEW YORK -- Consumer prices for April -- the next test of inflation's force -- are expected to slow, but not as much as the recent break in wholesale prices and not enough to make up for first-quarter damage. Besides the consumer price index, due out Wednesday, the week includes important readings on April industrial output and capacity utilization; housing starts and permits, plus the March trade deficit.

The April CPI is expected to rise about 0.2%, following two back-to-back months of 0.5% increases, according to MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc. Last Friday, the producer price index logged a decline of 0.3%, its third improved reading in a row.

But some economists cautioned against celebrating too soon. "Some of the outrages of the first quarter have come back to earth. But in an arithmetical sense, it's hard to get numbers below 5% for the year," said Neal Soss, chief economist at First Boston Corp. Among the first-quarter "outrages" -- when the annualized CPI rate jumped to 8.5% -- he cited the leap in apparel prices and other special seasonal factors.

Furthermore, in recent months wholesale prices have been better-behaved than the CPI, which is heavily weighted in the wage-driven services sector. Economists say this "structural" inflation -- such as escalating costs linked to wage or healthcare commitments -- is tougher to tame than inflation tied to volatile commodities, such as food and oil.

Nonetheless, Mr. Soss expects to see "slow, grudging progress" on inflation. Employers, he added, will be "on the front line, trying to control these health benefit costs that first show up in inflation, then as profitability pressures."

Elliot Platt, economist at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette, has been forecasting a long-term annual inflation rate of 4.5% since 1988. He still thinks the number is doable over the next few years. But Mr. Platt, too, noted "the terrible inflation news we had in the first quarter put us above that" for now. Besides high apparel prices, he cited a big jump in the CPI's housing component, which he figures was caused by technical factors that should begin to work themselves out.

Edward Campbell, economist with Brown Brothers Harriman & Co., agrees. In fact, he sees a "high possibility" the April CPI will be unchanged or even lower. "April is the time we get more news and less noise" from statistical quirks, he said. Among the "special circumstances" he thinks will abate are the big technical jump in March housing costs; increases in public transportation fares put in place by many cities early this year, and the recent increase in apparel prices.

Reports on April industrial output and capacity utilization, due out tomorrow are expected to bear out the current belief that the economy is sluggish. Output is expected to be unchanged or slightly lower following March's 0.7% increase. The operating rate is expected to drop a bit to 83.1% from March's 83.3%, according to MMS.

The March trade deficit, due out Thursday, is expected to widen to around $8 billion following February's surprising shrinkage to $6.5 billion. But First Boston's Mr. Soss noted that while "$8 billion is higher, it wasn't that long ago we were dreaming of getting this low a number."

The broader deficit is due to a jump in imports as evidenced by a jump in custom's duties receipts for March, economists said. Gary Ciminero, chief economist at Fleet/Norstar Financial Group, Providence, R.I., noted that the customs report found that incoming shipments of Japanese cars rose more than 10% in March and that petroleum imports rebounded after a decline in February due to mild weather.

Housing starts, due out Wednesday, are expected to drop a bit from March to an annual rate of 1.3 million units, according to MMS. Economists said housing continues to feel the impact of higher interest rates, cooler home prices and tighter lending policies.
