Some Find This a Bit Spaced-Out, And It Doesn't Rhyme With June

  • Author: Carrie Dolan
  • Date: 05/14/90

PALO ALTO, Calif. -- In a bid for immortality, a California company is shooting for the moon. Avcom Systems Inc., a closely held maker of geographical-information systems, is lobbying with the International Astronomical Union to have the Earth's moon named Avcom.

"It seems to fit," says the company's chairman, Brad Bishop. "Avcom doesn't mean anything." He got the idea after his children asked him the moon's name. "I thought it must have some Greek name or something. But when I found out it didn't, I thought, why not name it after our company?"

Astronomers, however, think Mr. Bishop is a bit starry-eyed. "I don't think there's any chance," says Ray Batson, chief of the planetary-cartography section for the U.S. Geological Survey. Commercial, religious and political names are typically shunned to avoid controversy. Anyway, "most of us feel `the Moon' is a perfectly acceptable name," he says.

The satellites of other planets have names, but often with a unifying theme. Uranus's moons, for example, are named for characters from Shakespeare and Alexander Pope's "Rape of the Lock." Features on Mercury are named for artists and musicians, while Venus's are named for goddesses and female explorers.

The public can submit names, which are chosen by a board of international astronomers. An English girl, for instance, submitted the name for Pluto. After the Challenger disaster, many Americans lobbied for newly discovered moons of Uranus to be named after the shuttle crew. (Instead, craters on the moon were named after the victims.)

It's considered poor form for astronomers to name discoveries after themselves, as etiquette requires them to wait for a colleague to honor them. And some suggestions are simply inappropriate, says Brian Marsden, of the Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory. A one-time head of the Soviet secret police was denied a place in the heavens because "he did in a lot of people."

It may be easier with asteroids, which are being discovered faster than they're being named (4,461 have been identified, though 1,000 remain nameless). Brahms, Beethoven and Bach are all asteroids, as is Karl Marx. Last month, four new asteroids were named after the Beatles, and another was christened Eric Clapton.
