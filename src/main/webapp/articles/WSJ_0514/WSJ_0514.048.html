International: Big Shake-Up By N.V. Philips Is Likely Today --- President Expected to Quit In Latest Drive to Turn Electronics Firm Around

  • Author: Mark M. Nelson
  • Date: 05/14/90

BRUSSELS -- Struggling to restore confidence in its sagging stock price, N.V. Philips is expected to announce a major management shake-up today -- one that is likely to include the resignation of its president, Cornelis van der Klugt. Spokesmen for the Dutch electronics giant said only that Philips would make "a major announcement" this morning. But people close to the company said they expected Mr. van der Klugt to be replaced by Jan Timmer, a 57-year-old Philips veteran. Mr. Timmer already had been designated to succeed Mr. van der Klugt upon the 65-year-old president's scheduled retirement in July 1991.

The Philips announcement, which comes in the wake of an unexpected plunge in first-quarter profit, hopes to mute stinging criticism of the company in recent days from analysts and investors.

Members of the Philips supervisory board met over the weekend at the company's headquarters in Eindhoven, the Netherlands, to devise a plan aimed at trying yet again to convince the world's stock markets and the company's own employees that Philips will finally come to grips with the problems that have been plaguing it for most of the past decade.

The job of reviving Philips will now apparently be left to Mr. Timmer, a respected and even feared manager who first impressed the company's management as head of PolyGram, Philips's music-recording division. He later took the helm of the consumer electronics division, helping to revive that business's fortunes.

Last year, the consumer-electronics division was among the best performers in the company in terms of its operating income, which rose 34% to 1.1 billion guilders ($598 million). But managing the sprawling whole of Philips -- which had sales of 57 billion guilders last year under such brand names as Magnavox and Philco -- will be a Herculean challenge, especially at a time when Japanese competitors are strengthening their grip on ever-larger parts of Philips's key markets.

The drama engulfing Philips began on May 3, when the company reported nearly disastrous first-quarter results that took the markets -- and, indeed, the company itself -- by surprise. Hit by a slump in its computer business, first-quarter profit from normal business operations plummeted to six million guilders, from 223 million a year earlier.

Analysts said the results, which came just weeks after Mr. van der Klugt had made an optimistic forecast for 1990, showed that the company had little control over its sprawling businesses or its financial management.

Company officials said they were unable to predict the sudden downturn because of the difficulty of getting quick and accurate information from all of the company's divisions, which are spread throughout more than 70 countries. The company was hurt, they added, not only by a world-wide slump in the computer business but also by unpredictable shifts in foreign-exchange and interest rates. The first-quarter results sent the company's stock on a downward spiral.

Speculation late last week that the hard-nosed Mr. Timmer would be taking control of the company has already helped to stem the price plunge. In trading Friday, the company's stock gained 40 Dutch cents, ending at 31.40 guilders ($17.07), after sinking to as low as 30.40 guilders during the day. Earlier this month, before the first-quarter results were announced, the shares were trading near 38 guilders.
