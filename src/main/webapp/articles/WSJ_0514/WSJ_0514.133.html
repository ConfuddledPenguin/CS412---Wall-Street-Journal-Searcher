Heard on the Street: Progressive Corp., an Insurer, Stands to Profit From Other Firms' Retreat From Car Coverage

  • Author: Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 05/14/90

Many insurers are beating a hasty retreat from the auto insurance market. But their departure could leave the market wide open for Progressive Corp. Based in Cleveland, this property/casualty insurer specializes in providing what is delicately known as "nonstandard" auto insurance. This isn't the coverage for the people who drive once a week, to church on Sunday. This is for motorists most insurers avoid -- the ones with traffic violations, accidents, even drunk-driving convictions on their records. It's a group that includes young drivers, especially men in their 20s, and motorists who drive expensive sports cars.

Major insurers such as Aetna Life & Casualty, Travelers, Cigna, Xerox's Crum & Forster unit, and some regional companies, are cutting back on the amount of auto insurance they write or pulling out of the market altogether. With their retreat, many drivers are left hunting for new insurance carriers and so Progressive could end up taking on a far better class of drivers, analysts say.

Investors haven't fully appreciated "the change in the nonstandard market. When that happens, this company can grow like a weed," says Michael Takata at UBS Securities. Progressive certainly thrived in the mid-1980s when auto insurers racked up losses and market capacity shrank, he recalls; the company's premiums, per-share earnings, book value and stock prices quadrupled.

Mr. Takata, who reiterated his buy recommendation on Progressive recently, doesn't see the same degree of upside potential this time. But "we could see a doubling of (Progressive's) premiums and earnings," he says.

The stock, which closed Friday at 38 1/8, currently sells for just over nine times Mr. Takata's 1990 earnings estimate of $4 a share. With annual earnings potential of $6 to $8 a share and an uptick in the price/earnings multiple to 10 over the next two years, he says, Progressive "would be a $60-to-$80 stock. This is one of the few great growth stocks in the insurance industry."

Gerald Lewinsohn at Merrill Lynch says: "Anytime you can buy a stock with (Progressive's earnings) record and rate of return, and selling for a low P/E, it's a bargain."

Insurers generally have lost money writing auto insurance in the past decade, even though they tend to insure only what they consider the safest drivers. At Progressive, though, net income has grown at nearly a 15% average rate as return on equity averaged 25.3% annually in the past 10 years -- nearly 12 points above the return of the Standard & Poor's 500-stock index.

Progressive's first-quarter earnings report, released last month, turned heads on Wall Street. Per-share earnings of $1.01 far exceeded analysts' expectations of 75 cents to 80 cents. (Many firms stopped following the stock closely when the company discontinued analyst meetings in late 1988.)

Tom Sargent at Conning & Co. has been telling clients "it makes sense to be buying the stock now." Although the stock has appreciated some 48% in the past 15 months, the departure of some insurers from the market presents tremendous potential for the company, the stock's admirers say. William Bitterli at Northington Partners believes the shares could sell at 50 by year end.

Since the late 1950s Progressive has collected a huge amount of data on high-risk drivers. It "cherry-picks among the bad risks," one analyst says. For example, when Progressive learned that older, married motorcyclists are better risks than most riders of big motorcycles, it benefited by charging them less than rivals did.

In California, where Progressive had more than 20% of its business, the company has adopted an extremely conservative strategy. It set aside $34 million last year and $13.9 million in the first quarter as reserves for possible premium refunds that might be ordered under California's auto insurance initiative, Proposition 103. The company has also been reducing its business in the state as much as the current insurance laws allow. Northington's Mr. Bitterli believes these reserves aren't likely to be needed and could be released into future earnings.

Progessive's long-haul trucking insurance unit, which had a loss in 1988 after a quick start two years earlier, is thought by analysts to have broken even in this year's first quarter after a restructuring last year. Progressive, which took a big hit in the 1987 market crash, today has a $2 billion portfolio that is invested largely in short-term, high-grade securities including some adjustable-rate bonds. Only about $240 million is in the stock market.
