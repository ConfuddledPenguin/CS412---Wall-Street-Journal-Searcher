Worthington to Report 4th-Quarter Net Slipped, but Period to Be Best for Year

  • Author: Ralph E. Winter
  • Date: 05/14/90

COLUMBUS, Ohio -- Worthington Industries Inc. has experienced a marked upturn in steel processing, its largest business, which will make the fourth quarter by far the strongest profit period for fiscal 1990, John H. McConnell, chairman and chief executive officer said. Profit for the quarter, ending May 31, is likely to be only a little lower than the 49 cents a share from continuing operations in the strong year-ago period, he said in an interview. For the first nine months, profit from continuing operations declined 16% to 90 cents a share from $1.06 a share in the comparable 1989 period.

Sales for the fourth quarter probably will be slightly higher than the $265.5 million from continuing operations in the year-ago period, Mr. McConnell said. Higher sales of processed steel will more than offset lower sales of plastic products, mainly auto parts.

For fiscal 1991, unless the overall economy takes a dive, Worthington will have record profit for the year, Mr. McConnell said. A major factor in the brighter 1991 outlook, he said, is an increase in the number of car models that will use Worthington's instrument panels and other plastic parts. The company also will be aided by the stronger steel market, major new facilities and higher demand for railroad castings, he said.

For the fiscal 1989 fourth quarter, Worthington had net income of $20.8 million, or 51 cents a share, including two cents a share from a pipe fittings business sold last January.

Mr. McConnell didn't make a specific earnings estimate for the fiscal year, but he indicated that profit from continuing operations will exceed $1.30 a share. The discontinued pipe fittings operations will cost the company five cents a share for fiscal 1990, all in the first nine months.

For fiscal 1989, Worthington earned $1.55 a share from continuing operations. Net income was $64.2 million, or $1.58 a share, a record high.

Sales for fiscal 1990 will be down slightly from fiscal 1989 sales from continuing operations of $939.3 million, Mr. McConnell said.

While it is too early to make a precise forecast of 1991 results, Mr. McConnell said analyst estimates ranging from $1.60 a share to $1.75 a share "appear reasonable." Sales will exceed $1 billion, he said.

Directors are likely to consider a dividend increase at a meeting later this month, he said. The board last August raised the quarterly rate to 14 cents, from 12 cents.

Worthington Industries Inc.'s steel processing business "is doing real well" Mr. McConnell said. Demand began picking up early in the year, and orders for both March and April were higher than a year earlier, he said. Sales have improved at all 10 locations where Worthington processes and distributes steel, he said, indicating higher production at a wide range of industries from automobiles to lawn and garden equipment.

"Pricing is coming back" after a period of widespread steel price discounting, he added. "Our margins are much better than they were in the third quarter," which ended Feb. 28, he said. The low point for steel processing margins came in November, he said, but margins remained under pressure for another quarter.

As a result, operating profits for steel processing in the current quarter will exceed year-ago levels, he said.

Startup costs at a new processing plant in Porter, Ind., which is four times the size of the Chicago plant it is replacing, are cutting into steel processing profits in fiscal 1990, he said. However, the plant, which will cost about $40 million when completed in calendar 1991, will contribute to profits next fiscal year, he said.

Worthington also is expanding facilities that stamp out rough shapes for customers, ranging from auto parts to garden shovels, Mr. McConnell said. That business "is growing by leaps and bounds," as Worthington buys more presses and signs up new customers, he said.

Lower profits from plastic products are a major factor in Worthington's expected fiscal 1990 earnings drop, Mr. McConnell said. Changes in car models and relocation of some auto production reduced sales, he said, and industry output dropped in the calendar 1990 first quarter. In addition, a new joint venture with a Japanese concern to make parts for Japanese-owned auto plants will have a loss in fiscal 1990 because of startup costs, but it has gone into the black in the last few months.

However, "we have business booked for 1991 models that assure us of a tremendous year at plastics without any big increase in car sales," Mr. McConnell said. Production of instrument panels and other parts for 1991 models will begin in June, he said.

Sales of steel castings are increasing, mainly because of higher railroad car business, he said. For fiscal 1990, both sales and operating profits will be slightly lower, he said, "but they're ending the year pretty strong." The company's foundry makes undercarriages, couplers and other parts for freight cars and rapid transit cars.

However, sales of steel cylinders for compressed gases are lower this year, he said, probably because of a new federal tax on refrigerant gases, he said. Profit margins are holding up well, however, because of new production methods that reduced costs, he said. While refrigerants account for less than half the company's cylinder sales, sales to the technicians who service air conditioning equipment still are a major market. Sales for gas barbecue grills, camping equipment and other applications are strong, he said.

Worthington Industries Inc. will invest about $60 million in new facilities during fiscal 1990, one-third more than the $45.1 million of 1989, said Joseph H. Stegmayer, vice president and chief financial officer. That figure excludes investment in the plastics joint venture, he said. The recently completed joint venture plant, 60% owned by Worthington, cost $12 million to $15 million, but it is being financed separately, he said.

The company has bought back two million of its shares over the last two years, and has board authorization to buy back another one million shares, he said. At year end, the company will have about 39.5 million shares outstanding.

Facilities spending and stock buy-backs in fiscal 1990 will be financed from cash flow and the proceeds of about $25 million from sale of the pipe fittings unit, Mr. Stegmayer said. Worthington will have $25 million or more of cash and short-term investments at year end, compared with $43.1 million a year earlier.

Capital spending in fiscal 1991 is likely to be lower, he said, but the company hasn't completed a review of all spending requests from the divisions. Internally generated cash will handle the 1991 spending, he said.
