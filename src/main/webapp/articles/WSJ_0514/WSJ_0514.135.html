What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/14/90

STOCKS AND BONDS SURGED on Friday as reports of moderate inflation and a slowing economy damped speculation that the Fed would soon boost interest rates. Producer prices fell 0.3% in April, the second decline in two months, while retail sales slid 0.6%, mainly due to a continued slump in auto sales. Both declines surprised the markets.

The Dow Jones industrials closed up 63.07, at 2801.58, near its record, in the heaviest Big Board trading this year. Long-term Treasury bonds jumped over two points, the biggest rally of the year. The dollar was mixed but slid 2.6% against the yen.

---

Bush and top aides tried to reassure Democrats that the president is sincere about negotiating a deficit-cutting accord. The administration, which sent mixed signals last week about possibile tax increases, reiterated that budget talks should begin tomorrow with "no preconditions."

---

Some top bankers said they see clear signs of a credit pinch in real estate, mainly due to tougher regulatory supervision. But the bankers suggested that last week's meeting with federal regulators could help prevent a broader pullback in lending.

---

Kidder Peabody may dismiss up to 10% of its staff as part of a new costcutting drive, sources said. One target of the layoffs is likely to be the investment banking division, which the GE unit recently beefed up.

---

N.V. Philips is expected to announce a big management shake-up today that is likely to include the resignation of president Cornelis van der Klugt. The changes are aimed at restoring confidence in the Dutch electronics giant's sagging stock.

---

U.S. auto makers plan sizable production increases for the third quarter, reflecting forecasts of stronger demand and fears of a possible labor strike in September. Car output is expected to jump 11%, while truck production is forecast to rise 6%.

GM's plan to nearly double the pensions of departing chairman Roger Smith and other top executives is drawing fire from some big holders. At least one is vowing to vote no.

---

Amax isn't expected to launch a bidding war for Peabody Holding, observers said. Amax offered $1.25 billion for the coal company Friday but the bid was quickly topped by Hanson, which owns 45% of Peabody. Amax's move spurred speculation of its own vulnerability to a takeover.

---

Prime Motor Inns agreed to sell a 65% stake in two franchise systems, Howard Johnson and Ramada, to an investment partnership for $200 million in cash and notes. Prime is under pressure due to a soft hotel and real estate market and a hefty debt load.

---

Laventhol & Horwath is facing new setbacks in the wake of litigation and earnings woes. The No. 7 accounting firm has lost a big Canadian affiliate, is seeing an exodus of key personnel, and is under pressure from its main lender to sell assets, sources said.

---

Drexel's commodities trading unit disclosed details of its debt in a court filing related to the parent company's Chapter 11 filing in February.
