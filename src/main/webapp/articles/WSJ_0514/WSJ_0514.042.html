Technology: IBM Is Expected To Unveil Wider Uses for CallPath

  • Author: Laurence Hooper
  • Date: 05/14/90

NEW YORK -- International Business Machines Corp. is expected to unveil tomorrow a major expansion in the capabilities of CallPath, a software product that links telephone switches to computers, industry executives said. IBM will announce CallPath support for telephone switches other than those made by Rolm, the telecommunications concern that it owns in partnership with Siemens AG, the executives said. It will also announce several new CallPath features, and will outline a set of protocols for companies that write software that works with CallPath, they added.

IBM declined to comment, but has scheduled a "major telecommunications announcement" for tomorrow.

CallPath identifies callers to a telephone switch known as a PBX, for private branch exchange, and automatically pulls information about them from an IBM minicomputer or mainframe. Telephone operators -- customer service representatives or brokers, for example -- can see callers' files displayed on a screen as soon as they pick up the phone, which saves time and opens up new marketing opportunities.

Currently, CallPath works only with Rolm PBXs, and only with incoming calls. The executives said IBM will announce CallPath support for PBXs made by Northern Telecom Inc. and American Telephone & Telegraph Co., among others. They also said CallPath will work with outgoing calls -- a big plus for telemarketing companies and others that depend on calling their customers.

AT&T, Northern Telecom and Rolm together dominate the PBX market. Since many companies keep their customer databases on IBM computers, IBM's announcement opens up CallPath services to a huge audience.

In addition, the industry executives said IBM will announce a set of standards and protocols that will enable software writers to develop CallPath applications that work in a large variety of environments.

"This is going to be great news for all the different vendors playing in this marketplace," said Dixon Doll, chairman of DMW Group, a consulting firm in Ann Arbor, Mich. "A very major vendor is putting a very big stake in the ground, and everyone can take their direction from it. Customers can now be sure that standards are going to emerge in this market."
