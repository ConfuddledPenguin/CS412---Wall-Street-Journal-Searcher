FOREIGN OWNERS OF U.S. LAND

Foreigners own just under 1% of U.S. agricultural land, a level that has varied little over the years, the Agriculture Department said.

The department made its 1989 year-end review under a 1978 law requiring foreigners to register U.S. agricultural land holdings. As of Dec. 31, 1989, about 12.9 million acres of agricultural land were owned by foreigners, an increase of 263,273 acres from a year earlier.

"Foreign ownership of U.S. agricultural land remained relatively steady from 1981 through 1989, slightly above or below 1% of the privately owned agricultural land in the U.S.," said John Lee, administrator of the agency. Corporations owned 81% of the foreign-held acreage; partnerships, 10%; and individuals, 7%. The remainder is held by estates, trusts, associations, institutions and others.

The state of Maine has the heaviest concentration of foreign ownership, with holdings of almost 2.1 million acres.

TOKYO'S RICE POLICY

Japanese Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu said his nation would maintain a ban on rice imports to protect domestic growers despite U.S. demands for improved market access. "Rice and rice cultivation have a special significance for our country and in light of this we will continue to adhere to the basic policy of self-sufficiency," Mr. Kaifu told legislators. Japanese consumers pay up to six times world prices for domestic rice as the government props up inefficient rice producers with substantial subsidies.

SWEDISH-HUNGARIAN DEAL

Sweden's largest newspaper, Dagens Nyheter, said it intends to buy a 45% stake in Magyar Nemzet, Hungary's second-largest newspaper. Dagens Nyheter said its negotiations are with the Hungarian state-owned publisher Pallas, which owns Magyar Nemzet. According to the deal, Dagens Nyheter and Pallas will own 45% each of the stock of the newly formed newspaper corporation. The remaining 10% will be controlled by a journalist organization. Dagens Nyheter wouldn't reveal the price of the deal.

CHINESE AID FOR LIBYA

China will send 350 technical advisers to Libya's Misurata Iron & Steel Complex, the official China Daily said. The technicians will work in hot and cold rolling mills and a steel-smelting shop, the paper said. They will join 430 Chinese workers and engineers already working in other parts of the Libyan complex. The Chinese workers come from the Wuhan Iron & Steel Complex in central China.

DENUNCIATION IN SWITZERLAND

A prominent Swiss animal-rights campaigner denounced government plans to allow sales of dog and cat meat as "monstrous and obscene." Franz Weber, writing in Lausanne's Le Matin newspaper, charged the proposed law endorsed "a kind of cannibalism" and would "lower us to the level of the disparaged monsters of the Philippines and Korea." Swiss authorities have proposed a revision of 1954 food regulations that would drop the ban on butchers' selling dog and cat meat.

NORTH ATLANTIC FISHING

The European Community is willing to study North Atlantic fish stocks in a bid to resolve Canadian concerns the resource is being dangerously depleted, EC Fisheries Commissioner Manuel Marin said. "I think we will be able to demonstrate the European Community is not the first enemy of Canadian interests," he said in Ottawa. Canadian fishermen are concerned about European boats plying the waters just beyond Canada's 200-mile economic zone and have been demanding action from Ottawa.

ANOTHER DOCKLANDS DEFECTOR

Stanhope Properties PLC has withdrawn from the Royal Docks redevelopment project in the London Docklands. It is the second development company to quit the area this year.

Announcing the Stanhope decision, the London Docklands Development Corp. blamed the "continuing deterioration of the property market." Redevelopment of the area, formerly the site of the decaying docks in London's East End, has been spotlighted by the Thatcher government as a showcase for the benefits of free enterprise; but it has been hard hit for over a year by high base bank-interest rates, which have undermined property values.

At the start of this year, the development company Rosehaugh PLC, which had been in partnership with Stanhope in the Royal Docks project, announced it was quitting. The 260-acre Royal Docks project for a shopping center, business park and leisure complex was one of the largest development sites in London.

"The (Stanhope) withdrawal leaves in tatters the London Docklands Development Corp.'s plans to regenerate one of the most neglected areas of the East End," the Times of London commented.

Iranian vice patrols arrested 20 people in Tehran at the start of a crackdown on clothing that reveals too many curves or too much flesh. Most single Japanese women think the main reason they haven't yet married is a scarcity of "nice men," according to a survey conducted by the Altmann Institute, a Tokyo matchmaking agency.
