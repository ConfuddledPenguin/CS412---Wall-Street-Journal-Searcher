International: Mexican Congress Approves Proposal To Privatize Banks

  • Author: Matt Moffett
  • Date: 05/14/90

MEXICO CITY -- Mexico's lower house of Congress approved a constitutional amendment that will allow the return of the commercial banking system to private investors. Even before the vote, which would repeal the 1982 bank nationalization, there were indications the plan was fortifying the confidence of Mexican investors. The proposal by President Carlos Salinas de Gortari was passed Saturday by a vote of 339 to 54, with 12 abstentions. The amendment needed approval of two-thirds of the 500-member lower house.

The bill overcame its only obstacle when deputies from the ruling Institutional Revolutionary Party pledged to the conservative National Action Party to draft restrictions on banking monopolies and preferential lending to the government. The amendment is expected to win approval easily in the Senate and state legislatures, which are controlled by the ruling party.

Analysts say the state's 66% stake in the 18 commercial banks could sell for between $3.5 billion and $6.5 billion. The government hasn't indicated how or when it will sell off its holdings.

The move to privatize the banks has already cheered Mexican investors. Last week, Mexican interest rates tumbled to 36.6% from 42.6%. At the lower level, the government would save about $3 billion in interest payments annually on its domestic debt. Interest rates are high in Mexico to encourage investors to keep their savings in pesos. The government is hoping the banking privatization will show the state is serious about overhauling the economy and inspire the repatriation of some of the $80 billion Mexicans hold abroad.

Important issues remain to be resolved this summer when Congress debates the regulatory framework of the privatized financial system. Some politicians want tight limits on the bank stock holdings of individual investors. There is also discussion over the extent to which foreigners should participate in the banking system and whether to keep at least one of the big commercial banks in state hands.

Observers were surprised the move to privatize banks didn't generate more controversy. One poll showed only 15% of those questioned opposed the idea, while more than one-third had no idea it was being considered. It was no coincidence, analysts say, that Mr. Salinas scheduled the Congressional debate to coincide with last week's visit by Pope John Paul II, which captivated millions of Mexicans.

Even so, the bank privatization, coupled with Mexico's recent decision to hold talks with the U.S. on a free-trade agreement, manifests a new boldness on the part of Mexican policymakers, analysts say.

Mexico has sold or liquidated about 700 state-run enterprises since 1986, but only recently has Mr. Salinas focused on important but politically delicate sectors such as finance, telephones and steel. Before his first state-of-the-union address last November, he never publicly uttered the word "privatization," opting instead for the less politically charged "disincorporation."

Analysts say the president is stepping up the pace of economic reforms out of the realization that the initiatives he launched in his first year in office have achieved only modest benefits. Last year, his key economic-policy accomplishments were the renegotiation of Mexico's $93 billion foreign debt and a liberalization of the nationalistic foreign-investment law. But neither measure has guaranteed Mexico the capital inflows it urgently needs to cover a deficit in its current account, the broadest measure of trade, estimated at between $4 billion and $5 billion.
