U.S. Firms Trading With China Fear Removal of Most-Favored-Nation Status

  • Author: Steven E. Levingston
  • Date: 05/14/90

NEW YORK -- U.S. businesses involved in trade with China are raising an alarm over the possible elimination of China's most-favored-nation trade status. Fearing the U.S. will refuse to renew the MFN status for political reasons, business associations and individual companies are launching campaigns to warn of the impact on U.S.-China trade and on U.S. consumers.

In a letter sent to its members, Congress and the White House, the American Association of Exporters and Importers estimated that the removal of MFN status would boost tariffs on goods from China an average 40%. A 21-page report distributed by the United States-China Business Council offers examples: Some imported pants that currently are subject to a 17.7% duty would face a 90% tariff. Some toys would be taxed at 70% compared with the current 6.8%.

Many members of Congress are bent on slapping the Beijing leadership for its brutal crackdown on the pro-democracy movement last June, and the MFN status provides a powerful and timely weapon. But it's uncertain if that view would prevail if President Bush, as expected, extends most-favored trading status for China. If the legislators object and pass a resolution revoking MFN status for China, Mr. Bush could veto it; congressional staffers who follow the issue say it would be difficult for Congress to muster the two-thirds vote to override a veto. Mr. Bush isn't expected to disclose his decision until close to the June 3 deadline.

In a series of interviews, people here who do business in China expressed outrage at the Beijing government's actions in Tiananmen Square last year. But they argued that a rebuke of Beijing is a foreign policy matter that should be kept separate from the trade issue. "We recognize what the Chinese government did to its people," said Kathleen Syron of the U.S.-China Business Council. "We're not against some form of punishment. But don't use MFN."

Any change in China's trade status could hit importers with rising costs, either because of the higher tariffs or higher prices for supplies in other countries. That, in turn, could mean higher prices to American consumers. At the same time, a drop in China's shipments to the U.S. would further shrink the country's foreign-exchange reserves and reduce its ability to pay for American exports. U.S. exporters such as wheat growers and aircraft manufacturers could suffer an additional hardship if the the Chinese leadership should retaliate and refuse to buy U.S. goods.

The debate will heat up on Wednesday and on May 23, when the House Foreign Affairs Committee convenes hearings on the issue. By June 3, one day before the anniversary of the Tiananmen Square massacre, President Bush must notify Congress whether or not he intends to seek a renewal of China's MFN treatment. China's MFN status expires July 3.

The U.S. extends MFN status, which entitles a country to favorable tariffs, to nearly all its trading partners. Even such countries as South Africa, Iraq and Syria are accorded MFN trade status. Only 16 nations, including North Korea, Cuba and the Soviet Union, don't receive MFN benefits. "MFN sounds like a privilege," said Ms. Syron of the Washington-based United States-China Business Council. "But actually it's not."

The elimination of MFN for China would come at a particularly bad time for both U.S. textile importers and apparel consumers. Several recent U.S. decisions could impose higher tariffs on a broad range of textile goods, causing prices to rise. In one case, the Commerce Department last month issued a preliminary decision against South Korea, Taiwan and Hong Kong that could result in anti-dumping duties on those countries' synthetic-fiber sweaters. In addition, India recently was singled out as an unfair trading partner. Among India's chief exports to the U.S. is clothing.

Clint Stack, president of International Development Systems Inc., a Washington-based textile consulting firm, calculates that the combination of the anti-dumping duties, the revocation of China's MFN status and the Indian case would have a severe impact on the U.S. consumer.

"You could end up with 20% of the total U.S. textile apparel imports being subjected to significantly higher duty rates," he said. China's textile and apparel imports account for 13% of the total.

Tariffs wouldn't be the only cause of higher prices. If textile importers sought to avoid the higher duties by sourcing goods elsewhere, business people say other countries would likely raise their prices.
