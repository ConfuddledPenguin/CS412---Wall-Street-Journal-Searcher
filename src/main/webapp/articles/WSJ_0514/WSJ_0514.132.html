International -- Washington Insight: Whispers of a Soviet-German Concord Are Heard Under Kremlin's Grumbling

  • Author: Walter S. Mossberg
  • Date: 05/14/90

WASHINGTON -- Despite Moscow's grumbling about reunifying Germany, there are signs of a budding Soviet-German concord in the wind. U.S. officials privately predict that the emerging economic ties between the two countries will temper Soviet opposition to German unification. But the Soviet Union and Germany also are finding common ground on political and strategic issues -- a development that could threaten Washington's hopes of keeping Germany firmly in the Western orbit.

The potential bond between Europe's two strongest powers is likely to be built on an economic foundation. "The Soviets need Germany economically," said one top Bush administration policy-maker, who reviewed the recent opening round of six-power talks on German unification.

"They depended on East Germany for so much," he said, "and they're going to need united Germany to continue that trade. Plus they need new investment from Germany. So they're not likely to want to be viewed as the only power blocking German unification."

Others agree. "The Soviet Union's largest East European trading partner is East Germany, its largest Western trading partner is West Germany," says Charles Gati, a specialist on Soviet-East European affairs at Union College and author of a new book on the subject. "So you can just imagine the economic stakes the Soviet Union has with the new Germany."

Adds former State Department official Robert Hormats, now with Goldman, Sachs & Co., "The economic relationship with Germany is Gorbachev's ace in the hole. In the final unity deal, he can extract economic concessions from the Germans."

Signs of such a potential trade-off were sprinkled through an otherwise negative Soviet performance at the unity talks, which are formally called the "Two-Plus-Four" negotiations. Shortly before laying down strong private objections to German membership in the North Atlantic Treaty Organization and making other demands, Soviet Foreign Minister Eduard Shevardnadze held a long, cordial meeting with West German Chancellor Helmut Kohl to discuss bilateral relations, including economic ties.

Mr. Shevardnadze said he and Mr. Kohl had "noted that the potential for cooperation between our two countries is as yet not fully exhausted. There are great reserves. There's a great deal yet to be done, and we discussed ways of making use of, of drawing on those reserves."

The Soviet foreign minister also refused to restate on West German television the strong Soviet objections to German membership in NATO, saying only that he was optimistic a settlement could be reached. His aides distributed his tough remarks, including a refusal to grant the new Germany full sovereignty, only to Soviet and East German reporters.

Mr. Kohl has pledged that the new Germany will honor existing trade and contract commitments between the Soviets and East Germany, a costly proposition for Germany, because it could require maintaining production of some items to meet Soviet specifications, even though they can't be sold in the West.

Last year in Bonn, Mr. Kohl and Soviet President Mikhail Gorbachev signed a "comprehensive economic collaboration" stepping up trade and investment. An investment treaty has been signed, Soviet executives are being trained in West Germany, and German banks have set up a $1 billion credit line for Soviet trade.

"The Soviets realize that the united Germany has a growth potential from which they can profit if they reform their economy," says a German diplomat. "We've made that very clear to them."

But money, important as it is to the near-bankrupt U.S.S.R., isn't the only tie that binds the two European powers. Aware of Soviet security fears, German officials have won U.S. support for a plan to keep NATO forces out of former East German territory after unification. They have invited the Red Army to remain in East German territory during a transition period that could last seven years. The new Germany will even pay for the troops.

Most importantly, there is broad agreement between German and Soviet leaders on the desirability of establishing some new, vaguely defined Pan-European security system under the auspices of the existing Conference on Security and Cooperation in Europe.

This idea, which makes U.S. officials nervous, has wide popular appeal and is embraced to varying degrees by all political parties in both Germanys. So far, German leaders insist the new system would complement, not replace, NATO. But the Soviet strategy is to build on this romantic German notion of a Pan-European security system and eventually drive NATO out of business by replacing it with the new security system.

And despite the bitter legacy of World War II, there are historic precedents for Russian-German cooperation. "When they weren't fighting each other, the Russians have used the Germans as advisers for years," says Mr. Hormats. "For centuries, there has been a love-hate relationship between Russia and Germany."
