Thornburgh Suffers One More Setback; Aide Quits After Dispute on Sentencing

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett and Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 05/14/90

WASHINGTON -- Attorney General Dick Thornburgh suffered another public embarrassment when a senior Justice Department official resigned two weeks after he was forced to rescind his endorsement of tougher sentencing guidelines for corporate crime. The departure of Donald Ayer, the deputy attorney general, reflects continued resentment within the Justice Department of Mr. Thornburgh's heavy reliance on a small group of longtime personal aides. The resignation, effective May 25, also adds to a string of high-visibility setbacks for the attorney general, who as a two-term governor of Pennsylvania had been widely hailed as a savvy politician and manager.

The White House nominated William Barr, an assistant attorney general, to succeed Mr. Ayer. The selection requires Senate approval.

In a statement released Friday, Mr. Ayer said he had decided to return to his partnership in the Washington, D.C., office of a large Cleveland law firm, Jones, Day, Reavis & Pogue. In a letter, Mr. Thornburgh praised Mr. Ayer's "valuable contributions" during his six months in office.

But the bland public exchange only thinly veiled the underlying tension over Mr. Thornburgh's management style. Mr. Ayer is known to have been unhappy that despite his formal designation as the attorney general's top deputy, he was in fact outranked by a handful of Mr. Thornburgh's personal assistants from the Harrisburg, Pa., statehouse.

The three most prominent Thornburgh assistants -- Robert Ross, David Runkel and Murray Dickman -- have been the targets of persistent criticism from some current and former Justice Department employees, who complain that the Thornburgh team lacks the experience to lead the 75,000-person agency.

Mr. Ayer, 41 years old, couldn't be reached for comment. A conservative former law clerk to Chief Justice William Rehnquist, he previously served as the Justice Department's deputy solicitor general and as U.S. attorney in Sacramento, Calif. Mr. Ayer's chief assistant, Peter Nowinski, also quit.

Mr. Thornburgh instructed his senior staff not to discuss the Ayer resignation, according to one aide. An administration official familiar with the matter called it "an unfortunate situation" and "just one of those things that didn't work out."

Specifically, the attorney general is said to have been disappointed by Mr. Ayer's execution of several assignments that resulted in public criticism of the Justice Department.

Chief among these was Mr. Ayer's written endorsement in February of tougher federal sentencing guidelines for corporate crimes. After big business lobbyists complained to the White House and President Bush's counsel indicated concern, the attorney general ordered Mr. Ayer to rescind the endorsement, pending further review. When the decision to back off from the tougher sentencing guidelines became the subject of politically embarrassing news reports, Thornburgh aides blamed Mr. Ayer for acting without approval from his boss.

In earlier public missteps, Mr. Thornburgh saw his initial choice for the deputy attorney general's job shot down by Senate conservatives and his recommendation for civil rights chief rejected by Senate liberals.

Mr. Barr, the nominee to replace Mr. Ayer, has made a name for himself as the author of controversial legal opinions authorizing U.S. agents to snatch fugitives abroad without the approval of foreign governments, and justifying the use of U.S. military forces to conduct American law-enforcement missions in other countries. The White House named Mr. Barr's top assistant, Michael Luttig, to succeed him as assistant attorney general in charge of the Office of Legal Counsel.

Separately, the White House named Robert Bonner, a federal trial judge in Los Angeles, to succeed John Lawn as chief of the Drug Enforcement Administration, a branch of the Justice Department.
