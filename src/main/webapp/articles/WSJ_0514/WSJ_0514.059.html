Amax's $1.25 Billion Offer for Peabody Topped by Hanson; Bids' War Is Doubted

  • Author: Randall Smith
  • Date: 05/14/90

NEW YORK -- Amax Inc. made a $1.25 billion bid for Peabody Holding Co., the largest U.S. coal producer, but the offer was immediately topped by Hanson PLC, which said it was "determined" to complete its previously announced plans to buy Peabody. Wall Street observers said that Hanson's quick response suggested that Amax, a natural-resources company based here, had misplayed the bid and succeeded only in drawing attention to its own vulnerability as a possible takeover candidate. The observers said they doubted that Amax would make a new bid.

Hanson acquired 45% of Peabody last month for $504 million from three other companies. In March, Hanson had announced a plan to buy the remaining 55% of Peabody for $715 million from Newmont Mining Corp. Hanson acquired a 49% stake in Newmont by buying Consolidated Gold Fields Ltd. last August.

Hanson's agreement with Newmont allows other bidders to top its offer for all of Peabody before the purchase is completed in June, to make sure Newmont's public holders receive a fair price. The Amax bid, which occurred around noon Friday, valued Newmont's Peabody stake at $718.5 million -- only a slight increase on Hanson's offer. Within two hours, Hanson boosted its bid to $725.6 million.

An Amax spokesman wouldn't comment on whether Amax plans a new bid. Wall Street observers said they doubted Amax would engage in a bidding war with the much larger Hanson, a British conglomerate with $9 billion in cash and interests in tobacco, specialty chemicals, and building products, particularly in view of Hanson's stated determination. "Hanson wants this company," said one individual close to Hanson.

But the sequence of events puzzled some observers. Said one: "The question is why would Amax openly compete with Hanson, and risk putting themselves in play {for a takeover}, with a bid that was such a small increase over Hanson's offer?"

An Amax spokesman suggested Amax may have misunderstood Newmont's March 29 announcement of the Peabody sale to Hanson, which stated that Hanson and Newmont "would accept" an offer above $1.24 billion for all of Peabody. "Reading that, it looks like they're inviting an offer and naming a price," the spokesman said. "Well, we took them at their word and now we see their response."

Vahid Fahti, a base-metals analyst at Prescott Ball & Turben Inc. in Cleveland, said that some people might interpret the Amax bid "as a defensive move." Amax has been the subject of off-and-on takeover speculation in the past six months, with unusually high trading volume in its stock.

News of the Peabody bid hurt Amax's stock price, which declined 12.5 cents a share to $25.25 Friday in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange despite a strong advance in the overall market. Some analysts value the company's parts as high as $50 a share.

In 1989, Amax reported profit of $360.4 million, or $4.19 a share, on revenue of $3.89 billion. Aluminum accounted for 65% of its sales, coal 14%, gold 10%, with the remainder in molybdenum and natural gas. It has slightly less than $1 billion in debt. Its Amax Coal unit is the third-largest U.S. producer, with 4% of the market compared with 9% for Peabody.

Mr. Fahti said he expects Amax's earnings to decline about 35% this year to $2.75 a share, mainly due to lower aluminum prices and softer demand in the housing and construction markets, the end-users of much of Amax's output. He said a coal acquisition that more than doubled Amax's debt could make the company "somewhat less attractive to a suitor."

The Amax bid amounts to about 12 times Peabody's 1989 profit of $101.9 million on revenue of $1.74 billion. Although Hanson doesn't have any other coal business, it has operations in several mining industries, including cement, gold, sand and gravel, and brick.

Last August, Amax made a $2.3 billion bid for Falconbridge Ltd., a Canadian nickel, copper and zinc producer, but was later outbid by Noranda Inc. and Trelleborg AB, two other metals concerns. The Amax spokesman said Amax had been invited to bid by Falconbridge, which was concerned about a "creeping takeover" by Noranda, which had acquired 28% of Falconbridge.

One company with a rumored interest in Amax is Peter Kiewit Sons Inc., a closely held concern in Omaha, Neb., with interests in construction and natural resources. A spokesman for Kiewit couldn't be reached for comment.
