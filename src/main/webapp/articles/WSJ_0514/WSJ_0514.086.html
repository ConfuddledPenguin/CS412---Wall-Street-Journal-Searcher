Economy: Data Make Fed Unlikely to Raise Rates --- April Producer Prices Fell 0.3%, Retail Sales 0.6%

  • Author: Alan Murray
  • Date: 05/14/90

WASHINGTON -- Two new government reports, suggesting that the economy is more sluggish and inflation less intense than previously thought, should end speculation that the Federal Reserve is about to push up interest rates this week. The new April statistics, showing a drop in prices at the producer level and a decline in retail sales, come as the Fed's policy committee prepares for a meeting this week. While many analysts had thought the Fed might boost rates to curb inflation, the new figures make it likely the committee will leave its grip on rates unchanged -- a prospect that helped produce a rise in bond prices and a surge in stocks after the reports came out Friday.

The Labor Department reported that producer prices dropped 0.3% in April, following a 0.2% decline the month before. Analysts say the two-month decline in producer prices should eventually bring down consumer prices, which galloped upward at a worrisome 8.5% annual rate in the first quarter of this year.

Separately, the Commerce Department said retail sales fell a steep 0.6% in April, due largely to a continued sharp decline in automobile sales. Retail sales fell a revised 0.2% in March.

The statistics add further confusion to the current economic picture, which has been characterized by intermittent signs of sluggish growth and rising prices. "People keep thinking we have to be doing one or the other, inflating or going into recession," says Donald Ratajczak, who heads the economic forecasting project at Georgia State University. "The truth is, we aren't doing much of either."

"While we should never draw simply from one month's numbers, we believe that the upsurge in inflation will abate as the year progresses and inflation will remain under control," said Michael Boskin, chairman of the White House Council of Economic Advisers. "There were many special factors affecting the consumer price index and producer price index in the first part of the year, and the inflation rate in the first quarter overstated the inflation problem."

"The economy is not resurging and inflation is not accelerating," said Lyle Gramley, chief economist for the Mortgage Bankers Association.

The slump in automobile sales heavily affected both of the latest economic reports. Auto sales plummeted 2.7% in April from the previous month, the Commerce Department said, dragging down the overall retail sales number. And efforts to boost sales by cutting prices caused the Labor Department's index of passengercar prices to drop 0.3% in April, following a 0.8% decline in March.

Excluding automobiles, retail sales were unchanged in the latest month. Rosalind Wells, who is chief economist for the National Retail Federation and who monitors retail sales closely, said that "consumer demand is holding up pretty well, except for durable, high-ticket items."

The drop in producer prices largely reflected a recovery from January's sharp spike in food prices caused by unusually cold weather. Tomato prices dived 73%, sweet corn prices fell 50% and cabbage prices dropped 36% in the latest month. Excluding the volatile food and energy components, producer prices in April rose a modest 0.2%, compared with a 0.3% increase the month before.

In financial markets, some traders speculated that new signs of a weak economy and low inflation would lead the Fed to ease its grip on credit at the meeting this week. But that's not likely. A number of Fed policy makers have recently expressed concerns about the inflation trend, and their views are unlikely to be changed by these two reports.

"I think it would be premature to conclude that . . . the Fed is about to ease policy," said Norman Robertson, chief economist for Mellon Bank. "I think the Fed has shown commendable caution about shifting policy on the basis of month-to-month changes in data."

"We've still got an inflation problem," agrees Mr. Gramley. "This number just says it's not fundamentally getting worse."

Producer prices of intermediate goods, which still require some processing, rose 0.1% in April after remaining unchanged in March. Crude-goods prices dropped 3.1% during the month, reflecting a sharp fall in prices of petroleum as well as food. Excluding food and energy, prices of crude goods rose a steep 2.2% in April following a 2% rise in March.

The drop in retail sales left them at $148.64 billion for the month. Sales of durable goods, which are intended to last more than three years and include autos, dropped 1.6% in April to $53.79 billion. Sales of nondurable goods were virtually unchanged at $94.85 billion.

Figures in both reports are adjusted for seasonal changes. Before adjustments, retail sales last month totaled $145.46 billion.

Meanwhile, meeting in a Virginia mountain resort during the weekend, leaders of many of the nation's largest corporations also predicted inflation will slow sharply from its swift first-quarter pace. Members of the Business Council, an association of chief executives, said they expect consumer prices to grow at a 3.3% annual rate in the second quarter, a big retreat from their 8.1% rate in the first. For all of 1990, they expect inflation to run about 4.8%.

The corporate chieftains' projections were based on a report by the council's advisory group of economists. Overall, the Business Council says the economy will grow about 2% this year. None of its 19 economists expects a recession this year or next.
