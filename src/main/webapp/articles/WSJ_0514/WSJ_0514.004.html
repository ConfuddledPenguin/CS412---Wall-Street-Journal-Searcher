Real Artists Don't Take Handouts

  • Author: Bill Kauffman
  • Date: 05/14/90

The Robert Mapplethorpe brouhaha has rapidly degenerated into caricature. Flying the flag of truth, beauty and the First Amendment is the culture establishment, righteously striving "to save the atmosphere for creative freedom of artists," in Congressman Pat Williams's (D., Mont.) words. The barbarians at the gate are a motley crew of right-wing philistines and homophobes, faces plastered with unctuous censor's smiles. How depressingly incomplete this picture is.

You'd never know it from watching CSPAN, but there are actually writers and artists in the American heartland who want nothing to do with the National Endowment for the Arts. We think that government subsidy is corrupting and crippling, suitable only for fat and happy eunuchs. Our creed was best expressed by Oxford, Miss.'s native son, William Faulkner, who said, "The writer doesn't need economic freedom. All he needs is a pencil and paper. I've never known anything good in writing to come from having accepted any free gift of money. The good writer never applies to a foundation. He's too busy writing something."

Faulkner's disdain for grant-grubbing is echoed by many of our finest contemporary writers, from Gore Vidal to the late great desert maverick Edward Abbey. The NEA's chosen few are made docile by the dole; Beat impresario Lawrence Ferlinghetti scorns them as "cooperating poets and publishers."

Is it then the artist's lot to starve and scrape, to eat rice and home-baked bread and scribble feverishly in the darkest hours? Yes] Comforted, coddled, cossetted artists create mediocre art. That's their right, of course, but why should the taxpayer get stuck with the bill?

Can anyone name one major piece of American fiction that would not have been written without the NEA's beneficence? How about a significant minor work?

Moreover, NEA grants are not "free." Uncle Sam is like any other sugar daddy: he's got a generous touch, but sooner or later he's gonna demand service. Andres Serrano, photographer of the ineffable "Piss Christ," should have thought about the dangers of subsidy before he cashed that $15,000 check.

How dismaying it is to watch novelists and poets troop before television cameras and beg for taxpayer dollars with all the dignity of defense contractors. I expect that of the arts bureaucrats, who are, after all, merely trying to save their jobs. But what in hell are Garrison Keillor and Larry McMurtry doing at the microphones? Since when did pork barrel for the hyper-literate become a matter of principle?

Sen. Jesse Helms (R., N.C.) and Rep. Dana Rohrabacher (R., Calif.) could have enlisted a fair-sized contingent of writers and artists to support their battle against the NEA. Alas, they wound up making all the wrong arguments. They dragged that old bugbear homosexuality out of the closet for yet another public flogging. They spoke fatuously of art that "glorifies" America: not the real America, which Whitman rhapsodized in his (unsubsidized) "Leaves of Grass," but a "Top Gun" America, an aseptic place agleam with military hardware.

This may shock Mr. Helms and Mr. Rohrabacher, but America has a rich literary tradition, predating even Tom Clancy. The two great flowerings of our national letters, in the 1850s and 1920s, occurred without any help from a ministry of culture. Whitman, Thoreau, Poe, Melville, Stowe, Hawthorne . . . not an NEA grantee in the bunch. Their grey eminence, Ralph Waldo Emerson, spoke to them and to writers unborn: "If you would learn to write, `tis in the street you must learn it. . . . The people, and not the college, is the writer's home."

And poverty is the writer's lot. (Until the mortgage becomes just too scary, and our noble young novelist slinks off to Hollywood, and the sun rather quickly sets.)

If Thoreau can write "Walden" and Melville can produce "Moby Dick" without Uncle Sam's patronage, then surely Robert Mapplethorpe's claque can express themselves without a government handout. Better to be poor and free than in the culture establishment's hip pocket.

---

Mr. Kauffman is a novelist in Batavia, N.Y., and author of "Every Man a King," Soho Press, 1989.
