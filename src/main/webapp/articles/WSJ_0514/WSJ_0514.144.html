White House Says It's Sincere On Deficit Talks --- Bush, Aides Try to Reassure Democrats That a Trap Isn't Being Set on Taxes

  • Author: David Wessel and Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 05/14/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush and his top aides sought to reassure Democrats that the administration is sincere about negotiating a compromise to shrink the swelling federal deficit. Mr. Bush, Chief of Staff John Sununu and Budget Director Richard Darman attempted to repair the damage Mr. Sununu did last week when he told reporters that Democrats could propose taxes, but that the White House would reject them. All three sought to return to previous declarations that the bipartisan talks begin tomorrow with "no preconditions."

Mr. Sununu was more cautious yesterday, saying on NBC's "Meet the Press" that "The White House would be likely to look askance" at taxes. He displayed a modicum of flexibility by noting that the administration finds some "user fees and other revenue raisers" more acceptable than others.

And, given the opportunity on several occasions during his television appearance, Mr. Sununu declined to repeat the president's no-new-taxes campaign pledge.

Democrats are proceeding with the talks, but with great wariness that the administration is setting a trap to blame them for the tax increases that are certain to be part of any significant deficit-reduction package. With each side insisting publicly that the other be the first to propose any tax increases, the talks could bog down. But the point of closed-door bargaining is to find a way to share the blame for an unpopular combination of spending cuts and tax increases. Accused by one reporter yesterday of being deliberately vague, Mr. Darman said, "That's constructive."

"The tricky part is getting all of the parties to get from where they are right at the moment to what they might agree on without getting themselves in such an argument that the thing falls through," Mr. Darman said on ABC's "This Week with David Brinkley."

Mr. Bush sought out reporters aboard Air Force One on Friday in an acknowledged attempt to restore Democrats' confidence in his good faith. "I made this proposal that we meet with no conditions, and that's exactly the way it's going to be." He wouldn't talk about his willingness or lack thereof to accept tax increases. "I'm not going to show you my hand," he said.

Dismissing suggestions that Mr. Sununu might be in trouble for roiling the waters before negotiations began, Mr. Bush insisted it is "silly . . . stupid" to suggest his chief of staff is in hot water. The president expects more flaps over the tax question before the negotiations are concluded. "Tomorrow there'll be another tidal wave, so keep your snorkel above the water level and do what you think is right," he said.

Democrats seemed prepared to accept Mr. Bush's assurances. Senate Finance Chairman Lloyd Bentsen (D., Texas), accompanying Mr. Bush on his flight to Texas, said that the budget summit is "back on track -- as long as we keep these fellows in line who are trying to redefine it."

But Mr. Sununu once again ruffled feathers on Capitol Hill, asserting yesterday on NBC that the catalyst for the president's invitation to talk isn't so much the economy, but the inadequacies of Congress. Acknowledging that Mr. Bush's budget plan isn't sufficient, Mr. Sununu complained that "the failure of Congress to do any constructive revision . . . since the president sent it up is part of the problem we have."

In response, Senate Budget Committee Chairman James Sasser (D., Tenn.) said, "The chief of staff is off base once again." He suggested pointedly that Mr. Sununu leave negotiating to Mr. Bush and Mr. Darman.

Mr. Darman said that negotiators should aim at a package to cut the deficit by between $45 billion and $55 billion -- even though he said meeting deficit targets set by the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law would require between $50 billion and $100 billion in cuts, or "over $100 billion" if all the spending on the thrift industry bailout is included. Part of any agreement is likely to be the exclusion of thrift spending from the Gramm-Rudman calculations.

The Gramm-Rudman law sets a deficit target of $64 billion for fiscal 1991, which begins Oct. 1. If Congress and the president fail to reach a compromise, deep across-the-board spending cuts will be triggered in October.
