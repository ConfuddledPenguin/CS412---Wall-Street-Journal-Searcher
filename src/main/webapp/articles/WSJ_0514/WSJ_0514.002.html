Food Stamps Fail To Cover Needs Of the Rural Poor

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 05/14/90

WASHINGTON -- The nation's rural poor are going hungry because food-stamp benefits aren't enough to cover higher food costs in impoverished rural counties, a new consumer survey shows. Public Voice for Food and Health Policy, a Washington-based consumer group, says that of nine million rural Americans living in poverty, many are food-stamp recipients who are paying higher prices for fewer calories than the poor in cities.

In its survey of 33 counties with a 25% poverty rate, the group found that the weekly $75 food-stamp allotment for a household of four isn't adequate to buy all of the food in the government's "thrifty food plan," which is regarded as the minimum diet from a nutrition standpoint. It takes $102 a week to buy the thrifty food plan if food-stamp recipients don't have access to competitively priced supermarkets and must shop at higher priced mom-and-pop stores, according to the survey.

What's more, even at rural supermarkets, the average cost of foods in the thrifty plan is 8% higher than the $75 allotment, the survey shows.

Ellen Haas, executive director of Public Voice, faults the Agriculture Department, which administers the food-stamp program, for failing to take the urban-rural price differences into account.

The results of the national survey, conducted last year, will strengthen the hand of Rep. Leon Panetta (D., Calif.) and other lawmakers who are pushing hunger-relief measures this year in Congress. The group also found that one of every three food-stamp dollars in poor areas is spent in crossroads stores and small-town grocery stores where supplies of fresh produce and meat are limited. Last summer, 23% of the stores surveyed didn't stock any fresh vegetables and 33% didn't stock any fresh fruits, while 30% didn't sell fresh meats.

In its survey, the group recommends increasing food-stamp allotments 17% by fiscal 1995 to boost the poor's purchasing power and re-evaluating the Agriculture Department's methods for determining food-stamp benefits. In addition, it calls for federal grants to help small rural stores set up wholesale-purchasing cooperatives and become more price-competitive with supermarkets.
