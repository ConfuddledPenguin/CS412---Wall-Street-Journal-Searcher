Laventhol & Horwath Faces More Woes As It Loses Unit and Key Executives

  • Author: Lee Berton
  • Date: 05/14/90

NEW YORK -- Laventhol & Horwath, the seventh-biggest U.S. accounting firm, is encountering new setbacks in the wake of its litigation problems and profit decline. The Philadelphia-based firm has lost a big Canadian affiliate, is experiencing an exodus of key personnel and is under pressure from its main lender to sell some of its divisions or units, people close to the situation said.

Laventhol declined comment on the personnel losses and the alleged bank problems. But it confirmed that its Canadian affiliate with 30 partners and 200 professional staffers in Toronto and annual revenue of $25 million Canadian dollars (US$21.3 million) is planning to join Price Waterhouse in Canada by July 1 or later.

In Toronto, Eric Slavens, co-managing partner of the Toronto office, said: "We're joining Price Waterhouse because we need more cross-country coverage for clients in Canada and Price Waterhouse gives us full 21-city coverage."

Edward Suarez, a national partner with Laventhol, which has 51 U.S. offices and annual U.S. revenue of $345.2 million, said that the Toronto office is a separate firm of Horwath International, Laventhol's international organization. "We still have three other affiliates in Canada in Vancouver, Winnipeg and Montreal," he added.

But other current and former Laventhol officials said that the Toronto affiliate and Horwath's London affiliate are key cogs in making Laventhol an international firm. "This loss will really hurt them," said one former Laventhol executive. Mr. Suarez said he believes that Horwath would seek another affiliate in Toronto.

The Canadian setback comes at a time when Laventhol is still reeling from large U.S. lawsuits. These have resulted in the firm's paying out more than $50 million over the past three years for alleged sloppy audit and financial-preparation work. Also, Laventhol's operating profit prior to litigation costs in the year ended last Jan. 31 declined 9% from a year earlier and Laventhol reduced a quarterly profit distribution to partners in mid-April by half.

Laventhol declined comment about its current problems, issuing a statement declaring that questions are a "rehashing of past situations and innuendo and unattributed false claims." Reached at home, Robert N. Levine, who will assume the firm's top post on June 1 and is effectively running Laventhol's operations now, declined comment beyond the statement.

Meanwhile, however, a person close to Laventhol said that Chase Manhattan Corp.'s Chase Bank, a lead lender of an $80 million loan package, has two employees at Laventhol's Philadelphia office monitoring Laventhol's receivables. The $80 million package is allegedly double what Laventhol had borrowed from banks prior to this year. The bank has suggested that Laventhol sell off two divisions -- Construction Monitoring and Valuation Counselors -- to put the accounting firm on a sounder financial footing, the person said. Laventhol declined to confirm or deny the report, and Chase officials couldn't be reached for comment.

Construction Monitoring, which values construction projects for financial institutions and monitors conformance with construction-loan agreements, is based in New York and has offices in San Francisco, Chicago and Los Angeles. Its annual revenue ranges between $3 million and $4 million.

Valuation Counselors, which values businesses, assets, property and equipment and does real-estate and health-care facility appraisals, is based in Chicago and has offices in Atlanta, Houston, Los Angeles, Boston and Princeton, N.J. Its annual revenue tops $19 million.

In addition, a growing number of partners and others are leaving Laventhol, though some say the reasons are unrelated to the firm's problems.

In Chicago, key strategic-planning and health-care consultants have told Laventhol of plans to go elsewhere. These include Barbara Shomaker and Edward Kazemek, consultants who are starting their own firm; Kristine E. Peterson, a healthcare consultant who said she plans to travel to Sweden for a year, and Bruce Richman, who said he plans to join the Chicago office of Coopers & Lybrand, a major accounting firm.

Asked why he was leaving, Mr. Richman, who joined Laventhol in 1987, said, "It's no secret about what's going on at Laventhol." He added: "One of the problems is that {we} are not always totally informed about the firm's problems."

Others who have left or who have announced plans to leave Laventhol include Philip Winik, area managing partner for the firm's western region out of Los Angeles; Al Zezulinski, a health-care consultant based in Philadelphia; David Love, managing partner of the Boston office; Peter Griffith, a consultant in Boston; David Ross, a management consultant based in New York; Bruce Ross, no relation to David, in litigation support in Los Angeles; and Ernest Ten Eyck, national accounting and auditing partner in Philadelphia.

Mr. Winik, 55 years old, said he plans to take early retirement for "personal reasons." He added: "My decision wasn't spur-of-the-moment and has nothing to do with the current state of the firm. I've planned it for some time."

David Ross said he planned to leave June 1 after being with Laventhol for 10 years. "I feel I'd rather be in industry than with a public accounting firm," he said, declining further comment.

Asked why he was leaving, Bruce Ross declined comment.

Mr. Ten Eyck said he plans to leave Laventhol next year after 10 years with the firm. He said that his leaving doesn't relate to any problems Laventhol is having "but that at the age of 46 I have decided to do something else other than public accounting."
