Maxxam-Unit Suit Notification Ruled Necessary by Judge

  • Author: Richard Turner
  • Date: 05/14/90

EUREKA, Calif. -- A judge said any prospective buyer of assets of Maxxam Inc.'s Pacific Lumber unit must be notified that the plaintiff in a pending lawsuit could be awarded those assets. Robert Martel, who filed the suit alleging fraud in Maxxam's 1985 takeover of Pacific Lumber, said Thursday's ruling by a Superior Court judge in Humboldt County would make it more difficult for the company to sell the unit.

"We claim in our underlying suit that Maxxam doesn't even own this property, that it belongs to the former shareholders of Pacific Lumber," he said. "So this ruling . . . warns prospective buyers or lenders that the property is in question. It stops the company from financially transferring property they don't own."

San Francisco attorney David Robertson of Morrison & Foerster, representing Maxxam, said he would petition an appellate court to reverse the ruling this week. He said the ruling was "procedural . . . the judge wasn't considering the merits." He added that the ruling would have no effect on the operations of the company.

But Attorney Thomas C. Petersen, who assisted Mr. Martel in his legal battle, said the ruling could make it more difficult for Maxxam to restructure its Pacific Lumber debt. Reports earlier this year said Pacific Lumber was considering a plan to retire its nearly $580 million of junk-bond debt, originally underwritten by Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., and instead borrow the money from banks and insurance companies, which would buy bonds backed by the company's rich timber assets.

Maxxam's secretary, Byron Wade, declined comment on its plans for the debt or any other issue related to the ruling.

The court ruling also means that any lender seeking collateral from Pacific Lumber assets must be notified that a lawsuit is pending which could award those assets to the plaintiff.

Mr. Martel, co-publisher of the Country Activist, a monthly journal in Humboldt County, brought suit last March against Maxxam; Maxxam Chairman Charles Hurwitz, a Houston investor; former Drexel junk-bond wizard Michael Milken; Ivan Boesky and others, alleging fraud in the takeover. The suit seeks to invalidate the merger, and also seeks class-action status.

It was unclear what effect the ruling would have on other litigation against Maxxam over the takeover, which includes a consolidated lawsuit filed by former shareholders seeking $2.25 billion, on grounds similar to Mr. Martel's suit. Pacific Lumber was also cited in the Securities and Exchange Commission's suit against Drexel and Mr. Milken.

In Thursday's ruling, Judge William F. Ferroggiaro Jr. denied a motion by Maxxam to expunge a "lis pendens" -- a right to notify people that litigation is pending and that company property could be won back by former shareholders if they prevail in their lawsuit.

The Maxxam takeover also became the focus of outrage from environmentalists after Mr. Hurwitz doubled the rate of cutting redwoods at Pacific Lumber in order to service the debt. Equally upset were local workers, concerned that the furious new production would deplete resources and attract the attention of environmentalists, jeopardizing their jobs.

Although Pacific Lumber was at one time well-regarded by environmental groups, two environmentally sponsored initiatives dealing with redwood preservation are awaiting signature certification to be placed on the California ballot this year. They would include bond measures for the state to buy and set aside old-growth redwood reserves.
