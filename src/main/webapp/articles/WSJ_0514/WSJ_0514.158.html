Drexel Subsidiary In Commodities Lists Assets, Debt

  • Author: Wade Lambert
  • Date: 05/14/90

NEW YORK -- Drexel Burnham Lambert Trading Corp., which was pushed into bankruptcy-law proceedings alongside its parent last week, disclosed details of its debt in a court filing. The commodities trading unit of Drexel Burnham Lambert Group Inc., which is based in Fort Lee, N.J., said its net liabilities as of March 30 were $504.5 million. Its total assets at that date were $587.3 million, including $558 million owed to the unit by the parent company. DBL Trading, which is no longer doing business, said those are the latest figures available.

Creditors of DBL Trading on Wednesday filed an involuntary bankruptcy petition to force the unit to liquidate. The Drexel unit's filing said that Nissho Iwai Corp., a major Japanese trading company, is owed $173.4 million, making it the largest creditor yet to emerge in the Drexel bankruptcy case. John Jerome, an attorney for Nissho Iwai, said the debt is from gold and platinum transactions. He said Nissho Iwai has a guarantee from the Drexel parent company for the claim.

The Drexel parent company filed under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code in February and is liquidating all of its operations. In federal bankruptcy court in Manhattan, Judge Howard Buschman III also converted DBL Trading's bankruptcy case to Chapter 11, under which the debtor remains in control of the company.

Mr. Jerome said Nissho Iwai has no objections to the case being handled under Chapter 11.

According to the court filing, the other major unsecured creditors of DBL Trading are the central bank of Portugal, which is owed $93.9 million; the central bank of Yugoslavia, $71.9 million; the central bank of Malaysia, $42.2 million; Ultramar Energy Ltd. of Tarrytown, N.Y., $10.8 million; the central bank of Poland, $10.6 million; CNCA HK, an affiliate of Caisse Nationale de Credit Agricole of France, $10.5 million; Al Baraka Investment Co. of London, $5.6 million; State Saving Bank of Warsaw, $4.8 million; and CRT Energy Ltd. of Chicago, $1.9 million. Some of the amounts may vary as exchange rates change.

Most of the debt involves precious-metals transactions, an attorney familiar with the case said. Some of the central banks leased small portions of their gold reserves to DBL Trading, which paid the foreign countries interest on the gold while investing it in other transactions. Some of the money owed to foreign central banks involves foreign currency transactions.
