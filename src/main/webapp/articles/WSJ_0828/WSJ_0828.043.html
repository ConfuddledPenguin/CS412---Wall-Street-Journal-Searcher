U.S. Expels Most of Baghdad's Envoys, Keeps a Hard Line on Talks With Iraq

  • Author: Gerald F. Seib
  • Date: 08/28/90

KENNEBUNKPORT, Maine -- The U.S. turned tougher in its diplomatic maneuverings toward Iraq, as the State Department expelled some Iraqi diplomats and President Bush staked out an uncompromising position for talks with Baghdad. The developments indicate that, while international attention remains focused on diplomatic rather than military developments in the Persian Gulf crisis for now, the prospects that diplomacy can succeed appear bleaker than they looked over the weekend.

In its first direct diplomatic retaliation against Iraq, the U.S. ordered the size of Iraq's diplomatic contingent in Washington slashed by two-thirds. The step was taken in response to Iraq's attempt to shut down the American embassy in occupied Kuwait by surrounding it with troops and shutting off its electricity and water.

Meanwhile, President Bush voiced little hope that new efforts to resolve the confrontation through negotiations will bear fruit. Mr. Bush endorsed plans by United Nations Secretary-General Javier Perez de Cuellar to meet Iraqi Foreign Minister Tariq Aziz this week, but indicated that the U.S. doesn't want the U.N. official to discuss anything except an unconditional Iraqi withdrawal from Kuwait.

"Saddam Hussein has been so resistant to complying with international law that I don't yet see fruitful negotiations," Mr. Bush told reporters at his vacation home here after a meeting with Canadian Prime Minister Brian Mulroney. Asked whether he is more hopeful there will be a diplomatic solution to the crisis than he was two weeks ago, Mr. Bush replied: "I don't particularly see more hope now, because it's so clear what the world is demanding of Saddam Hussein. Clearly the objectives remain the same."

The president said it is "inconceivable" that Mr. Perez de Cuellar would discuss anything beyond Iraqi compliance with United Nations resolutions demanding an unconditional withdrawal from Kuwait and release of foreigners being detained in Iraq and Kuwait. "Clearly we would oppose any compromise on these fundamental principles that have been laid down by the United Nations," Mr. Bush said.

Despite the increased diplomatic tensions, there continued to be signs that the U.S. and Iraq are trying to avoid military conflict for the moment. Iraq ordered at least one captain of an oil tanker in recent days not to defy the international naval blockade of trade with Iraq, according to U.S. officials. But they said they were unsure that the incident reflected any broader Iraqi decision on how to handle the blockage.

For his part, Mr. Bush indicated again that he hopes economic sanctions against Iraq will inflict so much pain on Saddam Hussein's regime that it will choose to withdraw from Kuwait to ease the pressure. "My policy is to do everything we can, working with other nations, to enforce the sanctions," he said.

The administration's order expelling Iraqi diplomatic personnel represented an attempt to lodge a highly visible protest against Iraq's treatment of American diplomats in Kuwait -- without going so far as to block avenues of communication with Baghdad. The State Department announced that it is ordering Iraq to cut the staff at the Iraqi embassy in Washington to 19 from 55. Seven diplomats, including all those who deal with commercial affairs, and 29 non-diplomatic staff members are being forced to leave, said State Department spokeswoman Margaret Tutwiler.

In addition, the U.S. is prohibiting the Iraqi diplomats who remain in Washington from traveling beyond a 25-mile radius of the city. The Bush administration clearly hopes that other nations will follow its lead and expel some Iraqi diplomats, but Ms. Tutwiler said only that the U.S. has "indications" other nations will follow suit.

The U.S. is particularly angered by Iraq's treatment of American diplomats after Iraqi officials ordered embassies in Kuwait closed last week. A small group of diplomats remaining in the embassy in defiance of that order are basically under siege, and another group of diplomats who left the embassy for Iraq are being refused the right to leave Iraq to return to the U.S. A group of 52 dependents of American diplomats based in Iraq were allowed to leave Iraq this week, but three male dependents over the age of 18 were stopped at the Iraqi-Turkish border as they were about to leave and forced back to Baghdad.

In addition, White House spokesman Marlin Fitzwater said that 63 other Americans are missing or being held by Iraqi officials -- 41 in Iraq and 22 in Kuwait.

Mr. Bush clearly wants to keep diplomatic channels to Iraq open. After his meeting with Mr. Mulroney, he insisted that Iraq is free to discuss the Gulf crisis with America's top diplomat in Baghdad. "I'm not saying we're not going to talk," Mr. Bush said.

But the president also suggested that there's little to talk about until Iraq withdraws its troops from Kuwait and the ousted Kuwaiti government is restored to power. "The position of the international law is not negotiable," he said. "The United Nations has spoken, country after country supporting the action taken by the Security Council. So there's no room for compromise or negotiation on that point."

All told, Mr. Bush's comments and remarks by his aides in recent days suggest that the administration is willing to let others explore the possibilities of a diplomatic solution to the Gulf crisis for a while, now that the U.S. has helped assemble a significant military force and won sweeping U.N. legal sanctions against Iraq. But Mr. Bush's tough tone about the limits of diplomatic discussions is a signal that the U.S. won't be drawn for now into any bargaining with Saddam Hussein.

It's likely that Mr. Bush plans to underscore his views personally to the U.N.'s Mr. Perez de Cuellar. The president is scheduled to talk with the secretary-general by phone before Mr. Perez de Cuellar meets Iraq's foreign minister in Jordan on Thursday.

Mr. Bush plans to take a break from his Maine vacation today for meetings in Washington on the Gulf crisis. He is due to meet with some 150 members of Congress to discuss the crisis, and he then plans to spend Wednesday and Thursday in meetings and briefings on the Gulf situation as well as coming budget negotiations with Congress. He is set to return here Thursday night for the long Labor Day weekend.
