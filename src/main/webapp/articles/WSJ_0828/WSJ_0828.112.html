What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/28/90

THE U.S. EXPELLED Iraqi envoys as Bush voiced pessimism on "fruitful" talks. In response to Iraq's attempt to close the U.S. Embassy in Kuwait, the White House ordered Baghdad to slash its diplomatic contingent in Washington by two-thirds. While Bush expressed doubts, he endorsed plans by the U.N.'s chief to meet Iraq's foreign minister, but the president indicated the U.S. wants the talks limited to an unconditional Iraqi pullout from Kuwait. Baghdad, meanwhile, reportedly ordered at least one captain of an oil tanker not to defy the international naval blockade of trade with Iraq.

The food situation in Iraq doesn't appear nearly as desperate as first thought, U.S. officials said, but the Iraqis are running into severe problems feeding their soldiers in Kuwait.

---

THE U.N. REPORTEDLY AGREED on a plan to end Cambodia's conflict.

The permanent members of the Security Council -- the U.S., the Soviet Union, Britain, France and China -- formulated a plan calling for the U.N. to administer key Cambodian ministries in a transition leading to free elections, diplomats said. The plan also is said to call for the U.N. to verify a ceasefire in the decade-old civil war and to verify that Vietnamese forces have withdrawn.

The proposals, which one official described as "Cambodia's last hope," will be submitted to Cambodia's warring factions for their approval.

---

Tens of thousands of blacks held a one-day strike in South Africa to bury their dead and to demand an end to factional fighting that has killed more than 500 people. Anglican Archbishop Tutu blamed police officials and pro-apartheid whites for the recent violence, and called for the creation of a new police force acceptable to blacks.

---

Quebec's premier broke off talks with Indians involved in a seven-week armed standoff with authorities and ordered the Canadian army to tear down Mohawk barricades in two communities. Bourassa, who charged that the Mohawks hadn't been negotiating in good faith on their demands, didn't say how or when the army would act.

---

Eugene Hasenfus lost his bid to be compensated for his role in the White House-sanctioned Contra supply mission. Jurors in federal court in Miami rejected the claims of the crew member of a plane shot down over Nicaragua in 1986. Most of his claims had been dismissed earlier, reducing his plea to $500.

---

A Utah brush fire raged out of control, killing two firefighters, destroying at least 18 homes and scorching 2,500 acres, officials said. About 300 homes were evacuated in the path of the wind-driven blaze, which broke out Sunday in mountains 30 miles east of Salt Lake City. The fire, of unknown origin, had simmered since late last week.

---

About 20,000 Bulgarians rallied in Sofia to show support for the governing Socialist Party, the former Communist Party, near the party's headquarters, which was attacked and burned Sunday by anti-Communist protesters. Police were instructed to use clubs and tear gas if necessary to prevent further violence in the capital.

---

A Michigan appeals court ruled that news organizations must turn over unpublished photographs and notes to people involved in civil lawsuits. It was the first time that a Michigan court has considered whether the First Amendment protects reporters from having to disclose nonconfidential information.

---

Scholastic Aptitude Test scores fell for the third straight year because of students' weakening verbal skills. Students headed for college this fall achieved an average combined mathematics and verbal score of 900, down from 903 last year. All of the decline was in the verbal score, which fell to 424.

---

Nigerian ships captured a boat carrying weapons for a Liberian rebel leader, according to a spokesman for the African forces trying to impose a cease-fire in Liberia's civil war. An official with the multinational force said the guns seized were destined for insurgent Charles Taylor, who heads one of two groups fighting to oust President Doe.

---

Ousted Pakistani leader Bhutto said charges of illegal weapons possession brought against her husband were politically motivated and designed to influence elections scheduled for Oct. 24. Police officials filed the charges Sunday night against the ex-prime minister's husband but didn't arrest the 37-year-old businessman.

---

Rival Christian forces battled in East Beirut, killing two soldiers and violating a three-month truce, security officials said. Troops of rebellious Gen. Aoun reportedly fought for two hours with militiamen of the Lebanese Forces. Aoun has rejected an Arab-sponsored peace pact to end 15 years of civil war in Lebanon.

---

Angola's leftist government opened direct peace talks with U.S.-backed Unita rebels. The long-delayed meeting, held in Portugal, was the third in five months aimed at ending the 15-year Angolan civil war. Officials close to the discussions said they were doubtful that much progress would be made toward a firm cease-fire accord.
