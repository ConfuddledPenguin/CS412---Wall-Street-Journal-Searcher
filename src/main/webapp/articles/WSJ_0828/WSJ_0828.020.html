Appeals Court Agrees to Review Reversal Of Chestman's Insider-Trade Conviction

  • Author: Wade Lambert
  • Date: 08/28/90

NEW YORK -- Responding to law-enforcement officials' claims that future insider-trading prosecutions were at risk, the federal appeals court in Manhattan agreed to a rare, full-court review of the reversal of former stockbroker Robert Chestman's conviction. The reversal decision, issued May 2 by three members of the appeals court, essentially gutted two major laws used by the Securities and Exchange Commission and the Justice Department in fighting insider trading. The U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals typically doesn't grant a full review of decisions by its three-judge panels.

In asking the court to rehear the appeal, the SEC said, "The panel decision, unless set aside, could undermine the ability of {authorities} . . . to police insider trading, which has been one of the most important areas of securities-law enforcement in recent years."

The U.S. attorney's office, which made the formal request for a rehearing, said in its brief that the panel's "decision may well be viewed by the securities industry as providing a safe harbor within which to trade on inside information."

Richard A. Kirby, the SEC's senior litigation counsel, called the decision to rehear the case "a positive step for the SEC."

The decision reversing Mr. Chestman's conviction focused on two areas: whether a fiduciary duty has to be violated for an insider-trading law to apply, and whether someone who trades on a stock tip violates the law if the tipper didn't specifically say that the information was confidential.

The appeals court announced that a majority of its judges had voted to reconsider the decision. Oral arguments on the appeal will be heard by the court at 1 p.m. on Nov. 9. The court could decide to uphold the panel's decision, which resulted in Mr. Chestman's release from prison, or it could rule that the conviction before the trial judge was proper.

The court said it will address issues raised regarding a securities regulation called Rule 14e-3, which makes it a crime to trade on misappropriated information involving a tender offer. The SEC commonly relies on Rule 14e-3 in pursuing insider-trading cases against defendants who aren't company insiders.

The court also will look at Rule 10b-5, which makes it a crime to trade on a stock tip that is nonpublic information. Law-enforcement officials said the earlier decision would prohibit the use of circumstantial evidence in showing that someone who received a tip knew or should have known that the stock information was confidential.

Already the appeals-court panel's reversal of the Chestman conviction has been cited in at least one lower-court decision in a federal insider-trading case. And Thomas Newkirk, the SEC's chief litigation counsel, said the panel's decision is being cited by defense attorneys in cases across the U.S. In its brief, the SEC said, "The impact of the panel decision is enhanced by the pre-eminent role of this circuit in the development of the law of insider trading."

"The original decision was potentially very damaging for" the SEC's law-enforcement division, Mr. Newkirk said. "The fact that this circuit is where all our good cases have historically originated makes it even more important."

The court's agreement to review the reversal means the decision now is not binding on federal district judges in the circuit.

Mr. Chestman's attorney couldn't be reached for comment.

Mr. Chestman, formerly a broker with Gruntal & Co., was found guilty in March 1989 of 31 counts related to alleged insider trading in Waldbaum Inc. stock just before the company's takeover in 1986 by Great Atlantic & Pacific Tea Co.

The trading was based on information that Mr. Chestman received fourth-hand, from an in-law of Waldbaum's then-president. The court reversed the conviction on the ground that Mr. Chestman hadn't been told that the information was confidential.
