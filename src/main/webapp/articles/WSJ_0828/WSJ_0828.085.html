Economy: Buy-Backs Begun, Extended Amid Market Decline

  • Author: Peter Pae
  • Date: 08/28/90

NEW YORK -- A steady decline in stock market prices since the Middle East crisis began has led a number of companies to start or extend stock repurchase programs hoping to snatch their own shares at bargain prices. The companies believe the buy-backs will bolster the value of the stocks in a stock market that has been battered by Mideast events and investor fears of a recession.

But some companies that had announced buy-back programs stayed away yesterday because of the market rally.

Russell Banks, chief executive officer of Grow Group Inc., a New York-based chemical concern, said the board approved a 750,000-share buy-back in response to the market drop, but no shares had been purchased because of the rally. The shares closed yesterday at $7.50, up 37.5 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading; in July, they traded as high as $10.

Nevertheless, companies continued to take advantage of deflated prices yesterday. Liz Claiborne Inc., a New York apparel retailer, expanded its buy-back and National Pizza Co., a fast-food chain based in Pittsburg, Kan., said yesterday it would begin one.

"We think it's a good use of our available cash," said Sam Miller, chief financial officer for Liz Claiborne, which is planning to buy back as much as an additional $50 million in stock. "This should improve our earnings per share and improve the stock price for our remaining shareholders."

Liz Claiborne closed at $27.50 yesterday, up $1.75, in national over-the-counter trading. Before the market rebounded yesterday, Liz Claiborne stock closed at $25.75 a share Friday, down from $30.75 a share Aug. 1, the day before Iraq invaded Kuwait.

Since Aug. 1, the Dow Jones Industrial Average had declined 366 points before rebounding Monday.

National Pizza also took advantage of deflated prices to initiate its buy-back and in turn watch its stock rise yesterday. In national over-the-counter trading, National Pizza closed at $17.75, up from $17 a share Friday, but still down from $21 a share on Aug. 1.

"At recent price levels our common stock represents an excellent investment," said Gene Bicknell, chief executive of National Pizza, which is planning to repurchase as many as one million shares, or 7.1% of its common outstanding.

Liz Claiborne and National Pizza join more than 50 companies that have announced repurchase programs in the last week.

Among others, A.O. Smith Corp., a maker of auto, truck and farm equipment, dusted off its 1987 authorization last week, to repurchase $7.5 million in stocks while Hasbro Inc., a Pawtucket, R.I., toymaker, said its board approved the buy-back of as many as three million shares.

Intel Corp., citing the market's "rapid retreat" and an opportunity to buy back shares at "attractive levels," announced Aug. 23 that it would buy back 20 million shares.

Since the announcement, Intel stock rose to $36.25 a share at close of national over-the-counter trading yesterday. That was up from $32.125 a share before the announcement, but still down from $42.50 a share Aug. 1.

Of course not all companies have the financial wherewithal to take advantage of deflated stock prices. "Not everyone has the flexibility to do it," said Liz Claiborne's Mr. Miller.

Other companies that are extending or starting buy-back programs include Nalco Chemical Co., Naperville, Ill. (one million additional shares); PacificCare Health Systems Inc., Cypress, Calif. (500,000 shares); and Dress Barn Inc., Stamford, Conn. (two million shares)

Yesterday, Jan Bell Marketing Inc., a Sunrise, Fla., designer and maker of jewelry, said it will buy back as much as 10% of the 22.6 million shares outstanding, while First Chattanooga Financial Corp., a thrift holding company in Chattanooga, Tenn., said its board approved spending an additional $2.5 million on top of $1.5 million to repurchase stock. Blount Inc., a manufacturing and construction concern, said it also plans to buy back as many as 300,000 Class A common shares, or 4% of shares outstanding.
