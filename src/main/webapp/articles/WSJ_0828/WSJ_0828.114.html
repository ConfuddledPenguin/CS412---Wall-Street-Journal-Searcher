What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/28/90

STOCKS AND BONDS soared and oil prices plummeted on the prospect of a peaceful solution to the Mideast crisis. The Dow Jones industrials leaped 78.71 points, this year's biggest gain, to 2611.63. Long-term Treasury bonds jumped more than 1 1/2 points, bringing their yield back under 9%. Crude prices sank as much as $4 a barrel, their sharpest drop on record. Early Tuesday in Vienna, OPEC ministers tentatively endorsed some members' moves to boost output. ---

Gold prices plunged about $27 an ounce in their sharpest one-day decline in more than seven years. Silver and platinum prices also sank. The recent Persian Gulf turmoil has generated only a modest increase in investor interest in gold in the U.S.

---

Tokyo stocks posted strong gains amid a firmer yen, a healthier bond market and an absence of bad Mideast news. The Nikkei index jumped 976 points Monday to 25141.76, then rose a further 518.52 Tuesday morning.

---

Many banks tightened their lending standards further in recent months, largely out of concern over the economy and the state of certain industries, a Fed survey found. Medium-sized firms felt the worst pinch. The report could help support an easing by the Fed of interest rates.

---

Consumer spending grew 0.5% in July, as personal income rose 0.6%. But fears about the economy may restrain purchases in the months ahead.

---

A tobacco antitrust award against Brown & Williamson was overturned. Brooke Group, formerly Liggett, had won the $148.8 million verdict.

---

Former Guinness PLC Chairman Ernest Saunders was convicted of conspiring to inflate the firm's stock price to help its $4.91 billion bid to take over Distillers Co. in 1986. Three co-defendants were also convicted in the case, which could boost the reputation of British financial regulators.

---

An insider-trading ruling that the government claims could hinder prosecutions will get a rare review by a full federal appeals court.

---

Stock buy-back programs are being launched or extended by many companies that hope to snatch their own shares at bargain prices.

---

Investors seeking arbitration of disputes with brokers will have to pay higher fees to launch their claims. The NASD has already imposed the increases, and the Big Board is expected to follow suit within weeks.

---

General Motors plans to triple the number of its U.S.-built cars with air bags as standard equipment. GM has trailed its U.S. rivals in this area.

---

Merrill Lynch will get $17.5 million from its insurers under a settlement of suits brought by the firm's shareholders on its behalf. The case involved trading and investment losses.

---

Tracor Holdings proposed to restructure $730 million of its debt and said it may seek protection from creditors if the plan isn't approved.

---

The IMF may lend more money to Brazil and other developing nations that import oil, a Treasury aide said.

---

General Cinema posted a 78% drop in profit for the quarter that ended in July. The theater operator and retailer cited lower movie attendance and reduced interest income.
