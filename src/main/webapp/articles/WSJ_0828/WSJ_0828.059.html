IMF May Lend More to Countries That Import Oil

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 08/28/90

WASHINGTON -- Because of higher world oil prices, the International Monetary Fund may lend more money to Brazil and some other developing countries that import oil, a senior Treasury official told reporters. "The question of access {to IMF resources} may need to be looked at," the official said. He stressed that no decisions have been reached, and he made it clear that any broadening of access to IMF resources wouldn't match the special IMF lending that followed the severe oil price rises of 1973-1974 and 1979.

Despite a sharp decline yesterday, oil prices remain about 40% above their level of six months ago. According to some estimates, Brazil's oil import bill may rise by as much as $2 billion a year because of the higher prices, the official said.

Brazil currently is seeking loans from the IMF to support economic reforms. It also is talking informally with a few of its biggest commercial bank creditors; the talks, which are expected to end by Sept. 14, are designed to enable the country's biggest lenders to tell Brazil what they want from a bank debt settlement and to learn more about the country's controversial economic reforms. Formal negotiations between Brazil and its commercial bank creditors are expected to begin this fall in New York.

The current exploratory talks "will probably help in getting a deal done later," the Treasury official said. Brazil hasn't paid interest on about $70 billion of its medium-term foreign bank debt for more than a year.

Of all Latin American countries, Brazil is probably being hit hardest by Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait and the subsequent international embargo on trade with those countries. Brazil is a large importer of oil -- particularly Iraqi oil -- and Iraq has been an important market for Brazilian airplanes, tanks, and other goods.

The crisis in the Persian Gulf won't affect the administration's Enterprise Initiative for the Americas, the Treasury official said. The Treasury is preparing legislation to back up that initiative, which would offer Latin American nations closer trade relations, cut the relatively small loans they owe the U.S. government, and attempt to boost foreign investment in the region. The legislation will be "prepared and ready to go to the Hill when Congress gets back in session," the Treasury official said.

Separately, the official said World Bank Senior Vice President Ernest Stern will join the new European Bank for Reconstruction and Development, the London-based development bank that will lend to Eastern Europe and the Soviet Union. Mr. Stern will be the senior U.S. official in that institution and is expected to fill the No. 2 job there under the bank's chairman, Jacques Attali, personal adviser to French President Francois Mitterrand.
