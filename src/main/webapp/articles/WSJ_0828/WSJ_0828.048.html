Economy: Banks Toughened Lending Standards In Recent Months, Fed's Survey Shows

  • Author: Paul Duke Jr.
  • Date: 08/28/90

WASHINGTON -- Banks continued to tighten their lending standards in recent months, largely out of foreboding over the economic future and the state of certain industries, a Federal Reserve Board survey of top lending officers found. The results of the August survey may give the Federal Reserve ammunition to ease interest rates slightly. The most recent rate cut, engineered by the Fed in July, came after Fed Chairman Alan Greenspan said he was worried that banks might be turning away some worthy borrowers, thus putting an unnecessary drag on the economy. But as reported, last week's meeting of Fed policy makers apparently gave Mr. Greenspan leeway to reduce short-term rates if the financial markets settle down.

In the Fed's survey, the first since a May report that also found tightening credit standards, bankers reported that they had tightened standards for loans not related to mergers, with medium-sized businesses feeling the worst pinch. Fully 43% of the 60 domestic banks surveyed said they had toughened their criteria for lending to medium-sized companies, while more than a third reported more stringent hurdles for large and small borrowers. The Fed defines medium-sized companies as those with annual sales of between $50 million and $250 million.

No banks reported easing standards for business borrowers of any size.

Amid the current real estate slump in nearly every region of the country, the survey found bankers again toughening their criteria for commercial real estate loans. About 75% of the banks said they had imposed stiffer standards for loans to finance commercial office buildings. In the May survey, 80% said they had tightened those standards.

However, few banks appear to be forcing consumers applying for home mortgages to meet tougher requirements. Only six banks said they had tightened their standards since May, and one bank reported easing.

More than one-third of the banks cited a "less favorable economic outlook" as their main reason for tightening lending standards. The survey was taken from mid-July until Aug. 1. Since then, many economists have begun to fear that the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait will further hurt the economy.

About 20% said "industry-specific problems" were a factor in the toughened standards, and about 15% noted "regulatory pressures." The banks didn't identify which industries presented problems.

The Fed does about six surveys of bank lending each year.
