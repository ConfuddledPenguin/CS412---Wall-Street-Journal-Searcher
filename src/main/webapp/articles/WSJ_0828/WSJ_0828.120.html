Heard on the Street: Cineplex Odeon Lures Investors Amid Signs Of Rebound, but Firm Has Long Way to Go

  • Author: Suzanne McGee
  • Date: 08/28/90

TORONTO -- A glimmer of a turnaround is sparking investors' interest in Cineplex Odeon, the beleaguered movie-theater chain. Even its diehard fans expect no near-term miracles from this battered stock. New management is shouldering heavy debts left behind by Cineplex's founder and former chairman Garth Drabinsky, who was forced out in December. But as the new team goes about its task of slashing debt, paring expenses and selling assets, a few investors are warming to the recession-defying potential of the company's line of business.

"People are always going to go to movies and buy popcorn and sodas," says Henry Bonis, a New York businessman and Cineplex investor.

Mr. Bonis says he owns about 50,000 shares, many of them acquired in recent weeks. "I've seen their theaters, I have confidence in the new management," he says. "It's going to take a year or so, but they'll get back in the black."

Cineplex closed yesterday at $2.50 a share, unchanged, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Although many securities analysts still don't recommend buying Cineplex shares, some, such as Lisbeth Barron, vice president of S.G. Warburg, say they are contemplating changing their recommendations to "hold" from sell. "This is a turnaround that's beginning to work," Ms. Barron says. "It just remains to be seen whether they can keep it going."

Investors give good reviews to the company's first-half operating performance. More people are walking through the doors of Cineplex movie theaters, they say, pointing to a 15.7% increase from a year earlier in first-half admissions revenue. And Cineplex far outstripped the industry average increase of only 0.4% for such revenue in the period.

"If somebody has the patience to wait it out, he could make a bundle on this stock," said Ernest Camponegro, a New York investment executive who has bought Cineplex stock in recent weeks and recommends it to his clients. "How many stocks are there with this kind of visibility, with a business that's a real cash cow, backed by major shareholders, that trades at $3?"

Reflecting its former expansion spree, Cineplex had $479.4 million in long-term debt at midyear, down from $578.1 million a year earlier. Besides using $75 million from asset sales to cut debt, Cineplex's president and chief executive, Allen Karp, is hoping to refinance, whittling away at interest expense. "We're looking at every way of doing this," he said. "We're in a process that could take several months."

There's little to cheer investors in the company's first-half net loss, which widened to the equivalent of $1.06 a share from 59 cents a share a year earlier. But in terms of cash flow, Cineplex had a net outflow of $13.2 million in the six months, narrower than the $30.6 million outflow a year earlier. After cutting general and administrative expenses 28% in the second quarter to $5.9 million, Mr. Karp expects to trim perhaps 10% to 20% more in the second half.

Investors are heartened by the presence of Cineplex's two controlling shareholders, MCA of Universal City, Calif., and interests associated with Montreal financier Charles Bronfman. If Cineplex fails to meet an agreement with its banks to raise $200 million through asset sales by year-end, MCA and Mr. Bronfman's Claridge Investments have agreed to chip in $50 million each to help cut debt; Cineplex presumably could turn to them for additional support if necessary.

Cineplex's theaters are in prime locations, such as New York, and as a result the company may have bargaining clout with studios over rising costs. A growing supply of new films, including about seven releases from Columbia Pictures Entertainment Co., will help boost admissions and concession revenue in the second half, Cineplex says.

But this month saw the collapse of a $31.8 million plan to sell some of Cineplex's U.S. theaters to Plitt Amusement Co., Los Angeles. Bankers are believed to have been reluctant to finance an acquisition valued at eight times cash flow. Mr. Karp insists: "We will not dispose of assets except at fair value. We're taking a firm stand and I have the strength of our shareholders {MCA and Claridge} behind me on this." He says a sale might still be completed, possibly at a lower price and with Cineplex retaining some of the theaters.

The Plitt affair suggests how hard it is to sell assets when the movie industry is already what industry people call "overscreened." As Mr. Karp puts it, "We couldn't have picked a worse time to restructure the company, from an industry standpoint." Cineplex may be "out of the woods," but the executive says he doesn't think "people should be throwing parties quite yet."
