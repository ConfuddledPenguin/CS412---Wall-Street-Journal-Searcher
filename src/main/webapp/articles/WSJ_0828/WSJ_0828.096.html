Education: Verbal Skills Slip as SAT Scores Fall

  • Author: Gary Putka
  • Date: 08/28/90

Students' weakening verbal skills pulled down scores on the Scholastic Aptitude Test for the third straight year, heightening worries about course content and trends in reading habits. Students headed for college this fall achieved an average combined mathematics and verbal score of 900, down from 903 last year. All of the decline was in the verbal score, which fell to 424, down three points from last year and tied for the worst performance ever, recorded in 1980 and 1981. Each component in the test has a point scale of 200 to 800, for a combined maximum of 1,600.

Some observers were particularly dismayed because the decline occurred despite a stable participation rate of about 40% of high school seniors, negating past explanations for the downward trend.

"We can no longer get away with saying scores are going down because the number of kids taking the SAT is going up, reducing the talent pool," said Lynne Cheney, chairman of the National Endowment for the Humanities. "I suspect the reason scores are going down is for all the other reasons: textbooks that are still dumbed-down, teachers who are not as prepared as they should be and curricula that are not as demanding as they should be."

The College Board, which sponsors the SAT, attributed the continuing verbal score decline to an erosion of reading time. Donald Stewart, president of the New York organization, said "reading is in danger of becoming a lost art among too many American students."

The College Board noted that the National Assessment of Educational Progress, a nationally representative test for students at three grade levels, concluded earlier this year that students spend little time reading and writing both in and out of school. Mr. Stewart said one reason was a preoccupation with television and video games.

Even some past defenders of schools' performance reacted soberly to the latest figures.

"Nationally, there's weakness in reading, and it's going to hurt us down the road," said Bill Honig, California's school superintendent. "We have to revamp the curriculum toward more writing, more problem-solving and more literature."

Mr. Honig has said that school critics aren't seeing the whole picture in the SAT scores. In California, he says, the major ethnic groups whose scores are broken out by the College Board -- whites, blacks, Asians and Hispanics -- all have shown score improvement of 3% to 4% annually since 1983. Pulling down the overall average is the higher participation of blacks and Hispanics, whose scores are much lower than whites.

The results also yielded a mixed message on new state education laws in the 1980s. Many of these dictated a higher graduation standard that included more so-called "academic" subjects, including mathematics, science, social studies and English, and fewer vocational electives, physical education and other courses.

The College Board said that students who took more than 20 high school courses had average scores 50 points higher than students who took fewer than 20 such courses. But the proportion of students with 20 or more academic courses increased to 40% from 39% last year -- and scores still declined.

Gordon Ensign, supervisor of assessment for schools in Washington state, said one possible explanation was a decline in the reading and writing content of courses that some systems count as academic. He said that high school journalism and film classes are increasingly counted as "academic" curriculum, although they make far fewer demands on students to understand literature.
