Less M.B.A. Demand As '80s Tide Ebbs

EAGER corporate climbers are still mobbing business schools' front doors, but demand at the other end -- for new M.B.A.s -- looks as though it's slowing.

Several top schools report that recruiting fell off this past spring, as some companies lowered hiring goals and strung along prospects longer. As a result, job hunts for many graduates "were much more stretched out and tougher to manage," says Jeanne J. Husain, director of placement at the University of Chicago's Graduate School of Business.

And when decisions did come in, "people tended not to sit on their offers as long as they had in the past," adds James J. Beirne of the University of Pennsylvania's Wharton School, where 309 companies came calling this academic year, down from 335 a year earlier.

The biggest slide occurred among struggling computer makers; at Carnegie Mellon's business school, Apple Computer Inc., Data General Corp. and Digital Equipment Corp. canceled campus interviews this year, while International Business Machines Corp. and Hewlett-Packard Inc. halved the number of jobs they offered. "We were just trying to match our recruiting efforts with what we felt our anticipated needs would be," says Hewlett-Packard's R. Kemp Bohlen.

The slump in the real estate industry and among Wall Street investment banks also hurt. Merrill Lynch & Co., for one, hired 49 new M.B.A.s this spring, down from 62 last year.

Recruiting activity wasn't down universally. Commercial banks, consumer-products makers and consulting firms maintained strong interest in M.B.A.s.

At the University of Rochester's Simon School of Business, the number of recruiting companies even rose -- to 109 from 101 last year. The school credits tougher application rules that have boosted the work experience -- and allure -- of its graduates. But the news isn't all good. Starting pay for Simon graduates rose just 3.2%, to nearly $44,500, following a 9.2% jump last year.

Widely Used Test Gets

More Flunking Grades

AFTER BEING used to screen millions of job applicants in recent decades, a government workplace test is rapidly falling out of favor.

The U.S. Department of Labor is proposing suspension of the General Aptitude Test Battery, which it developed in the 1940s for state and local employment offices. The offices have used the test to help place people in government and private jobs, including blue-collar positions with many Fortune 500 companies.

Criticism of the test began in the 1980s, after the department implemented a new grading system that effectively boosted the scores -- and job chances -- of minorities. The Justice Department challenged that arrangement, charging it could result in reverse discrimination against whites. Last year, the National Research Council endorsed the scoring practice, but questioned the GATB's structure and validity.

At its height five years ago, the exam was taken by nearly two million people annually in 44 states. Some 35 states were using it when the suspension was proposed last month, but a handful have since dropped it and more may follow. Pending a final decision by year end, the department is planning to review the exam -- and perhaps overhaul it.

"We're going to mount a vigorous look at what this instrument is doing in today's labor market," says Robert J. Litman, deputy director of the department's Employment Service.

Government job offices have other tests to gauge skills like typing, but without the GATB they are expected to rely more on those mainstays of assessment, interviews and reference checks.

Where the Jobs Are:

At Troubled Firms

AS SAVINGS and loans fail and leveraged buy-outs go sour, the demand for executives to help run troubled companies is growing.

This year, says Windle Priem, a recruiter with Korn/Ferry International, half his searches involve distressed institutions, mostly banks and thrifts. That's up from 20% two years ago. But the industry's turmoil is reducing executives' enthusiasm for moving. As a result, Mr. Priem adds, troubled organizations are successful only if they offer prospects a higher-ranked position, a minimum 30% increase in cash compensation, "three or four times" annual salary in stock options and an employment contract.

Some look for restless targets, someone "with a troubled company himself, or one of hundreds of senior staff people" made redundant by mergers, says J. Gerald Simmons, president of recruiter Handy Associates.

When recruiting new managers for Western Union Corp., which is embroiled in labor disputes and has flirted with a bankruptcy-law filing, Chairman Robert Ammon sought risk-takers. "I tended to take people who started their careers in large companies, but who were also {heads} of start-ups . . . and maybe had some experiences that got their noses bloodied a bit."
