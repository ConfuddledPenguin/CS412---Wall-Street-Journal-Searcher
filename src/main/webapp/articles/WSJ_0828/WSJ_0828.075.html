Phone, Phone on the Range: Now, It's Simpler to Call on the Hoof

  • Author: Pamela J. Podger
  • Date: 08/28/90

Cowpokes who ride tall in the saddle can now phone home without having to dismount. Three new pay phones, 80 inches from hoof to coin slot, were installed recently at the Lazy E Ranch just north of Oklahoma City, which plays host to the World Championship Quarter Horse Show each November. Spending 25 cents while astride a favorite steed should give new meaning to the term quarter horse, suggests St. Louis-based Southwestern Bell Telephone Co., a unit of Southwestern Bell Corp.

Convenience for cowhands is just part of Southwestern Bell's plan to go beyond its 187,000 traditional, 54-inch-high public phones.

This is the target market: active Americans who can't be bothered to move too many muscles to make a call.

Near the 15th tee at the Westwood Park Golf Course in Norman, Okla., is Southwestern Bell's first 50-inch-high Phone From Golf Cart, a boon for beeper-carrying doctors and executives whose athletic skills don't include the sprint, or bailing out of a golf cart for that matter.

"Golfers would have a heart attack if they had an emergency and had to run all the way back to our clubhouse," says Westwood golf pro David Lisle.

Along the Arkansas River is the Sooner State's only Phone From Bike. Without resort to a kickstand, bikers can dial up on a phone perched 50 inches above the bike path. And soon to be shipped are 36-inch-high phones for lakes and reservoirs in Southwestern Bell's five-state region, where boaters will be able to make calls without disembarking.

Ergonomical public phones have already gotten good reception. Southwestern Bell and other Baby Bells have been putting in pay phones with extra-long cords for drivers in cars and trucks for years.

But the trend's latest extension doesn't ring true for some beneficiaries.

"That's a curious adaptation of America's drive-through" tradition, says a spokesman for the Bicycle Federation, a Washington group promoting "a bicycle-friendly society." He isn't impressed: "People don't usually lose their balance and fall off the trail when they make phone calls astride their bikes."

Other telephone companies, too, are skeptical. "We just don't have enough horses," says an Ameritech spokesman in Chicago, who adds it isn't customizing phone booths "for the Guinness Book of World Records."
