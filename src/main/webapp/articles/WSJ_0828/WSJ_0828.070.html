International: Ireland's Parliament to Act On Bill to Aid Beef Firms

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/28/90

DUBLIN -- Prime Minister Charles Haughey called an emergency session of parliament for today to act on legislation designed to protect troubled beef producers from creditors. Ireland sends 44% of its beef exports to the Middle East and North Africa, with most of that going to Iraq, Iran and Egypt. But recent restrictions on shipments to the Persian Gulf have cut sharply into Ireland's beef exports.

One person who would benefit most from the proposed legislation is Irish beef baron Larry Goodman, who is responsible for about 50% of the country's annual 1 billion Irish pound ($1.72 billion) beef trade. Iraq alone owes Mr. Goodman about 180 million pounds.

The question is how big a salvage operation the Irish government is willing to undertake to save Mr. Goodman, Europe's largest meat processor.

Mr. Goodman's debts are said to be at least 50 million Irish pounds more than his assets. He owes up to 500 million pounds to 33 banks in Ireland, Europe, Japan, South America and Australia, and is believed to need at least 200 million pounds to back his day-to-day operations over the next few months.

The Irish government isn't expected to have any trouble getting the new legislation passed. The measure, which is modeled on Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code, provides for a court-appointed examiner to oversee financially ailing companies for three months.

Even Labor Party leader Dick Spring, who called the legislation a bill to protect rich men from their bankers, said Mr. Goodman "is too big a player to allow the banks to walk in and destroy him."
