Monday's Markets: Stocks, Bonds Soar on Optimism For Peaceful Settlement in Mideast

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 08/28/90

NEW YORK -- Investors went on a stock-and-bond buying spree, cheered by rising optimism for a peaceful solution to the Mideast crisis. The Dow Jones Industrial Average leaped 78.71 points -- the biggest this year -- in moderately active trading to close at 2611.63. The gain followed a 49.50-point increase Friday. The advance for the past two sessions totals 128.21 points, or 5.2%, cutting the market's decline since Iraq invaded Kuwait to 10%.

Bond prices jumped sharply before the U.S. stock market opened and stayed high, largely on positive Mideast news and falling oil prices. The dollar continued its decline; many traders bought yen because the drop in oil prices helps the economy of energy-importing Japan.

Adding to rallies in bonds and stocks were big declines in gold and oil prices: The October futures contract for crude oil sank $4 a barrel to close at $26.91. Gold plunged $26.70 an ounce to $388.30, the steepest one-day decline in more than seven years.

Investors were emboldened by the fact that war didn't break out in the Mideast over the weekend and by news that Iraq agreed to meet with the United Nations' secretary general Thursday, rekindling hopes for a diplomatic settlement. The perception of easing Mideast tensions first sparked rallies in stock markets overseas. Those gains, in turn, helped extend the bargain-hunting frenzy that began in the U.S. Friday.

Analysts discounted the influence on the stock and bond markets of a report that the Federal Reserve is ready to cut interest rates to prevent the U.S. from slipping into a recession. Byron Wien, chief U.S. investment strategist at Morgan Stanley, said the failure of war to erupt cleared the way for investors to pick up where they left off Friday.

Since the sharp decline in prices Thursday, stocks have remained relatively cheap, Mr. Wien said. Combined with the positive tenor of news out of the Mideast, those low prices looked especially attractive yesterday, he said.

"The embargo seems to be working," Mr. Wien said, "and it looks like we're moving toward a negotiated settlement. The market interprets anything other than shooting as positive."

Marjorie Corp, fund manager at One Federal Asset Management, Boston, said that at first the rally "appeared to be concentrated in the names that were strong before the invasion. We're not seeing any new direction or leadership." Indeed, among yesterday's big gainers were PepsiCo, Coca-Cola, Procter & Gamble, Johnson & Johnson and Merck -- stocks of consumer companies that many investors consider strong enough to withstand severe profit erosions should a recession materialize.

But the rally broadened during the day and by the close most stock-market barometers registered proportionally similar gains.

"What propelled the buying," said Ted Eggert, equity research manager at Yamaichi International, "were plunging oil prices, rising bond prices and all these tips and clues about a lowering temperature in the Middle East."

Brian Fabbri, chief economist at Midland Montagu Economics, noted that bond prices took off initially in Japan, helped by the rebound in the Japanese stock market.

Part of what boosted bond prices further throughout the day was the positive effect of investors buying back bonds they had sold Friday as a hedge against potentially plunging prices if hostilities had broken out in the Mideast over the weekend. But the major impetus was falling oil and gold prices. Long-term U.S. Treasury bond prices rose more than 1 1/2 points, or more than $15 for each $1,000 of face amount, in light trading.

"I'm just amazed with gold down as much as it was that bonds didn't go even higher," said Roberta Olson, fixed income portfolio manager at Banc One Asset Management, Columbus, Ohio.

While some analysts expected the report of imminent interest-rate easing in the U.S. to hurt the dollar, not all traders paid that much attention to it. Ian Foakes, foreign exchange manager at PaineWebber International, said the report had no influence on his trading activity. Most of the day's activity was focused on selling Swiss francs and marks to buy yen. That move was a bet that oil prices will fall, giving the import-dependent Japanese economy a lift.

"Frankly," Mr. Foakes said, "the currency market is trading like a second-stage-of-development animal -- it's all herd instinct."

In major market action:

Stock prices rose sharply. Volume was 160.2 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange, where advancing issues far outnumbered decliners, 1,453 to 256.

Bond prices jumped. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell to 8.99%.

The dollar fell. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.5510 marks and 144.25 yen, compared with 1.5605 marks and 146.70 yen Friday.
