Liggett Verdict Is Set Aside By U.S. Judge --- Brown & Williamson Case Involved Discrimination In Pricing of Cigarettes

  • Author: Wayne E. Green
  • Date: 08/28/90

A federal judge threw out a $148.8 million jury verdict Liggett Group Inc. won against Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp. In awarding the treble damages last March, a jury in Greensboro, N.C., had upheld Liggett's claim that B&W engaged in predatory price discrimination in the market for low-priced, generic cigarettes in 1984 and 1985. Liggett changed its name to Brooke Group Ltd. last month.

But U.S. District Judge Frank W. Bullock Jr. ruled yesterday in Greensboro that Liggett hadn't proved that B&W's aggressive pricing policies had damaged competition in violation of federal antitrust laws. Moreover, he suggested that companies must meet a stiff burden of proof whenever they rely on antitrust laws to attack price cutting by competitors.

"The antitrust laws' goal is to promote consumer welfare, not to discourage aggressive price competition," Judge Bullock said.

Liggett said it was "disappointed" with the decision and believes there are grounds for appeal.

The case stems from Liggett's highly successful strategy of using volume rebates to sell generic cigarettes in the early 1980s. By 1984, it was siphoning thousands of smokers away from other manufacturers and wreaking particular havoc on B&W. B&W fought back with its own volume rebate plan, giving rise to Liggett's allegations that B&W engaged in a predatory pricing campaign designed to "kill" the generic cigarette category.

The jury agreed that B&W hadn't fought fair, ordering it to pay Liggett $49.6 million for violations of the Robinson-Patman Act. The total is automatically tripled under the law to $148.8 million.

But Judge Bullock said the Robinson-Patman Act bars only the kind of price discrimination which may "substantially" lessen competition. In this case, involving a national market, B&W could only do that if it were in a position to control prices on a nationwide basis, the judge asserted. And he concluded that B&W was incapable of exercising such control.

"With at most 12% of the domestic cigarette market, B&W as a matter of law could not exercise market power unilaterally in either the whole cigarette market or the generic segment," Judge Bullock ruled.

Nor would the judge accept Liggett's contention that B&W exercised market control by acting in "tacit collusion" with other cigarette manufacturers. He said Liggett's own executives had testified at trial that there wasn't any such collusion.

In its statement on the decision, Liggett said it was only one of the victims of B&W's unfair competition, asserting that the others are "consumers who we still feel have been and will continue paying higher prices because of B&W's actions."

But B&W countered by saying the ruling underscores what is supposed to happen in a free enterprise system. It said in a statement that its introduction of generic cigarettes created "more product choice for consumers" and generated "aggressive price competition" in the industry.

Martin London, an attorney for B&W, called the decision a "ringing endorsement" of price competition. "It establishes that companies aren't going to be able to threaten competitors with antitrust suits merely because those competitors want to sell at low prices," said Mr. London of the New York law firm Paul, Weiss, Rifkind, Wharton & Garrison. "It says low prices are good, not bad."

On a separate issue in the case, Judge Bullock refused Liggett's request for a new trial on the question of whether B&W had infringed Liggett's trademark on design features of its cigarette packages. The jury had rejected Liggett's claim.
