Mutual Funds: Some Funds Fly High In a Holding Pattern

  • Author: Jonathan Clements
  • Date: 06/12/90

NEW YORK -- For a handful of funds, doing well consists of doing very little. Take Source Capital, a publicly traded fund managed by George Michaelis. This fund typically hangs on to a stock for three years or so, compared with 15 months for the average fund manager.

The result of all this inactivity? Mr. Michaelis's fund has returned 19.3% a year in the past decade, well ahead of the 17.7% notched up by the Standard & Poor's 500 stocks and 16% for the average stock fund.

The secret: Mr. Michaelis tries to buy low-debt companies with high, sustainable returns on equity that are trading below the market's multiple of earnings. And then he holds the stocks for a long time.

"If you're successful in identifying such businesses, you almost win by default," he says. "If you own a portfolio of companies that earn returns on equity of 20% or 25% over long periods of time, you can't help but be successful unless you pay an absurdly high price" for each stock.

This brand of buy-and-hold investing has been made respectable by the legendary value investor Warren Buffett, chairman of Berkshire Hathaway Inc., who is fond of saying, "Our favorite holding period is forever." Among Berkshire's core stockholdings are Coca-Cola Co. and Washington Post Co., both of which boast impressive returns on equity.

Mr. Michaelis got his stock picking education at the hands of Charles Munger, Berkshire's vice chairman and Mr. Buffett's longtime business partner. Shortly after Mr. Michaelis joined Source Capital in 1971, a Berkshire subsidiary bought 20% of Source's stock.

Why does Mr. Michaelis zero in on companies with high and sustainable returns on equity? Because a company with a high return can compound its retained earnings at a brisk pace. Even if a stock's multiple of earnings doesn't change, the stock's price will rise as retained earnings are compounded. A company with a steady 19% return on equity will double its earnings every four years if it plows back all profit into the business.

Mr. Michaelis has recently been buying stock of Minnesota Mining & Manufacturing Co. The company, whose long list of products include specialty chemicals, adhesive tape and printing plates, has averaged a return on equity of almost 21% a year in the past five years, but it trades at less than 15 times its trailing 12-month earnings.

What convinces Mr. Michaelis that Minnesota Mining can sustain that high return on equity? "A lot of companies have high profitability because they have one great business that carries the show," he says. "This is a very broadly based business with thousands of product lines. It doesn't have a franchise as clearly definable as Coca-Cola or Kellogg's corn flakes. The profitability is more the result of a high level of research and development."

Source Capital is a so-called closed-end fund. Unlike a regular mutual fund, a closed-end fund issues a fixed number of shares, which then trade publicly just like any other stock.

Source's shares closed yesterday at $41 in New York Stock Exchange composite trading, just barely below its portfolio value of $41.04 a share.

One of Mr. Michaelis's colleagues at Los Angeles's First Pacific Advisors, Christopher Linden, follows the same approach for the mutual fund he runs, the $60 million FPA Perennial. Among the stocks that Mr. Linden thinks are reasonably priced are Melville Corp., a specialty retailer, and Washington Federal Savings & Loan Association of Seattle, one of the best-capitalized thrifts in the country. Both trade at a discount to the market's price-earnings ratio of 17 but have five-year average returns on equity of well over 20%.

"Over a long period of time, shareholders' returns and those of the company are going to be similar," Mr. Linden says.

Other mutual funds also follow this buy-and-hold strategy. Despite the recent lackluster performance of small companies' stocks, GIT Equity Trust-Special Growth has posted impressive gains by buying and holding small company stocks. Over the past five years, the fund has had an average annual portfolio turnover of less than 26%, compared with 80% for the average stock mutual fund.

But in the category of snail-paced trading, Century Shares Trust takes the prize. The fund has averaged turnover of just 3.5% a year for the past decade. Century Shares, which concentrates its assets in insurance stocks with high returns on equity, is a firm favorite of Gerald Perritt, editor of the Mutual Fund Letter, a Chicago-based newsletter.

Mr. Perritt began recommending the fund in August 1988 because he thought the fund's holdings would benefit as interest rates fell and the cycle for property/casualty insurance rates turned upward.

But Mr. Perritt now figures that the fund is a good buy solely for its low-turnover investment strategy. "We're still looking at the potential for lower interest rates," he says. "And if you have a recession, the insurance stocks won't be affected like the cyclicals would be."
