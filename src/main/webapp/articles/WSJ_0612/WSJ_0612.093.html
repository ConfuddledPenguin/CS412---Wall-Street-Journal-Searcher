Labor Letter: A Special News Report on People And Their Jobs in Offices, Fields and Factories

  • Author: Albert R. Karr
  • Date: 06/12/90

WILL CLEANER AIR cost jobs or add them? The debate heats up as Congress moves to enact a major cleanup bill. The United Mine Workers sees lost jobs in high-sulfur coal areas, and steelmakers say the tough Senate bill could cost 25,000 jobs in closing most coke ovens by 2020. "We would be exporting pollution and the jobs" by buying coke abroad, says Bethlehem Steel. Chrysler sees eventual harm to employment.

But the work required to reduce acid rain will add 30,000 jobs, says Pure Air, an Allentown, Pa., firm involved in a joint Indiana venture. Use of more coal-consuming ethanol could add 2,000 jobs, says the Renewable Fuels Association. Westmoreland Coal and Walker Manufacturing may add jobs, for low-sulfur coal and auto catalytic-converter production.

Michael Silverstein, who edits an environmental-investing directory, says the bill moves work from polluters to "polluter cleaner-uppers."

HAPPY FATHER'S DAY. Now help out around the house, please.

Fully 86% of women and 84% of girls in a poll by Working Mother magazine and Walt Disney's Epcot Center say Dad, breadwinner or not, should help with household chores and child rearing; 69% of men and 64% of boys agreed. "America's view of a father's role . . . has shifted dramatically," says social psychologist Carin Rubenstein, on the poll of 2,962 adults and children.

Two-thirds of the women and 55% of men said the sexes can equally raise children. Only 38% of the boys believe women are as good as men at disciplining; 67% of girls think so. Dad doesn't do more with his children because he works hard, say 33% of men, 36% of boys, and 24% of women and girls. More women than men say he just doesn't want to get involved.

GIVE WORKERS MORE SAY, and they'll do more.

That's a key message of a report due next week by a 35-member Commission on the Skills of the American Work Force. The study of 400 employers will urge reorganizing work -- to emphasize teamwork, rigorous training and high skills, to be more competitive with foreign nations. But most U.S. firms opt for a doomed low-wage, rapid-turnover strategy, says Ira Magaziner, the panel's chairman.

Governments must require high-schoolers to meet firm educational standards and train non-college graduates better for job demands, says business strategist Magaziner. "The situation will get worse" without action, adds former Labor Secretary Ray Marshall, a commission co-chairman.

Teamwork urged by the panel would often be deemed cheating in the classroom, says Bill Brock, also a panel chief and former labor secretary.

JUSTICE DEPARTMENT sides with female workers and the Auto Workers union, challenging a Johnson Controls "fetal protection" policy that bars women of childbearing age from jobs exposing them to lead. In a Supreme Court brief, the department said Johnson didn't provide strong reasons for fertility-based discrimination.

A SIMPLE BINGO GAME it wasn't, the feds say, as a grand jury indicts six former or current Steelworkers union officials, five bingo-game operators and a Catholic priest, in Gary, Ind., for violations including illegal gambling and embezzling union funds. The Labor Department's inspector general and the IRS had investigated.

WORKERS URGED to strike last week in East Germany by retail, restaurant and other business groups responded mildly, with walkouts of only about an hour. The businesses are concerned that western competitors will grab big parts of their market after the two German states move in July toward merging economies.

IMMIGRANT NURSES involved in a strike against the Kaiser Permanente Health Plan in Los Angeles won't be deported, the Immigration and Naturalization Service said. The Service Employees union had sued, after the INS ruled in January that H-1, or temporary, workers who strike aren't maintaining required "status."

THE YOUTH SUPPLY shrinks for summer jobs, but more seekers often apply.

The 16-to-24-year-old force will reach 24 million in July, down 930,000 from last summer, the Bureau of Labor Statistics says, as that population narrows. Some hotel and resort owners say it's harder to find youngsters who want to work. But more-vigorous promotion of pay as high as $7 an hour and perks such as free bus service to work spurred 7,000 applications, up from 4,000 last year, for Six Flags Great Adventure, in Jackson, N.J.

Bids doubled for work at the Garden State Arts Center in Holmdel, N.J. A job fair produced the surge, helped by a slowing economy, officials say. Long Island's Town of Hempstead boosted starting pay for parks and beach workers to $4.50 an hour from $4, to compete with fast-food firms, and applications rose more than 10%.

Walt Disney World uses a three-day Food Feast to lure reluctant youths for food and drink service jobs.
