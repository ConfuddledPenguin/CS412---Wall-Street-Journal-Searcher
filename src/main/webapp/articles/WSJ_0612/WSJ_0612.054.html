Foreign Exchange: Dollar Rises Against Yen After Election But Falls in Relation to Other Currencies

  • Author: John Bader Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 06/12/90

NEW YORK -- Political news in Japan combined with technical factors to push the yen broadly lower against the dollar, but the U.S. currency ended lower against the other major currencies. Word that Japan's ruling Liberal Democratic Party lost a seat in the upper house of parliament to the socialists added steam to a yen sell-off already in progress as a result of the dollar's push above the 152.50yen level Friday, traders said.

Conversely, dealers said the U.S. currency weakened modestly against the mark and other European currencies after it failed to stay above 1.7000 marks following an attempt early in European trading to push it above that level. The dollar fell against the Canadian dollar following news that Canadian leaders had come to a preliminary agreement on that country's constitutional dispute.

The Liberal Democratic Party's loss came in a special election Sunday that was the first on a national level since an LDP majority was returned to the lower house of parliament in elections last February. The defeat brought out latent market nervousness about Japan's political stability, market participants said. The socialist candidate reportedly won the election on the strength of that party's opposition to a widely unpopular sales tax imposed last year by the LDP.

Support of the sales tax was considered one of the ruling party's greatest liabilities in elections earlier this year, and the socialist's victory on the back of opposition to the tax could signify that the issue might trouble the LDP in the future.

The market was already in a mood to sell the yen following the U.S. currency's break above technical resistance points Friday and a sharp drop yesterday of 453.11 points in Tokyo's Nikkei Stock Index, to 32540.18 points. Large movements in the Nikkei have attracted considerable market attention following a 23% plunge in the value of the index during the first quarter. The yen tends to shadow large movements on the Nikkei because the stock market is considered a barometer of foreign investors' confidence in Japan, traders said.

The dollar's current strength can be traced to the market's failure to push the currency below 150 yen last month, said Javier Santalla, a trader at Nippon Credit Bank in New York.

After a period of oscillation between 150.50 yen and 152 yen, the U.S. currency consolidated its position last week around 152.50 yen, persuading many by Friday to drop the long yen positions previously taken when it seemed the U.S. currency wouldn't rise much against the yen in the near term, Mr. Santalla said.

Late in New York trading, the dollar was quoted at 1.6930 marks, down from 1.6975 marks late Friday in New York, and at 154.77 yen, compared with 153.49 yen. Sterling was trading at $1.6860, up from $1.6845 late Friday.

In Tokyo Tuesday, the dollar was trading at 154.35 yen soon after opening at 154.38 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.6875 marks, down from Monday's Tokyo close of 1.6978 marks.

Following the dollar's break above 152.50 yen Friday, a new psychological target of 155 yen has been adopted by the market, with some participants calling for higher levels in the medium term, dealers said.

The strength of the dollar's upward momentum caused Japanese insurance companies and other large institutions to adjust their dollar hedges. They bought the U.S. currency in the belief that the dollar would remain steady at current levels or continue rising, said Morris Armstrong, vice president and manager of foreign exchange at Girozentrale Vienna in New York.

The hedges are maintained as a mechanism to minimize companies' potential losses on foreign investments as a result of daily fluctuations in foreign exchange rates.

The Canadian dollar gained against its U.S. counterpart after news that Prime Minister Brian Mulroney and provincial leaders had forged an agreement on the thorny issue of Quebec's constitutional status, traders said. However, market enthusiasm for the accord, which could lead to constitutional recognition of French-speaking Quebec's status as a "distinct society" within Canada, was limited by the prospect of continued wrangling over the agreement in provincial legislatures. The Canadian dollar finished at 85.38 U.S. cents, up from 85.18 cents Friday in New York.

The dollar's moves yesterday came amid a general lack of enthusiasm for aggressive position-taking ahead of this week's slew of U.S. economic indicators, Mr. Armstrong of Girozentrale and others said.

The market is awaiting the May reports on the U.S. consumer price index and producer price index for their possible interest rate implications, said Valerie Walker, vice president and corporate foreign exchange manager at Harris Trust and Savings Bank in New York.

Although market participants don't expect the Federal Reserve to change its current policy of maintaining steady interest rates, the fresh inflation figures will be an important factor in the formulation of traders' short-term outlooks on the U.S. economy, Ms. Walker said. Producer prices and consumer prices, scheduled for release Thursday and Friday respectively, are expected to have risen 0.2% in May, compared with a decline in April's PPI of 0.3% and rise in April's CPI of 0.2%, according to economists.

In Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $356.20 an ounce.
