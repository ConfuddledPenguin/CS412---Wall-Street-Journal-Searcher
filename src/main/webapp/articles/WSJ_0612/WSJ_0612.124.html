Foxboro Exploring `Strategic Alliance' With Other Firms

  • Author: William M. Bulkeley
  • Date: 06/12/90

FOXBORO, Mass. -- Foxboro Co. said it is "exploring alternatives" that could lead to the acquisition of some or all of its stock by another company. The maker of industrial process controls said it is considering "a possible strategic alliance with a limited number of firms which could add complementary strengths" to Foxboro's technology.

Officials of the company, which is struggling to turn around after losing money for several quarters and omitting its 10-cent quarterly dividend, declined further comment.

However, analysts noted that Foxboro's stock has risen in recent weeks due to speculation that it might become a takeover target. It closed yesterday at $30, up $1.50, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Robert Sullivan, an analyst with PaineWebber Inc., noted that about one-third of Foxboro's shares are held by the widely scattered descendants of the founding Bristol family. With the dividend omission, "probably some have been disillusioned," he said.

At its annual meeting in April, Foxboro added to its board two new directors representing a group that said it had acquired a 4.8% stake.

The new directors are Purnendu Chatterjee, president of Beall Technologies Inc., a North Bergen, N.J., computer parts maker, and Alan R. McFarland, partner in McFarland, Dewey & Co. a New York investment firm. The proxy statement identifies both as representatives of a group that also includes investor George Soros. Mr. Soros and Mr. Chatterjee were allied in two unsuccessful takeover attempts in the mid-1980s.

The proxy statement disclosed that the group holds a 4.8% stake and had agreed to limit its holdings to no more than 9.9% until the end of this year. It also said that Foxboro and the two new directors agreed that the men won't make public comments on Foxboro.

Foxboro has technologically advanced products and a good customer base, analysts say, but it has repeatedly posted disappointing earnings. Last year it reported a net loss of $4.6 million, or 37 cents a share, on sales of $597.8 million.

Mr. Sullivan of PaineWebber said he expects Foxboro's earnings to come back in the second half, resulting in net income of about 65 cents a share for the year and $1.75 a share next year. "My position is it's undervalued, either because of what it could earn a year or two out or because it could be taken over at a premium to the present price," he said.
