Freer Capital Flow Is Said To Aid World Economy

  • Author: null
  • Date: 06/12/90

BASEL, Switzerland -- The Bank for International Settlements, conducting its annual meeting here, said freer capital flows are helping avoid havoc from big trade imbalances in the world financial system. The BIS, which functions as a central bank for the world's major central banks, said the freer capital flow makes it easier for funds to flow to economies with large trade deficits, like the U.S., which posted a $104 billion current-account gap last year.

The BIS found that overall, capital flows increased in 1989, with the biggest investments pouring toward the 12 European Community countries. Total investment into the EC came to $63 billion in 1989, up from $47 billion a year earlier. Increased acquisitions by U.S. companies, along with greater investment by pension funds, insurance companies and unit trusts, accounted for much of the increase. Britain, perhaps the most open to foreign investors, won $26 billion of the investment last year, compared with $6 billion for West Germany.

The BIS said the most important developments of the decade were unexpected ones: the collapse of communism in Eastern Europe, and the durability of the economic expansion in the big industrial countries.
