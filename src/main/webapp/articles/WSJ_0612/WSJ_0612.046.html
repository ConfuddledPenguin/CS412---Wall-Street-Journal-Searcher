World Markets: Tokyo Share Prices Fall Back in Thin Trading; London Stocks Fail to Recover Ground at the Close

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 06/12/90

Stocks retreated sharply in Tokyo in thin dealings as a weaker yen and lower bond prices dried up buying interest. Meanwhile, stocks in London failed to recover ground at the close, despite a surge in the value of Wall Street shares.

Tokyo's Nikkei Index of 225 issues fell 453.11 points to 32540.18, the lowest daily close since May 24. The index lost 199.21 points Friday.

In early trading Tuesday in Tokyo, the Nikkei index had dropped 193.49 points to 32346.69.

While there wasn't any big-lot selling Monday, traders said, lack of buying and slight volume permitted small sales to pull down the Nikkei Index widely.

Index-linked selling by arbitragers and investment trust funds pushed down the index more than 300 points in the morning. The index declined further in the afternoon as the dollar rose above 154 yen.

The stronger dollar helped push up yields of the benchmark 10-year government bond, and the increase in long-term interest rates prompted sales of interest-rate sensitive stocks of companies such as steel makers, shipbuilders and real estate developers, observers said.

Only 350 million shares were estimated to have changed hands on the first section, down sharply from 675.5 million shares Friday. Losers outpaced winners 706 to 248, while 171 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues listed in the first section fell 18.77 points, or 0.78%, to 2393.01.

The second section index fell for the first time in 10 trading days. The index lost 12.12 points, or 0.30%, to 4056.95. Volume was estimated at 18 million shares, down from 28.5 million shares Friday.

Monday's sharp setback reflects investors' disappointment with the upturn of the dollar and domestic interest rates, said Hiroyuki Murai, general manager of stock trading at Nikko Securities.

While stock prices advanced in the past weeks because of expectations for lower interest rates, Mr. Murai said, participants learned such expectations were unfounded. Movements of interest rates need to be watched carefully, he added.

Economic reports scheduled to be disclosed later this week in Japan and the U.S. are expected to shed light on the direction of interest rates in each nation. The reports include the Bank of Japan's latest quarterly report and Japan's wholesale price index for May, as well as U.S. consumer and producer price data for May and trade figures for April.

Investors took a wait-and-see attitude ahead of these reports, traders said. But there is little expectation that the economic reports will encourage central banks of either nation to ease credit, the traders added.

Mr. Murai said he saw few investors trying to sell at Monday's low prices and predicted the Nikkei will hover around the day's closing level for a while.

But some observers said stock prices may fall further, with the Nikkei dropping below 32000.

Yoshiaki Mitsuoka, director of investment information at Daiwa Investment Trust & Management, said the advance of the dollar against the yen may be a short-term move, but it might have triggered the start of a major setback, often seen a few months after a plunge.

Since the Nikkei has continued to move higher for 10 weeks after posting a low of 28002 points April 2, "it's quite natural" to expect some setback in the next three to five weeks, the Daiwa official said. "In the long run, such a correction is welcome."

Some traders said another discouraging factor is that there are few attractively priced shares left because of the advances made recently.

Investors' enthusiasm to take wide and short-term profits had led to a rise of small-capital shares on the second section, over-the-counter trading, as well as the Osaka and Nagoya stock exchanges, in the past weeks. But stock prices in these categories also fell back.

Losers included shipbuilding companies, which had led the recent market advance. Hitachi Zosen fell 15 yen to 856 yen (10 cents to $5.58), while Mitsubishi Heavy Industries fell 20 yen to 1040 yen.

Steel shares were sold on higher interest rates, with Nippon Steel declining 11 yen to 597 yen.

London traders said that market opened weaker and continued to fall on disappointing retail sales figures. These reports showed that the volume of retail sales rose 1.2% in May largely because of increases in food and clothing sectors. The rise was unchanged from April's monthly increase and well above market expectations of a 0.4% rise.

The data suggest that current high interest rates aren't having the desired damping effect on consumer demand.

A lack of buy interest in quiet afternoon trading failed to bolster the market to match Wall Street's early climb. The London market "wasn't quite sure where New York" would close. Investors "didn't want to mark London higher," said one trader.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index fell 17.8 points to 2348.8. The index was off 28.1 points at the intraday low.

The Financial Times 30 Share Index dropped 14.8 points to 1877.9. Volume was 375.6 million shares, compared with 444.2 million issues Friday.

Traders said the recent euphoria appears to have evaporated, with the market now taking greater heed of negative economic data. There was still considerable buy interest, but investors are waiting for the remaining indicators to be disclosed as the week progresses, in the hope of securing better prices.

Overall, "people are getting cautious, they want to see some positive news," said one trader.

The insurance sector continued to benefit from bid speculation, with Commercial Union, up 6 pence to 517 pence (10 cents to $8.70), and Legal & General, up 5 pence at 418 pence, the favored takeover stocks.

ICI rose 6 pence to #12.22, on strong overseas demand, while Guinness gained 9 pence to 788 pence on LVMH's announcement that its tender for Guinness shares was oversubscribed.

British Steel eased 2 pence to 146 1/2 pence, despite full-year pretax profits up 24% at #733 million. This gain was broadly in line with market expectations, but one trader noted that the recent trend has been to sell on company results.

Meanwhile, shares closed higher in Madrid and lower in Stockholm, Paris, Brussels, Amsterdam, Milan, Frankfurt and Zurich. South African gold stocks rose.

Prices were lower in Hong Kong, Singapore, Seoul, Wellington and Taipei, and higher in Manila. Sydney was closed.
