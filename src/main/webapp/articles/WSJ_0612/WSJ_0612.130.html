Commodities: Pork Belly Prices Climb Daily Limit on Estimates More Frozen Bellies Were Shipped Than Expected

  • Author: Scott McMurray
  • Date: 06/12/90

Pork belly prices closed up the daily permissible limit of two cents a pound in response to estimates that more frozen bellies were taken out of storage last week than had been anticipated, analysts said. Live hog futures initially traded lower, but responded to the rally in pork bellies to close higher for the day.

Pork belly futures for July delivery closed up two cents a pound at 69.75 cents on the Chicago Mercantile Exchange, and July hogs closed up 0.77 cent a pound at 63.10 cents.

"We're within shooting distance of the contract high of 71.60" cents a pound for the July pork belly contract, said Jerry Abbenhaus, a futures analyst with A.G. Edwards & Sons in St. Louis.

Pork bellies shipped from warehouses outside of Chicago to meatpacking plants totaled as much as three million pounds, said Thomas Morgan, president of Sterling Research Corp., Arlington Heights, Ill. That's nearly three times the amount that would normally be shipped in a week this time of year from outside of Chicago warehouses as well as those in the Chicago area, Mr. Morgan said.

Summertime demand accounted for a portion of the increase in shipments, as did relatively light hog slaughters recently, analysts said. Prices for fresh pork bellies recently exceeded prices for frozen bellies, which also fueled the belly futures price increase, analysts said.

In other commodity markets yesterday:

COCOA: Futures prices plunged again, accelerating the decline that began in late May. The July contract dropped $54 a metric ton to $1,181, as speculators liquidated positions taken in an effort to capitalize on the political upheavals in the Ivory Coast, the world's largest cocoa producer. Despite earlier concerns, the upheaval "didn't affect cocoa or alter the overwhelming supply situation that had been such a depressant on price previously," said William O'Neill of Merrill Lynch Futures in New York. As this becomes clearer, he said, prices are "simply reflecting the prevailing conditions." Sandra Kaul, analyst for Shearson Lehman Hutton, said there was little market support for cocoa prices "because dealers had bought significant quantities of the beans earlier when prices were lower, and they have little incentive to buy at this time." At the same time, she said, rain in West Africa has improved crop prospects there, and the Brazil summer crop will soon be arriving on the market -- both factors that depress prices, she said. Ms. Kaul also cited continuing concern that the Soviet Union, because of credit problems, may have to forgo accepting delivery of cocoa it has previously bought. If that happened, the cocoa could end up being offered for sale on the world market, she said.

ENERGY: U.S. crude prices for July closed slightly higher after traders seemed to digest, but disregard, several tidbits of news. A fire continued to burn aboard a Norwegian tanker in the Gulf of Mexico, but the market apparently accepted Coast Guard predictions that the tanker, which has 38.2 million gallons of light crude, wouldn't sink. Prices were apparently hurt in the U.S. and in Asia by reports that Japan, a large petroleum importer, is counting on significant additional discounts from some members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries. Meanwhile, Exxon Corp. said it would reopen its Linden, N.J., refinery by mid-July; Norway's oil workers threatened to strike July 1, and a major Alaskan pipeline will be closed today so that corrosion can be repaired. Traders took all the news in and the result was that West Texas Intermediate -- like crudes for the July contract closed on the New York Mercantile Exchange at $16.82 a barrel, up four cents a barrel. Products finished the session lower.

COPPER: Futures prices declined in the wake of news that the second of three major U.S. copper mining companies had reached a new labor agreement. The July contract ended with a loss of 2.10 cents a pound at $1.0965. A spokesman for Phelps Dodge Corp., in Phoenix, Ariz., said the company had come to a tentative agreement with the United Steelworkers union; the rank and file will vote on the new contract today. Late last month, Kennecott Corp., a Salt Lake City unit of RTZ of Britain, reached an accord. This leaves Asarco Inc. of New York as the last major U.S. mining company yet to reach a new agreement. The U.S. labor pacts run out on June 30. The talks between managements and the union have been progressing harmoniously, according to analysts, but even so, the early settlement by Phelps Dodge came as a surprise, they said. The Phelps Dodge agreement diminishes the prospect of supply disruptions because of labor problems and enhances the possibility that a pact with Asarco will soon be reached, analysts said. They estimated that the London Metal Exchange today will announce an increase of 3,000 to 5,000 tons in warehouse supplies of copper. More ample inventories of the metal and a slowing U.S. economy, which could reduce demand, remain price-depressing factors and contributed to a narrowing of the premium of soonest-to-expire deliveries over more deferred contracts both in New York and London, they said.

GRAINS AND SOYBEANS: Analysts are divided over whether the Agriculture Department will lower its one-month-old estimate of the winter wheat harvest under way in the U.S. In May, the department forecast the harvest at 2.09 billion bushels, up 44% from the drought-damaged 1989 harvest. Some analysts are speculating that recent heavy rains damaged the soft red variety grown in Illinois and Indiana. Others, however, are projecting that the hard red variety grown in the Southern Plains is in better condition than indicated in May. Based on June 1 conditions, Richard Feltes, director of research at Refco Inc., Chicago, said he expects a winter wheat harvest of 2.14 billion bushels. Susan Leighty, a grain analyst at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc., New York, is predicting a harvest of 2.12 billion bushels. The Agriculture Department is also slated to update its supply and demand estimates for corn and soybeans. Among other things, analysts expect the government to begin trimming its rough estimate that the recently planted corn crop could total 8.1 billion bushels at harvest this fall. An unusually wet spring prevented many farmers from planting as many acres as the government had expected. Some crop analysts, however, are projecting a corn harvest of 7.4 billion to 7.7 billion bushels.
