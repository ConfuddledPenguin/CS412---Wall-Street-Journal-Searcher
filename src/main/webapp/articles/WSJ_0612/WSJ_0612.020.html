International: EC to Dismantle Several Tax Barriers Faced by Multinationals in Community

  • Author: Martin du Bois
  • Date: 06/12/90

LUXEMBOURG -- European Community finance ministers agreed to remove several tax barriers facing companies operating on a multinational basis in the EC. Three measures are aimed at removing the risk of double taxation for groups of affiliated companies operating in several EC states and for companies acquiring a firm in another EC member state.

At a time when the EC is striving for greater economic convergence, yesterday's compromise signals that the community is finally starting to tackle the delicate task of achieving more coherence in corporate taxation. Christiane Scrivener, the EC Commissioner in charge of fiscal affairs, described the accord as "a milestone" in the EC's program to remove all internal barriers by the end of 1992.

In recent months, European and U.S. companies operating in the EC had been lobbying hard for adoption of the three measures, which have been under consideration by the EC for almost 20 years.

The three measures, which will take effect Jan. 1, 1992, are:

-- A first directive will eliminate capital-gains taxes when assets are transferred between companies based in different EC member states in the event of a merger, corporate split-up, or share swap. Taxation will be deferred until the assets are realized, that is, sold or transferred to a non-EC company. The aim is to "eliminate the fiscal cost of cross-border operations," Mrs. Scrivener said.

-- A second directive will abolish the withholding taxes on dividends paid by a subsidiary to its parent company based in another EC country. West Germany had strongly opposed this measure. With high taxes on retained profits, Bonn worried that this would give too big a tax break to subsidiaries of foreign companies transferring their profits back home. As a compensatory gesture, Bonn may, until mid-1996, levy a 5% withholding tax on those dividends.

-- A third measure sets up an "arbitration procedure" to resolve conflicts between tax authorities in different member states, which often result in a company being taxed in two countries on the same operation. When such difficulties arise, the companies will be able to ask for the arbitration by a "panel of wise men," which is still to be set up. The panel's decision will be binding for the member states. "This strengthens the hand of taxpayers" in the face of tax authorities, said a British official.

Meanwhile, the finance ministers narrowed their differences over a proposed "investment-services directive" aimed at establishing a single license for brokerage and other non-bank financial services firms in the EC. Sir Leon Brittan, the EC commissioner for financial services, said the 12 member states vowed to reach an accord on the directive by Dec. 31.

Among other things, the investment services directive provides that banks will have direct access to trade on stock markets.

Belgium, Spain and Denmark indicated that they were ready to drop their opposition to that measure, Sir Leon said.
