Economy: Southwest Seen Leading Nation In Economic Growth Up to 2000

  • Author: Tuesday Georges
  • Date: 06/12/90

WASHINGTON -- Southwestern states are expected to lead the nation in economic growth through the end of the century, continuing the shift in economic activity to the Sun Belt, the Commerce Department projects. Nevada and Arizona will show the fastest growth rates in personal income, jobs and population between 1988 and 2000, the department predicts in a new report. One of every six jobs created during that period will be in California, the report says. And Florida, Utah and Hawaii also are expected to grow rapidly.

The report points to continued softness in northeastern states. While total personal income is expected to grow 46.2% after inflation in Nevada and 40.6% in Arizona, it is expected to expand only 21.6% in New England. Nationally, the department expects personal income to grow 26.2% by 2000.

New England surged ahead of most of the country in economic growth through much of the 1980s. But in 1988, its fortunes began to turn. Also during the past decade, increasing numbers of people and companies began to move to the Sun Belt, which stretches from the Southeast to the Southwest.

Kenneth Johnson, a Commerce Department analyst, said Arizona and Nevada "have exhibited strength already over the last few years. They have shown a competitive {growth} advantage over the other states and we expect that to continue into the year 2000."

In particular, growth in the transportation equipment and service industries will drive economic gains in the two states, said another department economist, Hugh Knox.

According to the report, four states -- California, New York, Texas and Florida -- will account for one-third of the nation's projected $4.1 trillion total personal income by 2000.

While California will continue to have the highest total personal income in the country, Connecticut will continue to have the highest per capita income, rising a projected 10.8% by the end of the century to $20,503 after adjusting for inflation.

That would be 34% above the national average of $15,345. New Jersey, Massachusetts, and the District of Columbia also are projected to have per capita incomes 20% above the national average.

Of the 18.9 million new jobs expected to be created in the U.S. by the end of the century, 3.4 million will be in California. Florida and Texas are projected to add more than a million new jobs each.

The projections suggest that Texas will continue to recover from the downturn caused by a plunge in oil prices in the 1980s. Its per capita income is expected to increase 18.2% between 1988 and 2000, compared with a meager 3.7% rise between 1979 and 1988.

Nevada is projected to experience the fastest population growth -- 29.8% -- followed by Arizona's 21.4% growth and Florida's 18.2%. In Wyoming, West Virginia, and Louisiana, population is predicted to fall slightly.
