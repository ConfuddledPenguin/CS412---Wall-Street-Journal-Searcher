Who's News: Nissan Taps First American, Mignanelli, To Head U.S. Sales and Marketing Arm

  • Author: Jacqueline Mitchell
  • Date: 06/12/90

Nissan Motor Co. named Thomas D. Mignanelli president and chief executive officer of its U.S. sales and marketing arm, making him the first American to hold such a title at any major Japanese auto company. Mr. Mignanelli succeeds Kazutoshi Hagiwara effective July 1. Mr. Hagiwara is returning to Tokyo to assume new responsibilities at the parent firm.

Mr. Mignanelli, 45 years old and currently Nissan's executive vice president of U.S. operations, said his promotion "raises visibility of Americans being accountable for the American market."

Mr. Mignanelli said he will have considerable autonomy. "We price our own products and we have a good deal . . . of the input on product-cycle plans," he said.

Nissan's move comes as Japanese auto makers are under fire from some U.S. executives who say they feel relegated to subordinate positions and isolated from key deliberations with executives of the parent companies in Japan.

"Japanese firms have been fairly slow to shift control of top management positions to Americans," said Robert Cole, a business professor at the University of Michigan. Nissan, however, has been "somewhat of a leader" in furthering that process, particularly at its Smyrna, Tenn., manufacturing facility, where there are "relatively few Japanese," he said.

Mr. Mignanelli will report directly to Yoshikazu Hanawa, president of Nissan North America Inc., the umbrella organization for Nissan's North American operations. Mr. Hanawa divides his time between Japan and the U.S. In contrast, the top U.S. sales executives at the U.S. sales arm of Honda Motor Co. and Toyota Motor Corp. have the title of executive vice president and report to Japanese executives in the U.S., who hold the title of president.

Mr. Hanawa said, "Mr. Mignanelli's appointment is in keeping with our strategy to become a fully localized, customer-oriented company in America. As we move toward realizing this goal, we will continue to localize management function of overseas subsidiaries and promote local decision-making on important issues."

Still, even with Mr. Mignanelli's high-profile promotion, Nissan has a long way to meet its goal. Americans now head just two of Nissan's 10 U.S. operating divisions. Besides Mr. Mignanelli, the other senior U.S. executive is Jerry Benefield, president and chief executive of Nissan Manufacturing Corp. in Smyrna, Tenn. Mr. Benefield also reports to Mr. Hanawa.

Moreover, Mr. Mignanelli has Japanese executives in his operation who not only report to him but have direct access to his superiors in Tokyo. These Japanese executives also have "career paths leading back to Japan," and, potentially, to bigger jobs with the parent company, a company spokesman said.

Mr. Mignanelli came to Nissan in 1987 after 18 years with Ford Motor Co., where he rose to national sales manager of the Lincoln-Mercury division. Mr. Mignanelli joined Nissan's U.S. sales arm as vice president of marketing, and was promoted to executive vice president of operations in 1988.
