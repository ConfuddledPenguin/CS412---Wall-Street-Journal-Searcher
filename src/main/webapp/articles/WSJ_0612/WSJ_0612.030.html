Marketing & Media: Australia to Block Offer by Maxwell For Newspapers

  • Author: S. Karene Witcher
  • Date: 06/12/90

SYDNEY -- The Australian government again threw cold water on Robert Maxwell's hopes of gaining a foothold in the country's newspaper business. Treasurer Paul Keating, who has the last word on foreign investment bids, said Canberra will block the British media magnate's proposal to take a 49% stake in the daily West Australian in Perth and other newspaper interests now controlled by Alan Bond's Bell Group Ltd.

Speaking at a news conference in London, Mr. Keating said, "We always have a restrictive view of newspaper ownership, particularly capital-city newspapers. A 49% interest from a shareholder that strong is a controlling interest, and we would not agree to it."

Mr. Keating defined a controlling interest as anything above 25%, but he wouldn't say how much of a holding by Mr. Maxwell would be tolerated. Australia now limits total foreign ownership in television and radio stations to 20%, though it has no firm guidelines for newspapers.

The treasurer added that the needs of the debt-burdened Mr. Bond, who is jettisoning assets in an effort to rescue his Australian-based empire from collapse, are "a side issue" to the country's larger interests.

In London, Mr. Maxwell said it was "injudicious" of Mr. Keating to comment before receiving an official application.

Mr. Maxwell's Mirror Group PLC and Bell Group announced that the British publisher had agreed to buy 49% of Bell Group's publishing interests for 175 million Australian dollars (US$135.1 million) in cash and assumption of A$75 million of debt.

The price Mirror Group agreed to pay for just less than half of the publishing interests effectively values the total assets at about A$500 million -- much more than some analysts think they're worth. "It seems to be a very high price," said Robin Forbes, of Perth stock-brokerage firm Hartley Poynton Ltd. Mr. Aspinall, however, called the price "very fair."

Mirror Group's move follows Mr. Maxwell's purchase in April, on his own behalf, of 14.9% of Bell Group. Australian regulators have petitioned the courts to unwind that transaction. The seller of the shares, David Aspinall, Bell Group's managing director, has been accused of violating corporate-takeover rules in the deal.

As part of yesterday's offer, Mr. Maxwell agreed to help fund a proposed buy-back -- most likely at a deep discount to face value -- of some A$550 million of bonds convertible into shares of Bell Group that are issued in Europe.

Bell Group also owes bank lenders about A$260 million, Mr. Aspinall said. If the proposed sale were completed, some of the proceeds would likely be used to reduce Bell Group's bank debt.
