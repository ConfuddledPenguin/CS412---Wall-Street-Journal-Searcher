Justices Uphold President's Authority To Send National Guard Outside U.S.

  • Author: Stephen Wermiel
  • Date: 06/12/90

WASHINGTON -- The Supreme Court upheld a federal law that gives the president the power to mobilize the National Guard for training missions outside the U.S. without approval of state officials. By a 9-0 vote, the high court upheld the constitutionality of a 1986 federal law that said the approval of a state's governor isn't required for the president to send the Guard abroad.

The 1986 law was challenged by the governors of Minnesota and Massachusetts in separate lawsuits objecting to the use of the Guard for training missions in Central America. The objections that Massachusetts Gov. Michael Dukakis voiced concerning this use of the Guard were raised as a campaign issue by some opponents in the 1988 Democratic presidential primaries, who tried to make him appear weak on national security.

Prior to 1986, federal law required that a state's governor approve calls to active training, but the 1986 amendment deleted the governor's authority. The two state officials argued that the amendment violated a provision of the Constitution that leaves it to the states to mobilize and train the "militia."

A federal appeals court in Boston rejected the Massachusetts' claim; a federal appeals court in St. Louis at first ruled in the Minnesota case that the 1986 amendment was unconstitutional, but then reversed itself.

In an opinion written by Justice John Stevens, the high court said the Constitution clearly gives Congress the power to order the National Guard to active duty without approval of state officials. (Perpich vs. Department of Defense)

Penalizing Lawyers

The justices ruled, 8-1, that federal appeals courts should defer to the decisions of federal district judges to fine lawyers for filing frivolous lawsuits. The decision will make it tougher for lawyers to win on appeal of their fines.

In an opinion written by Justice Sandra O'Connor, the court ruled that appeals courts should reverse fines imposed on lawyers only if the judge "abused his discretion." This is legal terminology for a lenient standard that gives district judges wide leeway.

The decision involved what is known as Rule 11 of the procedures governing civil lawsuits in federal court. Adopted in 1938, the rule was amended in 1983 to give judges the authority to impose sanctions on lawyers to discourage the filing of frivolous cases.

The justices resolved three issues. First, they ruled that a plaintiff's decision to get their lawsuit dismissed doesn't preclude a federal judge from considering whether to impose sanctions on the lawyer. Second, the justices said the appeals courts should defer to district judges in such cases. Finally, the high court said lawyers who appeal Rule 11 sanctions can't be penalized further by having to pay the winner's legal fees for the appeal.

The high court ruling is a defeat for Cooter & Gell, a Washington law firm that was engaged in a long legal battle on behalf of a competitor of Hartmarx Corp. A federal district court ordered the law firm to pay $21,452 and required the competitor, Danik Inc., to pay $10,701. (Cooter & Gell vs. Hartmarx Corp.)

State Taxes

Just a week after deciding two state tax cases that tied the high court in knots for two years, the justices agreed to hear two more challenges to state business taxes, one in Florida, the other in Georgia.

In one case, the court agreed to hear a challenge by James B. Beam Distilling Co. to a Georgia liquor tax that the company paid from 1982 through 1984. The tax was amended in 1985, but the company sued for a refund of $2.4 million it had already paid. The company said the tax discriminated by charging higher rates on liquor manufactured in other states and imported into Georgia.

The Georgia Supreme Court ruled that the tax before 1985 was unconstitutional since it directly interfered with interstate commerce. But the state court, saying that Beam wasn't entitled to a refund, said the ruling applied only to future cases.

Last week, in a Florida case, the justices ruled that states must give taxpayers relief if their taxes are ruled retroactively unconstitutional. But the court was deeply divided about when such rulings should be retroactive. The Beam case gives the court another chance to clarify when decisions that taxes are unconstitutional should be applied retroactively. (James B. Beam Distilling Co. vs. Georgia)

In the other case, Ford Motor Co.'s financing unit, Ford Motor Credit Co., challenged a tax assessment by the state of Florida of $2 million including penalties and interest for 1980 through 1982.

The Florida tax is imposed on intangible property. In this case, the tax was imposed on the value of outstanding car loans extended by Ford and owed by Florida residents for cars manufactured in other states and shipped to Florida.

The company argued that the tax was imposed in a way that would expose the same property to multiple taxation by Florida and other states where the company does business. The company argued that this is unconstitutional.

But a Florida appeals court upheld the tax. The appeals court said that the U.S. Supreme Court has never said the Constitution prohibits this risk of overlapping taxation when it comes to property taxes.

Yesterday, the justices agreed to hear Ford's appeal. (Ford Motor Credit Co. vs. Florida)
