Environment: Mobil Unit Said To Face Suit On Hefty Bags

  • Author: Randolph B. Smith
  • Date: 06/12/90

A task force of state attorneys general is expected to file legal action against Mobil Chemical Co. for making allegedly false advertising claims concerning environmental benefits of its Hefty garbage-bag line, people close to the situation say. Those people say lawsuits planned in seven states could be filed as early as today and would represent the first enforcement action since the task force began investigating environmental product claims last November.

The attorneys general, led by Hubert H. Humphrey III of Minnesota, are taking action against Mobil Chemical because Hefty products on store shelves in some states continue to claim they are "degradable," "even after they are buried in a landfill," those people say. They also say the actions could seek removal of the products from store shelves and payment of legal costs.

Barry J. Cutler, Federal Trade Commission director of consumer protection, confirmed the states planned to take legal action, but wouldn't identify the company. In general, he said, such lawsuits could produce guidelines defining when other companies could legally use the term "degradable."

Hefty trash bags contain an additive that accelerates decomposition of the plastic when it is exposed to sunlight, wind and rain. Critics complain that these conditions are not a factor once the trash is buried in landfills.

Allen Gray, spokesman for Mobil Chemical, a unit of New York-based Mobil Corp., said the company hasn't been notified of any pending legal action. He said Mobil agreed in late March to remove degradability claims and began changing labels in April because of "mounting confusion" over the meaning and value of such claims. "This is news to us. We're disappointed," Mr. Gray said, adding that Mobil had cooperated with the investigation and "substantiated" its product claims.

The states are believed to have singled out Mobil for deterrent value because after having originally claimed on labeling that Hefty bags were "degradable," the company had later said publicly that it didn't believe the bags would degrade when buried in landfills. Mr. Gray said Mobil made the claim because consumers were demanding degradable bags and Hefty risked losing market share to competitors that were making similar claims. Mr. Gray said company officials met several times with the attorneys general to settle the case, but those efforts apparently were unsuccessful.

Attorney General Humphrey wasn't satisfied in late March when Mobil indicated it would take "several months" to clear the offending packaging from store shelves. Earlier this month, government investigators found Hefty bags with very prominent "degradable" labeling is still being sold. Mr. Gray couldn't be reached for comment on when supplies of the old packaging would be exhausted.

Under federal and state laws, a company's decision to voluntarily withdraw product claims doesn't preclude legal action, Mr. Cutler said. "The purpose of an action would be to get a court order specifying what kinds of claims a company can make in the future. These cases will also provide guidance for other companies," Mr. Cutler said.

The FTC and the states are continuing to investigate a variety of products, such as disposable diapers, hairsprays and plastic foam containers, claiming to be degradable, recyclable or environmentally friendly. Earlier this year, DowBrands Inc., a unit of Dow Chemical Co., decided to delete "recyclable" and "photodegradable" labeling from its Handi-Wrap product line. Late last year, First Brands Corp. began removing some degradability claims from its Glad brand of trash bags.

People close to the Mobil Chemical investigation said the lawsuit would be filed in seven states: Minnesota, New York, California, Massachusetts, Wisconsin, Texas and Washington.
