Turmoil Gives Short Sellers Their Heyday

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 08/09/90

War fears? Increased chances of recession? These are the days that make Wall Street's short sellers smile.

Short sellers make money on bad news. They borrow a stock and immediately sell it, hoping the price will decline. Eventually, they must buy shares to replace the ones they borrowed. But if the stock drops enough in the meantime, they profit.

In the past three weeks, they have profited big -- but mostly by luck. Iraq's tanks rolled at exactly the right time for many short sellers, who had been increasing their positions for other reasons.

In mid-July, the Dow Jones Industrial Average, which had been surging, ran into resistance as it approached the 3000 mark; it then fell back. Later in the month, government reports showed increasing evidence of a sagging economy. These developments caused many short sellers to fatten up their positions.

So, when Mideast hostilities broke out, they were in a perfect position to benefit, even though they hadn't anticipated Iraq's military move any more than other investors did.

"I don't want people to think I'm looking for a war," says Kurt Feshbach, one of the three brothers in Palo Alto, Calif., who make up the nation's largest team of professional short sellers. "I don't want people to die. However, I wouldn't mind some fireworks."

Mr. Feshbach says his firm's managed accounts, entirely devoted to short selling, were up about 29.5% through July. Since Iraq invaded Kuwait, the accounts have jumped an additional 11.5 percentage points, bringing the year-to-date gain to 41%. (By contrast, the DJIA is down about 1% this year.)

Mr. Feshbach won't name the specific stocks he has shorted (there are about 200), but says many of them are bank issues. "We love our shorts in banks," he says. "We have some high-tech shorts, but not big names. Some biotech. A couple of insurance stocks {insurers with large real estate holdings}. And a couple of takeover plays."

Michael Murphy, a San Francisco money manager who runs a $40 million portfolio, almost all of it on the short side, says he's shorting full blast. His main accounts are 100% short, rather than carrying a 20% to 30% cash position as they normally do. This is "not because of Iraq," he says, but because of "fundamental problems in the economy." He predicts the Dow industrials will fall below 2250 by March.

Mr. Murphy's shorts are mainly in defense stocks, casinos, savings and loans, banks and airlines. In recent days, he has partially covered (bought back borrowed shares in) some of his positions in airlines such as AMR Corp. and Delta Air Lines Inc., and banks such as Bank of Boston Corp.

But Mr. Murphy is keeping a full short position in Continental Airlines Holdings Inc. and in various casino stocks. And he has actually increased his short position in defense stocks.

The bounce up in defense stocks on news of Mideast hostilities is "just silly," he claims. He thinks Congress will proceed with major defense spending cuts.

So far this year, Mr. Murphy says his short-selling accounts are up 22%; they were up about 10% at the end of June.

James Chanos, whose New York firm Kynikos Associates is among the nation's largest short sellers, says he is shorting "companies in the hotel business, a variety of New York large commercial banks, companies in the airline and airplane businesses, and companies that sell Nintendo cartridges." An investor in the firm says that accounts were up 27% through the end of July, and about 34% through Tuesday.

Mark Howard, co-owner of JRO Associates, a New York investment partnership, both sells short and buys stocks conventionally. At the moment, he says, he is about 60% on the short side.

One of Mr. Howard's biggest positions is in Japanese put warrants -- securities that rise in value if the Tokyo stock market falls. "This situation in Iraq . . . is a much bigger negative for Japan than it is for us," he says. "They are completely dependent on imported oil, and a decent part of it came from Iraq."

If the Iraqi invasion hadn't happened, "our markets would have been going down anyway," Mr. Howard says. "This just speeded up the process. Recession is sort of becoming obvious to everybody."

In the last few days, Mr. Howard covered his short positions in such big stocks as American Telephone & Telegraph Co., Dow Chemical Co., Compaq Computer Corp. and Digital Equipment Corp. He still has short positions in Genetics Institute Inc., L.A. Gear Inc. and Newbridge Networks Corp., among others.

Neil Dolinsky, a principal with C.C. Dunnavan & Co. in Minneapolis, runs a short portfolio of about 100 stocks. Last week, he added to his positions in 28 of them.

Unlike Mr. Murphy, he covered his positions in most defense stocks, including Lockheed Corp., Raytheon Co., General Dynamics Corp., Loral Corp. and Martin Marietta Corp., when hostilities broke out. However, he held his positions in McDonnell Douglas Corp. and Northrop Corp., citing their debt loads and the possibility that Northrop might be barred from military contracting.

John Brooks, an analyst with Davis, Mendel & Regenstein Inc. in Atlanta, does short-selling for his own account and also advises clients on short-selling. He says that it might soon be time to short-sell oil stocks, which have skyrocketed on the Mideast news.

"These oils have just gone nuts on the upside," he says. Although oil futures were trading yesterday at about $26.50 a barrel, Mr. Brooks thinks that's just a panic reaction. "My guess is somewhere between $20 and $21 is a more realistic level," he says.

Robert Doviak, who runs a Dallas investment partnership, Doviak Partners, says he is about "75% short" at the moment. "I think we're going to have a 100-point rally in the Dow the next week or so," he says. Then he plans to use borrowed money to "go more than 100% short."

He expects "a series of mini-crashes" in which the Dow industrials slide 50 to 150 points at a crack. Investors, he says, have gotten the idea from the 1987 crash and 1989 mini-crash that it's smart to buy when the market is down. "When everybody figures this is a sure formula to make money, the formula breaks."
