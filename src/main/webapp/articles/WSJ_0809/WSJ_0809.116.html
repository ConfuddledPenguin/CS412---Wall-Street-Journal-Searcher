Who's News: Peat Marwick's Horner to Leave Top Positions

  • Author: Robert Daniels
  • Date: 08/09/90

NEW YORK -- KPMG Peat Marwick, the consulting and accounting firm, said Larry D. Horner won't seek re-election as chairman and chief executive officer of the firm's U.S. and international practices. The 56-year-old executive could have run for an additional three-year term; Peat Marwick's mandatory retirement age for the positions is 60. But Mr. Horner, a veteran of 35 years with KPMG and its predecessors, said that the issues the firm faces require an executive who will be able to stay on for a longer tenure.

KPMG has 1,850 U.S. partners and a total of 6,100 partners world-wide, and is considered the largest of the Big Six consulting firms. The firm was formed out of the December 1986 merger of Peat Marwick International and Klynveld Main & Goerdeler.

Mr. Horner took over the top spots at Peat Marwick in 1984 and analysts say he was the driving force behind the merger. He took over KPMG's international organization, based in Amsterdam, in 1988. His terms at KPMG's U.S. and international practices end Oct. 3 and Dec. 31, respectively.

"He had the foresight to see this global economy we're growing into; . . . too many CPAs tend not to see the forest for the trees," says Arthur W. Bowman of Bowman's Accounting Report in Atlanta. "For the types of clients the Big Six serve, you need this more-global" operation.

KPMG's U.S. practice posted $1.77 billion in revenue for the year ended June 30, 1989. World-wide, KPMG posted total revenue of $4.75 billion for the year ended Sept. 30, 1989, reflecting revenue from firms that have been merged into KPMG since Sept. 30.

In a severely competitive climate, KPMG has a strong client base in most major markets world-wide. Mr. Horner says one challenge for his successor is to continue to build and solidify the firm's position. Prior to the merger, Peat Marwick had 70% of its business in North America and 30% elsewhere; those percentages at KPMG now are 45% and 55%, respectively, Mr. Horner estimated.

A major challenge facing Mr. Horner's successor is how to manage a firm in which the partners number in the thousands. "How do you coordinate the various interests?" Mr. Horner asks rhetorically. The solution "will take longer than the three {more} years I would have had" at KPMG, he says.

Neither Mr. Horner nor Mr. Bowman will speculate on a possible successor to KPMG's at the U.S. practice. Nominations for the positions will be finalized Sept. 20, and the U.S. firm's partners will vote early in October.

KPMG's executive committee has nominated P.J. Butler, senior partner of the U.K. practice, to succeed Mr. Horner at the international practice. The council overseeing the international practice will vote on that recommendation in September.

Mr. Horner sits on a number of boards of charitable organizations. He said he hasn't decided what he will do after leaving KPMG.
