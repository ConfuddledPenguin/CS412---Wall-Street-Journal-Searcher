Bear Stearns Aide Suspended by SEC In Insider Case

  • Author: Kevin G. Salwen
  • Date: 08/09/90

WASHINGTON -- A Securities and Exchange Commission administrative law judge suspended a Bear, Stearns & Co. broker for at least two years for his alleged role in a celebrated insider-trading scheme involving former LTV Corp. Chairman Paul Thayer. The decision, against William Mathis, an Atlanta sales representative for the New York-based brokerage house, stems from alleged trading on insider information in the takeover bids by LTV for Grumman Corp., Anheuser-Busch Cos. for Campbell Taggert Inc., and Allied Corp. for Bendix Corp.

The SEC said that Mr. Thayer, who was on the boards of Anheuser-Busch and Allied, passed inside information to Billy Bob Harris, a former A.G. Edwards & Co. broker who, in turn, tipped Mr. Mathis. In 1985, Messrs. Thayer and Harris pleaded guilty to criminal charges of obstructing justice and lying to the SEC during the investigation. They each received four-year prison terms and later were forced to disgorge profits.

Mr. Mathis, the former captain of the New York Jets team that won the Super Bowl in 1969, agreed to an SEC injunction in 1988 the day before he was to be tried for his role in the scheme. Without admitting or denying wrongdoing, he agreed to a settlement prohibiting him from violating securities laws in the future.

The SEC then attempted to sanction Mr. Mathis partly because the agency said he netted more than $750,000 from the transactions. But Mr. Mathis argued that he couldn't be sanctioned in a way that would deprive him of his livelihood without a separate finding by the agency that he violated securities laws. Since he hadn't admitted any wrongdoing in the consent injunction, he argued that SEC was overstepping its authority.

Mr. Mathis's lawyer said his client considers the two-year suspension "absolutely unjustified." Mahlon Frankhauser, the lawyer, said his client was being penalized "merely because he agreed to the entry of an injunction without admitting or denying any violations." Mr. Frankhauser said Mr. Mathis probably wouldn't appeal the decision because "he has been both financially and psychologically exhausted by the staff of the Securities and Exchange Commission."

An administrative law judge, Brenda Murray, rejected Mr. Mathis's arguments, siding with the SEC that it was in the public interest to bar him from the securities business. The administrative law judge agreed with the agency that Mr. Mathis lied to the SEC and to the New York Stock Exchange in their investigations about the trading.

For example, Mr. Mathis told the SEC that he borrowed money from Mr. Harris's father to buy Grumman shares and that Mr. Harris recommended that Mr. Mathis buy Grumman stock, the administrative law judge said. But in earlier SEC testimony, Mr. Mathis had told the agency that he never discussed Grumman with Mr. Harris, she added.

"Mr. Mathis's reasons for lying or misleading investigating authorities . . . do not in any way excuse or mitigate his actions," the administrative law judge said. "The evidence is that his actions were deliberate."

Bear Stearns Chairman Alan Greenberg said, "This is something that happened in 1982, and I feel bad about the whole thing. I certainly like Bill Mathis." A spokeswoman for the firm said Mr. Mathis, who has been employed there since 1970, would be leaving within 15 days.

Mr. Mathis can apply to be reinstated to the securities industry in two years.
