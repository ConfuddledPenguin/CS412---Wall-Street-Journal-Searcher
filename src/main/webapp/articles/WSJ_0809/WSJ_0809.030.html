Technology & Medicine: Scientists Identify Gene That Triggers Marijuana's `High' --- Fluke Discovery Could Spawn New Medicines That Have Herb's Therapeutic Effect

  • Author: Richard L. Hudson
  • Date: 08/09/90

LONDON -- A crucial clue to the mystery of how marijuana produces its "high" was discovered, opening the door to new medicines that have marijuana's therapeutic effects. Scientists at the U.S. National Institutes of Mental Health said they found and reproduced a gene that apparently confers sensitivity to marijuana. The gene is a blueprint for a natural chemical in the brain that binds with marijuana's active ingredient -- in essence, a chemical "lock" into which the marijuana "key" fits to produce some of its effects. The discovery was reported in today's issue of Nature, a leading British scientific journal.

Such a chemical mechanism has long been suspected and studied by scientists, but the cloning of a gene for it represents an important medical advance, said Solomon Snyder, a neural researcher at Johns Hopkins University in Baltimore. With the gene as a clue, he said, researchers can begin unraveling the mystery of marijuana's effects. Ultimately, he said, the research may lead to new medicines.

"Marijuana research, in principle, could offer terrific therapeutic drugs," said Mr. Snyder, whose discovery 17 years ago of a chemical "receptor" for opiates led to new, artificial pain killers. Marijuana has been known by Western scientists since at least the 19th century to dull pain, suppress vomiting and aid sleep. Though rarely allowed in the U.S., in some European nations marijuana and its derivatives are sometimes prescribed to treat glaucoma or control nausea in cancer chemotherapy patients.

The NIMH scientists, led by Lisa A. Matsuda, stumbled onto the marijuana discovery while following another avenue of brain-chemistry research. According to their report, they were studying rat genes for a known family of chemical "receptors," a class of molecule that receives the chemical messengers, or neurotransmitters, involved in communication among brain cells. One of the genes, which they isolated and reproduced using standard genetic technology, served no obvious function.

By coincidence, they noticed that the gene -- labeled SKR6 -- showed up most often in those parts of the rat brain most stimulated by marijuana in lab studies. Subsequent tests confirmed that the gene, in rodents anyway, was the template for a natural receptor molecule that binds with marijuana's psychoactive ingredient, called delta-9-tetrahydrocannabinol. The scientists have yet to find a similar gene in humans, however.

The receptor gene, the scientists said, may be one of many involved in marijuana's effects. Each gene may control a different effect of marijuana -- some blocking pain, others producing euphoria. By identifying those receptors involved in the drug's therapeutic effects, a medicine could be tailored to bind only with them -- and not with the psychoactive receptors. Some drug companies, including New York-based Pfizer Inc. and Sterling Drug Inc., have already been hunting such marijuana substitutes.
