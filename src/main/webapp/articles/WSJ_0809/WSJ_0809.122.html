He Avoids the Risk of Censorship Just by Not Needing Endowments

  • Author: Dianna Solis
  • Date: 08/09/90

HOUSTON -- John Peters's work gives new meaning to the phrase "commercial art." He doesn't photograph tractors or design corporate logos. The artist, whose portfolio includes Warholesque acrylic tributes to pop stars, sells advertising space on his canvases.

"There aren't enough {art buyers} in Houston to support me in the horrible style I'm accustomed to," the 32-year-old says, deadpan. So, for a fee, this tall Texan lets advertisers hawk discos, hair salons, clothing stores and the like to gallery goers. As many as a dozen ads, presented in footsquare boxes along both sides and the bottom edge, adorn his creations. They are silk-screened -- the "quintessentially careerist art form," he sniffs.

For critics, who might argue that the ads obscure his art, Mr. Peters has this ready reply: "My art is already obscure."

Mr. Peters got his inspiration from a Houston art show last December called "Primal Screen." Ads sold by Mr. Peters and the other exhibiting artists paid for the cost of materials for 18 canvases. "Primal Screen" promoters called it a celebration of "the notion that nothing could be more valid than art with no integrity whatsoever." Press kits for the event included a so-called review proclaiming the art-ads merger a "touchingly blatant tryst of culture and sponsor."

Next month, Mr. Peters plans a solo exhibition with ads nestled around his art, which is heavy on stylized images of such "celebrity idols" as comedian/actor Eddie Murphy and singer/actress Madonna. This time, the artist has hired a salesperson to sell ads on commission for as much as $1,000 a pop. Several advertisers, including a nightclub, a clothing boutique, an art gallery and Houston's Menil museum, seem happy to pay Mr. Peters's price.

John Hilliard, a Houston art collector, has purchased several of Mr. Peters's canvases. At first, he concedes, he thought Mr. Peters's works were a joke. But now that he owns seven Peters paintings, the young collector considers them "cutting edge."

For advertisers who really believe in the medium, one Peters work bought by Mr. Hilliard must have been especially tempting. Unfortunately for them, Mr. Peters sold no ad space on it.

In huge, black letters, that canvas reads simply, "Your ad here."
