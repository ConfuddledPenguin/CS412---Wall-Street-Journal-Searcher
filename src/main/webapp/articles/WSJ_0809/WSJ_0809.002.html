U.S. Seeking Ways To Curb Demand For Imported Oil --- Proposals Reject Rationing Of Gasoline, New Rules And Controls on Prices

  • Author: Barbara Rosewicz
  • Date: 08/09/90

WASHINGTON -- The Bush administration is trying to think up ways it can quickly curb Americans' big appetite for imported oil without appearing to look like the Carter administration. The Energy Department is brainstorming on a laundry list of proposals for conserving energy, increasing domestic production and switching fuels that is a far cry from the Carter days of thermostat controls and crash programs to build synthetic-energy plants.

"Americans everywhere must do their part," President Bush said yesterday, plugging energy conservation in his televised explanation of why he's sending troops to defend oil-rich Saudi Arabia.

The proposals, which are still undergoing changes but could be sent to the president's desk later this week, reject the concept of new regulations, gasoline-rationing plans or price controls. Instead, they focus on ways to remove barriers to production and rely more on carrots than sticks in encouraging energy conservation.

One of the most controversial ideas on the list is dropping the speed limit back down to 55 miles per hour, which would save 100,000 barrels of oil a day. The administration isn't endorsing the idea, an Energy Department official said, but wants to present all options and leave it up to the White House to decide what's politically feasible.

In the end, it also will be up to the White House to decide whether there's enough of an energy shortfall to issue a national call for more conservation and domestic production, similar to President Carter's famous cardigan sweater address in which he called the 1979 energy crisis the "moral equivalent of war."

Another measure would encourage fuel-switching by industries; estimates are it could replace 160,000 barrels of imported oil a day. Higher prices have already begun to persuade industries, many of which adopted dual-fuel capacity after previous energy crises, to switch from oil to more plentiful natural gas. Energy Department officials said they are trying to identify ways to remove barriers, such as transportation problems, that could inhibit fuel-switching.

Simply asking Americans to inflate their car tires to manufacturers' specifications could save 70,000 to 90,000 barrels a day. Other advice is: don't top off gasoline tanks, double up on car trips, and drive the more efficient car if there's a choice.

"There are a number of things that could be done that don't impair people's lifestyle, but if we did enough of them it would make a difference," said Linda Stuntz, deputy undersecretary of energy for policy. "It sounds almost hokey, but it really could make a difference."

On the production side, the Energy Department is considering ways to boost sagging domestic output, including asking states to delay or relax specific environmental and drilling regulations that reduce production for the duration of a shortage, said Ms. Stuntz.

Department officials in particular are studying the new $2.5 billion Point Arguello oil-drilling project off the coast of Santa Barbara, Calif. The project's three drilling platforms, built by a consortium led by Chevron Corp.'s Chevron U.S.A. Inc., are ready to pump as much as 100,000 barrels of oil a day, but the company can't get the oil to shore because it can't get state and local permission to build a pipeline or use tankers because of environmental concerns.

Ms. Stuntz said ways are being explored to try to free up the production, including perhaps involving the Coast Guard in ensuring the safety of tankers. "We're trying to turn over every rock we can find. There aren't that many places where there's that much production virtually available at the turn of the tap."

The department hasn't yet totaled how much imported oil could be offset by the measures it has in mind. The list is still subject to review by Energy Secretary James Watkins before going to the White House.
