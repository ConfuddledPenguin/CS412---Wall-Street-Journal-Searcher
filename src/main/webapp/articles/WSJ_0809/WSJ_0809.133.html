Heard on the Street: Analysts Say Iraq Move to Have Little Impact On U.S. Defense Firms, but Some See Gains

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 08/09/90

LOS ANGELES -- As unseemly as it sounds, arms makers know that a little war can be good for business. After Iraq crossed the border into Kuwait late last week, some defense stocks climbed 10%, even as the rest of the market plunged on fears that higher oil prices would accelerate inflation and, at the same time, prompt a full-blown recession.

In the past two days, the run-up has faltered a bit. But the question remains: Just how much have the bellicose Iraqis bolstered the fortunes of U.S. military suppliers?

All in all, many securities analysts insist, very little has changed during the past week to actually reverse the deterioration of earnings and balance sheets across the industry. "The rally has been driven by emotions, not fundamentals," says defense stock analyst Michael Rosen of Smith Barney, Harris Upham.

"None of these companies are going to recover overnight," adds Howard Rubel, aerospace analyst at Cyrus J. Lawrence. He says that difficult-to-meet Pentagon contracting terms and the melting of Cold War tensions will continue to take their toll on most weapons producers and defense stocks, as they have for the past year.

Still, many predict that the situation in the Persian Gulf, serious enough to trigger the dispatch of American aircraft and soldiers to Saudi Arabia, will at least somewhat slow the defense stock slide. And clearly, there are specific Defense Department programs -- surface ships and fighter jets, for instance -- that are likely to benefit from the flex of Saddam Hussein's muscle if Congress decides to soften plans for tough military spending cuts.

"This is a stark reminder of the volatile world in which we live and the irrationality of certain national leaders," asserts Stanley Pace, the chairman of General Dynamics. "This fact necessitates that the U.S. remain militarily strong and able to meet all contingencies."

General Dynamics' lot, some think, has improved because increases in orders for M-1 tanks and F-16 fighters are a strong possibility. The company also makes submarines, and virtually all firms that outfit the Navy may find Kuwait's loss their gain. Among the biggest surface-ship makers are Litton Industries and closely held Bath Iron Works. Robert Costello, former Pentagon acquisition chief, says that one area potentially spurred by the Iraqi incursion is fast sealift ships, large vessels that are "swift and highly flexible."

Missile makers also may find more opportunities. Raytheon is perhaps the most prominent producer of missiles, including the Patriot, which the Saudis and Israelis want to add to their arsenals. Iraq's show of force virtually ensures that Congress will approve those sales, which could eventually account for $2 billion or more in revenue. The Iraqi attack also cements the relatively solid position of defense-electronics companies such as Loral and E-Systems.

But for every defense dollar that pours into one of these projects, it likely takes away from another program. Even after the Iraqi assault, for instance, the Senate proposed scaling back the Strategic Defense Initiative, the space-based missile shield known as "Star Wars."

Strategic weapons, such as Northrop's B-2 Stealth bomber, also shouldn't find much relief in the current climate. Although some lawmakers argued this past week that Iraq's actions were sound reason to keep the embattled program going, the fact remains that the plane has been sold primarily for its nuclear-bomb-carrying capability. "I'm not sure that all the talk really changed any votes," says one congressional aide. In the case of other programs targeted by budget-cutters, such as Grumman's top-gun F-14 fighter, it may simply be too late to try to save them.

Of course, which companies ultimately win and lose will depend a lot on whether Iraq dares to push into Saudi Arabia and risk a major conflict. The trickiest thing to sort out, meanwhile, are those companies that may see both pluses and minuses as a result of the Iraqi onslaught.

Take, for example, McDonnell Douglas. The nation's No. 1 defense contractor can certainly make a better case to Congress now to keep open its F-15 fighter line; Saudi Arabia, fearful of what Iraq's next move could be, is among the countries that want more of the planes. In addition, the company's C-17 military cargo aircraft is just the kind of project that is likely to get a lift from the turmoil in the Middle East.

Yet McDonnell Douglas also has a $1.8 billion contract with Kuwait to deliver 40 F/A-18 Hornet strike fighters beginning in 1992. That sale suddenly becomes uncertain. Moreover, a recession could have dire consequences for McDonnell Douglas's massive commercial aircraft division.

Boeing is another mixed bag. The company could conceivably see its faltering military segment helped by the Iraqi invasion. Along with Textron's Bell subsidiary, Boeing makes the V-22 tilt-rotor Osprey. The White House wants to kill the potential $20 billion program, but some analysts think that lawmakers now will be more open to the aircraft.

But, even more important, Boeing's robust passenger-jet business could be hurt by an economic slowdown. Although a rise in oil prices could eventually force airlines to buy more fuel-efficient planes, Boeing would probably see orders dropping off and options going unfilled, at least in the near term.
