Bush Seeks a Cut in Deficit Despite Slowing Economy

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/09/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush said it's still "absolutely essential" to get an agreement with Congress to reduce the federal deficit even in the face of a slowing U.S. economy. Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady, meanwhile, said budget negotiators "ought to pick up the pace" and urged that the deficit be reduced while the economy is still growing, however slowly.

In remarks to the National Press Club, Mr. Brady said he doesn't think the U.S. is slipping into a recession, but he reiterated his view that the economy isn't growing as rapidly as the administration wants.

Because of the deteriorating economy and the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait, congressional budget negotiators already are rethinking their original goal of reducing the projected deficit for fiscal 1991, which starts Oct. 1, by $50 billion. The talks are to resume after Labor Day.

Mr. Bush, in a news conference called to discuss the Middle East, declined to criticize congressional Democrats, but he signaled that he may do so soon. "Stay tuned," he said. Before the budget talks were suspended for the August congressional recess, Mr. Bush told top Democrats that he was abrogating their agreement to avoid partisan criticism. So far, he hasn't said anything in public.

Mr. Brady, referring to a possible securities-transfer tax as "one of the 80 possibilities looked at by the administration and Congress," said that "nobody's proposed it."
