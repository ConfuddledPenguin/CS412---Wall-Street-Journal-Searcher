Foreign Exchange: Dollar, Yen Climb as Standoff Continues In Mideast but Oil-Supply Threat Lessens

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/09/90

NEW YORK -- The U.S. dollar and yen climbed against European currencies as the standoff in the Persian Gulf continued, but a crimp in world oil supplies grew less likely. Comments by President Bush during the early New York trading session that U.S. military forces had been deployed to Saudi Arabia only for defensive purposes and not to oust Iraq from Kuwait kept dollar buying from dominating the foreign exchange market.

But with little chance of a quick resolution to the crisis, few bold dollar-sellers emerged. The dollar wavered above and below 1.5900 marks throughout global trading and ended in New York at 1.5925 marks, up from 1.5875 marks late Tuesday. The U.S. unit was unchanged at 1.3385 Swiss francs.

The yen, which had been the weakest major currency since Iraq's invasion, gained from profit-taking sparked by an unsubstantiated rumor in the Asian market that the Bank of Japan was about to raise the discount interest rate. The yen's appreciation, especially against the mark, encouraged stop-loss yen buying when European and North American banks opened.

Abigail Cook, a New York vice president for corporate foreign exchange with Hongkong & Shanghai Banking Corp., said that technical demand for yen had accumulated since last Thursday as the Japanese unit declined with hardly a pause.

Improving attitudes toward the yen were a 3% rebound in Tokyo stock prices and Iran's announcement it would supply Japan with 550,000 barrels of oil a day to help make up for lost Iraqi and Kuwaiti production. Venezuela also said it could increase its output by as much as 500,000 barrels of oil a day.

Late Tuesday, U.S. officials had said Saudi Arabia agreed to increase its own massive oil production. The Saudis didn't pledge a specific amount, but the U.S. had been seeking an additional two million barrels a day, half the normal output of Iraq and Kuwait.

The improved world supply picture, combined with profit-taking, pushed crude oil prices lower yesterday. The September contract on the New York Mercantile Exchange fell $2.35 a barrel to $25.96 a barrel.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted about a yen lower at 149.80 yen, compared with 150.95 yen late Tuesday. The mark and Swiss franc fell to 94.15 yen and 112.03 yen, respectively, from 95.06 yen and 112.86 yen late Tuesday.

In Tokyo Thursday, the dollar was trading soon after the opening at 149.65 yen, down from Wednesday's Tokyo close of 150 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5935 marks; in Tokyo Wednesday, it closed at 1.5848 marks.

"The yen has been like a pingpong ball in a hurricane," said Jerry Egan, chief dealer for Bank of Boston.

Sterling sank to 280.15 yen from 282.41 yen previously. Late in New York, the pound was also trading at $1.8696, down from $1.8720 late Tuesday. The British currency, however, climbed to 2.9753 marks from 2.9710 marks late Tuesday.

Dollar trading remained agitated and headline driven. A "deep dichotomy" between dollar-bearish U.S. economic weakness and dollar-bullish Mideast posturing is "tearing at the market," Mr. Egan said.

"The most important thing for the people in the pits is to not try to explain movements, but to be ready to quote a price that's not going to lose you money," Mr. Egan said. "This is one of the most difficult markets I've seen."

Noted Ms. Cook of Hongkong & Shanghai, "a lot a people are tuckered out. They just don't have the stamina to keep these {long-dollar} positions. People are getting really bitter about holding positions."

Ms. Cook explained that dealers want to keep enough dollars to protect themselves from a new military clash, but that whenever the situation seems to calm down, "you see some of the big boys trying squeeze out those long-dollar positions so they can buy dollars cheaper."

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $383.20, down $1.30. Estimated volume was a heavy five million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Thursday, gold was quoted at $381.75 an ounce.
