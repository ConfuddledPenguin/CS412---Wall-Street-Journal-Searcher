CHINA'S NUCLEAR INDUSTRY

China's nuclear enterprises, which have been losing money due to declining military orders, are looking for foreign investment to help increase production of civilian products, an official report said.

About 40,000 of China's workers in the nuclear industry are idle, a result of a sharp decrease in the demand for military orders in the past few years and a two-year austerity program that tightened credit to halt runaway inflation, the China Daily reported. Last year, the industry posted a profit for the first time in three years, due largely to its civilian products, the report said.

In 1989, civilian goods accounted for 42% of the industry's production -- six times more than in 1980. They included rare earth metals, man-made diamonds and medical equipment as well as such non-nuclear items as smoke alarms and chemical fertilizers, the report said.

The nuclear industry has set a goal of producing two billion yuan ($422 million) of civilian goods by 1995 -- a 150% increase over last year, the paper said.

ITALIAN JEWELRY CHAIN SOLD

Kuwayama Kikinzoku Corp., a Japanese jewelry maker, and Japanese trading company C. Itoh & Co. bought 15% of the outstanding shares of Valestra 1882 S.p.A. of Italy for $3 million, the Japanese companies said. According to officials, Kuwayama bought a 10% stake in Valestra, while C. Itoh acquired 5% of the outstanding shares of Valestra, described as the top jewelry chain in Europe. Valestra is in Vassano, Italy, and has sales networks in 30 countries, officials said.

NO MORE GOOSE STEP

East German soldiers will no longer march the goose step because it is out of step with the country's new democratic ways, East Germany's defense minister announced. The goose step, widely linked to Germany's militaristic past, no longer fits the armed forces' image, Defense Minister Rainer Eppelmann said, according to the ADN news agency. A trademark of Adolf Hitler's Third Reich, the goose step was dropped by West Germany after the nation was divided, but East Germany retained it.

TAIWAN'S CHINA INVESTMENTS

Taiwan investors poured US$240 million into China's Fujian Province in this year's first six months, double that of the year-earlier period, according to an official report. Wang Hao, a senior provincial official dealing with overseas investment, was quoted in the China Daily as saying investment had built up momentum over the course of the year, with the average expenditure rising to $2 million from $800,000 in 1988. Increasing amounts have been put into technology projects and infrastructure works instead of the traditional labor-intensive factories, he added.

LANGUAGE LESSONS

France and Germany are promoting their respective languages in Eastern Europe. In France, the cabinet said the promotion of French in the newly democratic region should be "a major component of foreign policy." The Hachette Foundation is sending 100,000 French-language books to Romanian libraries, while Alliance Francaise plans to open schools to teach French in the Soviet Union, Bulgaria, Czechoslovakia and Hungary; it already has schools in Romania, Poland, Hungary and Yugoslavia. In West Germany, meanwhile, the Goethe Institute plans to open schools to teach German in Moscow, Prague and Warsaw.

TRINIDAD RECONSTRUCTION

Trinidad and Tobago acting Prime Minister Winston Dookeran announced a reconstruction program to repair businesses damaged during the recent coup attempt.

Mr. Dookeran also announced a social-assistance program and said public buildings, including the fire-gutted police headquarters in Port-of-Spain, would be repaired soon.

The acting prime minister said that the cabinet at its next sitting would work out the details of the programs.

Industry and Enterprise Minister Bhoe Tewari has estimated that property damage in Port-of-Spain is at least $70 million. The official death toll from the failed coup and subsequent looting is 24.
