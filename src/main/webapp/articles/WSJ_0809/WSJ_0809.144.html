Unions Seeking UAL Win More Time, But Chances of Success Still Uncertain

  • Author: Judith Valente and Randall Smith
  • Date: 08/09/90

The union group trying to acquire United Airlines' parent UAL Corp. won a 60-day extension of today's deadline to obtain financing for its proposed $4.38 billion, $201-a-share buy-out. The UAL board also approved a possible restructuring of the union offer in order to reduce the need for bank debt, as long as the revised terms provide "commensurate value" in the opinion of UAL's financial and legal advisers, First Boston Corp. and Davis Polk & Wardwell.

But the extension, which was expected, may do little to improve the unions' chances of completing the acquisition. Takeover stock traders remain skeptical. Although UAL stock rose $13.50 a share to $114.50 yesterday in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, it remains far below the nominal price of the union offer because traders doubt the proposal will be completed.

UAL's board, in granting the union group's request for extra time, reserved the right to terminate the April 9 acquisition agreement at any time, and said "UAL will terminate the merger agreement" if the unions can't obtain financing by Oct. 9.

Putting the extension on a day-to-day basis -- an unexpected move by the board -- may hamper the unions' ability to nail down financing by creating additional uncertainty, one analyst said last night.

The three UAL unions had asked for a 90-day extension because "the recent turmoil in world oil markets had slowed the progress of its discussions with financing sources." But the group's progress had faltered even before Iraq invaded Kuwait a week ago.

The UAL board didn't give any indication whether it would continue to hold off on placing an aircraft order valued at about $10 billion so that the unions can attempt to obtain financing from manufacturers of aircraft and engines.

The unions have been trying to use the pending aircraft order as bait to gain junior financing for the acquisition from the suppliers. But Stephen Wolf, UAL's chairman, who would lose his job if the union buy-out is completed, has attempted to foil that strategy by seeking to place the order promptly. Last month, the UAL board blocked that effort after the unions protested.

Currently, the union offer gives each UAL shareholder $155 in cash, $35 in notes, and a security representing proceeds from the share of United's interest in its computerized reservation system. The cash part of the offer alone would require about $3.4 billion. A restructuring that reduced the cash portion could reduce the unions' need for new bank debt.

One person familiar with the buy-out proposal said that four to five additional banks have indicated interest in participating in a loan of about $2.5 billion for the acquisition, in addition to the core bank group of Bankers Trust New York Corp., Manufacturers Hanover Corp., and Security Pacific Corp.

Gerald Greenwald, who would become the airline's chairman if the unions can complete the buy-out, told the board the unions had made progress and would have lined up bank commitments if it hadn't been for the Mideast war, according to people familiar with details of the meeting.

Yesterday's rebound by the UAL stock price was the first since July 30, when it began a 62-point decline. That sell-off began when three other banks withdrew from the financing; it steepened when soaring oil prices raised the threat of higher jet fuel costs and a general economic slowdown that would cut airline profits.

The stock rebound occurred on news that oil prices were falling for the first time since Iraq invaded Kuwait, and on speculation that the buy-out group would receive an extension.
