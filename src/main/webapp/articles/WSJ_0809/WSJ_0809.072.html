Wary of Mideast Blowup, Many Brokers Aren't Pushing Stocks

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi and William Power
  • Date: 08/09/90

NEW YORK -- Just a few weeks ago, many of Ted Peck's clients were awash in stocks. Some were even borrowing money to buy still more shares. Now the Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. broker says he has slashed some clients' stock exposure to 40% and cut back the leverage. It's no time to bet big on stocks, he tells many of them.

"You don't bottom fish in shark-infested waters," advises Mr. Peck. Noting the external forces playing on the stock market these days, he sighs and says, "This is in the laps of the gods."

Since the October 1987 stock-market crash, Wall Street has lectured investors to buy on stock-price drops, and that strategy has largely paid off. But some brokers and Wall Street executives say this time is different: There are too many unpredictable forces -- Middle East tensions, volatile oil prices and the budget deficit -- to prod investors back into the market just yet, they say.

With such potential market minefields out there, "It's kind of hard to ask people to take risks," says Raymond "Chip" Mason, chairman of brokerage firm Legg Mason Inc., Baltimore. "That's why you're not seeing many (brokers) saying, `Go, go, go.'"

In recent days, several big securities firms have urged investors to slash exposure to stocks and bonds, including Merrill Lynch & Co., Morgan Stanley Group Inc., Sears, Roebuck & Co.'s Dean Witter Reynolds Inc. and Primerica Corp.'s Smith Barney, Harris Upham & Co.

Dean Witter, in fact, suggested that investors move a hefty 20% of their investment portfolios into cash, compared with zero before. Smith Barney pushed its cash recommendation up to 20% from 15%, reversing a recommendation made just three weeks earlier.

"We may be in for a period of extreme volatility" in the market, says Smith Barney portfolio strategist A. Marshall Acuff Jr.

Many brokers privately say they don't always follow their firm's advice. But at least in this case, they are simply preaching the only gospel that small investors are willing to listen to. Investors are still smarting from the 1987 crash, the 1989 "minicrash" and the tumultuous declines of last spring. While investors don't seem to be engaging in panicky selling -- another lesson from the 1987 crash -- many aren't willing to believe the market has hit bottom yet, either.

"October 1987 cured a lot of people, frightened them," says William Lauder, a 68-year-old retired attorney and active investor in Amityville, N.Y. "The little fellow . . . is going to be cautious, and his brokerage house -- if it has any sense of the small clients -- it's going to reflect that, too."

Mr. Lauder says he would ignore his broker if the broker were touting buying opportunities now. "This Mideast thing has kind of exacerbated the situation to the point where I'm very cautious," he says, adding, "I think it's going to get worse on a gradual basis before it gets better."

Not every broker, however, has told clients to cool it. Stanley Lee, a PaineWebber Inc. broker in New York, says he's telling investors to buy selectively even though he expects volatility in the next few months and says prices could fall further. Investors who buy Philip Morris in the mid-40s, for instance, might see it fall to $38 a share in a crunch, Mr. Lee says, "but it's still a good opportunity." Philip Morris closed yesterday at $45.625 a share, up 37.5 cents, on New York Stock Exchange composite volume of three million shares.

Some Wall Street executives think they've got the Middle East situation figured out. It's "analyzable," says Michael A.M. Keehner, head of the 1,400 brokers in General Electric Co.'s Kidder, Peabody & Co. unit. "We see this by its nature as basically being a short-term crisis" that the Bush administration will take care of, he says. Investors should "take a look at high-quality growth stocks which do seem to be available," Mr. Keehner urges.

Still, many brokers are reluctant to push too hard too fast. Despite exhortations of his branch manager to "go out there and sell," a Dean Witter broker confides, he has counseled caution to clients wanting to jump in and buy stocks.

The broker, who asked to remain anonymous for fear of reprisal, said "I'll go through the month without the (sales) numbers I need rather than burn the hell out of people."

Even brokers who emphasize the long term are cautious. "I haven't called a single client and said, `We've got all this cash, let's go in and buy,'" says Michael Cohen, a broker for A.G. Edwards & Sons Inc. in St. Petersburg, Fla. "But I'm saying buy slowly -- particularly if we see more dips in the market."

One thing's certain: Any spurt in the market -- such as yesterday's 24.26-point rise in the Dow Jones Industrial Average -- makes it easier for brokers to persuade their clients to jump back in.

For example, at Raymond James Associates Inc. in Sarasota, Fla., branch manager Arthur O'Connor says he suddenly had success selling "high-quality growth stocks" to his mostly older clients, some of whom had left the stock market completely in the past couple of months. One investor has just put half his money back into stocks, Mr. O'Connor says, and he hopes others will put back at least a third.

His pitch? "We've been through these kinds of (oil-related market plunges) before, in 1973 and 1974, and it worked out before," Mr. O'Connor says.

But he quickly adds: "I'm not saying it's going to be a smooth ride by any means back to 3000."
