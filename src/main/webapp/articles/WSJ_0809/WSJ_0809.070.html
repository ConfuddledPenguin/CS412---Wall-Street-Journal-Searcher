Letters to the Editor: A Taxpayer's Outrage Must Be Seen in Context

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/09/90

With regard to "Folklore and Furnace: A Tale of Two NEA Grantees," Leisure & Arts, July 24, I had two reactions. The first is that being a large employer in an area is a strange justification for an arts grant. Perhaps Appalshop Roadside Theater, with an annual budget of $1.6 million and 37 employees, might just as appropriately qualify for assistance under state or federal small-business assistance programs, but it might have to meet some minimum criteria that NEA recipients don't have to meet -- like being self-supporting after having received subsidies for 20 years.

The second reaction is that I am glad Franklin Furnace Archives Inc. was able to raise $17,000 from willing contributors. Surely it is better for society overall that contributions to the arts be voluntary rather than coerced. I find it disturbing that it is considered a fair exchange that money I have worked to earn and pay taxes with is considered well spent if it pays someone to smear chocolate syrup on herself in public. And I am baffled that while I can go to jail for not paying my federal income taxes, someone gets tax-free money from the government to behave in ways that I attempt to prohibit my six-year-old from doing in his own home. (His actions, just as Karen Finley's, also must be seen in context to be understood.)

The issue is not obscenity -- the issue is that the federal government (or any government) should spend tax money on things that are at least as important and serious to society as a whole as the efforts the government expends in raising funds to pay for such expenditures.

Diane L. Warriner

Camp Hill, Pa.
