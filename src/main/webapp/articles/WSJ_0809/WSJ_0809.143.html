Utility Debt Ratings Aren't Likely to Fall Due to Mideast Crisis

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/09/90

NEW YORK -- The crisis in the Middle East is not likely to have a significant impact on debt ratings of most utilities, Standard & Poor's Corp. said. Despite climbing oil costs, most utilities will be able to protect their profits by passing along higher costs to consumers, S&P said in its CreditWire report. During the oil price shocks of the 1970s, utility regulators in northeastern states approved provisions that allow utilities to adjust their rates to cover fuel increases. And some New England utilities have added nuclear generating capacity or converted plants to other fuels, S&P said.

Consolidated Edison Corp. and Long Island Lighting Co., both in New York, face significant exposure to soaring oil costs. Con Ed imports nearly 50 million barrels of oil a year, while Lilco imports just over 20 million barrels. Under current regulatory provisions, however, the two utilities will be allowed to recover 100% of any increased fuel costs by boosting consumer rates.

But it remains unclear how regulators and political leaders will react to steep rate increases that are only partly related to the current oil crisis, S&P said. Some utilities, including Lilco, Boston Edison Co., New England Power Co., Central Maine Power Co. and Commonwealth Energy System, already had requested rate increases for unrelated reasons.

S&P also predicted that utilities relying heavily on natural gas may have a harder time recovering costs. That could hurt utilities such as Louisiana Power & Light Co., Mississippi Power & Light Co. and New Orleans Public Service Inc., units of Entergy Corp., given the tough regulatory environments and weak economies they operate in.
