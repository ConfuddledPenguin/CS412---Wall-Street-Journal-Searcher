Investment Banks Suggest Clients Buy Back Stock

  • Author: Craig S. Smith Special to the Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/09/90

NEW YORK -- Investment bankers are telling client companies to consider buying back their stock now that share prices have plummeted in the recent market plunge. "We are talking to clients and suggesting they invest in their own equities," says Peter DaPuzzo, co-head of Lehman Brothers world-wide equities division.

Buy-backs, whose popularity soared after the October 1987 stock market crash, trailed off significantly in recent months. But now, they may be back.

So far this week, nearly 30 firms have announced buy-backs although many of those decisions had been made prior to the most recent market turbulence. Investment bankers say more are expected as the market stabilizes.

Richard Franyo, head of investment banking at Alex. Brown & Sons Inc., says he alerted the firm's bankers Monday that they should be looking for corporate stock buy-backs.

"It's clearly something we should be calling our clients and talking about," Mr. Franyo says.

Alex. Brown itself yesterday authorized the buy-back of as many as 500,000 additional shares in a buy-back program that now totals about 760,000 shares.

Jim Timmins, a partner at Hambrecht & Quist Inc. in the capital markets department, says that for some time his firm has been advising mature technology companies to consider buying back stock. (Most young technology companies consume any cash on hand through expansion and don't have room to consider buy-backs or dividends, he notes.) Many of the firms that had been advised responded by setting prices for their stocks at which they would consider such action.

"The recent sell-off brings prices into the target ranges that people had established previously," Mr. Timmins says.

He says his firm has gone back to those firms now that share prices have dropped, suggesting they take another look.

"Before it was theory, and now it's practice," he says.

However, Lehman Brothers investment banker Michael Madden notes corporations may be less enthusiastic about buy-backs this time around than they were after the October 1987 market crash or the jolt in October 1989.

"A number of events have come about in the last few months or days that have influenced their thinking so they may not be as aggressive as in the prior two periods," Mr. Madden says.

Among the most significant of those events are the sudden spike in the price of oil and the looming possibility of a recession soon. Those considerations are likely to make companies think twice about spending cash now that could provide a financial cushion later on.
