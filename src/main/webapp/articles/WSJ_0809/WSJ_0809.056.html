Money-Fund Assets Surge $6.8 Million On Mideast Crisis

  • Author: Ellen E. Schultz
  • Date: 08/09/90

NEW YORK -- Assets of money-market mutual funds soared $6.8 billion in the latest week, as many investors sought refuge from the fallout of the Middle East crisis. Assets of 431 taxable funds surged $5.6 billion to a record $391 billion at the close of business Tuesday, according to IBC/Donoghue's Money Fund Report. Investors also poured $1.2 billion into tax-exempt money funds, which reached a record $79.9 billion.

Some mutual-fund companies said that much of the money-fund inflow was from investors switching out of stock and bond funds. "A lot of people are taking a wait-and-see attitude, and money funds are a good parking place," said a spokesman for Boston-based Fidelity Investments.

A spokesman for Vanguard Group in Valley Forge, Pa., said 40% of the "substantial inflows" into its eight money funds came from exchanges, while the rest was from new sales.

The $5.6 billion jump in the assets of taxable money funds was greater than the increase in the week following the 1987 stock market crash, when investors poured in $5.4 billion. But while inflow following the crash was equivalent to a 2.3% rise in money-fund assets, the latest inflow represented a smaller 1.4% increase, said Donoghue's editor, Martha Wittbrodt.

The largest weekly increase in money-fund assets was in the week ended Jan. 9, when they ballooned by $10 billion. Fund assets rose $6.7 billion in the week ended July 11, 1989. In both cases, however, the increases were largely caused by technical factors, Ms. Wittbrodt said.

The inflow in the latest week came despite a further decline in money-fund yields, the Holliston, Mass.-based Donoghue's newsletter reported. The average seven-day compound yield, which assumes reinvestment of dividends, dropped to 7.78% from 7.84%.

Average maturity of the taxable funds increased to 45 from 44 days. That's within the range of the last few months; the average maturity has been as short as 29 days and as long as 53 days in recent years. Money funds invest in such things as shortterm Treasury securities, commercial paper (short-term corporate IOUs) and bank certificates of deposit.

The average seven-day simple yield of the taxable funds slipped to 7.50% from 7.56%. The average 30-day simple yield also eased, to 7.58% from 7.62%, and the average 30-day compound yield dropped to 7.87% from 7.91%.

Yields on tax-exempt funds were also down. The average seven-day compound yield of 226 tax-free funds dropped to 5.19% from 5.21%, Donoghue's reported. The latest yield is equivalent to 7.21% for an investor in the 28% tax bracket and 7.75% for someone paying 33% in taxes.
