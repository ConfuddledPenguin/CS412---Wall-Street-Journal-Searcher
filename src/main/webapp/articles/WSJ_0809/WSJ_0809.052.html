Tracking of Assets Of Kuwait in U.S. Proving Difficult

  • Author: Peter Truell
  • Date: 08/09/90

WASHINGTON -- The Treasury is having trouble tracking down the tens of billions of dollars of investments that Kuwait holds in the U.S., but expects to issue regulations for these frozen assets soon, according to an administration official. The regulations will lay down codes of conduct for the treatment of these assets, which along with Iraqi investments in the U.S. were frozen by President Bush following Iraq's invasion of Kuwait.

Kuwait is estimated to hold about $50 billion of assets in the U.S., according to David Mizrahi, editor and publisher of MidEast Report, a New York-based newsletter that tracks Arab investments in the U.S. These are mainly held in real estate, stocks and bonds, he says, adding that Kuwait's investment in oil refiner Santa Fe International probably ranks among the largest Kuwaiti holdings in the U.S.

The Treasury -- for the time being, at least -- declines to quantify Kuwait's investments in the U.S. In a recent briefing, Richard Newcomb, the department's director of foreign assets control, would only say they are "significant."

Iraq, in contrast to Kuwait, has relatively small investments in the U.S., probably a few hundred million dollars or less.

One of the reasons that Kuwaiti investments may be hard for the Treasury to track is because the country and its citizens, in common with many rich Arabs, have generally preferred discreet and low-profile investments in the U.S., bankers familiar with Kuwait's investment strategy say. Kuwait has generally avoided shareholdings of more than 5% in publicly quoted companies in order to avoid the requirement to disclose holdings.

Kuwait's desire to build up an international oil exploration and refining business seems to be the exception to that general rule. A Commerce Department report on foreign direct investment in the U.S. for 1988 lists only one Kuwaiti investment in the U.S. for that year: the $28.6 million acquisition of Exploration Co. of Louisiana Inc. by the Kuwaiti government.

In the mid-1970s then-Treasury Secretary William Simon agreed that rich Middle East nations wouldn't be subject to public country-by-country breakdowns of their investments in the U.S., according to Mr. Mizrahi. But Mr. Mizrahi contends that such breakdowns have been kept confidentially by the Treasury.

Meanwhile, the administration recently set out its policy to Kuwaiti banks in a letter dated Aug. 5 from the Treasury's Mr. Newcomb to the Federal Reserve Bank of New York.

Mr. Newcomb's letter sets out four categories of Kuwaiti banks. The first comprises banks that are considered "blocked entities" that can't make transactions with persons under U.S. jurisdiction. They include Bank of Kuwait and the Middle East, Burgan Bank, Central Bank of Kuwait, Credit de Bergues, Kuwait Finance House and Savings and Credit Bank. Also blocked are banks over which Kuwait is deemed to have "de facto control" -- Al-Ahli Bank, Commercial Bank of Kuwait, Industrial Bank of Kuwait and Gulf Bank.

There are also five banks with some Kuwaiti government ownership; these institutions' transactions will be authorized pending a final determination by Aug. 18. Another category is made up of 11 banks that Mr. Newcomb's letter says "shouldn't be considered" blocked entities but on which the department is seeking more information by Aug. 18.
