World Markets: Tokyo Stock Prices Rebound, Gain 856.07 Points; London Shares Edge Up in Hectic, Choppy Trade

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 08/09/90

Tokyo stock prices rebounded sharply following a series of steep declines since Iraq invaded Kuwait a week ago. London stocks ended fractionally better in hectic, choppy trading, as investors assessed the minute-by-minute developments in the Middle East.

In Tokyo, after a volatile morning session, stocks rose steadily in the afternoon encouraged by firming bond prices, traders said. Both markets responded favorably to reports that Saudi Arabia had agreed to increase oil output to help make up for the shortage in world supplies because of the embargo of Iraqi oil.

The Nikkei Index of 225 issues ended Wednesday at 28509.14, up 856.07 points. Thursday morning in Tokyo, however, stocks fell 247.26 points to 28261.88.

Wednesday's volume on the first section was estimated at 580 million shares, up from 526.4 million shares Tuesday. Advancers outnumbered decliners 819 to 173, while 102 issues were unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index of all issues in the first section gained 53.60 points, or 2.62%, to 2100.02.

The second section index rose 75.06 points, or 1.87%, to 4090.69. Volume in the section was estimated at 14.1 million shares, in line with 14.4 million shares Tuesday.

Observers saw Wednesday's sharp advance as a technical rebound from repeated plunges over the previous five trading days, during which the Nikkei Index dropped 3382.59 points, or 10.9%.

The market opened with more buy orders than sell orders for the first time since Friday, as overnight stability on overseas stock markets helped to encourage institutional buying of oversold large-capital stocks, traders said.

"Investors regained calmness," compared with Tuesday, when they sold in panic on the "oil shock," said Takatoshi Okuyama, general manager of the investment advisory department at Daiwa Securities. Even discounting the possible impact of the Iraqi issue on the Japanese economy, stocks had been oversold, he said.

Hiroyuki Murai, general manager of stock trading at Nikko Securities, said, "We're not overjoyed with Wednesday's rebound," noting that uncertainty about Persian Gulf tension hasn't changed.

"In addition to the fact that Japan has enough oil reserves, which ensures oil supply in terms of quantity, Saudi's decision to increase output reduced fear for higher oil prices," Mr. Murai said.

"But any reverse move can push back stock prices down," he said.

"Both interest rates and oil prices remain high. Investors who bought {Wednesday} are ready to dump the shares at any moment," he added.

A broad range of large-capital shares were targets of institutional and dealer buying. These interest rate-sensitive shares had plunged in the past week on interest rate surges. But institutions such as life insurance companies decided these shares had neared bottom, traders said.

All of the Big Five steel makers climbed above 500 yen ($3.32) Wednesday, with Kawasaki Steel up 32 yen to 514 yen and Nippon Steel up 41 yen to 531 yen.

Among shipbuilders, IHI was up 55 yen to 900 yen, while Mitsubishi Heavy Industries gained 25 yen to 855 yen.

Construction shares also advanced. Ohbayashi increased 70 yen to 1410 yen and Shimizu rose 100 yen to 1750 yen.

Resource shares advanced strongly on rising oil prices. But the issues finished well below intraday highs as the report of increased oil production by Saudi Arabia prompted selling in the afternoon session, traders said.

Mitsubishi Oil gained 50 yen to 1320 yen.

Nippon Oil was up 70 yen to 1450 yen. Teikoku Oil, which owns a natural gas field in Japan, finished at 1820 yen, up 300 yen.

Meanwhile, "confusion remains extremely high in world equity markets," said Paul Harrison, a vice president at Salomon Brothers International in London.

"Money managers don't have a clear view on how the Middle East situation will resolve itself over the next days or weeks. Volatility is expected to remain high with constant watching of the news tapes for the next five-minute trend," he added.

On balance, the flood of major U.K. corporate news was reasonably good, traders said, but the impact on the market was negligible, given the environment in the Persian Gulf.

Shares opened on a strong footing but were reduced to losses as war rumors flooded the market. However, stocks were driven from midafternoon lows on an early burst on Wall Street.

The Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100 Share Index gained 1.7 points to 2237.5. The Financial Times 30 Share Index edged up 0.1 point to 1748.5. Volume was 429.2 million shares compared with 497.5 million Tuesday.

Throughout most of the session, investors awaited remarks from President Bush on the deployment of U.S. troops in Saudi Arabia. These remarks were followed 90 minutes later by news from Iraq that it is annexing Kuwait.

The London market initially rose on these comments, relieved the announcements "weren't of greater magnitude," said one trader. However, upon further reflection, some said the developments thrust the spotlight back to the U.S., which has said that one of its objectives in the Middle East is to free Kuwait from Iraqi occupation.

Oils stocks did well, with British Petroleum advancing 2 pence to 364 pence ($6.82), Enterprise Oil rising 3 pence to 693 pence, and Shell up 2 pence at 491 pence. Shell and BP report second-quarter results Thursday.

Even Ultramar, after reporting net profit slumped 42% to #36.1 million for the first half, rose. The oil producer and refiner's shares gained 7 pence to 354 pence.

Dealers said part of Ladbroke Group's 10-pence fall to 291 pence is a perception that tourists would shy away from using its Hilton hotels in the Middle East because of the tensions there, dealers said.

Standard Chartered gained 14 pence to 402 pence after reporting pretax profit of #110 million for the first half. The bank also maintained the dividend at 12.5 pence a share, after earlier profit warnings.

Meanwhile, share prices closed lower in most European cities: Paris, Frankfurt, Zurich, Amsterdam, Stockholm and Madrid. Stocks ended higher in Brussels and Milan. South African gold stocks gained.

Investors in Asian-Pacific stocks moved to grab bargains, sparking a turnaround on most of the region's markets. But analysts noted a lack of commitment among investors, many of whom seemed ready to sell at any hint of escalation in the gulf crisis. Stocks closed higher in Sydney, Hong Kong, Singapore and Wellington, were mixed in Manila, and lower in Taipei and Seoul.
