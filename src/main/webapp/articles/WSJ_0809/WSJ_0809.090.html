Abreast of the Market: Industrials End Six-Day Decline, Rising 24.26 as Mideast Fears Ease

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 08/09/90

NEW YORK -- Setting aside its concerns about the turmoil in the Middle East, the stock market advanced for the first time this month as investors went looking for bargains. But the worries about recession that have intensified since Iraq invaded Kuwait and oil prices soared continued to hold sway over the market. Shares of companies expected to deliver better earnings in any economic climate paced the market's reversal.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average broke a six-session losing streak by gaining 24.26, or 0.9%, to 2734.90. The average had fallen a total of 7.1% during its string of setbacks, with most of the loss following the invasion.

Among broader measures, Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index climbed 3.52 to 338.35, the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index gained 1.95 to 185.40 and the Dow Jones Equity Market Index gained 3.48 to 313.56. Advancing issued totaled 1,015, compared with about 601 decliners.

Although trading remained active, Big Board volume failed to surpass 200 million shares for the first time since the invasion. Volume reached 190,400,000 shares, compared with 231,580,000 Tuesday.

Analysts said the U.S. decision to send troops to Saudi Arabia -- in what President Bush and administration officials described as purely a defensive role -- eased the market's fears that the Middle East crisis would worsen.

"Bush gained a lot of respect, and I think he's got a lot of people rooting for him now," said William M. LeFevre, a market strategist at Advest Inc., Hartford, Conn. "Investors are convinced that the problem has been contained."

Share prices rose throughout the morning and peaked at midday as investors digested the statements from Bush, along with statements from Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein saying he would seek "full unity" of Iraq and Kuwait.

Meanwhile, the rise in oil prices collapsed after the Saudis said they would increase their oil production to offset losses resulting from the international boycott of oil from Iraq and Kuwait. Crude-oil futures fell sharply in New York trading, with September contracts on West Texas Intermediate losing $2.35 a barrel to $25.96.

The Dow Jones Transportation Average rose 26.64 to 991.42, its first gain since July 26. A rebound in UAL, whose stock had fallen 38% since the average last recorded a gain, accounted for most of the latest move.

The plunge in oil prices took its toll on energy stocks. Exxon fell 1 5/8 to 51 3/4 on trading of two million shares, Texaco dropped 1 7/8 to 62 3/4 on 1.7 million shares and Chevron went down 1 3/4 to 76 7/8; those losses depressed the industrial average by about 10 1/2 points.

In response to the Saudis' promise to boost oil production, Dean Witter Reynolds recommended that investors cut their cash levels in half, to 10% from 20%. The firm boosted its recommended stock and bond allocations by five percentage points each, to 70% and 20%, respectively.

John D. Connolly, chairman of the firm's investment committee, said the recommended cash position had been raised from zero to 20% immediately after the Iraqi invasion. As a result of the Saudi decision, "The dire outlooks about recession will have to be rethought," Mr. Connolly said.

Investors snapped up consumer stocks with a track record of earnings improvement, along with other growth stocks.

Philip Morris added 3/8 to 45 5/8 on three million shares, Coca-Cola gained 1 5/8 to 44 and Procter & Gamble jumped 2 3/4 to 80.

Boeing rallied 1 to 53 on two million shares and Waste Management rose 1 1/2 to 39 1/8 on 1.5 million shares.

SmithKline Beecham equity units rose 3 1/2 to 46 7/8, pacing an across-the-board rally among drug stocks, as 1.3 million issues were traded. The British-based company reported a 6% increase in second-quarter pretax profit from a year earlier.

Alza climbed 2 1/2 to 43 1/4 on the American Stock Exchange. The Food and Drug Administration granted marketing approval to a treatment for chronic pain which uses Alza's drug-delivery system to administer a drug produced by a unit of Johnson & Johnson.

Johnson & Johnson rose 1 7/8 to 67 1/8. Among other major pharmaceutical issues, Merck rose 2 1/8 to 85 1/8, Bristol-Myers Squibb rose 1 3/8 to 61 1/2, Schering-Plough gained 1 1/4 to 46 1/2, American Home Products went up 2 1/8 to 47 7/8 and Eli Lilly added 2 5/8 to 78 3/8 on 1.5 million shares.

UAL jumped 13 1/2 to 114 1/2, breaking a seven-session losing streak in which the stock plunged a total of 62 points. American Banker reported that the union-led group seeking to acquire United Air Lines' parent company in a $4.38 billion buy-out will ask the UAL board for another 30 to 90 days to obtain financing.

Woolworth, which posted a 4.2% increase in second-quarter earnings, advanced 1 1/2 to 28 7/8. Other retailing issues to post sharp gains included Wal-Mart Stores, which went up 1 5/8 to 30 1/4 on 2.3 million shares; Toys "R" Us, up 1 1/2 to 28 1/2; Gap Inc., up 2 3/4 to 53 3/4; and U.S. Shoe, up 1 to 15 1/2.

MCA gained 1 1/2 to 47 and CBS climbed 6 3/4 to 176 after Morgan Stanley cited the two companies as potential takeover targets for Walt Disney. Analyst Alan Kassan said in a report to the firm's sales force that he thinks Disney is considering a major acquisition, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report. Both Disney and MCA declined comment.

Other media and entertainment stocks also rallied, including Disney, which rose 4 5/8 to 108. Eastman Kodak rose 1 1/8 to 41 1/8 on 1.5 million shares, Paramount Communications went up 7/8 to 37 1/4, Time Warner rose 1 to 85 3/4 and Mattel added 1 3/8 to 17 7/8.

Ethyl rose 1 5/8 to 30. The company told analysts that General Motors endorsed its new gasoline additive in a letter delivered to the Environmental Protection Agency, according to the investor report. The EPA is expected to decide by Nov. 5 whether to approve the additive, designed to cut auto emissions.

Pep Boys Manny, Moe & Jack added 3/4 to 13 3/4. Standard & Poor's is adding the issue to the S&P 500 after Monday's close to replace General Instrument, the target of a buy-out by Forstmann, Little & Co.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 0.19 to 340.03. Volume totaled 15,020,000 shares.
