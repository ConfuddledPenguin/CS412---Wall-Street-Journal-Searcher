Stocks Gain as Oil Prices Slump; Treasury Bond Auction Goes Poorly --- Credit Markets: Signs Are Worrisome For 30-Year Auction

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 08/09/90

NEW YORK -- Turmoil in the Persian Gulf reverberated once again through the credit markets yesterday, disrupting the Treasury's big quarterly auction of government securities. "It was a horror show," said F. Ward McCarthy Jr., a managing director at Stone & McCarthy Research Associates. "This auction was an absolute disaster. The bidding was sloppy, and it looks like a lot of people tried to miss the auction."

The disappointment in yesterday's auction spilled over onto the broader bond market, causing prices to gyrate wildly. The market recovered later in the day with the benchmark 30-year Treasury bond posting gains of about 3/8 point, or $3.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount.

The Treasury had difficulty selling $10.5 billion of ten-year notes yesterday in the second portion of the government's three-part $32.25 billion quarterly refunding of the federal debt. The ten-year note sale followed a weak three-year note auction on Tuesday.

Analysts said investors and dealers avoided the auction due to fears that spiraling oil prices and Mideast turmoil will lead to rising inflation -- the bane of the bond market. The poor auction results also reflect the lack of buying by Japanese and other foreign investors.

"When I called some of my clients overseas, they seemed to suggest that U.S. rates are not that attractive and not worth the currency risk," said Maria Fiorini Ramirez, president of Maria Ramirez Capital Consultants Inc.

Foreign institutional investors -- especially from Japan and the Middle East -- have been big buyers of U.S. Treasury securities at previous auctions. But in recent months, yields on government bonds in Japan have risen sharply and are now nearly as high as U.S. yields. As a result, Japanese investors are keeping their cash at home. Investors from the Middle East, preoccupied with more important matters, didn't show up in a big way either.

At yesterday's Treasury auction, the average yield on the 10-year notes was 8.77%. But because there wasn't enough demand at the average yield, the Treasury was forced to accept bids as high as 8.84% to complete the auction.

The uncommonly wide spread between the average and high yield, called a "tail," is an indication that bond dealers were reluctant to bid for the securities. The tail at yesterday's auction was the longest since May 1980, according to Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

"Dealers did not bid aggressively because they did not see any interest from their client base," said Lawrence Leuzzi, a trading manager at S.G. Warburg & Co. (Only a small group of brokerage firms and commercial banks are allowed to bid at Treasury auctions. These firms then resell the securities to customers. Individuals, however, can purchase securities directly from the Treasury through "noncompetitive" tenders.)

Another indication that the Treasury had difficulty selling its securities was the "bid-to-cover" ratio, which measures the number of bids the Treasury receives for each one it accepts. At yesterday's sale, bid-to-cover ratio was 1.59, the lowest since 1978, according to Dean Witter. The bid-to-cover ratio averaged 2.37 at the 12 previous 10-year note auctions and was 3.0 at the previous 10-year note sale in May.

William Brachfeld, manager of trading at the New York office of Japan's Daiwa Securities Inc., estimates that the "Big Four" Japanese securities firms bought 15% of the ten-year notes, down from 25% to 30% at recent 10-year auctions. And much of yesterday's purchases went into speculative trading accounts, not long-term investment accounts.

Shortly after the auction ended, dealers who bought the bonds were forced to cut prices in an attempt to attract buyers. As prices slid, traders said that demand suddenly materialized.

"Some investors were afraid to buy at the auction, but when the average yield reached 8.80%, investors found that to be very attractive," said Thomas Murphy, a managing director at Salomon Brother Inc.

Noncompetitive bids at yesterday's auction, the best indication of demand from individual investors and small institutions, totaled $560 million compared with $659 million at the previous 10-year note auction in May.

The auction results sent shock waves through the bond market and caused prices to abruptly reverse directions. Bond prices were 1/4 point higher before the auction results were announced, then fell 1/4 point when the government released the auction statistics at 2:30 p.m. EDT. The market improved later in the day and prices ended mixed.

The Treasury's quarterly auction continues today with the sale of $10.25 billion of 30-year bonds.

Economists and dealers are not optimistic about the prospects of today's auction. "If {investors and dealers} are running away from the 10-year notes like this, one can only wonder what's going to happen when it comes time to buy the {30-year} bond," said Robert Brusca, chief economist at Nikko Securities.

Treasury Securities

Treasury prices ended mixed yesterday, with short-term notes posting modest losses while long-term bonds posted gains.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of about 99 3/32 to yield 8.82% compared with 98 23/32 on Tuesday to yield 8.86%. The new three-year notes, which were auctioned Tuesday, ended yesterday at a price of 99 17/32, down from 101 3/32 on Tuesday. The issue's yield rose to 8.16% from 8.14%.

Prices of the old ten-year notes ended at 100 27/32, up from 100 21/32 on Tuesday. The yield fell to 8.72% from 8.75%.

Corporate, Junk Bonds

The junk-bond market disregarded stock market gains and ended weaker yesterday.

Although the Dow Jones Industrial Average closed almost 25 points higher at 2734.90, most junk-bond prices finished between 1/4 and 3/8 points lower. Junk bonds are high-yield high-risk corporate securities that have low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all.

In the investment-grade corporate debt market, high-grade corporate bonds closed unchanged to slightly higher.

Foreign Bonds

Japanese government bonds ended sharply higher as short-covering in the final hour of trading developed into a mad scramble to buy Japanese government bonds and futures contracts.

The rally was triggered by news that Saudi Arabia plans to increase production of oil. Fears that oil shortages would lead to higher prices and spiraling inflation has shaken bond markets around the world, but the Japanese market was hurt the hardest.

The hectic short-covering drove down the yield on Japan's benchmark 10-year bond below 8%. The jump in prices quickly pushed developments in the Persian Gulf into the background as traders feared the market would get away from them.

"Traders didn't have time to think about the situation in the Middle East," said one dealer at a major Japanese commercial bank. For traders taking positions day-to-day the late surge in prices "was very fearful," he said.

The benchmark No. 119 10-year bond ended at a price of 83.97, up 0.93 point. The issue's yield fell 0.20 percentage point to 7.875%. (When calculated using the semiannual compound method, the yield on the No. 119 ended at 7.29%, according to Salomon Brothers Inc.)

The heavy buying hit futures first and quickly spread to the cash market. "Then there was massive buying by securities houses," said one dealer, especially by the so-called Big Four Japanese securities firms. "People went on a sort of panic buying spree" as prices started to pick up in the middle of the afternoon and "shorts were afraid the market was moving away from them."

Dealers and professional traders, betting that prices will fall, sometimes sell borrowed securities and hope to profit by replacing what was borrowed with cheaper securities. That is called a short sale. Short-covering occurs when the dealers buy securities to replace what they borrowed.

Bond prices ended weaker in Europe, although trading was generally light in most markets. French government bonds fell sharply as market participants remained gripped by the Middle East crisis. But prices were just moderately lower in West Germany and Britain.

Municipal Bonds

Municipal bonds were generally flat to 1/4 point lower.

The market's downward bias was primarily because of swings in Treasurys and continued inflation worries.

Among actively traded issues, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% securities of 2018 finished virtually unchanged at 97 3/4, with the yield falling to 7.37% from 7.38% late Tuesday.

In new issue activity, $600 million of Massachusetts general obligation bond anticipation notes were priced by a Goldman, Sachs & Co. group.

The notes, which are due Oct. 17, 1990, are 7% issues priced to yield 5.95%.

Mortgage-, Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities ended unchanged to slightly lower, after once more following Treasurys in a session dominated again by oil prices and the crisis in the Middle East.

The market's volatility didn't stem a rush of new issuance in the real estate mortgage investment conduit and assetbacked sectors.

Three Remic issues totaling $1.3 billion and two asset-backed deals totaling $660 million were priced this session.

In the secondary market, Government National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues were off 1/32 at 98 14/32, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9.5% issues were off 1/32 at 98 5/32 and Federal National Mortgage Association 9.5% issues were also down 1/32, at 98 22/32.

In the market for new Remics, details were announced for a $500.25 million Fannie Mae deal through Kidder, Peabody & Co. that is backed by Fannie Mae 9.5% 30-year securities; a $300 million Fannie Mae issue through Citicorp that uses Fannie Mae 9% 30-year securities and a $500 million issue of collateralized mortgage obligation Remics through Salomon Brothers Inc. that uses Ginnie Mae 9% 30-year securities.

As for the asset-backed sector, two of the many deals that are expected to be priced in the near-term were priced yesterday.

There was a $500 million issue of Discover Card securities backed by credit-card receivables. The securities have a coupon rate of 8.75% and were priced at 99.765 to yield 8.87% through Dean Witter Reynolds Inc.

The underlying credit-card receivables were sold by Discover Receivables Financing Corp., a unit of Sears, Roebuck & Co., and are being serviced by Greenwood Trust Co.

In the second offering, $160 million of Southeast Bank securities backed by credit-card receivables have a coupon rate of 8.60% and were priced at 99 21/32 to yield 8.762% through Goldman, Sachs & Co.

The seller and servicer of the underlying loans is Southeast Bank, a unit of Miami-based Southeast Banking Corp.
