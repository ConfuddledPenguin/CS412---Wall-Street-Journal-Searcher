Japanese Investors in U.S. Firms Grow More Willing to Take Minority Stakes

  • Author: Michael R. Sesit
  • Date: 08/09/90

Japanese buyers of U.S. companies are increasingly preferring to ride in the back seat rather than take the driver's seat. After displaying a marked preference for obtaining controlling stakes in U.S. companies, Japanese corporate buyers are more willing to accept minority equity positions when investing in the U.S.

Of 103 takeovers and related transactions in the first half of this year, 42, or 41%, were minority investments, according to Ulmer Brothers Inc., a New York-based merchant bank specializing in Japanese investments. That compares with 29 minority investments in the comparable period of 1989 and nine for 1988.

Overall, Japanese direct investment in the U.S. continued to grow: Japanese investors paid $3.91 billion for their 103 publicly reported acquisitions and investments. That's up 39% from $2.79 billion for stakes in 74 U.S. companies in the first half of 1989, according to an Ulmer Brothers report.

However, analysts noted that the amount was still below the $5.99 billion that the Japanese invested in U.S. companies in the first half of 1988. Moreover, some said there was a good chance that investment for 1990 would be below the $13.7 billion total for 1989. They noted that Japanese companies have shown a growing appetite for acquisitions in Europe and Asia.

Japanese portfolio investment in American securities has also been falling. In the first quarter, Japanese investors sold a net $700 million of U.S. stocks and unloaded $3.9 billion of U.S. Treasury securities. Analysts believe the trend has continued since then.

Japanese foreign portfolio investment, which involves the buying and selling of stocks and bonds, is normally much more volatile than their overseas investments in companies, which tend to be long-term.

"Political sensitivity is the prime factor for the increase in minority stakes," said Ko-Yung Tung, a New York-based partner at O'Melveny & Myers who specializes in U.S.-Japanese business. He added that Japanese government officials have been jawboning Japanese companies "to have a lower profile in foreign investments, particularly in the U.S."

Mr. Tung said, "We have gone from trade friction to investment friction." The root cause of this tension lies in the expanding overall presence of Japanese multinational corporations in the U.S.

But Mr. Tung said the most recent strains can be attributed to "the one-two punch" of Sony Corp.'s $3.45 billion acquisition of Columbia Pictures Entertainment Inc. late last year followed immediately by Mitsubishi Estate Co.'s purchase of 51% of Rockefeller Group Inc. for $846 million. That gave Mitsubishi a minority stake in Rockefeller Center in New York.

In addition, some earlier big Japanese investments haven't worked out so well, contend analysts. "Some Japanese companies have found that they simply paid too high a price for U.S. acquisitions, something that has also been noticed by the Japanese corporate community at large," said Daniel M. Schwartz, managing director at Ulmer Brothers.

For instance, he said that many American investment bankers contend that Bridgestone Corp. paid too much in its $2.6 billion acquisition of Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. in 1988. Shortly after the deal closed, a major Firestone customer, General Motors Corp., began phasing out the tire maker as a supplier, except for the new Saturn division.

Another factor is that more small and medium-sized Japanese companies are investing abroad for the first time. That, Mr. Schwartz said, accounts for the increase in smaller transactions, especially those valued at less than $20 million, as well as the growth in minority investments. Japanese companies, even very big ones, often prefer to experiment with an initial small investment when venturing overseas for the first time.

Overall, Japanese merger activity in the first half concentrated on the computer industry, followed by chemicals and financial services. California, accounting for 31 deals, remained the most popular investment location, followed by New York, Pennsylvania, Washington, Massachusetts, Hawaii and New Jersey.

Despite this healthy pace, Mr. Tung of O'Melveny & Myers and others believe that by year end the picture may change. They contend that while the number of Japanese acquisitions and investments in the U.S. will remain the same or increase, their dollar value will decline.

They say the aggregate value of all transactions can be skewed by a single mega-deal. And "these transactions are so visible that many Japanese companies are loath to make these kind of investments in the U.S.," said Mr. Tung. "Rather than being accused of taking over corporate America, they will take a lesser percentage to appear as co-investors with Americans."

Moreover, merger experts said Japan's corporations are increasingly turning their attention to Europe to take advantage of the planned abolition of trade and investment barriers between the 12 countries of the European Community in 1992 and the recent opening of Eastern Europe.

"The bloom is off the flower in terms of Japanese investment in the U.S.; they're tired of America already," said Mr. Tung. Besides Europe, he said, Japanese companies are increasing their direct investment in Asia, "their backyard." He pointed specifically to Japanese investment in Thailand and to Japan's increased reliance on Indonesia as a supplier of oil.

Mr. Schwartz argued that "the U.S. will remain the major recipient of Japanese overseas acquisition funds, but European candidates are looming larger in the minds of potential Japanese acquirers."
