Letters to the Editor: Playing It Straight Helps Teen Addicts

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/09/90

Kenneth H. Bacon's July 23 article on drug-abuse treatment so clearly points out the need for a re-evaluation of the approach to funding treatment. I have a 19-year-old son who is making remarkable progress in his recovery from drug addiction and alcoholism through the efforts of the Straight New England program in Stoughton, Mass. Yet, despite the fact that he (and our family) will get a year's intensive treatment, much of it the functional equivalent of conventional inpatient treatment, for a cost comparable with 60 days of traditional residential programs, and despite the fact that the rate of sobriety among adolescents two years after graduation from Straight is at least as good as conventional treatment, it has been impossible so far to get significant reimbursement from my company's health insurer.

As Mr. Bacon's article points out, adolescents need longer treatment than adults; programs like Straight New England provide this treatment at reasonable cost.

The insurance-reimbursement process seems to create an incentive to look first to conventional treatment, even if it's neither clinically more effective nor preferred by client or family.

Only after exhausting available benefits is there a (financial) reason to try a clinically and economically effective nontraditional program such as Straight New England.

This is a waste. And all it requires to be rectified is a willingness and procedure for insurers to evaluate and accept evolving cost and clinically effective treatment programs promptly.

Richard S. Carey

Portsmouth, N.H.
