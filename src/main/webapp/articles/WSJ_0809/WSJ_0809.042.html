The Corn Caper: Did North Korea Seek U.S. Shipment? --- Rumor in Grain-Trading Pits Says Buyer Got Spooked When Word Leaked Out

  • Author: Scott Kilman
  • Date: 08/09/90

CHICAGO -- Did North Korea try to buy U.S. corn? That's the summer's most intriguing tidbit in the grain-trading pits here, where brokers use a simple strategy: buy on rumor, sell on fact.

Many rumors never pan out. But this time the details, sketchy as they are, are enough to have grain exporters worrying privately that one of them might have unknowingly broken the law by helping the hard-line Communist government.

"It is strictly taboo," said an official of one major grain firm. U.S. firms are banned from doing business with North Korea by the Trading With the Enemy Act. In recent years, transgressions have been on the mundane side, involving travel agents and tour operators arranging trips to three other countries on the enemies list: Vietnam, Cambodia and Cuba.

Grain, however, is a political powder keg.

Washington got upset in the 1970s when U.S. grain merchants quietly sold large amounts of crops to the Soviet Union. Amid soaring food prices in the U.S., Congress required grain exporters to disclose their sales to the government.

"This one might have fallen through the cracks," said an export expert at the Agriculture Department, which tracks overseas grain sales.

A spokesman at the Treasury Department, which enforces the trading ban, said it is aware of the North Korean report but hasn't any evidence of a misdeed. The department can grant a license to ship goods but usually it does so only for humanitarian purposes.

Policing the grain trade is difficult. The origin of a shipment such as corn can easily be lost as it changes hands. And attractive deals can pop up in the most unlikely places. For example, several U.S. grain merchants have received overtures to buy rice from Vietnam, which has had an usually large harvest.

Here's the North Korea rumor: Because that country can't openly buy corn from the U.S., a Singapore dealer is thought to have acted as the middleman to arrange shipment. While the corn was en route, however, word about it started to pop up in the West. The North Korean government, perhaps anxious about how it might look for it to need corn from the U.S., got spooked and scrapped the deal, the story goes.

Traders believe that North Korea eventually got the corn it needed from China.
