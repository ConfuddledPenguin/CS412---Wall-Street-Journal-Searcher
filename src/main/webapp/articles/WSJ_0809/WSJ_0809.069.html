Letters to the Editor: Episcopalians Happily Avoiding Extinction

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/09/90

Let us hope your readers got past the eye-catching but irrelevant headline on Wade Clark Roof's July 20 editorial-page article highlighting some trends in U.S. religion -- "The Episcopalian Goes the Way of the Dodo." The headline was not only misleading but, happy to say, erroneous. Those interested in statistics might reflect on the following: There are about 2.5 million Episcopalians in the U.S., in 7,500 congregations (a number that has stayed fairly constant), with 8,200 full-time priests in 100 dioceses. Far from extinct, wouldn't you say? And, Sunday attendance is up 24.7% since 1974 in Episcopal churches, which have the highest per capita of giving of all the mainline churches.

It's also interesting to note that George Gallup, whose data was cited in the table accompanying the article, said in an overview of a recent study done by the Gallup Organization of "The Spiritual Health of the Episcopal Church," that the church is "substantially orthodox . . . committed to growth . . . open to change . . . and {wanting more involvement in} ecology, matters of justice, war and peace."

Headline aside, thanks for running the article and for the challenge it contains for all of the churches, to quote: "The religiously non-affiliated have grown to 9% today from 2% in the early 1950s. . . . Many within this group have dropped out from the mainline Protestant and Catholic churches. . . ."

This time, as we approach the millennium, is the Decade of Evangelism for the Anglican Communion, the Roman Catholic Church, and others as well. We have a saving message to share. Let us get beyond the numbers game and our concerns about "how we are doing" as institutions and share the Good News in word and deed.

The Rev. A. Wayne Schwab

Evangelism Officer

The Episcopal Church

New York

Regarding Yale President Ezra Stiles's erroneous prediction in 1783 that a future America would be "divided about equally among Congregationalists, Presbyterians and Episcopalians," Thomas Jefferson's crystal ball was even more clouded.

Writing to a friend in 1822, Jefferson said, "I rejoice that in this blessed country of free inquiry and belief, which has surrendered its creed and conscience to neither kings nor priests, the genuine doctrine of only one God is reviving, and I trust that there is not a young man now living in these United States who will not die as Unitarian."

Lyda Smayling

Minneapolis
