Pacific Enterprises Says Large Charge Led to $229 Million Second-Period Loss

  • Author: Frederick Rose
  • Date: 08/09/90

LOS ANGELES -- Pacific Enterprises, stumbling in its effort to diversify, said it took $275 million in charges on its drugstore and oil operations, resulting in a $229 million second-quarter net loss. The loss, equivalent to $3.34 a share, compared with net income of $53 million, or 78 cents a share, in the year-earlier quarter. Revenue rose 3.8% to $1.60 billion from $1.55 billion.

Pacific Enterprises stock closed at $39.75, up $1.125, yesterday in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

However, analysts said the earnings report included disquieting elements, including apparently growing problems for the company's retailing operations. "The indication is that the problems there are a lot worse than some of us had anticipated," said Janet Dzwierzynski, an analyst at Duff & Phelps Inc., Chicago.

Pacific Enterprises disclosed, among other things, that its head of retailing resigned effective July 31, that it may close as many as 10% of its 1,070 drug and sporting-goods stores, and that further major management changes may be in store.

Based in Los Angeles, Pacific Enterprises is the parent of Southern California Gas Co., the nation's largest gas-distribution operation. It acquired Thrifty Corp., a chain of drug and sporting-goods stores, in 1986 for about $800 million in stock, a transaction that left many analysts leary at the time. Pacific Enterprises also has built up its oil and gas operations over a period of years.

The charges include $175 million in write-downs of Pacific Enterprises' large oil and natural-gas operations. The writedowns, which were anticipated by analysts, are required under accounting rules as a result of low oil and gas prices prevailing at June 30. The company said it wouldn't have to have taken the charge at today's elevated prices. However, once taken, the charges can't be reversed.

In many ways, the write-down of oil and gas assets "removed a sword that had been hanging over the company's head," said David N. Fleischer, an analyst at Prudential-Bache Securities Inc.

Far more surprising was the $100 million in write-downs for retailing operations. More than half of that amount was due to anticipated closings of between 5% and 10% of the company's stores over the next two years, a spokesman said. The remainder is for write-downs to store inventories.

Pacific Enterprises said that Daniel A. Seigel, 48 years old, an executive vice president of the parent company, was named president and chief operating officer of Thrifty effective immediately. He succeeds Richard G. Eils, 52, president and chief executive officer of the unit, who resigned "to pursue other interests."

According to a spokesman, Pacific Enterprises is conducting a search for a number of new senior executives for Thrifty. He didn't say how many. The new employees may include a new chief executive officer, a post that has been left vacant in the wake of Mr. Eils' resignation.

Setting aside the write-down, Pacific Enterprises retail operations showed a profit of $2 million, down 82% from $11 million a year earlier. Pacific Enterprises cautioned that "disappointing retailing earnings are expected to persist through the remainder of the year."

Excluding its $175 million charge, the company's oil and gas operations had profit of $16 million, a 20% decline from $20 million a year earlier. The latest period's profit includes the benefit of reduced depletion expense from the write-down, a benefit that analysts said will boost reported profit in future periods. Cash flow, however, fell in the latest quarter to $43 million from $53 million a year earlier.

The only operation to show a higher profit was the utility, which earnings rose 7.4% to $58 million in the latest quarter from $54 million a year earlier.

In the first half, the net loss totaled $161 million, or $2.44 a share, compared with net income of $120 million, or $1.80 a share, a year earlier. Revenue increased 2.9% to $3.51 billion from $3.41 billion.

The latest results underscored the continuing questions about Pacific Enterprises, analysts said, as to whether -- and when -- the company can make its diversification efforts pay off.

Prudential-Bache's Mr. Fleischer sees at least one hopeful sign. Pacific Enterprises has halted the series of retail and oil acquisitions that added to its woes. "It seems that they're going to stop digging a deeper hole and deal with the hole they're in," he said.
