GORBACHEV GAVE himself two years to improve the Soviet Union's economy. On the third day of a Communist Party congress, the Soviet president and party leader told a TV reporter, "I think that in two years, if there are no changes, this leadership must go." It was the first time he set a deadline for turning around the country's economic and social problems. Contrary to some expectations, the congress hasn't turned into an all-out attack on Gorbachev, though both he and his Politburo colleagues have taken sharp criticism. Instead, moderates and hard-liners are engaged in a painful search for ways to ensure the future existence of the party.

"We need to retain socialism, but how?" asked one delegate from the Ukraine. "Stalin built his model. Now we don't like that, but we have no new model. We're in an ideological crisis."

Secretary of State Baker proposed that 24 Western nations establish a Central American aid program to help "broaden the support for democracy and development." Several countries said they would consider the idea. The proposal came as the two dozen countries agreed to offer aid to four Eastern European nations.

---

Albania's officials stated they would meet with a representative of foreign embassies sheltering over 200 Albanian asylum-seekers in Tirana, the capital. In Vienna, diplomats said embassies were told that people seeking to leave would get passports. Mobs had stormed the embassies by crashing trucks through gates or scaling walls.

---

Iran's president said his nation had moved closer to peace with Iraq over the past few months. Rafsanjani remarked that he felt "the atmosphere between the two countries has improved." On Tuesday, Iranian and Iraqi foreign ministers held their first direct meeting since an August 1988 cease-fire that ended an eight-year war.

---

Bush plans to propose, at a NATO summit opening today in London, that Soviet leader Gorbachev be invited to address the Western military alliance, according to diplomatic sources. The U.S. also is recommending that Moscow and the Warsaw Pact be allowed to send permanent observers to NATO headquarters.

---

Thatcher welcomed Mandela to London, three years after she denounced the African National Congress as a terrorist organization. The British prime minister and South African black nationalist leader called their three-hour meeting a success despite disagreement about economic sanctions and the ANC's commitment to armed struggle.

---

East Germany agreed to Bonn's agenda for political union by proposing Dec. 2 for the first free vote in a united Germany since 1932, preparing the way for talks to formally end German division. West Germans viewed the accord as a sign that East Berlin's leadership has overcome internal opposition to swift unification.

---

Studies found that aging men and cancer patients can benefit from hormonal treatments. The increase in fat tissue and muscular atrophy seen in many elderly men can be reversed by injections of human growth hormone, one report said. Another study concluded that a sex hormone helped cancer patients gain weight.

---

A government panel blamed the pilot for a USAir crash at New York's LaGuardia Airport last Sept. 20 that killed two passengers. The National Transportation Safety Board said the captain failed to detect a rudder-control problem as he taxied for takeoff, and then failed to correct the situation aggressively.

---

Liberia's president met with U.S. Ambassador Peter de Vos in Monrovia as rebels tightened their grip on the capital. Washington officials said Doe declined an offer for help in fleeing the city, which has been without electricity and water for over a week.

---

The Energy Department estimates the cost of cleaning up its nuclear-arms plants and other facilities soon could rise to $6 billion a year -- about twice as much as the current level. The department said it could cost $31.6 billion from fiscal 1992 through fiscal 1996 to remove, treat and store waste from nearly 100 sites.

---

The New Jersey Supreme Court ordered two Princeton University eating clubs to admit women as members. The court's ruling came in an 11-year-old lawsuit filed by a former Princeton student who claimed that the clubs' males-only policies discriminated against women.

---

A constitutional crisis was defused in Taiwan as the ruling Nationalist Party government and opposition leaders agreed on democratic changes, including a popular vote for president to end four decades of one-party domination. Under the accord, native Taiwanese will be given more political power.
