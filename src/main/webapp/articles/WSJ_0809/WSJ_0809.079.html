U.S. Investigates Alleged Dumping By Japanese Firms

  • Author: Eduardo Lachica
  • Date: 08/09/90

WASHINGTON -- The Commerce Department is investigating charges of alleged "dumping" of advanced electronic displays by Japanese firms. The inquiry is opposed by some U.S. computer makers that use such components in their portable and laptop models. The department is acting on a complaint by U.S. display producers that 12 Japanese firms, including Sharp Corp., Matsushita Electric Industrial Co. and Hitachi Ltd. are selling these displays to U.S. users at far lower prices than their home-market or production costs.

If the department verifies these charges and the International Trade Commission determines that such dumping is hurting U.S. display producers, the U.S. will be required to impose anti-dumping duties on these Japanese imports to offset whatever price advantage they may have over domestic products.

The Advanced Display Manufacturers of America, the trade group that filed the complaint, said it conservatively estimates the value of the Japanese imports at more than $90 million annually. The seven small or medium-sized producers that make up the ADMA also claim that the Japanese have 90% of the U.S. market for these displays.

The ADMA petition has drawn sharp opposition from Apple Computer Inc., Compaq Computer Corp. and Tandy Corp., which warned the ITC Tuesday that higher duties for Japanese displays could price their own models out of the market. Lawyers for International Business Machines Corp. said the firm is preparing a statement also opposing the ADMA petition.

The products involved in the petition are thin-panel displays, using such technologies as active-matrix liquid crystal displays, electroluminescence and gas plasma. They are mostly used for laptop computers and aircraft cockpit displays, but there is potentially a more lucrative market in high-definition television.

The Japanese companies, seeking to have the petition dismissed, have challenged the U.S. producers' ability to adequately supply the American market. "The petitioners do not have the technology, the production know-how or the plant capacity that the commercial market requires," Lawrence Walders, a counsel for the Japanese firms, said in a statement to the ITC.

The other Japanese suppliers named in the petition are Fujitsu Ltd.; Hosiden Electronics Co.; Kyocera Corp.; Matsushita Electronics Corp., a unit of Matsushita Electric Industrial; NEC Corp.; Optrex Corp.; Seiko Epson Corp. and Seiko Instruments & Electronics Ltd., both units of Hattori Seiko Corp.; and Toshiba Corp.
