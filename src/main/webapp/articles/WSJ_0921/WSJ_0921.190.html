International: Polly Peck Halts Share Trading As Price Dives

  • Author: Richard L. Hudson
  • Date: 09/21/90

LONDON -- Troubles at Polly Peck International PLC neared a climax as the company's stock price plunged 55% on a flurry of rumors and the company announced plans for a special board meeting today. The company didn't offer any explanation for the price crash, and a spokesman declined to comment on a spate of unsubstantiated market rumors that ranged from debt-payment problems to the imminent resignation of Asil Nadir, the company's chairman and chief executive.

Following a halt in trading of the stock on the London Stock Exchange yesterday, Polly Peck said it had requested the suspension "in view of the effect of the numerous current rumors on the share price." It didn't elaborate. Before the 2:21 p.m. suspension, Polly Peck shares dove by 135 pence, to 108 pence ($2.04). Trading volume was heavy, at 36 million shares.

The stock collapse came as Britain's Serious Fraud Office confirmed that police searched the London offices of South Audley Management Ltd. on Wednesday. South Audley manages property and investments for Nadir family members and family trusts, according to a Polly Peck spokesman. The fraud office didn't disclose the purpose of the search, but numerous London newspapers reported that British authorities are investigating trading by present or former South Audley associates in Polly Peck shares. The Polly Peck spokesman said South Audley "has nothing to do with" Polly Peck itself.

On Aug. 12, Mr. Nadir told a special Polly Peck board meeting that he was considering taking the company private. Mr. Nadir changed his mind just five days later, claiming that some shareholders had protested to him about his plan. Since then, following repeated news reports of the government probe, the company's stock has been sinking.

The London exchange, meanwhile, passed on to the Department of Trade and Industry a report it had compiled on the August events. In it, the exchange criticized Mr. Nadir for inadequately preparing his original bid plans.

Mr. Nadir, a Turkish Cypriot, through the 1980s built the former British textiles company into a mini-conglomerate, with its biggest investments in fruit trading and electronics. Polly Peck bought the tropical-fruit business of RJR Nabisco Inc.'s Del Monte unit and acquired control of Japan's troubled Sansui Electric Co.

Early in the 1980s, the company's stock price had soared -- only to collapse as its earnings growth faltered.
