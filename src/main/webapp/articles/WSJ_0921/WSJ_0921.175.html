Credit Markets: Bond Prices Sink on Reports That Some Leaders In Congress May Try to Delay Spending-Cuts Date

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 09/21/90

NEW YORK -- Reports that some congressional leaders may seek to postpone automatic spending cuts, which are called for under the Gramm-Rudman deficit reduction law, sent jitters through the bond market yesterday. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond fell 3/8 point, a loss of about $3.75 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. Yesterday's decline nearly wiped out Wednesday's rally and pushed the yield on the benchmark bond back up to 9.03%.

Bond prices were little changed during early trading but sank suddenly on news that members of Congress have been discussing the possibility of delaying across-the-board spending cuts to give congressional negotiators more time to work out details of a deficit reduction plan. Unless Congress comes up with a plan, $85 billion in automatic, across-the-board spending cuts are scheduled to go into effect Oct. 1. By Oct. 15, an additional $20 billion could be cut.

Lawrence Leuzzi, a managing director at S.G. Warburg & Co., said the reports were a big disappointment to bond investors, who have been counting on a quick solution to the nation's big budget deficit problem. Among other benefits, a smaller deficit would reduce the size of future government borrowings, which, in turn, could lead to lower interest rates.

"This could be the first chink in the Gramm-Rudman armor that could potentially lead to the unraveling of the legislation," Mr. Leuzzi said. "If they delay Gramm-Rudman a few weeks for the budget negotiations, then next they may want to delay it until the Persian Gulf crisis is resolved. And that would be very negative for the markets."

Relentless increases in oil prices, and reports that additional price rises could be in store, also hurt bond prices.

On the New York Mercantile Exchange, prices of the October crude oil futures contract settled at $34.71 a barrel, up $1.53.

If that wasn't bad enough, the World Bank said yesterday that world oil prices could skyrocket as high as $65 a barrel if the Persian Gulf crisis turns into a shooting war.

Sentiment in the bond market is apparently so bearish that not even plunging stock prices encouraged investors to buy bonds. This was surprising, considering that the bond market often benefits from sharp declines in stock prices, as investors sell stocks and invest the proceeds in Treasury securities, which are considered ultrasafe.

Brian Fabbri, chief economist at Midland Montagu, said the bond market had been anticipating some "bond for stocks" trading, "but that never really materialized." At one point, the Dow Jones Industrial Average was down 50 points, but ended 39.11 points lower at 2518.32.

Mr. Fabbri said the thin trading conditions may have overstated yesterday's bond market decline. "If more people were in the market," he said, "it would have been more balanced in matching bids with offers." Mr. Fabbri said he expects trading volume to be light again today, with few fresh economic developments on which to focus.

Treasury Securities

Treasury securities ended moderately lower to little changed in light trading.

The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 96 30/32, down from 97 9/32 on Wednesday. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction of its price, rose to 9.03% from 9%.

Short-term securities were little changed. The price of two-year notes ended at 100 3/32, to yield 8.05%, unchanged from Wednesday. Rates on Treasury bills were slightly higher. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended at 7.39%, for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.61%, and the rate on six-month bills was 7.32%, for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.69%.

The Treasury sold $10.5 billion of one-year bills yesterday at an average yield of 7.25%. That was down from 7.4% at the previous one-year bill auction Aug. 23 and was the lowest yield since 7.21% at the Jan. 11 auction.

Junk and Corporate Bonds

Falling stock prices weighed on the junk-bond market, driving prices moderately lower.

But while prices of most high-yield, below-investment-grade bonds were 1/2 point lower, the junk bonds issued by casino operators posted larger declines. The casino-bond rout was led by Goldriver Finance Corp., which plunged more than 30 points on news that the company won't meet interest due Nov. 1 on its 13.875% first mortgage bonds. The company also said it may not be able to make other bondholder payments, as well.

That news cast a pall over the entire casino sector, where issues such as Caesars World and Aztar Mortgage Funding Inc. registered losses of as much as one point.

Though markets for Goldriver bonds were wide, high-yield traders quoted the securities at bid prices ranging from 35 to 39, down at least 33 points from Wednesday's closing levels.

Meanwhile, Caesars World 13.5% subordinated notes, due in 1997, slipped 1/2 point to 98 and Aztar Mortgage Funding's 13.5% first mortgage bonds, maturing in 1996, fell one point to 81.

In the high-grade market, bond prices finished unchanged to 1/8 point lower.

Among new issues, Premark International Inc. offered $100 million of notes, which were priced to yield 10.502%. The noncallable notes were priced at a spread of 1.6 percentage points above the Treasury's 10-year note, through underwriters led by Goldman, Sachs & Co.

Municipal Bonds

An unexpected oil price flare-up sent municipals lower late in the session, with tax-exempt bonds unchanged to off 1/4 point in light dealings.

A World Bank report, which estimates that the price of oil could rise to $65 a barrel if a shooting war breaks out in the Persian Gulf, was generally viewed as the impetus for oil's latest rise. After the report, a municipal trader said, "The market's down on oil trading."

Trading remained light, as marketwary investors are continuing to sit on the sidelines, given the lingering uncertainty about the Middle East. "They're very careful," the trader said of investors. "They're involved only if they have to be."

The New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.2% issue due 2018 was 1/8 point lower at 97 to 97 3/8, to yield 7.42%.

In new-issue dealings, a Goldman, Sachs & Co. group set the preliminary pricing for the Metropolitan Transportation Authority, New York's $165.9 million transit and commuter facilities service contract bonds, to yield from 6.4% in 1992 to 8.13% in 2016. Ratings of single-A/triple-B-plus are expected.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities experienced a session of lackluster trading, as many market players were absent because of a religious holiday.

Even though active mortgage issues traded in a tight range for most of the day, mortgage securities lost ground in mid-afternoon, prompted by the sharp rise in oil prices.

Mortgage issues gave up nearly 1/4 point when oil prices shot up. Government National Mortgage Association, or Ginnie Mae, 9 1/2% issues for October delivery were off 5/32 at 98 8/32, down 7/32 from the high of 98 15/32. Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp., or Freddie Mac, Gold 9 1/2% issues and Federal National Mortgage Association, or Fannie Mae, 9 1/2% issues were slightly stronger, down 4/32, at 98 25/32 and 98 18/32, respectively.

In the real estate mortgage investment conduit, or remic, market, Merrill Lynch Capital Markets announced the details of Merrill Lynch Trust 47. The $300 million remic is backed by Ginnie Mae 9% issues and consists of nine classes.

The first two classes are sequential pay classes. A sequential pay is a series of classes whereby cash flows are used to retire each class in order of maturity. Of the remaining seven classes, five are planned amortization classes, or PACs. A remic is a type of security underwritten by Wall Street firms that provides a method of dividing up the income flows from many mortgages into new securities with various interest rates and maturities, to attract banks, insurers and other big investors to the home-mortgage market.

Mortgage-Treasury yield spreads were slightly narrower. Ginnie Mae 9 1/2% issues were yielding 9.91%, assuming a constant annual prepayment rate of 5.5. The yield differential was 100 basis points over the 10-year Treasury note, one basis point narrower than Wednesday. A basis point is equal to 1/100th of one percentage point.

Freddie Mac Gold 9 1/2% issues were yielding 9.82%, based on a constant annual prepayment rate of 6.9, and were at 90 basis points over the 10-year Treasury note, three basis points tighter than Wednesday.

Foreign Bonds

British government bonds surged in a flight-to-quality rally that was triggered by falling stock prices.

The price of Britain's benchmark 11 3/4% government bond, or gilt, was quoted at 100 10/32, up 22/32 from late Wednesday, to yield 11.69%.

"We started to take off the moment the Footsie started to plummet, with one or two people looking for a refuge," a trader said. Footsie is the sobriquet for the Financial Times-Stock Index of 100 issues. "It's just gone up and up and up," the trader said. "For every 1/2 point down in the Footsie, we're putting on a tick or two."

The bond rally began late in the day and provided some excitement to an otherwise torpid market.

Gilt prices were slightly lower at the opening, because of continued worries about delays in sterling's entry into the European Exchange Rate Mechanism, following Wednesday's comments by the West German Bundesbank, or central bank, president, Karl Otto Poehl.

In Japan, bankers and dealers warned investors to expect another increase in Japan's long-term prime rate next week. Many analysts said the rate could rise to as high as 9%.

But bankers expressed worry that the jump to the psychologically significant 9% level, while justified by the costs of funds of banks, would chill demand for their loans. An official at one of Japan's three long-term credit banks, which reset the prime rate late each month, said his bank may try to limit the rise in the long-term prime to below 9%.

The long-term prime rate is a benchmark for pricing many corporate loans, including subordinated loans being taken by Japanese banks to raise capital, as well as housing and personal loans. At 9%, the prime rate would be at its highest level in eight years.
