Jury Weighs Whether Boeing or Insurers Should Pay for $97.5 Million Cleanup

  • Author: Rick Wartzman
  • Date: 09/21/90

A federal court jury in Seattle began deliberations this week to determine who should pay $97.5 million to clean up two hazardous waste sites in the Puget Sound area: Boeing Co. or its insurers. During the four weeks of testimony, Boeing tried to elicit that its insurance carriers are responsible for covering the costs because it never expected pollution problems. The insurers sought to prove that the airplane maker had long been alerted to the potential fallout from years of dumping but chose to ignore those warnings.

The case is being closely watched by manufacturers in other industries, which face similar Superfund disputes. Experts say that only one other Superfund case of this scope has actually gone to trial. In December 1988, Shell Oil Co. lost to its insurers over the $1 billion cleanup of a toxic-waste site near Denver.

The Boeing case involves two dumps in King County, Wash., where from the mid-1950s through the mid-1970s the company shipped some 24 million gallons of waste, including solvents, heavy metals and some acids. Boeing later agreed in court-ordered settlements with regulators to clean up the properties under the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation and Liability Act of 1980, commonly known as Superfund.

In the trial, Boeing called witnesses who maintained that the company pressed the owners of the waste sites to comply with environmental regulations. The company also argued that the state permitted it to dump when and where it did, and that the methods of disposal it used were better than any available alternatives.

T.A. Wilson, who retired as Boeing chief executive officer in 1986, testified that he never realized the company was polluting. "I don't think we thought we were," he said.

But the insurers, which include Aetna Life & Casualty Co., Hartford Accident & Indemnity Co., American International Group and underwriters for Lloyd's of London, countered with their own witnesses. They brought to the stand a former Boeing chemical engineer who said that the company had been warned about the possibility of contamination at the sites, but turned a blind eye to plans that called for more sophisticated -- and more expensive -- waste-treatment systems. Lawyers for the insurance companies drew the same point from some of Boeing's own witnesses during cross-examination.

For the world's biggest commercial jet builder, the stakes are high. Boeing has sued a number of other companies that over the years also used one of the waste sites, Western Processing, in an attempt to have them contribute more to the cleanup. But these companies, including Unocal Corp., Lockheed Corp., Precision Castparts Corp. and Contintental Can Co., have balked; they say they've paid enough already, and Boeing still hasn't owned up to its fair share of the trouble.

"Even though we're on the industry side, we're interested this time in what the insurance companies are able to show," said Larry Hard, who is representing Unocal. He said his client and some of the other companies are preparing to go to trial in about a year.

Besides the $97.5 million of expenses being mulled now, Boeing potentially faces cleanup costs at 13 other locations around the country.

Whether this case would serve as precedent, though, isn't clear. Four state supreme courts and three federal appeals courts have split over whether insurers can even be brought to trial in Superfund cases. In January, the Washington State Supreme Court ruled in a landmark 9-2 decision that Boeing's claims for cleanup costs may be covered by the comprehensive general liability policies at issue, paving the way for the Boeing trial.

The Seattle jury is expected to return a verdict any day now, according to attorneys close to the situation. In closing arguments on Tuesday, Boeing lawyer Robert Sayler accused the insurers of engaging in "Boeing bashing" and said pointedly: "They want to portray themselves as the Audubon Society. They are not."

"We think we presented a good case on the facts," countered George Bassett, a lawyer for AIG who presented opening arguments for the insurers.

"But insurance companies aren't always so well liked, and Boeing is very well liked in this community," he acknowledged. "We know it's going to be an uphill battle."
