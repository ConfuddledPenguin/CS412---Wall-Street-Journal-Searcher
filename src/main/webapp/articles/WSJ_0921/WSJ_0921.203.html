Your Money Matters: Thinking the Unthinkable: Abandoning Your Mortgage

  • Author: Jill Bettner
  • Date: 09/10/90

As the real estate slump drags on, a lot of people are thinking the unthinkable: What would happen if I just walked away from my mortgage? While most people wouldn't act on the fantasy, some people are abandoning their mortgages, turning their house over to the bank.

After years of double-digit appreciation, many homeowners already have seen the value of their homes drop by as much as 20%. That can be enough to wipe out a homeowner's equity, so that the remaining mortgage is greater than the value of the house.

"A lot of people bought when prices were going up; now they're going down," says Kenneth Rosen, chairman of the Center for Real Estate and Urban Economics at the University of Southern California at Berkeley. "More of those people view walking away as an option. It happened in Texas and Arizona, and now it's happening in the Northeast. It's not a massive problem, but it could become one."

But simply letting the bank take your house when you can afford to hang on through the current real-estate slump is a terrible idea, says Roger Sirlin, a Mamaroneck, N.Y., lawyer who represents lenders.

"A lot of people think they can just mail back the keys, but it isn't that simple," he says. A foreclosure often has several serious, long-term repercussions.

For one, it blackens a homeowner's credit record, where a foreclosure stays for seven years. Because lenders view mortgages as some of the safest loans they can make, a foreclosure can make it hard to get new credit of any kind.

Banks consider the way people manage their house loans as "probably the best indicator of their general creditworthiness," says Joe Belew, president of the Consumer Bankers Association, a Washington trade group.

A foreclosure can also mean losing existing credit, such as one's credit cards. "It's up to the individual lender, but many of them check credit reports periodically to look for problems," says Michael Van Buskirk, a vice president at Cleveland-based TRW Inc., the nation's largest credit bureau. "If they see a foreclosure, they could take away your credit cards, reduce your limit or just not renew the cards."

Some banks say they wouldn't revoke the credit cards of someone who defaulted on a mortgage, so long as that individual kept up the payments on the credit cards. Defaulting on a mortgage also wouldn't necessarily preventetting new credit cards from some banks. "It makes it difficult, but not impossible," says William L. Capps, who heads credit-card operations at Security Pacific National Bank, Los Angeles.

Mr. Van Buskirk of TRW also doubts that a homeowner who walks away from a mortgage, and also has an outstanding auto loan, would have the car repossessed. "It's uncollateralized loans that are most likely to be called," he says.

But there are plenty of other headaches the homeowner could face. Many houses have fallen so far in value they aren't fetching enough in foreclosure auctions to cover the remaining mortgage.

As a result, more and more lenders are using the laws in some states, such as New York, to force borrowers to make up the difference. They can do that by taking a homeowner's car and savings accounts or garnisheeing paychecks for as long as 20 years.

Such "deficiency judgments" were relatively rare when inflation was boosting home values year after year. Now, says Mr. Sirlin, lenders are "beginning to go after people."

Tax rules also are stacked in favor of hanging on to your house. Unhappily, if you sell the house at a loss, that loss isn't deductible on your income taxes. Under tax laws, "a house is considered personal property, just like a car," says William G. Brennan, a tax partner in the Washington office of accountants Ernst & Young.

But if you walk away from your mortgage, you could end up owing the taxman. Some states don't permit lenders to seek deficiency judgments. If a lender can't go after the difference between the auction price and the outstanding debt on the house, the Internal Revenue Service can. The IRS considers any unclaimed shortfall "forgiveness of debt," in effect, a payment to you from the lender. Then it becomes taxable income.

The tax consequences of abandoning a mortgage could be even worse for homeowners who refinanced before the bottom fell out of the housing market.

Many people took advantage of spiraling real estate values to take cash out and refinance for more than they originally for their homes. Walking away now could trigger a tax double-whammy, says Ernst & Young's Mr. Brennan.

Mr. Brennan offers this example: "Suppose you bought a paid house a few years ago for $100,000 and took out a mortgage for $90,000. The house doubled in value to $200,000, so you refinanced for $170,000. Since then, the house has dropped in value to $150,000.

"Now, you walk away. The bank takes back the house, sells it for $150,000 and doesn't go after you for the other $20,000 still left on the mortgage.

"That $20,000 of forgiven debt is taxable income," he says, but that isn't all. "Since your tax basis in the house is still $100,000 because that's what you paid for it, but the bank sold it for $150,000, you also have a $50,000 taxable gain."

A financially strapped homeowner who can't avoid foreclosure might be better off filing for bankruptcy, says Ray Martin, a Dallas lawyer. In many states, the owner gets to keep a house, even if it isn't fully paid for, because the house is an "exempt asset that doesn't go into the bankruptcy estate," he says.

Mr. Martin adds, however, that the mortgage holder could still foreclose if a bankrupt homeowner doesn't meet the mortgage payments. A bankruptcy also stays on an individual's credit record for 10 years.

Homeowners with serious debt problems should keep in mind that lenders aren't eager to get back houses they have to turn around and sell themselves at current depressed prices.

"The last thing they want to do is foreclose," says TRW's Mr. Van Buskirk. "If you've had a problem or lost a job, most lenders will help you work it out. But if you just walk away, that's very difficult, if not impossible, to explain. In short, it's a big mistake."
