Foreign Exchange: Dollar Gains Against Most Currencies, Partly Because of New Mideast Jitters

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 09/21/90

NEW YORK -- The dollar gained slightly against most major currencies, reflecting technical buying, fears about the heightening of Middle East tensions, and hopes that the Federal Reserve will postpone cutting U.S. interest rates. With the start of Rosh Hashana, or the Jewish New Year, a national holiday Monday in Japan and a meeting of the Group of Seven industrialized nations Saturday, the market traded as if yesterday were the end of the week.

"This has been a rather tricky market to keep up to speed on," said Keith Cheveralls, New York foreign-exchange manager for Nippon Credit Bank. Ahead is a "big weekend," he said, both in duration because of the holidays and in importance because of the G-7 conference in Washington.

Late in the New York trading day, the dollar was quoted at 1.5773 marks and 1.3205 Swiss francs, up from 1.5715 marks and 1.3094 francs late Wednesday in New York. The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 137.65 yen, rising from 137.52 yen.

In early trading in Tokyo Friday, the dollar was quoted at 137.75 yen, up from 137.05 yen at the close there Thursday.

Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5773 marks, up from Thursday's Tokyo close of 1.5733 marks.

On Thursday, the dollar rose as high as 1.5835 marks and 1.3270 francs and fell as low as 136.40 yen. Its movements reflected, in part, cross trading between the yen and European currencies.

Asian dealers began a trend that lasted almost to the close of the North American session, of buying yen and selling European currencies to cover short, or oversold, yen positions. As a result of its role as a medium in such cross trades, the dollar was much higher against the European currencies but lower against the yen for most of the global trading day.

Those differences narrowed when the yen's rise reversed in a burst of activity about two hours before the close of New York trading.

Dealers couldn't pin down a fundamental reason for the sudden turnaround in the cross market. But they said a rally in oil prices at about that time may have served as an excuse to unload yen. Japan imports almost all of its crude and is considered vulnerable to rising oil prices.

Meanwhile, independent dollar buying was encouraged by a green light from technical charts, a jeep accident in Saudi Arabia and continued optimism about the postponement of U.S. rate cuts after Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan's congressional testimony Wednesday.

Ian Foakes, PaineWebber's foreign-exchange manager, said a technical indicator signaled a dollar-buying opportunity early yesterday, in response to the greenback's recent gains. The indicator gauges how well a currency sticks to highs it sets, and the U.S. currency looked good yesterday after its advances Wednesday.

"The dollar is fairly well underpinned," Mr. Foakes said. "It looks better than the last time it was up here" last week.

After drifting lower in early European trading, the dollar bounced higher on news that a U.S. soldier was killed and two others injured in a jeep accident in Saudi Arabia. The incident created a belief that tension might escalate in the Mideast, and that the dollar might serve as a "haven" currency for investors in case of war.

"Of course, this {incident} won't trigger a war, but the market was already short" dollars and primed to move higher, said Rainer Marian, analyst at Hessische Landesbank in Frankfurt.

The dollar ended European dealings mostly higher, helped against sterling, the mark and the Swiss franc by cross movements into the yen and expectations that U.S. interest rates aren't about to ease just yet. In Mr. Greenspan's congressional testimony Wednesday, the Fed chairman had expressed concern about the dollar's recent losses. The dollar climbed as traders interpreted those comments as a sign that the Fed won't cut interest rates soon. In general, higher interest rates make a currency more attractive to investors.

The dollar finished Asian trading mixed Thursday. It dropped against the yen, closing in Tokyo at 137.05 yen, a full yen below its 138.05 yen Wednesday close.

Tokyo traders attributed some of the yen's gains to unwinding of short yen positions before Japan's holiday weekend.

Meanwhile, sterling was trading late in New York at $1.8740, down from $1.8845 late Wednesday. However, the pace of the pound's retreat slowed after a sharp fall Wednesday, when Bundesbank President Karl Otto Poehl cast a cloud over sterling's prospects for full membership in the European Monetary System.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery settled at $389.10 an ounce, up $3.10. Estimated volume was a light 2.1 million ounces.

In early trading in Hong Kong Friday, gold was quoted at $389.90 an ounce.
