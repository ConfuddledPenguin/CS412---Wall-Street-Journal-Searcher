Chase to Raise Loan Reserves, Slash Its Staff

  • Author: Fred R. Bleakley
  • Date: 09/21/90

Chase Manhattan Corp. will announce today a major increase in its reserves for problem loans and staff reductions of more than 3,000 employees, according to banking executives. The announcement is aimed at clearing the air of uncertainty about the extent of problem loans, especially in real estate, and the ramifications of a previously announced goal to save $300 million a year in expenses.

Details of the plan were still being worked out yesterday afternoon after being approved by Chase's board Wednesday.

A Chase spokesman declined to comment, other than to say, "We are currently finalizing plans to reduce expenses and enhance profitability."

James McDermott, head of research at Keefe, Bruyette & Woods, said he had been told by Chase to "be on alert" for an announcement. "They've got to come out with something that is hard and comprehensive here," he said. He said he is expecting a reserve for problem loans of at least $325 million to be announced, an increase from the $250 million added to reserves in each of the first two quarters.

Chase's stock has been hammered all week by persistent rumors of larger than expected problems at the bank. Yesterday it was off 12.5 cents to $13.75 a share in New York Stock Exchange composite trading, hitting a new 52-week low from its high of $44 last October. The rumors began after Chase was forced to pay a rate of more than 13% to reprice some existing bonds last week.

A restructuring of Chase's European operations is also expected as part of the announcement. A former executive of the bank said it would involve "a substantial downsizing of operations" that would result in sharp cutbacks in Chase's retail operations there and sale of its West German banking operation.

An AP-Dow Jones wire report yesterday said that Chase's Spanish bank said it would begin laying off employees as part of a world-wide move to cut costs. Chase Manhattan Espana employs 400 people in Spain in 14 offices and has been showing losses.

In London, a Chase spokesman confirmed that the company's European retail operations are one of a number of units under review for cost-cutting.

In Chase's U.S. operations, analysts expect staff reductions in the investment banking and corporate lending sectors, which expanded in the late 1980s to participate in corporate takeovers. "With deal flow off sharply, I expect there will be cutbacks in those areas," said Mr. McDermott.

It was uncertain whether Chase's announcement would address the continuance of Chase's stock dividend at its present level. The shares are now yielding more than 17%. A 50% cut in the dividend would save the bank more than $100 million a year but would be a blow to investors. Analysts are divided on whether Chase would make such a drastic move.

Analysts had been urging management to provide details of the expected restructuring but an announcement wasn't planned until after Oct. 1, when Thomas G. Labrecque succeeds Willard C. Butcher as chief executive officer and a new management team will be in place.

When Mr. Labrecque was named to succeed Mr. Butcher in June, he said there would be a restructuring that analysts estimated would result in the loss of 3,000 jobs through voluntary retirement and layoffs. An executive at the bank says the figure will be higher than that. More than 1,500 employees have recently opted to be part of a voluntary retirement program, exceeding the bank's goal of 1,200, the executive said. Chase has 41,000 employees world-wide.
