Thursday's Markets: Stocks Fall 39.11 as Price Of Oil Soars --- Triple Witching Adds To the Dow's Slump; Dollar Edges Higher

  • Author: Christopher Winans
  • Date: 09/21/90

NEW YORK -- The program trading "witch" and surging oil prices conspired to send stock prices tumbling. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 39.11 points in moderate trading to 2518.32. October crude-oil futures contracts jumped $1.53 a barrel to $34.71, which also helped push bonds lower. The dollar edged up, with little news to guide traders.

Another decline in the Tokyo stock market and routs in European markets set the tone for the U.S. stock market. Tokyo's Nikkei Index fell only 0.52%, but yesterday was the second consecutive day that the index hit a low for the year. Stocks in London and West Germany both dropped under previous post-Kuwait invasion lows, set Aug. 23.

The Dow Jones industrials started sharply lower in the first hour of trading as index players bought futures and sold stocks ahead of today's triple expirations of options on individual stocks, and futures and options on Standard & Poor's 500-stock index, the New York Stock Exchange composite and the Major Market Index. This quarterly event is known as the "triple witching hour."

But some analysts think the program traders got the selling out of their system yesterday. "Usually the volatility occurs a day or so early" when the indexes are trading at a discount to the underlying stocks as the expiration date nears, said A.C. Moore, research director at Argus Research.

On top of that, "there's growing concern about the banking problem, and there's a lack of any good news," said Susan Del Signore, stock trader at Travelers Investment Management. She said that yesterday she was getting "the most negative calls I've gotten since I've been in this business. People are losing heart."

In addition to banks' health, said Thomas Walsh, head of equity trading at Nikko Securities International, investors are being turned off by "the potential of 6% inflation and of a fairly major recession."

Some of the oil-price rise came from technical trading related to yesterday's expiration of the October oil futures contract. Adding to the surge was a World Bank statement that a Mideast war could nearly double oil prices to $65 a barrel.

Those comments, and reports that some congressional leaders might try to delay automatic spending cuts pending a negotiated budget plan, sent bonds lower.

In major market action:

Stock prices sank. Volume was 145.1 million shares on the New York Stock Exchange. Declining issues swamped advancers, 1,258 to 300.

Bond prices fell 3/8 of a point, or $3.75 for each $1,000 of face amount. The yield on the Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose to 9.03%.

The dollar was higher. In late New York trading, the currency was quoted at 1.5773 marks, up from 1.5715 Wednesday. Against the yen, the dollar rose slightly to 137.65 from 137.52.
