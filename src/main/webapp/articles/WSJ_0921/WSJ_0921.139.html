Marketing & Media: Goldwyn Concern, Heritage Propose Stock-Swap Merger

  • Author: Kathleen A. Hughes
  • Date: 09/21/90

LOS ANGELES -- Samuel Goldwyn Co., one of the few remaining independent movie companies, offered to merge in a stock swap with Heritage Entertainment Inc., a troubled owner of theaters. The move, which is subject to execution of a definitive agreement and approval by holders and boards of each company, would allow the Goldwyn concern to become a public company. Closely held, the company is controlled by Samuel Goldwyn Jr., son of the late Samuel Goldwyn, one of Hollywood's early moguls.

Under the proposed transaction, Heritage said it would issue at least 90.8 million shares to Goldwyn's holders for all of the Goldwyn company's stock.

Currently, Heritage has about 7.6 million common shares outstanding. The transaction would leave Heritage shareholders with an 8.5% stake in the combined company that would keep the Samuel Goldwyn Co. name.

Yesterday, in composite trading on the American Stock Exchange, Heritage shares closed at $1.375, up 12.5 cents.

Heritage shareholders also would receive Class A warrants, giving them the right to purchase a total of 3.4 million shares in the combined company at $2.20 each. The warrants would be redeemable for 30 cents each. Heritage holders also would receive Class B warrants, allowing them to buy 6.2 million shares at a 10% discount to the average trading price over a 30-day period.

Samuel Goldwyn Co. has produced "Mystic Pizza" and has distributed "Henry V," "Wild at Heart," and "Longtime Companion." Recently, the company announced that it plans to boost movie production to about five movies a year.

Heritage owns about 88 movie theaters in 10 states, including California, Washington and Oregon, and a film library. It also sells movies for television syndication and produced the "Flipper" TV series that Goldwyn distributes.

Heritage posted a net loss of $4 million for fiscal 1990, ended March 31, and the company has been seeking investors to raise capital.

"The most popular way to go public is to buy some languishing entity," said Jeffrey Logsdon, an analyst at Seidler Amdec Securities. "Goldwyn is a nice little company with art-type films." However, he added: "No company has been particularly successful at enhancing its movie division by owning theaters."

Meyer Gottlieb, president and chief operating officer of Goldwyn, said the transaction is part of a continuing plan to "expand and diversify."
