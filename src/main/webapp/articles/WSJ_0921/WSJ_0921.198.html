Administration Turns Shy About S&L Bailout Funds

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/21/90

WASHINGTON -- Who dares to ask for more savings-and-loan money? Everyone knows the massive S&L cleanup will require at least $120 billion in the next year, more than twice originally forecast.

But Congress -- with a nervous eye on the calendar, less than a month away from its recess and a month and a half from the election -- is waiting for the Bush administration to formally ask for the funds. And administration officials won't make the request. The administration says it's only "prepared to discuss" a range of funding options.

So far, the unpleasant topic has been relegated to the closed-door budget summit, which is bogged down trying to slash the federal budget deficit.

Just this week, William Seidman, chairman of the Resolution Trust Corp., the bailout agency, tallied up the needs for the cleanup. The final costs are certainly going to be at the high end of the administration's projections, approximately $130 billion, not including hundreds of billions more in interest, he said. By the end of the year, the bailout operation will run out of funds.

"I am astonished that we have not received a request for $100 billion from the Treasury," said Sen. Timothy Wirth (D., Colo.) at a Senate Banking Committee hearing yesterday. "They aren't going to waffle out of this. This enormously important effort will be shut down because they won't have the ability to operate."

Sen. Donald Riegle (D., Mich.), chairman of the committee, promised to write "a strong letter" to Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady, urging him to make the specific funding request. "We aren't going to sign any blank checks around here," he said. House Banking Committee Chairman Henry Gonzalez (D., Texas) has already dispatched a similar appeal to the Treasury.
