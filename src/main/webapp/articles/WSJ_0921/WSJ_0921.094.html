Politics & Policy: Americans Support Bush's Tough Stance on Iraq, Backing Military Action Despite Hostages...

  • Author: Robert S. Greenberger and Michel McQueen
  • Date: 09/21/90

WASHINGTON -- Is President Bush willing to impose a death sentence on Americans held hostage by Saddam Hussein? Mr. Bush insists the Iraqi dictator's threats to use U.S. citizens as shields against an American attack, or even to starve them, won't weaken his demand that Iraq withdraw from Kuwait. He feels that a preoccupation with hostages' safety wrecked Jimmy Carter's presidency and nearly derailed Ronald Reagan's. "Our hearts go out to the hostages and their families. But our policy cannot change. And it will not change," the president told Congress last week.

So far, the president's tough stance against Saddam Hussein wins public support. A Wall Street Journal/NBC News poll taken earlier this week found that 53% of the registered voters responding would favor the U.S. taking military action against Iraq while there are still American hostages in Iraq and Kuwait, while 34% would oppose such action. "At this stage, the public is saying, `We're playing for keeps,'" say pollsters Robert Teeter and Peter Hart, who conducted the survey. "The idea that the hostages may get harmed is accepted."

But if the Persian Gulf crisis drags on, public pressure against jeopardizing hostages' lives could mount, posing a tough test of Mr. Bush's determination to break a 10-year pattern of manipulation of the U.S. by hostage-takers.

"There's a big distinction between the short-term, when people are rallying around the president and the flag, and what happens month after month when either there's a deadlock or a very bad turn in the military situation," warns James McGregor Burns, a Williams College political scientist and an authority on the presidency.

Indeed, as Mr. Bush spoke last week, there were some faint sounds of discontent. "The president's statement came across like a death sentence," says Michael Saba, an Illinois businessman who founded Coming Home, a group that is organizing hostage-family members to push for the release of Americans trapped by Iraq's invasion, now estimated at 1,350 citizens. "Our people are saying that there just have to be other ways to meet the president's objectives."

Mr. Saba, who was in Baghdad when Iraq invaded Kuwait but escaped seven days later, says the 500 family members he has contacted are upset that Mr. Bush lists freeing the hostages last after his other goals of forcing Iraq to withdraw, restoring Kuwait's government and ensuring regional security. "Does the fourth goal have a lower value than the other three?" he asks. The group plans to focus its attention on Congress, he says.

For now, most lawmakers strongly support Mr. Bush's policy. The hostages "are in our prayers but they cannot dominate our planning," said Sen. Sam Nunn (D., Ga.), chairman of the Senate Armed Services Committee, as he led a congressional delegation on a visit to Saudi Arabia recently. "You can't run a foreign policy on hostages," agreed GOP Sen. Jake Garn of Utah.

Senior Bush administration officials say that from the beginning of the Gulf crisis there was little debate over how to handle the hostage issue. While everything possible would be done to safeguard the hostages, says a senior official, it was understood that if they got caught in harm's way, that would be a necessary consequence of dealing with the situation created by Saddam Hussein.

Mr. Bush's views on dealing with hostage situations were formed when he was vice president and chaired a task force on combatting terrorism, says Marlin Fitzwater, the White House spokesman. "The hostage position is so well known and strongly felt" following the experiences in Iran and Lebanon, says Mr. Fitzwater, that "it's just not a matter that's been open to negotiations" during the current crisis.

The 1986 task force report stressed that the U.S. shouldn't make concessions to terrorists -- a mistake that was being made by the Reagan administration, which was trading arms to Iran for hostages, even as the report was being written. According to a member of the task force, the classified version of the report referred more specifically to hostage-holding. It concluded that "there are so many Americans wandering around the world, that if you make concessions to win someone's release, the terrorists can easily take another," the task force member said.

From the beginning of the Gulf crisis, the administration played down the hostage aspect, referring to the Americans trapped in Iraq and Kuwait as "detainees." When asked about the status of these U.S. citizens on Aug. 14, Mr. Bush said: "The status is inconvenienced people who want to get out." He waited until Aug. 20, during a speech to the Veterans of Foreign Wars, to use the word "hostage."

Despite his tough stance, however, President Bush at times violates his own rules about maintaining a low public profile on the hostage issue. Long before the Persian Gulf troubles, when the focus was on Lebanon, a decision had been made to not greet freed hostages at the White House, according to an administration official. But last April, when hostage-takers in Lebanon released Robert Polhill, Mr. Bush invited him for a visit.

And Vice President Dan Quayle said in an interview earlier this week that "not a day goes by without an inquiry about the hostages. They're very much on {Mr. Bush's} mind. We're in constant contact with the families."

Mr. Fitzwater insists that 10 years of agonizing over hostages has taught officials and the public valuable lessons about dealing with the problem. He said Mr. Bush isn't getting the kind of pressure from hostage families that he did when, as vice president, he was the Reagan administration's liaison to the families of U.S. hostages held in Lebanon. "People understand the policy," Mr. Fitzwater asserts, adding that some hostage families now realize that negotiations for some hostages only prolongs captivity for everybody else.

But Noel Koch, who headed the Pentagon's counter-terrorism effort from 1981 to 1986, warns that this could be the calm before a storm. "It's still early in the game. Until this thing becomes protracted and the suffering sets in or people start getting killed, there will be a tendency on the part of the families to keep quiet. But that could change abruptly."
