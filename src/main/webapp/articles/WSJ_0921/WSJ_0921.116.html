Who's News: SCEcorp's Bryson Named Chairman, Chief Executive

  • Author: Frederick Rose
  • Date: 09/21/90

SCEcorp said John E. Bryson was elected chairman and chief executive officer of the utility holding company and its operating unit, succeeding Howard Allen, who will retire from those posts Oct. 1. The selection of Mr. Bryson, a one-time environmental activist and former chairman of California's Public Utility Commission, ends a two-way race for the top position at the nation's second-largest investor-owned electric utility in terms of customers.

Mr. Bryson's rival for the top slot, Michael R. Peevey, was elected president of SCEcorp, a third title held by Mr. Allen, who has dominated SCEcorp and its Southern California Edison Co. unit for nearly a decade.

Mr. Bryson, 47 years old, is currently executive vice president and chief financial officer of SCEcorp. Mr. Peevey, 52, is also an executive vice president of the Rosemead, Calif., concern.

The selection of Mr. Bryson was warmly received by analysts. "I think it's a big plus for the company," said Edward J. Tirello Jr., an analyst at Smith Barney, Harris Upham Inc. Mr. Tirello said increasing competition in the utility business as well as complicated environmental pressures call for the broad background that Mr. Bryson will bring to the position.

Mr. Bryson and Mr. Peevey have for much of the past five years appeared an odd couple of prospective successors to Mr. Allen, who is a forceful personality in political and business circles in California and nationally in the utilities business. Mr. Allen will remain chairman of SCEcorp's executive committee and a director of both the holding company and its utility unit -- suggesting that he may continue to wield considerable power at SCEcorp.

Even as they were being groomed for the top position, Mr. Bryson and Mr. Peevey shared only a little of the corporate limelight with Mr. Allen. Both men were elected directors of SCEcorp and the utility only yesterday. Their pictures haven't appeared in the company's annual report since 1984.

Nonetheless, the men were being given a succession of responsibilities. And speculation was rampant at SCEcorp about who would get the final nod. Mr. Bryson, a cerebral lawyer, rarely made formal public appearances outside the company and has looked after financial and legal matters inside it. Mr. Peevey, a garrulous economist and former labor lobbyist, has spearheaded SCEcorp's tough and very public lobbying for acceptance of its plan to merge with neighboring San Diego Gas & Electric Co. in a $2.8 billion stock swap.

SCEcorp is still fighting for approval of the merger, which is being considered by the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission and the California Public Utility Commission, which are expected to rule before the end of the year. The combined utility would supplant Pacific Gas & Electric Co. as the nation's largest investor-owned utility in terms of customers.

If allowed, the consolidation of the operations with San Diego Gas will be the principal task facing SCEcorp's new chairman and will take considerable attention.

But, as part of a wider strategy, the company under Mr. Bryson is also likely to emphasize its burgeoning non-regulated businesses, which include construction of generating facilities that sell power to other utilities. In an interview, Mr. Bryson noted that this effort has moved overseas, with a massive project now under consideration to build a one million-kilowatt generating station in Britain.

"We believe that our non-utility businesses . . . are a very significant part of our own future," said Mr. Bryson, who added that the industry "is dealing with an increasing rate of change."

Mr. Bryson has a background at least as diverse as the company he will head. Shortly after graduation from law school, he was a founder of the Natural Resources Defense Council, a national public-interest law group.

In the mid-1970s, he was chairman of the California State Water Resources Board. And from 1979 to 1982, he was president of the California Public Utilities Commission, where he is credited with revitalizing parts of the staff and encouraging broader regulatory approaches.

Mr. Bryson was hired by Southern California Edison after a two-year stint with a San Francisco law firm and became senior vice president, law and finance. He was later made an executive vice president and chief financial officer. (Michael L. Noel, currently vice president and treasurer, will assume the added title of chief financial officer Oct. 1.)
