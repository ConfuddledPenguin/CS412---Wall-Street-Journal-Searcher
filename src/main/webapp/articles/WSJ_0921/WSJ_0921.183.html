Significant Leakage of Food to Iraq Seen by Agriculture Department Aides

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 09/21/90

WASHINGTON -- Agriculture Department analysts, in a break with the Bush administration's high expectations of choking off trade with Iraq, are projecting considerable leakage in the food embargo against the embattled nation. The department's World Agricultural Outlook Board estimates that despite the international embargo, Iraq will be able to import one million metric tons of wheat and wheat flour between last July 1 and June 30, 1991, about one-fourth of what it customarily uses annually. The panel also projects Iraq will import 350,000 metric tons of rice next year, almost half of its annual consumption. In addition, the panel expects Iraqi imports of barley and other coarse grains to total 400,000 metric tons between Oct. 1 and Sept. 30, 1991. Typically, Iraq uses about 1.4 million metric tons of barley a year.

Yesterday, several grain traders and a senior Treasury official were skeptical of the panel's projections. But should the estimates prove correct, it suggests the regime of Saddam Hussein could hold out longer than has been generally thought.

The panel's forecast assumes that much of the food will be smuggled into Iraq by truck, ship, even camel caravan, and that some will be provided openly by famine-relief organizations under United Nations auspices.

"There are a lot of traders in the Mideast who really don't have any flag," said Gerald Rector, chairman of the outlook board's feed grains and wheat committee. "They will take a commodity wherever they can get the best price."

The board's surprising forecast represents the first disquieting government assessment of the prospects for embargo-busting since Iraq's Aug. 2 invasion of Kuwait and the swift imposition of U.N. sanctions against Baghdad. Administration officials have repeatedly extolled the cooperation of other nations in honoring and enforcing the sanctions, and they have cited Iraqi bread lines and shortages of sugar, cooking oil and other commodities as proof that the embargo is beginning to bite.

In an interview yesterday, Deputy Treasury Secretary John Robson described the international sanctions as "an amazingly tight net," and dismissed the possibility of imports' slipping through in any appreciable volume.

"I don't think you're going to have an absolutely air-tight embargo," he said, "but I'm not going to sit around and worry about a truckload of tomatoes getting in."

The outlook board members are analysts with the Foreign Agricultural Service and Economic Research Service, who tap the expertise of U.S. officials outside the Agriculture Department in making projections of world agricultural trade.

The board expects Iraq to devote part of its diminishing foreign exchange to rice purchases. In its Sept. 12 report, the panel projects Iraqi rice imports for 1991 at 350,000 tons, just 100,000 tons less than what was imported this year before sanctions were imposed.

"Rice should be the primary commodity they try to bring in," said Mr. Rector. "Typically, it's traded in bag form. You can put it on a camel or a boat and bring it in."

As for the one-million-metric-ton forecast for wheat and wheat flour, an estimated 300,000 tons were imported before Iraq was embargoed, he said. The panel assumes that the current glut of wheat on the world market will help Iraqi efforts to obtain through middlemen an additional 700,000 tons.

"If you've got a lot of wheat looking for a home," said Mr. Rector, "it makes it more likely that you have people willing to buy it and get it across the {Iraqi} border."

The board expects nations such as Libya and Yemen to help Iraq by serving as transshipment points for food purchased elsewhere, Mr. Rector said. In addition, leakage is expected across the Jordanian, Turkish and Iranian borders. The shipments would take roundabout routes, he added. One possibility foreseen: Persian Gulf traders would ferry grain by boat into Iran, and Iranian truckers would take it across the border.

"Iran and Iraq traded across the border even when they were fighting" in their eight-year war that ended in 1988, he said.

If the Iraqis import as much food as the analysts forecast, the odds of their holding out until next year's harvests would be improved considerably. Last month, an Agriculture Department report estimated that Iraq already had enough wheat, rice, barley and other coarse grains to last four to six months.

Several grain-trade experts expressed doubts about the willingness of any nation to engage in embargo-busting in the face of overwhelming world support for the sanctions. "I can't imagine what country would load {grain}," said Bernard Steinweg, a Continental Grain Co. executive. "That would be a ridiculous risk."

Furthermore, grain traders for multinational agribusiness companies would detect any big shipments, said a Cargill Inc. spokesman, who added that the entire market acts as an "intelligence network."

Some Agriculture Department officials are also skeptical about the potential for leakage. "Shiploads of grain are easy to see," said one official. "Truckloads going across the border may be happening, but they won't make a big dent {in the embargo}.
