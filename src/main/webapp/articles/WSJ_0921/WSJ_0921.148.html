Resistance Grows To Plan to Boost FHA Loan Costs

  • Author: Joe Davidson
  • Date: 09/21/90

WASHINGTON -- Consumer and industry groups alike are fighting a Bush administration plan that would boost up-front costs for borrowers of loans backed by the Federal Housing Administration. The plan is designed to protect the solvency of the FHA's mutual mortgage insurance fund, which insures low down-payment mortgages on single-family homes. Officials of the Department of Housing and Urban Development, citing a study by Price Waterhouse, say the fund isn't operating on a sound basis, in part because of poor management and lax monitoring during the Reagan administration. The officials predict that the fund will be insolvent by the middle of the decade.

The administration, among other things, wants to add a 0.5% premium to high-risk loans and require home buyers to pay two-thirds of the closing costs in cash at settlement. The HUD plan would eliminate as many as 100,000 borrowers annually, according to the National Association of Realtors.

The Senate housing bill has incorporated the administration's plan. But a different proposal, which is backed by several trade and consumer groups, is contained in legislation passed by the House. The two versions must be resolved by a conference committee.

The House version, known as the Vento-Ridge alternative, would reduce the current up-front premium to 1.35% from 3.8%, but add a 0.6% annual insurance premium. The up-front cash needed by home buyers would remain about the same as under current FHA requirements.

Currently, the buyer of a house selling for $100,000 would have to pay $6,600 at settlement. That figure would rise to $7,866 under the HUD proposal, according to figures released by the Senate Committee on Banking, Housing and Urban Affairs.

Both the HUD plan and the Vento-Ridge alternative would keep the FHA fund financially stable, according to Price Waterhouse. Administration officials have said they would recommend a veto of the House housing legislation, in part because of its FHA measure.

HUD Secretary Jack Kemp and his aides have met with industry groups in an attempt to resolve the differences. "We've been talking with all elements of the industry," said Mary Brunette, a department spokeswoman. "We're working to resolve some of the differences so the conference can go forward."

Warren Lasko, executive vice president of the Mortgage Bankers Association, said his organization doesn't believe the financial troubles of the FHA are as bad as the administration contends.

The HUD proposal has made some strange bedfellows of its opponents. Consumer advocates, such as the Consumer's Union and National People's Action, usually find themselves on the opposite side of issues from trade associations such as the National Association of Realtors.

"It's one of those issues where their profit interest in putting people in houses parallels our interest in putting people in houses," said Michelle Meier of the Consumer's Union.
