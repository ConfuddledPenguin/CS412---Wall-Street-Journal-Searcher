Japan's Growth Slows to a 3.6% Rate; Officials Remain Optimistic on Outlook

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 09/21/90

Economic growth in Japan slowed to a 3.6% annual rate in the second quarter of this year from an 11.4% pace in the prior three months, but officials remained optimistic about the economic outlook despite rising oil prices and interest rates. The Economic Planning Agency said gross national product, the nation's total output of goods and services, grew an inflation-adjusted 0.9% in the calendar second quarter, which is the first quarter of the Japanese fiscal year, from the prior quarter. In the first quarter, GNP expanded 2.7% from the preceding three months.

Despite the slowing, agency officials said they don't expect growth to be hindered, at least for the rest of the year.

Domestic demand accounted for growth of 2.4% in the second quarter compared with the prior quarter, but external demand contracted 1.5%, resulting in the overall quarter-to-quarter growth of 0.9%. Within Japan, such factors as personal consumption and corporate investment in plant and equipment have been expanding steadily.

The report comes amid growing concern about the global economy and a world-wide squeeze on capital -- and the large role that Japan plays in both. For instance, the recent plunge in Tokyo's stock market is eroding the capital base of Japan's big banks, which in turn is decreasing the flow of capital to other countries. With less capital coming in to the U.S., interest rates stay higher and economic recovery could be delayed.

Shinyasu Hoshino, Economic Planning Agency administrative vice minister, said there weren't any signs of an overheating economy. He said that the economy is continuing to grow smoothly in the current quarter and that there is no reason for pessimism about 1990's final quarter.

In a bid to hold down inflation, the central bank has raised its discount rate -- the rate it charges on loans to financial institutions -- five times since May 1989, to 6%.

Many economists and analysts expect Japan, which depends on imported oil for most of its energy needs, to cope well with the surge in oil prices in the wake of Iraq's invasion of Kuwait. But some research groups have said the higher prices will curb economic growth in Japan.

Hirohiko Okumura, chief economist for Nomura Research Institute, said higher interest rates and oil prices may reduce the annual GNP growth rate by about 0.5 percentage point in coming quarters. Still, he expects growth for the current fiscal year of about 5%.

Mr. Okumura said that combined with the 11.4% annual growth rate of the first quarter, the latest data show that "the Japanese economy is expanding very smoothly."

Nomura Research forecasts real economic growth easing to about 4% in the fiscal year starting next April 1, with inflation in consumer and wholesale price inflation remaining below 3%, Mr. Okumura said.

Some government economists have said that the central bank's monetary tightening, as well as the yen's strength, which makes imports denominated in other currencies -- including oil -- less expensive, will counterbalance the impact of higher oil prices. Still, the government's forecast of 4% growth in GNP for the current fiscal year is more guarded than those of many private economists, who predict expansions greater than 4%.

The economy has grown for 46 consecutive months, marking the second-longest postwar expansion after a 57-month period of growth between October 1965 and July 1970. Before the Middle East crisis began, many economists had predicted that the current expansion would break that record. Most retain their optimism. A series of surveys conducted recently by the Bank of Japan and the Finance Ministry also showed that the economy is maintaining its momentum.

Kenneth Courtis, a Tokyo-based economist for Deutsche Bank AG, noted that such indicators as auto sales, housing starts and midyear bonuses point to "very strong" growth in the current quarter. But he predicted capital spending by Japanese corporations will tail off in coming months, partly because higher oil prices are causing other countries to reduce their growth projections.

Personal spending, which accounts for nearly 60% of GNP in Japan, grew an inflation-adjusted 1.4% during the quarter from the January-March period, when such spending rose 0.4%. Corporate-equipment investment grew 3.8% from the January-March quarter, when such investment expanded 3.5%.

Housing investment grew 1.8% in the latest quarter, down from 2% in the January-March period.

External demand served to brake growth as exports fell 3.9% from the January-March quarter, when exports surged 9.1%, and as imports grew 3.2% from the prior three months, when the pace was 2.1%.

Government capital spending in the second quarter grew 2.1%, compared with a 1.4% expansion in the first period.
