Treasury Considers Clarifying Tax Benefits For Buyers of Thrifts

  • Author: null
  • Date: 09/21/90

WASHINGTON -- The Treasury Department said it is considering whether legislation or other action is needed to clarify the status of the tax benefits that were part of 96 government-assisted sales of insolvent thrifts in 1988. In a series of hearings, Congress is reviewing the much-criticized transactions, which heaped more than $4 billion in tax benefits on buyers of thrifts. The benefits occurred primarily through nontaxable government notes and were shared among the buyers of the sick thrifts and their affiliates.

The Resolution Trust Corp. has said it could save as much as $4 billion if it received $22 billion in funding to pay off the high-interest government notes that were part of the deals.

Yesterday, William Seidman, chairman of the RTC, said government savings could be even higher, depending on rulings from the Internal Revenue Service and the Treasury regarding the tax benefits. "The tax benefits are complicated by what assurances the government gave buyers at the time," said Mr. Seidman.

Mr. Seidman said that just having the $22 billion on hand might give the thrifts the clout to renegotiate the sales, without spending all the money. "Then the funds wouldn't have to be spent, and we would have the particular pleasure of returning them to the Treasury."

Jack Kemp, secretary for the Department of Housing and Urban Development, suggested that tax breaks might be considered to help the RTC sell its swelling inventory of properties seized from S&Ls, which currently includes about $31 billion in real estate or troubled real-estate loans. Mr. Kemp, a member of the RTC's Oversight Board, said tax incentives could "drive some of this property back to the private sector."
