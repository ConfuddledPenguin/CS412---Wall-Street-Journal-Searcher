Marketing & Media: American Express to Unveil Magazine For Cardholders in College This Fall

  • Author: Patrick M. Reilly
  • Date: 09/21/90

NEW YORK -- American Express Co.'s publishing unit will distribute a new magazine this fall on college campuses, testing a market where several others have flunked out. The company this week is mailing the first issue of a new quarterly called Connections to 600,000 students on 600 campuses who are holders of its American Express card. Like other so-called custom magazines, the magazine is free and offers its advertisers, Nissan Motor Co. and Apple Computer Inc., exclusive sponsorship arrangements.

Connections is the second project spawned by the Custom Media Group of American Express Publishing, a unit of American Express Co. The Custom Media Group is also publishing Your Company, a magazine sent out for the first time this week to one million American Express corporate-card holders who run small businesses. Your Company's major sponsors are International Business Machines Corp., Cigna Corp. and United Parcel Service Inc.

College campuses have been a graveyard for major magazine publishers in recent years. Washington Post Co.'s Newsweek, Time Warner Inc. and Whittle Communications L.P. have all folded their college magazines, largely because of a lack of advertising support. Only Whittle returned to campuses with a new magazine for women called In View, which is distributed on 926 campuses; it is published five times during the school year and sponsored by Maybelline Co.

But American Express officials say the use of their cardholder lists for distribution is an advantage, and that such custom magazines represent a stable publishing arrangement. Nissan and Apple pay in advance to sponsor Connections, covering all costs and providing a "locked-in profit level," said Thomas O. Ryder, president of American Express Publishing. There is, however, a history of sponsors suddenly withdrawing support, resulting in the swift disappearance of such magazines.

Both Connections and Your Company use two of the latest magazine publishing innovations: "demographic" binding of ads and ink-jet printing. In the case of Connections, certain ads from Nissan and Apple can be bound in issues sent, for example, to students at "classier" schools or those who travel a lot, while different ads go elsewhere, said Paul D. Wolff, publisher of Connections and vice president of Custom Media. Ink-jet printing allows a sponsor's local dealer to print personal messages on the magazine.

Mary Simons, editor in chief of Connections, said the magazine will primarily carry service articles that tell students how to do such things as land a job or ski on a snow board. "We stay away from sex, drugs and rock 'n' roll," she said.

Mr. Ryder said the new custom magazines represent a new source of revenue for the company, which is scaling back its plans to acquire city and regional magazines. "We aren't leaving behind city magazines, we just aren't on the street buying {magazines}," he said.

Magazine Publishers of America, a trade group, said the three best-selling magazines in college bookstores in 1989 were Hearst Corp.'s Cosmopolitan, Conde Nast Publications' Glamour and Time Warner Inc.'s People.
