Senate Unit Backs Product-Liability Limits in Lawsuits

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 05/23/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Commerce Committee approved a bill that would impose new federal limits on plaintiffs' rights in lawsuits over injuries allegedly caused by defective or dangerous products. The legislation would make it more difficult or, in some cases, impossible for injured plaintiffs to recover monetary punitive damages -- those assessed to punish a defendant company, as opposed to damages that compensate a victim. The committee voted 13-7 to approve the bill.

The legislation would bar, in the absence of fraud, punitive damages against makers of pharmaceuticals, medical devices or aircraft if the products had received prior federal regulatory approval. Most rules governing injury suits are set by states, and thus may vary.

The Judiciary Committee is expected to consider the bill before it goes to the Senate floor, where opponents promise a fierce fight. A similar measure has been introduced in the House, and the Bush administration supports such legislation.

For years, big business has pushed unsuccessfully for federal product liability limits, claiming that injury suits drive up insurance rates, inflate prices for consumer goods and inhibit product development. In an effort to win approval, proponents of the bill shifted their strategy by stressing that current state product liability laws put U.S. companies at a disadvantage when competing against foreign counterparts.

Opponents of such legislation, supported by consumer advocates and trial lawyer groups, have so far managed to block enactment of federal product liability limits, arguing that the restrictions would put injured individuals at an unfair disadvantage when they sue companies. Public Citizen, a liberal Washington advocacy organization, called the legislation a "windfall for makers of unsafe products" and "the most anti-consumer bill facing the U.S. Congress."

Sen. Bob Kasten (R., Wis.), who has sponsored such legislation in the past, left out of this year's version certain stringent limits on plaintiffs' rights in order to gain broader Democratic support. Tacitly acknowledging the modifications, Commerce Committee Chairman Ernest Hollings (D., S.C.), the leading Senate foe of federal product liability restrictions, agreed to allow the panel to vote on the measure and send it to the full Senate.

But proponents of the legislation are expected to try to add tougher provisions later. Perhaps the most controversial of these is an amendment that would require a plaintiff to prove that a corporate defendant had been negligent in making or distributing a product. In many states, "strict liability" laws require that a plaintiff prove only that a defendant's product was defective or unreasonably dangerous, regardless of whether a defendant had been negligent.
