Private Market Attracts Issuers Of Junk Bonds

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Anita Raghavan
  • Date: 05/23/90

NEW YORK -- Unable to sell debt in the battered public junk-bond market, a growing number of corporations are selling junk bonds in the private-placement market. Investment bankers say more than a dozen new junk-bond issues are being negotiated in the $170 billion private market, where insurance companies and other big investors buy and sell securities free from public disclosure requirements.

Among the issues are high-risk, high-yield junk bonds of Ethan Allen Inc., Colorado Prime, CaterAir, Jack Eckerd Corp. and Multimedia Corp., according to Anthony Napolitano, a McCarthy Crisanti & Maffei Inc. analyst who tracks the private-placement market. Also on the private calendar is Caldor Corp., a discount retailer that is expected to privately place $300 million in junk bonds tomorrow. If completed, the Caldor issue would be the largest junk issue privately placed so far this year.

Most of these junk bonds were expected to be sold publicly. But ever since the public junk-bond market hit hard times last summer, the number of new issuess sold to the public has quickly dwindled. So far this year, just four new junk issues have been launched, raising a total of $382 million for corporate borrowers, according to Securities Data Co. That is down sharply from the 46 issues totaling $11.35 billion that were sold publicly by this time a year ago.

Away from the glare of the public and the media, investment bankers are "quietly approaching the big investors looking for lead buyers," said Mr. Napolitano of McCarthy Crisanti. Rather than scouring the country for buyers for publicly traded junk bonds, the bankers instead have limited their search to "Newark, Hartford, Boston and New York," where the nation's biggest insurance companies are based, he says.

The big investors who buy debt in the private-placement market get more control over how the deals are structured, giving them more protection if the issuer gets into financial trouble. In turn, the investors are accepting interest rates on new private deals that are slightly lower than the rates paid on comparable junk bonds sold in the public market.

"In the past six months, we've {accepted} rates in the private market that have been a bit lower than those we could have obtained in the public market," said Charles Morss, an executive vice president at Boylston Capital Advisors, a money-management subsidiary of The New England insurance company.

However, Mr. Morss, added, "we get substantially stronger covenants," particularly in guarding against early repayment of bonds and takeovers and other "event risk" that could hurt the value of a company's bonds. For example, he said that insurance companies have become nearly as strict as commercial banks these days in setting limits on the amount of debt issuers can have without violating covenants.

One drawback to the private market is that it can't absorb the large billion-dollar offerings that once were easily sold during the public junk-bond market's heyday. Most private transactions are smaller than $200 million.

The growing move by borrowers to the private market represents a return to the historic roots of junk bonds. Prior to the development of the public junk market, chiefly by Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. and its former junk-bond chief Michael Milken, most low-rated corporate bonds were sold privately to insurance companies. "That's the old style; it's creeping back," Mr. Morss said.

The private market operates differently from the public junk-bond sales. "In the private market, it is normal for potential buyers to sit down with {investment bankers} and negotiate" how the deal should look, said Garrett Moran, manager of the high-yield department at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette Securities Co. "In that sense, it's more work," he said. However, Mr. Moran said that the private market opens the doors to a larger group of potential investors.

To entice private investors, investment bankers at Donaldson Lufkin restructured the Caldor deal significantly, adding sweetners such as an equity stake, or so-called equity kicker. Also, the size of the issue was cut to $300 million from the $460 million originally planned.

Donaldson Lufkin couldn't comment on Caldor because the issue hasn't closed yet. However, according to talk in the market, the Caldor transaction now consists of $100 million of 15% senior subordinated notes that include warrants giving bondholders the right to buy 12% of the company. The issue also includes $200 million of senior secured notes, equally divided among notes that float at a rate 2.75 percentage points above the London interbank offered rate and fixed-rate 12 7/8% notes.

For issuers like Caldor, the private market clearly offers several advantages over the public market. To lure private investors, investment bankers can customize terms. For instance, floating-rate debt can be sold to one set of buyers, and fixed-rate debt to another group.

Also, an issuer's costs -- both in terms of time and money -- are much lower in the private market.

Each time a company seeks to change the terms of a pending deal in the public market, it has to register the change with the Securities and Exchange Commission. In the private market, by contrast, deals can be restructured and overhauled without re-registering with the SEC.

Fred Sutherland, vice president of corporate finance and development at ARA Group Inc., which tapped the private market with a $125 million junk issue last month, agrees that the private market can save on both time and money.

"The reason we went the private route is because of the uncertainty in the public market," said Mr. Sutherland. Also, by "going the private route we could get the deal done much more quickly -- it's a more streamlined process."

Mr. Sutherland said he isn't prepared to give "last rites" to the public junk market, but he believes "the private market, going forward, will play a more important role than it has."
