PARTY GETS THE BLAME

Senior Chinese leader Deng Xiaoping blamed poor party leadership as the root cause of last year's pro-democracy demonstrations, West German sources said.

The new Communist Party chief, Jiang Zemin, said he couldn't rule out more such demonstrations this year, the sources said. But a Chinese official said only police forces would be used to quell any further unrest.

The comments came in talks this week with former West German Chancellor Helmut Schmidt. The sources said the Chinese leaders were generally conciliatory toward students in the demonstrations, which soldiers crushed in June.

The sources also said Mr. Schmidt exchanged letters with former party chief Zhao Ziyang, who was purged in June for being sympathetic to the students. It is the first time Mr. Zhao, under house arrest in Beijing, is known to have communicated with the outside world.

SPAIN DEMANDS GOLD CARD LIST

A Spanish appeals court ordered American Express Co. to give the government a list of its gold card holders in Spain, the latest twist in a drive against tax evaders. The court rejected the company's appeal of a 1987 government request for the list, saying the request didn't infringe on constitutional rights to privacy. American Express officials last night hadn't decided whether to appeal. American Express doesn't say how many of its 270,000 members in Spain hold gold cards, which provide extra services. There are about 35 million American Express cardholders world-wide.

ROMANIA'S UNORTHODOX OFFER

A revolutionary group that helped topple Romanian dictator Nicolae Ceausescu has an unorthodox offer for Communist Party members to cover their political past. For just one lei (less than one cent), Communists can buy a certificate saying the holder doesn't want to be a member of the party or its youth branch any longer. The Romanian Communist Party never formally dissolved, but nobody claims membership anymore. Before the revolution, there were 3.7 million Communist Party members.

KOREAN FIRM VS. DU PONT

Korea Engineering Plastics Co. filed an anti-dumping suit against Du Pont Co., Hoechst AG's Hoechst Celanese Corp. unit in the U.S. and Asahi Chemical Industry Co. of Japan, the South Korean Ministry of Finance said. Korea Engineering claimed the foreign firms have dumped their polyacetal resin products in South Korea since 1988. The government has six months to investigate. South Korea's polyacetal resin market totaled $35 million last year. Korea Engineering supplied 47% of the demand; imports supplied the rest.

EAST GERMANS REDISCOVER FRUIT

For years, East Germany's Communist rulers neglected the growing of fruits and vegetables; only the party elite had regular access to citrus fruit. But while stores are finally stocking veggies, people aren't familiar with them. So Ulrike Hohmuth is writing a vegetarian cookbook, to address such issues as how to tell when fruit is ripe. Because traditional German cooking caters to meat eaters, Ms. Hohmuth has had to borrow recipes from elsewhere, or make them up.

ISRAELI SUPERCOMPUTER

Israeli scientists are doing research on a supercomputer system they say is more powerful than anything the U.S. has, according to Israeli university officials. Three Israeli institutions had applied for licenses to buy Cray Research Inc. and International Business Machines Corp. supercomputers, but the U.S. delayed the permits because of concern Israel might use them to develop nuclear weapons and missiles. Technion University is seeking funds in the U.S. to build its own computers, and has started theoretical research.

AUSTRALIAN MEDIA CURBS

Australia will limit aggregate foreign ownership of radio and television companies to 20%, to prevent skirting of existing rules. Individual foreign investors are limited to a 15% stake.

The toughening of foreign-ownership limits undermines efforts by the country's three television networks to raise funds. The TV industry had its first loss in 30 years last fiscal year.

Foreign investors now have a large stake in one of the three TV networks, but were expected to acquire stakes in the others. Broadcasters have three years to comply with the limits.

One of the three major networks, Bond Media Ltd.'s Channel 9, was negotiating for capital injections from companies including Paramount Communications Inc. and CBS Inc. in return for a 30% share in the Australian network.

POSTSCRIPTS . . .

Hong Kong Chinese executives attach more importance to success in business than keeping their wives happy, according to a survey of 1,000 men by Price Waterhouse Management Consultants. The survey found a majority of businessmen polled approved of having a mistress, and ranked impressing their wives after impressing friends and business associates. . . . One in six Japanese public junior high schools requires male students to wear crew cuts, a lawyer's group survey showed. Some respondents said crew cuts encourage good study habits.
