Equipment Making By the Baby Bells Clears Senate Unit

  • Author: Mary Lu Carnevale
  • Date: 05/23/90

WASHINGTON -- The Senate Commerce Committee approved a bill that allows the Baby Bells to manufacture telecommunications equipment, a long-sought but limited victory for the seven companies. The measure, passed on voice vote, would lift the ban on manufacturing contained in the consent decree that broke up the Bell system. Only Sen. Daniel Inouye (D., Hawaii) opposed the bill in a brief discussion prior to the vote.

But the legislation, introduced by Commerce Committee Chairman Ernest F. Hollings (D., S.C.), doesn't touch consent decree restrictions on the Bells entering computerized information services and the long-distance industry. And in a provision the Bells find onerous, the measure stipulates that manufacturing is to take place within the U.S. from U.S.-made parts. The bill leaves policing largely up to the Federal Communications Commission, and gives the FCC authority to waive the "domestic content" requirement if a phone company can prove "extraordinary circumstances."

In addition, the measure requires the Bells to obtain a portion of the financing for their manufacturing ventures from outside investors.

The Bells' reaction was restrained. "It is an important first step in the process," said Laird Walker, head of U S West Inc.'s Washington office and the chairman of an informal Bell committee that has been working to lift the antitrust restrictions. "The domestic content language still needs work," he said.

Nevertheless, with a busy schedule and little time left to consider complex telecommunications legislation, there is only a slim chance that "free-the-Bells" legislation will pass both the Senate and the House this year. For starters, the Commerce Committee bill could be delayed by a possible Judiciary Committee review or Finance Committee concerns over its implications for international trade. Last week, Sen. Howard Metzenbaum (D., Ohio), chairman of the Judiciary Committee's antitrust panel expressed concern about the Hollings measure.

Meanwhile, key House members would like to see a more comprehensive package that would allow the Bells to enter information services. The Energy and Commerce Committee's telecommunications panel has been circulating a staff draft of legislation, trying to get consensus on a measure that could move quickly.

The vote comes after six years of intensive lobbying by the regional phone companies to lift all restrictions in the consent decree. This year alone, the Bells budgeted $21 million for a lobbying and advertising blitz aimed at upsetting the decree.
