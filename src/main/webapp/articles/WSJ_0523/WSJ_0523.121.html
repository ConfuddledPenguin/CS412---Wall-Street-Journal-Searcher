What's News -- Business and Finance

  • Date: 05/23/90

TREASURY CHIEF BRADY will ask Congress today for massive new borrowing authority to finance the S&L bailout, officials said. The secretary is expected to acknowledge that his department badly underestimated the cost of the thrift rescue and to seek authority to borrow $80 billion to $140 billion. The new estimates could put the bailout's total cost to taxpayers at $300 billion over 10 years.

Thrift regulators' new tactic of selling S&Ls in pieces has sharply increased the short-term cost of the bailout. The approach is threatening to swell the budget deficit and hasten the thrift industry's demise.

---

TWA slashed round-trip fares on domestic flights by up to 30%, fueling an unexpected fare skirmish among airlines. United Air initiated the battle Monday when it ran ads offering $35 off certain round-trip fares.

Safety seats should be required for small children on all airline flights, a federal board urged, which would effectively end their free rides.

---

Pinnacle West rejected PacifiCorp's $1.82 billion takeover offer as well as Salt River Project's $500 million bid for certain assets. PacifiCorp said it is reviewing options, which could include directly approaching Pinnacle shareholders.

---

Citicorp's credit ratings were lowered by Moody's, including the rating on commercial paper. S&P made a similar move last month but affirmed Citicorp's commercial-paper rating.

---

The U.S. criticized Japan for the slow pace of financial deregulation. Tokyo also was warned it may face trade sanctions unless it agrees soon to decontrol bank deposit rates.

---

Federal Express stock slid nearly 10%, to $47, after the firm disclosed that shipment growth slowed sharply in April. Recent company statements had prompted analysts to predict a turnaround in the current quarter.

---

A plan to give the SEC more clout, including oversight of stock-index futures, will soon be unveiled by Bush officials. But support for the proposal appears to be waning in Congress.

---

Exxon and Du Pont's Conoco unit settled allegations by a Bermuda oil trading firm that they and six other oil companies rigged crude prices.

---

Bond prices rallied on heavy foreign buying, which helped lift stocks hurt by profit-taking. The Dow Jones industrials closed up 7.55 points, at a record 2852.23. The dollar was mixed.

---

Chrysler has stopped seeking a buyer for its defense-related operations, apparently because it didn't get a satisfactory offer. The auto maker said it will revamp and boost research and development funding at the unit.

---

Greyhound's striking workers made an unconditional offer to return to work. But the move is intended to set the stage for a potentially huge back-pay claim against the company.

---

Eight publicly traded funds originally sponsored by VMS Realty Partners announced a return to profitability, but sharply cut their book values.
