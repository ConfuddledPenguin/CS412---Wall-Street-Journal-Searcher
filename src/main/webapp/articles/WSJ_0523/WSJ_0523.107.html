Technology: Abrupt Halt to Smoking Works Best, Study Finds

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/23/90

CHICAGO -- Most people who successfully stop smoking cigarettes do it without help from formal programs; they are most likely to quit if they stop "cold turkey," according to a new study. In analyzing the results of a 1986 telephone survey conducted by the U.S. Centers for Disease Control, researchers found that about 48% of those interviewed, who quit smoking during the previous 10 years, had done so on their own; while only 24% of those who quit were helped by a smoking cessation program or by using nicotine gum.

Researchers at the University of Wisconsin said their findings, published in today's Journal of the American Medical Association, were also important because the survey found that 30% of people who had smoked during the previous year had tried to kick the habit. If this were true for the entire U.S. population, it would mean that 17 million people each year try to quit smoking, but that only 1.3 million of them are successful. The researchers said their report was the first to determine which methods seemed to work for most people.

The researchers found that about 70% of people who tried to quit had done so at the urging of their doctors, while only 46% of all the smokers said their physicians advised them to stop. The researchers said that while formal programs were helpful for some people, advice designed to help people quit on their own might be more useful for most people who are thinking about quitting.
