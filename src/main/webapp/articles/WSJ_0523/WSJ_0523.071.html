Law -- Legal Beat: Boesky Takes the Stand in Mulheren Trial

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen and Wade Lambert
  • Date: 05/23/90

Ivan F. Boesky, whose cooperation led to the government's sweeping insider-trading investigation, finally made his debut as a witness yesterday, testifying that he made illegal arrangements to sell securities to John A. Mulheren Jr. Mr. Boesky avoided making eye contact with Mr. Mulheren and seemed impatient and sometimes even angered by ques- tions asked of him by assistant U.S. Attorney E. Scott Gilbert and Mr. Mulheren's lawyer, Thomas Puccio. Mr. Boesky, once Wall Street's best-known stock speculator, was cooperating with the government as part of his 1986 insider-trading plea agreement.

Neatly dressed in a dark grey suit and white shirt, Mr. Boesky appeared gaunt and older than his 53 years. Shortly before his release from prison earlier this year, Mr. Boesky was photographed with long grey hair and a beard. But yesterday, his hair was cropped short and he was clean-shaven.

Under direct examination by Mr. Gilbert, Mr. Boesky told of receiving inside information on various stock transactions from investment bankers Dennis Levine and Martin Siegel, both of whom have pleaded guilty to felonies relating to securities fraud. Mr. Boesky, who spoke of agreeing to pay for this information, said he often gave that information to Mr. Mulheren in 1985, although he said Mr. Mulheren never had "knowledge of my source." Nor did Mr. Boesky ever tell Mr. Mulheren that his information was obtained illegally, Mr. Boesky testified.

Based on the inside information Mr. Boesky provided him, Mr. Mulheren bought stocks in companies that were takeover targets, Mr. Boesky said. Mr. Boesky said he also advised Mr. Mulheren when to sell stock. Mr. Boesky said he illegally sold stock in Unocal Corp. to Mr. Mulheren to evade taxes and later illegally agreed to buy back the shares and cover Mr. Mulheren for any losses his securities firm might suffer on the stock.

Mr. Mulheren is charged with 41 counts of conspiracy, securities and mail fraud and keeping false records. He is not charged with insider trading. The charges, brought against Mr. Mulheren last year, were based largely on information provided by Mr. Boesky.

Mr. Boesky, who spoke in a firm voice, said he was once a close friend of the 40-year-old Mr. Mulheren, whom he has known for about a decade. Mr. Mulheren once served as co-trustee of the trust Mr. Boesky established for his children. But after Mr. Boesky agreed to plead guilty in 1986 to a single felony count and to pay $100 million in fines and cooperate with the government, he implicated Mr. Mulheren. Mr. Boesky was recently released from prison after serving most of a three-year term.

In response to questions from Mr. Gilbert, Mr. Boesky said he is currently employed as a consultant to a company, which he didn't identify. Mr. Boesky said he advises that company on "different sorts of business planning that the company is undertaking."

At times, Mr. Boesky seemed to want to take charge of the questioning. When a juror complained that she couldn't hear Mr. Boesky, he instructed Mr. Gilbert to repeat the question, a task usually undertaken by the judge.

When asked by Mr. Puccio about certain records kept by his brokerage firm, Seemala Corp., Mr. Boesky refused to answer questions. "When were these records kept?" Mr. Puccio inquired. "You'd have to ask the keeper," Mr. Boesky responded. "Were these records retained by your company?" Mr. Puccio asked. "You'd have to talk to the retainer about that," Mr. Boesky said.

In an interview, Mr. Mulheren described Mr. Boesky's testimony yesterday as "pretty boring." He said: "When you hear my testimony, you'll realize he wasn't much of a friend." But Mr. Mulheren said that if Mr. Boesky's performance as a government witness doesn't improve, Mr. Mulheren may not have to testify at all.

Mr. Boesky testified that his securities firm, Seemala, violated the net capital requirements of the New York Stock Exchange by having too little capital between June and October of 1985. But Mr. Boesky said he never worried much about the violations because he always believed that his accountants were underestimating the firm's capital position, a practice referred to by Boesky employees as the "fudge factor."

Mr. Boesky said that Stephen Conway, the president of his holding company, warned in a June 1985 memo that Mr. Boesky was "on a self-destruct course." Mr. Boesky said Mr. Conway told him he was seriously below capital requirements and that his business strategy was "reckless."

The net capital violations are important because the government has alleged that Mr. Mulheren manipulated the price of Gulf & Western Inc. shares and "parked" stock for Mr. Boesky to help Mr. Boesky defeat net capital requirements. Parking occurs when an investor sells securities with the understanding that they will be repurchased, usually at a prearranged time and price.

Mr. Boesky's cooperation with the government has led to guilty pleas by Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc., former Drexel junk bond wizard Michael Milken, Mr. Siegel, a former investment banker at Kidder Peabody & Co. and Boyd Jefferies, former chairman of Jefferies Group Inc., among others. Mr. Mulheren is the only individual implicated by Mr. Boesky who has chosen to fight the charges against him in court.

VERMONT CAN'T FORCE appointed judges to retire at age 70, court rules.

The U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals said appointed judges, like most other employees, are protected under federal age-discrimination law. The appeals court panel rejected Vermont's arguments that the judges were "appointees on the policy-making level" and thus exempted from the law. The court also rejected Vermont's claims that the age-discrimination law violated constitutional protection of state sovereignty.

The appeals court said, "We conclude that the language and structure of the definition of `employee' {in the age-discrimination law} suggest that Congress meant the policy-maker category to comprise only policy makers working closely with the elected official."

The decision, however, is at odds with rulings in similar cases by two other federal appeals courts, each of which found that Congress intended for appointed judges not to be covered by the law.

William B. Gray, an attorney for the state of Vermont, said a decision on whether to seek U.S. Supreme Court review of the decision probably won't be made for several weeks.

LITERARY AGENCY is formed by Boston law firm.

The Palmer & Dodge Agency will review manuscripts, provide editorial help, and negotiate contracts for authors and writers, says Ronald Kessel, managing partner of 150-lawyer Palmer & Dodge.

Since the early 1980s, many law firms have opened non-legal affiliates in such areas as consulting, public relations and real estate. But this is believed to be the first time that an established law firm has opened its own literary agency.

The agency's director is John Taylor Williams, a Palmer & Dodge partner who co-authored a book about publishing law. Its managing director is Jill Kneerim, a former editor and publisher who is not an attorney.

The agency has signed up more than a dozen clients, including former New England Patriots football player Brian Holloway, who is writing a book about child rearing, and adventurer Rory Nugent.

CAREER MOVES: Charles A. Shanor, general counsel of the U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission, will join the Los Angeles law firm of Paul, Hastings, Janofsky and Walker, which has more than 400 lawyers. Mr. Shanor will work in the law firm's 36-lawyer Washington office in an "of counsel" capacity, specializing in labor and employment issues. . . . Noah H. Huffstetler III, a health-care attorney, joined the Winston-Salem, N.C., law firm of Petree Stockton & Robinson as a partner in its Raleigh office. Mr. Huffstetler, 39 years old, was formerly a partner at the firm of Moore & Van Allen in Raleigh, N.C. He is the primary outside counsel for the North Carolina Hospital Association.

LONDON-BOUND: Piper & Marbury said it plans to open a London office in June to serve clients with investments and businesses in Europe. George P. Stamas, a partner in the Baltimore firm's corporate and securities department, will coordinate the office, which initially will have one full-time resident lawyer.
