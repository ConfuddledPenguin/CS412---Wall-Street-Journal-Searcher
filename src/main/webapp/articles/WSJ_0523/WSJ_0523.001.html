Inside Track: Corporate Insiders Continue to Purchase Bank Stocks Despite Industry's Problems

  • Author: Georgette Jasen
  • Date: 05/23/90

NEW YORK -- Despite the well-publicized problems in the banking industry, bank stocks remain popular with corporate insiders. Reports filed with the Securities and Exchange Commission by officers, directors and other insiders who buy shares in their companies continue to list bank stocks among the most-bought issues. It could be a case of bargain-hunting. The stocks are selling at prices well below what they were just a few months ago. "Just about every bank stock is depressed," said George Salem, an analyst who follows banks for Prudential-Bache Securities Inc. "Some may be takeover targets, but it would be because they're ailing, not because they're beautiful."

At Miami-based Southeast Banking Corp., SEC filings show, Alfonso J. Fanjul Jr., a director, paid $3.4 million to buy 250,000 shares last month. While the stock was selling for more than $20 at the beginning of the year, Mr. Fanjul paid between $13 and $13.50 a share, the filings show. Southeast Banking stock closed yesterday at $13.125, down 12.5 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading.

Mr. Fanjul, who a Southeast Banking spokeswoman described as an investor with a number of holdings, was out of the country and couldn't be reached for comment. According to SEC filings, his latest purchases bring his Southeast Banking holdings to 251,584 shares held indirectly and 3,183 held directly.

Insiders at First Interstate Bancorp in Los Angeles also bought company stock last month, SEC filings show. Director William F. Miller bought 1,000 shares for $35.25 each, bringing his holdings to 1,310 shares; Warren Christopher, another director, bought 500 shares at $35.13 each, raising his total holdings to 1,545 shares. First Interstate's stock price, which was about $44 in early January, closed yesterday on the Big Board at $38.50, down 25 cents. Mr. Miller and Mr. Christopher couldn't be reached for comment.

Other banks where insiders have bought stock recently include Boatmen's Bancshares Inc., St. Louis; Crestar Financial Corp., Richmond, Va.; First Essex Bancorp Inc., Lawrence, Mass.; and Shawmut National Corp., Boston.

"The worry list is still out there," said James McDermott, a bank analyst with Keefe, Bruyette & Woods Inc. "But for those willing to brave some of the near-term uncertainty . . ., there are some very attractive yields."

SELLING THE STORE: The chairman and the president of Circuit City Stores Inc. sold significant portions of their company holdings last month, SEC filings show.

Richard L. Sharp, president and chief executive officer of the Richmond, Va.-based electronics retailer, sold 110,000 shares, about 19% of his holdings, for nearly $3 million. Alan L. Wurtzel, chairman, sold 85,000 shares, about 30% of his stake, for about $2.2 million.

Neither was available for comment. A company spokeswoman said Mr. Sharp sold to diversify his personal holdings; he still holds 454,530 shares. She said Mr. Wurtzel has been gradually selling his stock since he retired from active management of the company in 1986; he continues to hold 197,500 shares, plus options.

Mr. Sharp sold most of his shares at $27.25 each and the remainder at $27 a share. Mr. Wurtzel sold at between $26.75 and $27.13 a share. The stock closed yesterday at $26.50, unchanged, in Big Board trading.

Circuit City reported a 12% increase in earnings for the year ended Feb. 28 and reported increases in sales for the first two months of the current fiscal year.

SELLING MICKEY AND DONALD: Gary L. Wilson, who stepped down as chief financial officer of Walt Disney Co. at the end of last year, sold $17.3 million of Disney stock last month, reports filed with the SEC show. Roy E. Disney, vice chairman and a nephew of the company founder, sold about $3.6 million of the stock, according to the filings.

The 150,000 shares Mr. Wilson sold were his entire stake in the Burbank, Calif.-based entertainment giant, although he continues to hold options to buy more than 300,000 Disney shares. The executive, who remains a Disney director and adviser, was part of the group that bought Northwest Airlines for $3.65 billion last year.

Mr. Wilson said the Disney shares he sold were bought through the exercise of options in August. "You just can't afford not to sell from an economic point of view," he said, noting the tax advantages of selling six months after an option-related purchase. "I have great confidence in {Disney} and think it's one of the great companies in America," he added.

Mr. Disney wasn't available to comment on his sale of 32,000 shares, which was just slightly more than 1% of his total holdings. A spokesman said the sale was for estate and tax-planning purposes and to help pay for a new house.

Mr. Wilson sold his stock at between $115 and $115.38 a share, Mr. Disney at between $112.13 and $112.50 a share. The shares closed yesterday in New York Stock Exchange trading at $119.50, up $3.25.

BUYING IN: SEC filings show that nine Safeway Inc. executives bought stock late last month in the company's initial public offering of 10 million shares. The Oakland, Calif.-based supermarket chain, which went private in November 1986 in a leveraged buy-out led by Kohlberg Kravis Roberts & Co., sold 10% of the company back to the public at $11.25 a share. The stock closed yesterday at $12, down 12.5 cents, on the Big Board.

The nine executives, including seven vice presidents and two divisional managers, reported buying between 100 and 15,200 shares each. A company spokesman said the purchases were individual decisions and that he didn't know how many employees bought stock in the offering.

In addition, James H. Greene Jr., a Safeway director and a KKR associate, bought 7,000 shares. George R. Roberts, a KKR partner, reported the purchase of 200,000 shares, held by a trust for the benefit of his children, and Robert I. MacDonnell, another KKR partner, reported the purchase of 30,000 shares held by a trust for the benefit of his children.
