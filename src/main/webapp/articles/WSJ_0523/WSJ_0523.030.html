Marketing & Media: Study Says Minors Buy $1.25 Billion Of Tobacco a Year

  • Author: Kathleen Deveny
  • Date: 05/23/90

Children and teen-agers purchase $1.25 billion in tobacco products annually, according to a study published in today's Journal of the American Medical Association. Although 43 states prohibit the sale of tobacco to minors, American children between the ages of eight and 18 consume 947 million packs of cigarettes and 26 million containers of smokeless tobacco each year, according to the survey.

The survey also estimates annual federal and state tax revenues from tobacco sales to children at $325 million.

The authors of the study -- Joe Tye, president of Stop Teenage Addiction to Tobacco, and Joseph DiFranza, an assistant professor at the University of Massachusetts Medical Center -- blame the problem on lax enforcement of minimum-age laws by local officials and indifference to the problem on the part of tobacco marketers.

"It really is an enormous business," said Dr. DiFranza, "In medicine we treat tobacco as an addictive substance, yet an 11-year-old can buy a pack of cigarettes."

Some critics fear that's happening more frequently. Although unit sales of cigarettes in the U.S. slumped 3% last year, one independent study, the Gallup Youth Survey, shows that the percentage of teenagers aged 13-15 who smoke jumped to 11% last year from 7% in 1988. At the same time, sales of moist snuff, which is popular among teen-age boys, increased 4.6%.

Although most states have laws in place that restrict tobacco sales to minors, few are enforced, according to the JAMA study. Several small communities, however, have enacted tough new laws, and early results indicate they can be effective. In Woodridge, Ill., a Chicago suburb, an ordinance enacted a year ago requires merchants selling tobacco to purchase licenses from the town. Retailers who repeatedly violate the ban may lose their license and be fined up to $400.

To test compliance, the Woodridge police department regularly sends out 13-year-old "agents" to try to buy tobacco from the village's 38 licensed vendors. Last March, three stores lost their licenses for supplying cigarettes. Since then, according to local police officials, no retailers have gotten caught in the sting operations.

Tobacco marketers deny targeting children. The $40 billion industry steadfastly maintains its ads and promotions are meant for adults only. "The tobacco industry is diametrically opposed to kids using tobacco," said a spokeswoman for the Tobacco Institute, an industry trade group.

Manufacturers argue that their customers are wholesalers, rather than consumers, and that they can't always control retailers' actions. A spokesman for Philip Morris Cos. said the company provides retailers with signs that proclaim age limits, and gives out brochures to instruct store clerks about tobacco laws.
