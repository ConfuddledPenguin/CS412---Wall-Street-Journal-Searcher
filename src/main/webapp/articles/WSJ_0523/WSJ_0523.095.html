Technology & Health: Prospects for Mandatory U.S. Inspection Of Seafood Improve After Vote in Senate

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 05/23/90

WASHINGTON -- The prospects for a mandatory seafood-inspection program improved greatly as a Senate panel voted to put the nation's $30 billion seafood industry under a triad of regulatory agencies. The Senate Agriculture Committee approved a bipartisan bill that would give the Agriculture Department the lead in inspecting 1,900 seafood processors nationwide. Currently, only a fraction of the four billion pounds of seafood consumed each year in the U.S. undergoes some form of product-grading or inspection.

Consumer advocates are optimistic that a mandatory inspection bill will be passed this year. Yesterday's unanimous vote marked the first time in more than 20 years that they have succeeded in getting such a bill out of committee.

Nonetheless, it wasn't a complete victory for them. The committee, in a 10-9 vote, deleted provisions designed to protect from reprisals company whistleblowers who tip off government inspectors about unsanitary conditions or contaminated products. Ellen Haas, executive director of Public Voice for Food and Health Policy, a consumer group, assailed the lawmakers for weakening the legislation and predicted a floor fight over the issue of whistleblower protection.

The measure, sponsored by Senate Majority Leader George Mitchell (D., Maine.) and co-sponsored by 29 other senators, "has a lot of horsepower behind it," says Richard Gutting, lobbyist for the National Fisheries Institute, the industry's leading trade group. From his perspective, he adds, "it's a good, solid bill" that can be improved upon.

Yet to be reconciled are differences between Mr. Mitchell's measure and a seafood-inspection bill now pending in the Senate Commerce Committee, which would make the Commerce Department's National Marine Fisheries Service the lead regulatory agency.

In the House, Commerce Committee Chairman John Dingell (D., Mich.) and Agriculture Committee Chairman Kika de la Garza (D., Texas) are pushing rival bills. Their battle essentially is a fight over turf. Mr. Dingell would give the lead to the Food and Drug Administration, which his committee oversees, while Mr. de la Garza would give the lead to the Agriculture Department, which falls under the Agriculture panel's jurisdiction.

Mr. Dingell's bill easily cleared the Commerce Committee in March and awaits floor action.

The Agriculture Department already inspects meat and poultry for bacterial and chemical contaminants, among other things. The Mitchell bill would add fish and seafood to its duties. It also would empower the FDA to set limits on bacteria and other contaminants, and it would give the Fisheries Service authority to close polluted waters to fishing and to shellfish harvesting.

Currently, Agriculture Department inspectors are posted at beef, pork, lamb and poultry slaughterhouses whenever they are in operation. Under the Mitchell bill, there wouldn't be continuous inspection of seafood processing plants. Instead, inspectors would make unannounced visits at seafood plants and focus mostly on so-called critical control points. They would look to plant workers for information on improper or unsanitary practices when they aren't there.

For this reason, consumer advocates regard whistleblower protection as indispensible. "You either have to have continuous inspection or you have to have protection of those workers," asserted consumer advocate Carol Tucker Foreman.

But Sen. Thad Cochran (R., Miss.) persuaded his colleagues to strike whistleblower protection from the bill. He argued that the provision would delegate too much power to workers, including the right to refuse to work if they believe there was a food-safety violation.

Consumer advocates also criticized the committee for not providing for inspection of fishing vessels, a task they would entrust to the Fisheries Service.
