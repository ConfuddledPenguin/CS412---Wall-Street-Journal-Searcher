Credit Markets: Treasury Bond Prices Rise as Foreign Investors, From Mideast and Japan, Stage a Buying Spree

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Stuart Drown
  • Date: 05/23/90

NEW YORK -- Foreign investors flush with cash went on a bond buying spree yesterday, pushing bond prices higher in moderate trading activity. The Treasury's benchmark 30-year bond rose 3/4 point yesterday, a gain of $7.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction from its price, eased to 8.61% from 8.68% on Monday.

Traders said the market started to climb early in the morning as Arab and Japanese investors used proceeds from some maturing Japanese government bonds to buy U.S. Treasury securities.

Raymond Stone, a managing director at Stone & McCarthy Research Associates, estimates that $22 billion of Japanese securities matured yesterday, providing fixed-income investors with a huge amount of cash to re-invest.

Bonds were also supported by a story in yesterday's Chicago Tribune, which suggested that the federal budget deficit was smaller than official forecasts. Such news can boost bond prices because investors reason that a smaller deficit would reduce the government's borrowing needs and would eventually lead to lower interest rates.

The story, which traders eventually discounted and economists discredited, said that 13 federal agencies had accumulated $50 billion in unspent appropriations. By afternoon, analysts had concluded that the $50 billion did not represent cash that could be applied to the government's deficit.

Still, prices continued to climb due mainly to short-covering by dealers. Traders said the short-covering occurred as bond futures on the Chicago Board of Trade moved through technical resistance levels. In a short sale, dealers and professional traders sell borrowed securities and later seek to make a profit by replacing what was borrowed with cheaper securities. Essentially, these dealers are betting that prices will fall. But when prices rise instead, many dealers rush to cover the short before prices move any higher.

Bond dealers said that yesterday's trading activity could cast a positive light over the Treasury's notes sales, which are scheduled to take place this week. The Treasury will sell two-year notes today and five-year notes on Thursday.

In when-issued trading, the two-year Treasury notes were quoted to yield 8.55%, compared with 8.61% on Monday. The five-year notes set for sale Thursday were quoted to yield 8.59% compared with 8.68% late Monday.

Junk Bonds

Continued buying interest by retail investors helped sustain momentum in the junk-bond market, forcing prices of speculative-grade issues higher once again.

Most junk bonds, or high-risk corporate bonds that have low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, gained 1/4 to 1/2 point.

Pay-in-kind bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. continued to outshine the broader high-yield market, recording gains of as much as 7/8 point. Pay-in-kind bonds, which are extremely volatile, pay interest in the form of more securities instead of cash.

Market participants say the bullish sentiment, which first hit the market's top-tier issues, is rapidly spreading to the middle-tier and lower-tier names, which also registered impressive gains this session.

Among the leading winners were junk bonds of Charter Medical Inc. and McCaw Cellular Communications Inc. Charter Medical's 14% senior subordinated debentures due in 2000, chalked up one point to finish at 52 1/2 while McCaw Cellular's 12.95% senior subordinated debentures, due in 1999, surged two points to end at 80 1/2.

Joseph Bencivenga, director and manager of high-yield research at Salomon Brothers Inc., said that since "high-quality, high-grade names do not have the upside potential of the other two tiers of the market," many investors are focusing less on these names and concentrating more on the middle-tier and lower-tier junk bond issues.

"The bottom two tiers -- that's where the real value lies," he said.

High-yield analysts said the junk bond market's recent rise has been fueled by the relative scarcity of new issues in which investors can reinvest cash interest income.

According to Robert Long, managing director and head of high-yield research at First Boston Corp., about $6.5 billion of cash interest income was generated from outstanding junk bond issues during the first quarter.

In the absence of a heavy new-issue calendar, most of these funds will have to be reinvested in the secondary market.

Foreign Bonds

Canadian bond prices plunged as investors reacted to uncertainty over the outcome of the country's constitutional debate.

Reports that a key minister in the Canadian government, Lucien Bouchard, would resign from the Progressive Conservative Party triggered the rout.

In a letter to Prime Minister Brian Mulroney, Mr. Bouchard said he was strongly opposed to any change to the proposed Meech Lake constitutional accord, which recognizes Quebec as a "distinct society" within Canada.

The announcement follows the resignations of two other Conservative Members of Parliament from Quebec.

Investors are concerned that if the accord fails to pass by the June 23 deadline, Quebec will begin serious talk of separating from the rest of Canada. Quebec independance would create a large gray area about currency value, debt and stability, analysts said.

Canadian bond prices sank as much as 2 3/8 points by midafternoon. The market recovered slightly and the Canadian 20-year bellwether bond closed about 2 points lower at 89.00 to yield 10.82%.

Dan Gosselin, chief bond trader at Wood Gundy Inc., said selling pressure was broadly based, but trading activity was thin. The thin market conditions, he said, exaggerated the decline. He calls the drop "significant" and said a decline of this size hasn't occurred in "recent memory. I can't recall a day being this bad."

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 101 15/32, up from 100 20/32 on Monday. Ten-year notes ended at 101 15/32 to yield 8.63% compared with 100 27/32 to yield 8.73%.

Short-term rates were little changed yesterday. The discount rate on three-month Treasury bills ended unchanged from Monday's average auction of 7.74% for a bond-equivalent yield of 7.98%. The rate on six-month bills was slightly lower when compared to Monday's average auction. The rate ended yesterday at 7.76% for a bond-equivalent yield of 8.17%.

Mortgage-, Asset-Backed Securities

Mortgage-backed securities ended as much as 3/8 point firmer, reflecting Treasury gains.

Spreads widened slightly from late Monday, with Government National Mortgage Association 9% bonds for June delivery now yielding 1.13 percentage point more than 10-year Treasurys, compared with a spread of 1.11 percentage point late Monday.

Ginnie Mae 9% issues for June delivery were last quoted at 95 19/32, up 3/8 point, to yield 9.78%.

Two new issues priced this session are said to have been well bid.

In the mortgage-derivative market, Fannie Mae issued a $300 million real estate mortgage investment conduit offering backed by its 8.5% bonds via Merrill Lynch Capital Markets.

In the asset-backed arena, Citicorp's $1.41 billion issue of global credit card-backed bonds priced in the morning was all sold soon after its launch.

More than $625 million of securities were sold to non-U.S. buyers.

Citicorp has made other credit-card issues to international buyers, but those deals didn't allow settlements in different markets. This issue was the first that could be settled through the U.S. Depository Trust and both Eurobond clearing agents, Euroclear and Cedel.

The issue was lead-managed by Salomon Brothers International, and consisted of $1.25 billion of Tranche A bonds carrying a 9 3/8% coupon that were priced to yield 0.80 percentage point over five-year Treasury notes. A much smaller Tranche B, $155 million of 9 5/8% bonds, yielded one percentage point over the five-year Treasury.

Corporate Issues

Most investment-grade corporate bonds ended between 1/4 point and 1/2 point higher yesterday.

The major news in the market yesterday was that Moody's Investors Service Inc. reduced Citicorp's senior-debt rating to single-A-3 from single-A-1 and its commercial-paper rating to Prime-2 from Prime-1.

Moody's cited Citicorp's comparatively low reserve and capital levels and rising number of problem loans.

Analysts say the Moody's downgrade, which came late yesterday, may have only a modest impact on the bonds during trading today. Some expect spreads on Citicorp debt to widen by about 0.1 percentage point.

"I think the {market's} expectation was probably {a downgrade} of one notch with some chance of two," said Jay Weintraub, a director in the corporate bond research department at Salomon Brothers Inc. "This represents the downside but was not totally unexpected."

Municipals

Tax-exempt issues were up 1/8 to 3/8 point in quiet, late dealings, as they underperformed U.S. Treasurys.

While an early report of $50 billion in surplus U.S. government funds was later discounted, Treasurys continued to exhibit strength, which helped to pull municipals higher, traders said.

However, with more than $1 billion in new issues priced, activity during the session focused on the new issue market, traders said.

In one of the largest deals of the session, a Bear Stearns & Co. group set the preliminary pricing for an issue of $250 million of Connecticut special-tax, general-obligation, transportation-infrastructure bonds, to yield from 6.10% in 1992 to 7.23% in 2007.

The bonds are rated single-A-1 by Moody's Investors Service Inc. and double-A-minus by Standard & Poor's Corp. and Fitch Investors Service Inc.

Among actively traded bonds, the New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 was up 3/8 point at 97 7/8 to yield 7.37%.

No large competitive deals are expected to be priced today.
