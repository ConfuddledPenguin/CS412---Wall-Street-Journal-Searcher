Politics & Policy: Study on School, Job Testing Finds Bias And Misuse, Urges Special Regulator

  • Author: Gary Putka
  • Date: 05/23/90

School and employment testing is growing out of control and needs a special regulator, according to a study commissioned by the Ford Foundation. The report, the outcome of three years of work, says that standardized multiple-choice tests in the schools are widely misused, misreported and cheated on, with virtually no meaningful regulatory monitoring. As a result, the report said, minority groups and female students are unfairly discriminated against, and all students are distracted from learning.

Although various groups have criticized the tests in the past over particular issues, the study was the first from a broad-based group of politicians, educators and businessmen to be so critical of mass testing. Many of the new tests were produced by state laws and other school-reform initiatives in the 1980s.

The report estimated that U.S. students from kindergarten to 12th grade are taking 127 million standardized tests a year -- almost three per student and higher than any previous estimate. The report said that from 1972 to 1989, the number of statewide school testing programs grew to 50 from one. It also said more schools are relying heavily on tests to decide entry to and exit from kindergarten, promotion from grade to grade, placement in remedial programs and graduation from high school.

"Current testing, predominantly multiple choice in format, is over-relied upon, lacks adequate public accountability, sometimes leads to unfairness in the allocation of opportunities and too often undermines vital social policies," the report said.

Although no tests were named in the count, included in the study were exams such as the Scholastic Aptitude Test for college admissions, publishing-house products such as the California Achievement Test and the Metropolitan Achievement Test, and assessments created and given by state departments of education. The study's authors, calling themselves the National Commission on Testing and Public Policy, included Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, Julius Chambers of the NAACP Legal Defense Fund, and officials from Apple Computer Inc., Pacific Telesis Group, and Aetna Life & Casualty Co.

Testing-industry officials and others disputed the report's claims and questioned the need for an oversight body for testing.

"Based on your characterizations of what's in it I'd say {the report} is doing a gross disservice to test publishers," said David Deffley, president of the test-writing unit of MacMillan/McGraw-Hill School Publishing Co., a joint venture of McGraw-Hill Inc. and Maxwell Communication Corp.'s MacMillan subsidiary. Mr. Deffley, who said he hadn't yet seen the report, added that test publishers shouldn't be blamed when a school uses their tests for things they weren't designed for, such as promotion or graduation decisions.

The report gave no estimate for the number of employment tests being given, but concluded that they were growing rapidly. Walter Haney, a professor at Boston College who worked on the study, said that more than half of all employers use some form of entry test to screen applicants, and that more government authorities and professional groups have mandated licensing and certification tests in recent years.

The commission said it was most concerned with over-reliance on multiple-choice tests as "gatekeepers" that "allocate initial opportunities in education, employment and the military." In addition to less testing, it advocated more use of alternative assessments such as reviews of work samples, essays, and oral presentations.

The study said that teachers and school officials are being held more accountable for their students' test results, even though the tests being used weren't designed for that purpose. As a result, it said, schools are being driven to exaggerate results, by couching scores that are disclosed in the most favorable terms.

Christopher Cross, the head of research at the U.S. Department of Education, said there shouldn't be a federal role in regulating school testing, since education is a state and local responsibility. He also said he wasn't sure of the reliability of the study's estimates, noting that 127 million tests might take more time to administer than the half-day per year that the group estimated was being spent by each student taking tests. But Cinthia Schuman, executive director of the National Center for Fair and Open Testing, a Cambridge, Mass., anti-testing group, said "government regulation is the only way we'll make the testing industry accountable."
