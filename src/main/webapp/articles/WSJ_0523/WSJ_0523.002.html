President's Son Neil to Appear on Hill Today to Explain Role in S&L's Demise

  • Author: Paulette Thomas
  • Date: 05/23/90

WASHINGTON -- Neil Bush, son of the president, will be asked to explain publicly today why he and others approved a series of transactions that led to the demise of a Denver-based thrift. He is to appear before the House Banking Committee to testify about his role as a director of Silverado Banking, Savings & Loan Association. Mr. Bush didn't gain directly from the transactions but, as a director, he approved loans to his outside business associates. In at least one case, Mr. Bush didn't disclose on financial forms that he had a business relationship with a borrower, whose $106 million in Silverado transactions eventually soured. Thrift regulators are pursuing an administrative action against Mr. Bush, which he is contesting.

Silverado differed from other big thrift failures, regulators said. For example, compared with Lincoln Savings & Loan Association, Irvine, Calif., and CenTrust Savings, Miami, whose failures may end up costing taxpayers twice as much, Silverado's records and underwriting were more complete. And it was less combative with regulators than other more high-flying S&Ls.

But regulators accuse Silverado, whose losses could total $1 billion, of requiring borrowers to also become Silverado investors by tying Silverado loans to the purchase of Silverado preferred stock that the thrift could count toward capital. It also inflated appraisals and granted preferential loans to officers, not including Mr. Bush. It also hid losses by selling pieces of a loan pool to borrowers, who took millions in Silverado's losses, which violates accounting rules.

"Much of Silverado's high-flying was in the same stratosphere with the Lincolns and the CenTrusts, but the Denver thrift wins the title for the most creative self-financing mechanisms," said Rep. Henry Gonzalez, (D., Texas), chairman of the committee.

As previously disclosed, JNB Exploration, Mr. Bush's energy company, had oil and gas deals with two of Silverado's major borrowers, real-estate developers Kenneth Good and Bill Walters. Although Mr. Bush didn't vote to approve Silverado loans to Mr. Good, in 1986 he did approve $106 million in loans and other transactions to Mr. Walters.

So far, thrift regulators said they have made 11 referrals of criminal activity to the Justice Department for possible legal action. They declined yesterday to identify the parties involved or the actions that are the subject of the referrals.

Five former directors and officers of Silverado have signed consent agreements barring them from working in the financial industry without receiving permission from regulators.

Mr. Bush, asserting he did nothing wrong, refused to sign a consent agreement in January, and the matter is being pursued in an administrative proceeding. That action would prohibit Mr. Bush from engaging in similar lending agreements if he were to serve on the board of any financial institution in the future. At that time, Mr. Bush also said that he didn't benefit directly or indirectly from the loans to Mr. Walters.

The banking committee also said it plans to issue a subpoena to Kermit Mowbray, former president of the Federal Home Loan Bank of Topeka, Kan., who declined to appear yesterday.

The committee, which asked Mr. Mowbray to testify, said it wants to ask him why, as the top regional regulator, he waited weeks after a staff recommendation to seize the institution -- until after the 1988 presidential election.

Federal regulators said yesterday that hundreds of ailing thrifts were being seized during late 1988, and that the election and the presence of Neil Bush on the Silverado board, didn't influence the timing of Silverado's takeover.

In December 1988, Silverado was sold to First Nationwide Bank, the thrift subsidiary of Ford Motor Co.
