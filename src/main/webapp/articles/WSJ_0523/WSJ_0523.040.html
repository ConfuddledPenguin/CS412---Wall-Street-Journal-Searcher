Credit Ratings For Citicorp Cut by Moody's --- Downgrade Involves Debt Of $31 Billion; Reserves, Capital Levels Are Cited

  • Author: Robert Guenther
  • Date: 05/23/90

NEW YORK -- Moody's Investors Service Inc. lowered its credit ratings on Citicorp, including its commercial paper, citing comparatively low reserve and capital levels and a rising number of problem loans. The move, which follows a similar downgrading by Standard & Poor's Corp. last month, affects an estimated $31 billion in long-term and short-term debt. S&P had affirmed its rating on Citicorp's commercial paper, a regular source of funding for the nation's largest banking company.

Moody's dropped its ratings on senior debt to single-A-3 from single-A-1 and on commercial paper to Prime-2 from Prime-1. Subordinated debt and preferred were reduced to Baa-1 from single-A-2 and from single-A-3 to single-A-2, respectively. Moody's said a two-notch reduction in a rating is unusual.

The Prime-1 rating on the short-term deposits and letters of credit of its flagship, Citibank, were confirmed. However, long-term deposits and long-term letters of credit were reduced to single-A-1 from double-A-2.

It is unclear whether investors have fully discounted the downgrading. When the Moody's review was announced in April, the pricing on Citicorp paper rose relative to other issuers. Though Citicorp remains an investment-grade debtor, the lower rating on its commercial paper will make the bank's paper off-limits for some institutional investors.

Since the review was announced, Citicorp has downplayed the significance of a downgrading, noting that the company satisfies only about 2% of its funding needs through the commercial-paper market. In addition, the bank has no plans to issue long-term debt.

The company has defended its reserves and capital positions, emphasizing its unique global character and its rigorous underwriting standards in arguing why it shouldn't be held to the standards of other banks.

Nevertheless, the downgrading, which comes on the heels of similar reviews for dozens of other banks, could crimp Citicorp when it raises funds in the future.

The announcement was made after the close of trading. In composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, Citicorp was down 12.5 cents at $23.75 a share.

Kenneth J.H. Pinkes, a Moody's vice president, said, "They're still a strong company, but they have a low level of capital for a bank with their old rating. The company has always operated with a thin margin of safety, while pursuing multiple business opportunities. There isn't much of a cushion."

At the end of last year, Citicorp's nonperforming loans and those loans 90 days or more past due amounted to 6.6% of total loans. Meanwhile, its reserves totaled 44% of its problem loans.

By comparison, the averages for U.S. banks with more than $30 billion in assets are a 4.5% level of problem loans and reserves totaling 89% of such loans. BankAmerica Corp.'s loan problems, for instance, were 5.8% of its loans, and its reserves stood at 70% of problem portfolio.

Nevertheless, Moody's said that while Citicorp's real-estate portfolio is geographically diverse and while its underwriting standards appear to be sound, it is vulnerable to the deteriorating national real-estate market.

In a statement, Citicorp said, "We're very disappointed with Moody's action. We don't expect any significant increase in overall funding costs. Increasing securitization of assets has substantially decreased Citicorp's use of long-term debt, while deposit growth has reduced commercial paper to 2% of funding needs. Citibank retains the highest short-term deposit rating."

As previously indicated, Citicorp said it will rebuild capital throughout 1990 from retained earnings and sale of non-strategic assets as well as what it called "vigorous asset management."

Citicorp pointed out that the Aaa ratings on its most recent offering of securities backed by credit-card receivables aren't affected. Ratings on some mortgage-backed pass-throughs were lowered one notch. Additionally, several tranches of multiclass mortgage securities were reaffirmed.
