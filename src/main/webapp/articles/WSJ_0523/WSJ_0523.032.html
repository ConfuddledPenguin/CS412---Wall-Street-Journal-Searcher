Chrysler Drops Its Plan to Sell Defense Unit

  • Author: Jacqueline Mitchell
  • Date: 05/23/90

Chrysler Corp. says it has stopped seeking a buyer for its defense-related operations and will restructure and increase funding for research and development at the unit. The move comes nearly six months after the Highland Park, Mich., auto maker announced plans to sell Chrysler Technologies Corp. At the time of that announcement, and again earlier this month, Chrysler officials said they wouldn't sell any of the subsidiary's businesses if they didn't get satisfactory offers.

In March, Chrysler sold aircraft maker Gulfstream Aerospace, by far the largest of Chrysler Technologies' operations, for $825 million. Chrysler has been unsuccessful, however, in unloading the remaining three defense-related companies: Electrospace Systems, a defense and electronics parts company; Pentastar Electronics, a spin-off subisidary of Electrospace; and Airborne Systems, a manufacturer of systems for military and commercial airplanes.

Initially, Chrysler said it wanted to divest Technologies to focus on its core auto operations. But despite a significant drop in the auto maker's first-quarter net income, there is "no dire need to sell the {subsidiary} at a value less than what {it's} worth," said John Stellman, Chrysler Technologies vice president and chief financial officer. "Chrysler has a strong cash position."

Partly because it wasn't a surprise, Chrysler's announcement had little impact on the auto maker's stock, which closed yesterday at $15.875, down 25 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading. "There is no pain in continuing to hold" the three businesses, said David Healy, auto analyst at the investment banking arm of Barclays Bank. "Clearly {Chrysler doesn't} need the money and the units are profitable."

Since putting Technologies up for sale last December, Chrysler has recorded its financial results as discontinued operations. Mr. Stellman said the subsidiary now will have to be "reconsolidated" into Chrysler's financial report, but the effect of this wasn't immediately available.

Chrysler originally paid a total of $1.04 billion for the businesses that it eventually combined into Chrysler Technologies. When the auto maker announced they were for sale, some analysts doubted the auto maker would recover its expenditures. The defense industry was in a downward spiral and widespread funding cuts for defense-related contracts had brought more sellers than buyers to a depressed market.

Keeping the three units will allow Technologies to move ahead with plans put on hold last December, said Paul E. Wright, Chrysler Technologies chairman. "The matter at hand is to posture the companies for the 90s and to continue their momentum and earnings growth," he said in a statement.

This will mean increasing funding for research and development. Expansion at Airborne Systems in Waco, Texas, also will continue; capacity there was doubled last year. Chrysler officials didn't disclose how much investment these moves will require.

Chrysler Technologies' defense-related contract awards rose 12% in 1989 from a year earlier and "we hope 1990 will be better," Mr. Stellman said. "We see healthy growth in new business opportunities."

Ford Motor Co. also is trying to sell its aerospace operations, but doesn't expect it will have the same difficulties as Chrysler. "I don't anticipate this kind of a problem," Philip E. Benton Jr., president and chief operating officer, said at a news conference in Phoenix, Ariz., yesterday. "In this kind of a market we could run into the same problem, but right now there are enough prospective buyers out there to give us an optimistic outlook." Mr. Benton said Ford is "in the midst of talks" on the subject, but didn't provide any details.
