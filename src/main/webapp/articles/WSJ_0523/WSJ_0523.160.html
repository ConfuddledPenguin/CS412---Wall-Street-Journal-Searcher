Men Get Same Parental-Leave Benefits As Women Under Appeals Court Ruling

  • Author: Clare Ansberry
  • Date: 05/23/90

PITTSBURGH -- A federal appeals court here ruled that any employer with more than 15 workers must provide a man with unpaid child-rearing leave if that benefit is offered to female co-workers. The decision by the Third U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals is only binding in Pennsylvania, Delaware, New Jersey and the Virgin Islands, but is likely to be influential on federal trial courts nationally.

The ruling could have far-reaching implications, affecting thousands of U.S. employers who offer parental leave benefits but traditionally provide such options to women only. Though an estimated 37% of employers with 100 workers or more offer parental leave, Congress is currently considering a family- and medical-leave bill that would provide broader coverage.

The appeals court found that the Pittsburgh Board of Education discriminated against teacher Gerald Schafer by denying him a one-year unpaid leave to take care of his son. The unpaid-leave benefit was included in a union contract but had only been used by women and was specifically denied to Mr. Schafer in 1981. The court ruled that the board violated Title VII of the Civil Rights Act, which prohibits sex or race discrimination by employers with more than 15 workers.

"This says an employer can't give benefits like child-rearing leave to one sex and not another," said Edward Feinstein of the Pittsburgh law firm Begler, Kowall & Ombres, who represented Mr. Schafer.

The appeals court's finding doesn't mean that fathers are entitled to sick leave or disability benefits. Rather, the three-judge panel said in its opinion, "If mothers can have child-rearing obligations immediately upon the birth of their child, then fathers also are entitled to exercise the option of using child-rearing leave immediately upon the birth of their child."

Michele Lord, senior research associate of the Family and Work Institute, said: "This sends a clear message that fathers ought to try to enforce their rights under Title VII." Few courts, she pointed out, have enforced the rights of fathers regarding child care.

Barbara Berish Brown, an employment-law specialist for the Washington firm Paul, Hastings, Janofsky & Walker, said the finding may surprise employers. She suspects many employers allow women to take leave beyond the period of physical disability, but men, asking for a similar leave, would run into opposition.

Yet while the ruling is significant legally, she said, "in real life I wonder how many men will ask to take advantage of child-rearing leave."

Donna Lenhoff, legal director of the Women's Legal Defense Fund, said the ruling also underscores the need for states and the federal government to adopt gender-neutral leave policies when considering parental leaves, as well as family and medical leaves.

In the Schafer case, the school board unsuccessfully argued that the leave was a maternity benefit and that Mr. Schafer, not having been disabled by the birth process, wasn't entitled to the leave.

Mr. Schafer resigned his position as a special-education teacher when alternative child-care arrangements fell through. He filed a complaint with the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission in 1982.

In 1987, a concent decree was signed in which the school agreed to grant leaves to men in the collective-bargaining agreement. However, the school didn't admit that it had violated Title VII, and didn't offer any back pay.

Mr. Schafer is seeking back pay, an issue the appeals court remanded back to the district court to determine whether he had any other alternative short of resignation. If not, he could receive back pay.
