K mart Holders Seek Voting Power On `Poison Pill'

  • Author: James P. Miller
  • Date: 05/23/90

K mart Corp. shareholders bucked directors' wishes and approved a proposal that seeks to have the company submit a 1988 "poison pill" rights plan to a binding stockholder vote. The proposal had been introduced by the State of Wisconsin Investment Board, and it marked the second victory for the big institutional holder this month. Last week, Champion International Corp. holders approved a similar proposal introduced by the investment board, which manages about $423 billion on behalf of state teachers and other employees.

Such shareholder resolutions impose no legal obligation on K mart's board. A spokeswoman for the Troy, Mich., retailer said "the board will take up the matter for review in the near future," and subsequently "make a recommendation as to whether they will put it forward for a vote."

The Wisconsin institutional holder, among the top 10 employee investment boards, controls 2.9 million of K mart's 199.7 million shares, and it also has a substantial Champion holding. The investment panel offered similar measures at Pfizer Inc. and United Technologies Corp. this spring, but lost with votes of about 38% and 41% respectively, an official said.

The investment board has campaigned since 1986 against what it sees as the improperly unilateral means by which poison-pill anti-takeover measures are put in place, as opposed to other measures that require shareholder approval.

Poison-pill measures are designed to deter hostile takeovers by making them prohibitively expensive, typically by allowing current shareholders to buy more stock of their corporation at a large discount if a hostile bidder acquires more than a specified percentage of the company's stock. Supporters of poison pills contend that their adoption forces bidders to negotiate with a corporation's directors, allowing the board to better pursue the company's longterm interests. But critics contend that they can be used to entrench management, at the expense of stockholders who might otherwise have received top-dollar bids for their holdings.

The Wisconsin investment board argued in its proxy materials that poison-pill measures "so significantly affect shareholder interests that shareholders should vote on such plans."

Jacqueline Doeler, the investment board's investment services manager, said "We aren't saying shareholder-rights plans are good or bad." Rather, she said, "our concern is the process of how they are adopted," exclusive of shareholder input. Ms. Doeler pointed out that other anti-takeover measures, such as staggered terms for directors or provisions addressing two-tier price offers, typically require advance approval by shareholders.

In its rebuttal to the resolution, K mart's board said it considered the "proponent's view that plans such as the company's rights plan deter legitimate acquisition proposals and result in decreased stock values" before it adopted the plan; the board concluded then, it said, that "such arguments were speculative, at best, and did not outweigh the potential benefits to stockholders" such plans afford.
