Energy Department Mulls Soviet Offer On Uranium Services

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/23/90

WASHINGTON -- Energy Secretary James Watkins confirmed reports that the Energy Department is weighing an offer to buy uranium-enrichment services from the Soviet government. Speaking to reporters after giving a speech to a nuclear-industry group here, Adm. Watkins broke his agency's official silence on the offer, saying it was being considered "very informally" and with the approval of the State Department. "They {the Soviets} have a cash-flow problem and we're sympathetic," he said.

Nuclear-industry officials estimate that a deal between Energy Department and the Soviet Ministry of Atomic Energy and Industry could generate as much as $200 million a year that, under one version of the proposal, would be spent by the Soviet Union on goods and services in the U.S.

In the past, the department has exercised a near-monopoly on uranium-enrichment services, which consists of increasing the percentage of the unstable U-235 isotope that is found in natural uranium. But the Soviet Union, using more modern equipment, is able to sharply undercut the U.S. price for such services, in some cases charging about half of the Energy Department price.

One result is that the department has begun losing some of its major customers among U.S. utilities to the spot market for uranium-enrichment services, which is dominated by the Soviet marketing agency V/O Techsnabexport, part of the Soviet Ministry of Atomic Energy and Industry.

Although some people in the industry said they expect the announcement of a deal as early as next week's summit in Washington, Adm. Watkins said he is still studying the political and economic aspects of such a trade. He pointed out that he wouldn't approve it if it turned out to be a political obstacle to efforts to develop more modern, privately run enrichment plants in the U.S.
