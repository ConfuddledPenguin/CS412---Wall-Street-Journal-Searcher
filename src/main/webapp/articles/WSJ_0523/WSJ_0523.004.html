Striking Greyhound Union Makes Offer To Return in Move Tied to Back-Pay Bid

  • Author: Robert Tomsho
  • Date: 05/23/90

DALLAS -- In a maneuver that further clouds the future of troubled Greyhound Lines Inc., the union representing striking Greyhound employees has made an unconditional offer to return to work. But instead of being an earnest effort to end the often-violent walkout that began March 2, Amalgamated Transit Union officials acknowledged that the action was a legal tactic aimed at setting the stage for a potentially huge back-pay claim against the struggling unit of GLI Holding Co.

The offer, made in a letter to the company, comes one week after the National Labor Relations Board issued a complaint alleging that Greyhound had engaged in an unfair labor practice in contract negotiations with the ATU.

Greyhound has promised to fight that complaint through the federal courts if necessary, but if unfair bargaining practices and not economic issues are ultimately found to have caused the strike, the company could be forced to rehire striking drivers and pay them for time lost during the strike.

"In order to start the clock on back pay running, we have to make an unconditional offer to return to work," said Earle Putnam, ATU general counsel. "That is what the (union) has done."

"They have done what was smart and that's a first," said P. Anthony Lannie, Greyhound executive vice president and chief labor negotiator. He estimated the company's back pay liability at about $13.6 million a month.

Mr. Lannie said Greyhound would offer its remaining job openings to striking union members under the terms of a May 5 contract offer rejected by the union. "But the bottom line is that we just don't have many vacancies left," he said.

Since the strike by 9,400 ATU members began, Greyhound has struggled to operate a slightly smaller regular-route system with a work force that now includes 598 union drivers who crossed picket lines and 3,177 newly hired replacement drivers.

Although Greyhound could need as many as 500 additional drivers in the event of a busy summer travel season, company spokesman George Gravley said Greyhound has openings for only 108 full-time drivers now, and 98 of those positions are part of a local transit contract that expires July 1.

The union's offer comes a day after Greyhound said it would offer to redeem about $191 million in face amount of debt for about $86 million. Some bond analysts said the union's move could make it more difficult for debt-burdened Greyhound, which has already suffered huge losses from the strike, to finance the tender offer.

"It does have a negative impact on the company's ability to raise the financing," said Leigh Walzer, a bond analyst with R.D. Smith & Co. "Now, anyone who would put the new money into Greyhound, aside from the other economic risks they face, has to worry about a back-pay claim that the union could win down the road."
