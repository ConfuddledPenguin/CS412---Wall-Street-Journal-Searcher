Foreign Exchange: Canadian Dollar Moves Lower As Political Impasse Intensifies

  • Author: John Urquhart and Lynne Kilpatrick
  • Date: 05/23/90

OTTAWA -- The Canadian dollar and Canadian bonds fell as Prime Minister Brian Mulroney's chief Quebec political lieutenant, Lucien Bouchard, resigned over Canada's constitutional impasse. Currency and bond traders said Mr. Bouchard's resignation heightened fears about the government's ability to settle the constitutional dispute and keep Quebec within Canada.

The Canadian dollar closed in Toronto yesterday at 84.15 U.S. cents, down from 84.96 cents Monday. The Canadian unit fell as low as 83.79 U.S. cents yesterday, but rallied in later trading after buying support came from the Bank of Canada.

Canadian bond prices were down as much as 2 3/8 points, or C$23.75 for a bond with a C$1000 face amount, but cut part of their loss in late trading. A 20-year bellwether government bond closed off C$20 to yield 10.82%.

Michael Manford, chief economist at ScotiaMcLeod Inc., predicted that Canadian financial markets will remain volatile until the constitutional uncertainties are settled.

Meanwhile, a written comment by Treasury Under Secretary David Mulford that the U.S. would welcome a stronger yen under certain conditions cut short the U.S. dollar's rally against the Japanese currency and sent the U.S. unit nearly 2 1/2 yen lower on the day.

The U.S. dollar, however, continued its gains against the mark amid deepening market nervousness about the eventual cost of the Germanys' unification.

In late New York trading yesterday, the U.S. dollar was quoted at 1.6736 marks, up from 1.6626 marks late Monday, and at 151.20 yen, down from 153.72 yen late Monday. Sterling was quoted at $1.6928, up from $1.6875 late Monday.

In early trading in Tokyo Wednesday, the dollar was quoted at 151.65 yen, down from Tuesday's Tokyo close of 152.40 yen.

Canada's impasse developed over proposed constitutional amendments that would recognize Quebec as a "distinct society" within Canada and would extend additional powers to the country's 10 provinces in such areas as immigration. The amendments are opposed by three provinces, Newfoundland, New Brunswick and Manitoba.

Mr. Bouchard told a news conference that he resigned because he objected to current initiatives aimed at overcoming the impasse by weakening the provisions of the the proposed constitutional plan, known as the Meech Lake accord. He said the distinct society designation was an important "symbol" for the largely French-speaking Quebec and should be left intact.

Mr. Bouchard, who was environment minister in Mr. Mulroney's cabinet, said he didn't intend to back down from his position. "Read our lips because we mean business," he said.

Prime Minister Mulroney told the House of Commons that Quebec's constitutional demands were "reasonable." He said Quebec "said yes" to Canada in a referendum in 1980. "Now is the time for the rest of Canada to say yes to Quebec."

Mr. Mulroney, looking somber, said that current discussions to settle the dispute haven't made much progress. He said he wouldn't call a meeting of provincial government leaders unless there were prospects for success. The proposed amendments will die if they aren't ratified by all provincial legislatures by June 23, a legal deadline.

Mr. Bouchard said he will remain in the House of Commons as an independent member and will "use my newly found freedom of speech in the best interests of Quebec and Canada." Two other Conservative members of Parliament also announced they will quit the party and sit as independents to promote Quebec's interests.

Mr. Bouchard, a close friend of Mr. Mulroney, was responsible for overseeing the Conservatives' political interests in Quebec, the party's main power base. He agreed to join Mr. Mulroney's cabinet in 1988 even though he had campaigned in the 1980 Quebec referendum for the separation of Quebec from Canada.

Bond prices and the Canadian dollar were further depressed by a new public opinion poll that showed that a majority of Quebecers support eventual separation of their province from Canada.

Dan Gosselin, chief bond trader at Wood Gundy Inc., said the bond price decline was the sharpest "in recent memory." He said the selling pressure was broad and the price decline was accentuated by an absence of buyers.

"Nobody is sure what would happen if Quebec decided to separate," said George Hamilton, a senior trader of Hong Kong Bank of Canada. "There are so many unknowns out there. Foreign investors are just running for the hills."

Patrick Paridiso, a vice president of Deutsche Bank in New York, said Canadian financial markets are in a state of uncertainty because it is "very hard to quantify what would happen if the constitutional accord isn't passed."

Despite the uncertainty, Canadian share prices rose, taking their signal from Wall Street's advance Monday. Canadian stock exchanges were closed Monday because of a national holiday. The Toronto Stock Exchange's 300-share composite index rose 19.29 yesterday to close at 3540.54.

Stephanie Tramdack, a portfolio analyst at Levesque Beaubien Geoffrion Inc., said the Canadian dollar's decline also helped share prices because many Canadian companies benefit from a lower Canadian dollar.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, gold for current delivery rose $2.30 to $375.60 an ounce. Estimated volume was a heavy five million ounces.

In Hong Kong Wednesday, gold was quoted at $375.45 an ounce.
