International: U.S. Treasury Aide Assails Japan's Pace In Financial Reform, Warns of Sanctions

  • Author: Marcus W. Brauchli
  • Date: 05/23/90

TOKYO -- The U.S. sharply criticized the pace of financial reform in Japan, warning that unless Tokyo speeds up liberalization it could face sanctions. Completing two days of bilateral talks on financial issues, U.S. Treasury Undersecretary David C. Mulford effectively issued an ultimatum to Japan's Finance Ministry to agree soon to lift controls on all bank deposit rates within a year or face a harsh review in a market-access study that the Treasury Department must submit to Congress by year's end.

That study, required under the 1988 Omnibus Trade Act, could give impetus to legislative measures enabling retaliation against Japanese financial institutions operating in the U.S. One bill, the proposed Fair Trade in Financial Services Act, could emerge from the Senate Banking Committee as early as this week, Mr. Mulford said.

"Rapid action to complete the deregulation process in Japan would avoid what otherwise will become an area of serious political conflict," Mr. Mulford said.

Senior Japanese officials sought to dodge the implications of his message by saying that they have agreed in principle to liberalize deposit rates at "an early stage." Vice Finance Minister Makoto Utsumi said both sides agree that deposit-rate controls should be abolished, but differences remain over the timing.

At a joint news conference, U.S. and Japanese officials said cooperation between the two countries remains good, and they noted several key points of agreement in their talks. Most notably, Japan agreed to allow individuals and corporations greater freedom to set up offshore deposit accounts.

On matters involving foreign access to Japan's market, the Finance Ministry said it will grant specialized banking licenses to foreign securities companies here to allow them to handle foreign-exchange transactions. Japan also pledged to approve by June the use by Japanese investors, including banks, of Globex, a new automated futures and options trading system that will be used on Chicago's main exchanges. Both matters had been held up by Japanese regulators trying to avoid breaching a post-war barrier between the banking and securities industries.

But the gaps between the two sides clearly dominated this week's meeting in Tokyo, the eighth in a series begun in 1984 as the so-called Yen-Dollar Talks. "It is hard to understand how the country that ranks as the second-largest economy and financial market in the world can be so out of step with the rest of the leading industrialized nations," Mr. Mulford said. He noted that the Finance Ministry still hasn't eliminated a tax on Treasury bills, or made them available to individuals. And he said foreign investment trusts still have a tough time cracking Japan's market.
