Ex-Northrop Official Gets Prison Sentence Of One Year, One Day

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/21/90

LOS ANGELES -- A former engineer at Northrop Corp.'s now-defunct Precision Products division was sentenced in federal court here last week to one year and one day in prison for his role in falsifying tests on parts for the Navy's Harrier jet. The engineer, Howard Hyde, 49 years old, pleaded guilty in January to one count of conspiracy and two counts of making false statements in connection with tests of a part that provides stability during the Harrier's vertical take-offs and landings.

The only other individual who was successfully prosecuted in the fraud case is Clarence Gonsalves, 62. As previously reported, Mr. Gonsalves, a former Precision Products manager, was sentenced last week to three years in prison and fined $10,000 for falsifying tests on the Air Force's air-launched cruise missile.

U.S. District Judge Pamela Ann Rymer said Mr. Hyde's lighter sentence, compared with that for Mr. Gonsalves, owed to Mr. Hyde's "extraordinary cooperation" wth the government at an early stage of the investigation. But she said a prison sentence was necessary as "a signal to industry that this kind of behavior is unacceptable."

Just as opening arguments in a criminal fraud trial against Northrop were to begin earlier this year, the aerospace concern pleaded guilty to 34 counts of making false statements to the government in connection with testing parts for the cruise missile and the Harrier. It also agreed to pay $17 million in fines.

As part of that settlement, the Justice Department agreed to drop 141 additional counts. It also dismissed all charges against the two highest-ranking corporate officials implicated in the case, who were reinstated by Northrop after the settlement. As part of the agreement, federal authorities also closed their investigations into alleged improprieties related to Northrop's making of MX missile parts and the B-2 bomber.

Corrections & Amplifications

CLARENCE GONSALVES is a former manager of the now-defunct Western Services Department, Pomona, Calif., of Northrop Corp.'s Precision Products division, and Howard Hyde is a former senior engineer of the department. Recent editions mistakenly said that the Precision Products division, Norwood, Mass., was the unit that closed.
