Heard on the Street: Somethings's Up at Geico, and Talk Has Buffett, 44% Holder, Looking to Cut That Stake Down

  • Author: David B. Hilder
  • Date: 05/21/90

NEW YORK -- How long is forever? In the case of the Omaha, Neb., billionaire Warren E. Buffett, who has said of his stock investments, "Our favorite holding period is forever," the end may be nearer than you think.

The talk among some Wall Street merger specialists -- they have lots of time to talk these days -- is that Mr. Buffett has asked Salomon Brothers to explore the possible sale of his 44% stake in Geico, a highly profitable auto and homeowners insurance company.

Insurance industry executives who know Mr. Buffett and Geico say the merger specialists are only half right. They say that something does seem to be up at the insurance company, but that the various possible outcomes are unlikely to involve an outright sale of the 44% Geico stake held by Berkshire Hathaway, which Mr. Buffett controls.

John Gutfreund, chairman of Salomon, is personally involved in the project, the merger folks believe, probably because Mr. Buffett, through Berkshire Hathaway, also owns 13% of Salomon.

Mr. Buffett didn't return phone calls last week seeking comment on these reports. Neither did Mr. Gutfreund or William Snyder, chairman of Geico. A Geico spokesman declined to comment.

Buffett buffs point to a sentence that has appeared in the annual report of Berkshire Hathaway for the past seven years: "Regardless of price, we have no interest at all in selling any good businesses that Berkshire owns."

But the insurance executives familiar with Geico acknowledge that something is up at the company and that a change in Mr. Buffett's ownership position -- most likely, but not necessarily, a decrease -- could result.

The merger-makers are salivating at the size of a potential transaction. Mr. Buffett's 6.85 million Geico shares, for which he paid $45.7 million between 1976 and 1981, have an indicated market value of about $1.03 billion at Friday's closing price of $150.50 a share. The stock market value of all Geico shares is about $2.32 billion. A buyer of Mr. Buffett's block or the entire company would be likely to pay a premium above the market price.

Despite Wall Street's excitement about a possible sale, insurance industry executives say that whatever changes are brewing in the Buffett-Geico relationship don't stem from any dissatisfaction on Mr. Buffett's part with Geico. Rather, the executives say, any change is more likely to reflect an effort by Geico's management to keep the company's strong return on equity from eroding.

In a sense, Geico has been too successful for its own good. The company's basic auto insurance business -- policies sold to good drivers by phone and mail -- has helped generate 23% annual growth in per-share earnings over the past five years. Though that growth is slowing, analysts still expect growth of about 15% a year.

Retained earnings are piling up fast; Geico's per-share earnings last year of $13.74 far outstripped its current $2-a-year dividend rate. This increasing equity base makes it tougher to produce an impressive return-on-equity figure.

Geico managers "have some real hard thinking to do right now," Gerald Lewinsohn, a Merrill Lynch insurance analyst, says. "If they just sit where they are and continue disciplined underwriting, the company's return on equity is going to fall."

The company's basic strategy of avoiding high-risk drivers limits its growth. And its modest attempts to diversify into reinsurance or to expand its life insurance sales have been disappointing.

One way to boost return on equity, of course, would be to limit the growth of shareholders' equity by paying bigger dividends. Geico, in fact, increased its quarterly dividend to 50 cents a share from 45 cents in February.

Another way to improve returns is to buy back stock, which Geico also has been doing. But the company hasn't found long-term holders in any rush to part with their shares.

All this leads some investment bankers and insurance executives to conclude that Geico might offer to buy back some of Mr. Buffett's shares, perhaps as part of a tender offer for part of its stock. Though it might seem logical for Geico to try to buy out Mr. Buffett entirely, Merrill Lynch's Mr. Lewinsohn says Geico couldn't afford a buy-back that big.

Yet another option would be for Mr. Buffett, through Berkshire Hathaway, to offer to buy out the remaining public shareholders. At $200 a share, which is what Mr. Lewinsohn believes Geico is worth, that would cost about $1.71 billion.

Berkshire Hathaway could certainly raise the money. But Mr. Buffett hasn't become a billionaire by paying full price for mature companies.
