VA to Give Benefits To Vietnam Veterans With Some Cancers

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/21/90

WASHINGTON -- The Department of Veterans Affairs for the first time acknowledged a link between cancer and exposure to Agent Orange, a herbicide used in the Vietnam War, and announced it will pay disability benefits to Vietnam veterans with soft-tissue sarcomas. In March, the VA said it would grant disability benefits to Vietnam veterans with non-Hodgkin's lymphoma, another rare cancer. It was the first time the government recognized a connection between cancer and service in Southeast Asia. That conclusion was based on a Centers for Disease Control study that found a correlation between non-Hodgkin's lymphoma and service in Vietnam.

But the CDC study didn't find any connection betwen exposure to Agent Orange and cancer. Veterans groups have long contended that exposure to Agent Orange has led to a higher incidence of cancer among Vietnam veterans, and in its ruling Friday, the VA department conceded that there may be such a connection.

The latest decision rejects part of the CDC study, which didn't find a link between service in Vietnam and five other cancers, including soft-tissue sarcomas. But a VA advisory committee reviewed scientific literature on the connection between soft-tissue sarcomas and exposure to herbicides containing dioxin and recommended that cancers be considered service-connected and eligible for benefits.

The ruling by VA Secretary Edward Derwinski means that approximately 1,100 veterans or their survivors will qualify for compensation. The department estimates it will recognize an additional 50 new claims a year for soft-tissue sarcomas, which are difficult-to-diagnose tumors found on muscles and connective tissue or in body fat. The VA projects it will pay about $8 million a year as a result of the decision.
