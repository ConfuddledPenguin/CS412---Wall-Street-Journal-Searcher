International: Germans Set Up Funds, Sign Pact For Unification

  • Author: Terence Roth
  • Date: 05/21/90

FRANKFURT, West Germany -- Officials celebrated the "hour of birth" of a united Germany even as West Germans continued to calculate how to pay for unification without raising taxes. The West German Finance Ministry rolled out a second 1990 supplementary budget of 4.75 billion marks ($2.88 billion), including a 2.75 billion mark provision to rescue East Germany's tottering unemployment and pension systems. The remaining two billion marks are to be Bonn's first cash contribution to the 115 billion mark German Unity Fund, set up last week to help finance East German budget deficits through 1994.

In a first supplementary budget in February, Bonn allocated 6.87 billion marks to support hundreds of thousands of East German refugees.

Separately, the West German central bank Friday announced a record six billion mark, 10-year government-bond issue. The issue was seen as the start of a long period of heavy borrowing to finance other East German support programs.

At Friday's signing ceremony in Bonn of the treaty to merge the two German economies and social systems on July 2, government leaders were upbeat. "What we are experiencing here is the hour of birth of a free and united Germany," West German Chancellor Helmut Kohl said.

East German Premier Lothar de Maiziere, responding to widespread anxiety in East Germany, said: "I only find it amazing that in various parts of Germany there are more worries than hopes." East German farmers and industry workers fear mass unemployment if their border is thrown open to free market competition with Western producers.

In an important appendix to the treaty, East Germany agreed to permit foreigners to buy land for commercial purposes, despite fears of a wholesale buy-out of their country. Bonn set the condition of foreign private ownership as a prerequisite for the treaty, arguing that only industry investment in East Germany can provide for full economic recovery.

Predictions of other East German funding programs in the future have swelled fears of tax increases and rising inflation after the West German mark is introduced as East Germany's currency.

But Finance Minister Theo Waigel sought to allay those concerns. "We would be taking leave of our senses if we crippled a well-functioning {West German} economy with increased taxes," he said during the weekend.

Indeed, Bonn is counting on West Germany's bustling economy to help pay for East German relief. Humming along at a 4% growth rate, West German business activity is expected to generate 8.2 billion marks more in tax revenue than originally planned. The Finance Ministry said the windfall could hold Bonn's 1990 budget deficit to 30.9 billion marks, compared with an earlier projected 32.9 billion marks.
