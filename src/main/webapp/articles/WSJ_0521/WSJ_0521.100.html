Abreast of the Market: Strategists Don't See Bull Run --- Analysts Say Surge In Stocks Signifies New Trading Range

  • Author: Craig Torres
  • Date: 05/21/90

NEW YORK -- Stock market skeptics had their hands full last week, clinging to their disbelief. The Dow Jones Industrial Average set records on three days, though it fell back 11.80 Friday to close at 2819.91. Moreover, some prominent individual stocks that aren't in the industrial average, such as Compaq Computer, also surged to new highs.

But few strategists and professional investors think stocks have embarked on a new bull run of any great significance. Instead, they think the market is merely nudging the top rail a little higher on the trading-range fence. Instead of the industrial average being confined below roughly 2800, now a top of about 3000 is seen as a possibility. But the market's trading-range syndrome, they assert, will prevail.

The latest upward push began two weeks ago with a 63-point rally in the Dow that vaulted the average over 2800. The engine driving stocks' thrust was a powerful bond market rally that pushed down long-term interest rates to 8.6% from more than 9%. The big moves in both markets convinced many strategists that declining interest rates will be essential for stocks to rise further in the weeks ahead. Investors will push up stock prices faster than corporate profits can grow in a slowing economy, the theory goes, if interest rates fall as inflation eases.

Skeptics have an arsenal of arguments to shoot holes in this theory. For example, David Rajala, director of equity investments at One Federal Asset Management, contends inflation won't necessarily ease up as the economy grows slowly.

"The Wall Street consensus is that slow growth means lower inflation," says Mr. Rajala. "But we have trouble finding a historical precedent for that. Normally, slow growth is associated with high rates of inflation." He thinks inflation will stubbornly remain around 5% this year, indicating that bond yields will drift between 8% and 9% and stocks will be confined in a range of about 2200 to 3000 in the Dow industrials.

"It is hard for us to be convinced that this {latest rally} isn't a move to a top of a trading range in the stock market," says Mr. Rajala. "For us to believe that rates are going to come down significantly, we feel we have to have {some} quarters of negative economic growth" -- though not necessarily consecutive quarters, which could qualify as a recession.

There are other forces that will keep interest rates high and cap the stock market rally, skeptics say. Demand for capital from Eastern Europe; less foreign investment capital from Japan; borrowing demands by the Treasury and by Resolution Trust Corp., the thrift bailout agency -- all these will keep rates up, says Byron Wien, managing director at Morgan Stanley and the company's chief U.S. investment strategist.

"You aren't going to get enough support from the bond market to get a bull move," Mr. Wien says. If the industrial average vaults over 3000 while bond yields stay at current levels or move higher, Mr. Wien says he will sell stocks.

In the meantime, he has put a little bit more money to work, taking his cash position down to 12% last Monday from 15% previously. Mr. Wien is one of those who think corporate profits will start improving in the second half of this year. "The economy still has a significant amount of natural momentum," he says.

Even so, Mr. Wien is focusing on growth companies. He would still buy technology issues, such as Sun Microsystems, Intel, and Compaq Computer, even though they have rallied strongly. Technology carries the heaviest weight of any sector in his portfolio. Mr. Wien says he bought these shares earlier this year because they were cheap, and the companies had new products that would most likely create favorable earnings surprises.

Skeptics also doubt any lasting buoyancy will come out of the budget deficit-reduction process. To be sure, curbing federal demand for capital would help lower interest rates, which would probably boost stocks, says Arnold Moskowitz, director of investment strategy at County NatWest Securities USA. But for that happy outcome, he estimates at least $50 billion must be lopped from the fiscal 1991 deficit, which he considers a big stretch for politicians facing an election year.

But, just in case, Mr. Moskowitz has projected the effects of declining interest rates: accelerating a trend of higher capital spending, and lowering the dollar, which would stimulate U.S. exports. To play higher capital spending and rising exports, Mr. Moskowitz suggests such stocks as General Signal, Honeywell, Eaton, General Electric, W.W. Grainger, and Sundstrand.

There also are skeptics among analysts who focus less on economic fundamentals and more on market history and other technical factors. One of these is Laszlo Birinyi, president of Birinyi Associates, a stock research company, who thinks the most recent runup obscures rather than illuminates what's happening in the market.

Mr. Birinyi closely examined the week that included the rally on May 11 when the industrial average soared 63.07 to 2801.58. He found broad, indiscriminate moves in all stocks. For example, the 50 largest stocks in Standard & Poor's index were up 3.7% that week and the 50 smallest were up 2.3%. The 50 S&P stocks with the highest projected earnings increases for the next five years rose 3.6%, while the 50 with the lowest five-year earnings forecast rose 3%.

Normally, when investors pour money into most segments of the stock market, analysts rejoice. But Mr. Birinyi doesn't, because that spate of across-the-board buying didn't change the underlying trends that have prevailed all this year.

This year through Friday, the 50 largest stocks in the S&P 500 are up 3.9% while the 50 smallest in the index are down 4.4%. As measured by five-year earnings forecast, the first, second and third groups of 50 stocks each are up 5.6%, 3.9% and 6%, respectively, this year; the three bottom 50-stock tiers are down 3.6%, 4.1% and 4.9%, respectively.

The rally, Mr. Birinyi concludes, is still concentrated in relatively few growth stocks, which doesn't bode well for a broad advance in the market, and not even for the 30 Dow industrials.

While the industrial average closed Friday just a few points below its latest record, broader indexes were far below their all-time highs -- the S&P 500 by 1.4%, the New York Stock Exchange composite index by 2.9%, and the Nasdaq composite by 7.7%.

Friday's Market Activity

Stocks drifted lower Friday, but finished the week with a gain.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 11.80 to 2819.91. Advancing issues outpaced decliners 742 to 725 on the New York Stock Exchange, where volume totaled 162,520,000 shares.

For the week, the industrial average advanced 18.33 points, or 0.65%, following a 3.37% gain in the previous week. Standard & Poor's 500-stock index gained 0.75% for the week, while the New York Stock Exchange composite rose 0.68%.

Prime Motor Inns jumped 1 5/8 to 13 3/4. The stock was one of the Big Board's most active issues. Traders suggested that buying by traders who previously had borrowed shares and sold them, anticipating a price decline, was responsible for the move, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

Two weeks ago, Prime agreed in principle with Blackstone Capital Partners to form a joint venture to manage and develop Prime's Howard Johnson and Ramada franchise systems.

That news spurred Manor Care to announce that it was "extremely interested in making a proposal . . . more favorable to Prime" than the Blackstone agreement. Manor Care said Friday that it is still interested in buying Prime Motor Inns, Professional Investor Report said. Officials at Prime Motor Inns weren't immediately available for comment.

Chiquita Brands closed 3/8 higher to 20 1/2 on volume of more than 1.8 million shares. A 6.1 million-share secondary offering was priced at $20.125 a share Thursday.

A 4,250,000-share secondary offering of Leslie Fay, also priced Thursday, pushed the stock up among the most active issues. The issue was priced at $12.75 a share. Leslie Fay closed at 13 1/2, up 1/2, on volume of more than 1.1 million shares.

Home Depot hit another 52-week high, adding 1 1/4 to 56 1/2. Prudential-Bache Securities repeated its "buy" recommendation on the stock for institutional and retail clients last week.

Kay Jewelers added 5/8 to 8 3/8. Certain of the company's officers and directors, who own a total of 40% of the company's common stock, have said that they intend to buy as much as one million more shares. Last Wednesday, Kay Jewelers fell 2 3/8 after three banks announced that they may stop lending money to the company.

UJB Financial added 1/4 to 16 3/4. Chilmark Capital has acquired a large stake in UJB, and will be filing, as required, with the Securities and Exchange Commission within the next 10 days, according to Dow Jones Professional Investor Report.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index rose 1.38 to 356.32. Amex volume totaled 13,041,010 shares, compared with 13,760,530 shares Thursday. There were 275 issues advancing, 267 declining and 255 were unchanged.

Home Shopping Network gained 1/2 to 7 1/4. A Florida-based newspaper reported that the company has hired First Boston to help find a buyer for the company. Neither Home Shopping nor First Boston officials could immediately be reached for comment. The newspaper also reported that Home Shopping had applied for listing on the New York Stock Exchange.
