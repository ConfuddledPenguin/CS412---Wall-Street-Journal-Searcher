Economy -- Tracking the Economy: This Week's Data Likely to Be Sluggish, Starting With Revised Estimate for GNP

  • Author: Pamela Sebastian
  • Date: 05/21/90

NEW YORK -- Recent reports of sluggishness in the economy are likely to be reflected in this week's data, starting with the revised estimate of first-quarter real gross national product. The government's second shot at figuring GNP, or the total value of the nation's output of goods and services, is expected to be lower than the 2.1% annual growth rate logged in the advance report at the end of April. There's reason to believe the week's other offerings will also be weaker, including: April durable goods orders as well as personal income and personal consumption.

Michael Moran, economist at Daiwa Securities USA, revised his first-quarter GNP growth estimate down to about 1.6% from the early 2.1% estimate "almost exclusively due to {lower} inventory investment." The consensus view calls for a drop in GNP to a 1.8% growth rate when the second estimate is reported Thursday, according to MMS International, a unit of McGraw-Hill Inc., New York.

"I think what happened was a planned inventory correction, so I don't think you'll see a sharp rebuilding by business in the second quarter," Mr. Moran said. Nonetheless, he noted that even a return to normal inventory investment in the current quarter "could push growth on the higher side of underlying trends." Many economists foresee an average GNP growth rate of about 2% per quarter for the next couple of periods.

The chief affected industry, of course, is auto making. Mr. Moran figures that during the first quarter auto makers assembled cars at the annual rate of 5.9 million units. So, if output simply keeps pace with sales during the second quarter, the industry should produce at an annual rate of 6.5 million units, he said.

Robert Dederick, chief economist at the Northern Trust Co. in Chicago, said inventory adjustments subtracted about one percentage point from GNP in the first quarter and should add a like amount in the current period. "We ought to get a pretty good inventory rebound," he said.

Personal income figures, due out Friday, are expected to show smaller gains than in March, largely because of April's weaker-than-expected employment data. Manufacturing employment fell during April as did the number of hours worked in factories. The consensus view calls for a 0.3% gain in April personal income, down from a gain of 0.8% in March.

Personal consumption is expected to hold up somewhat better, strengthened by continued gains in spending on services and some pickup in non-auto durables, economists said. The consensus view calls for a 0.4% increase in April consumption, the same increase as in March.

Economists expect a large fallback in April durable goods orders following March's 6.7% surge. The consensus view on the report due out Wednesday calls for a drop of about 2% with the gap largely attributed to a slowdown in volatile aircraft orders.

While recent indicators show a sluggish growth pace, economists are waiting for more employment data before calling any major turns. April jobs data, due out a week from Friday, could help confirm the lethargic pace or it could throw another curve into forecasts by picking up some steam.

In the meantime, Samuel Kahan, chief economist at Fuji Securities Inc., Chicago, is keeping a close eye on weekly jobless claims, for hints on the direction of the next unemployment report. The latest report for the week ended May 5 showed that initial claims for regular state unemployment benefits rose to a seasonally adjusted 353,000 from 349,000 the week before.
