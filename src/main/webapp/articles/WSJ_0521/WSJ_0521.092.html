Coalition Pushes for Beer Companies To End Sponsorships of Motor Sports

  • Author: Laurie McGinley
  • Date: 05/21/90

Anti-drunk-driving activists want beer companies to abandon their multimillion-dollar financing of car racing and other motor sports, saying such sponsorships send the wrong message. To bolster the case, the National Coalition to Prevent Impaired Driving, a group of more than 100 traffic-safety and public-health organizations, is holding a press conference today to promote a new report, "Beer and Fast Cars: How Brewers Target Blue-Collar Youth." The study concludes that beer companies spend about $50 million a year "in a sophisticated campaign to strengthen associations between beer, cars and speed in a demographic group at elevated risk of drinking and driving."

The coalition wants beer companies to drop their motor-sports sponsorships voluntarily, and non-brewers to pick up the sponsorships. If the brewers don't move, the groups vow to campaign for a legislative ban.

The attack is the latest public-relations headache for an industry that in recent years has come under increasing criticism for allegedly targeting young people, blacks and Hispanics. James Sanders, president of the Beer Institute, a trade association, calls the groups' complaint "ridiculous." He says it "reached an absurd level that defies common sense."

But the report's author, David Buchanan, an assistant professor at the University of Massachusetts school of public health, contends that the beer industry, faced with declining consumption by health-conscious, white-collar Americans, is targeting blue-collar youth involved in the car culture. "This target group is accurately perceived by the industry to include the largest number of heavy beer drinkers," he says.

At the same time, Prof. Buchanan says, young males from blue-collar backgrounds are at disproportionately higher risk of being killed in alcohol-related crashes.

The report, financed by the AAA Foundation for Traffic Safety and produced by California's Marin Institute, a nonprofit research and advocacy group, finds that motor sports, which include drag racing, motorcycle racing and "monster-truck" competitions, receive more sponsorship money from the beer industry than any other type of sport.

"Beer companies are saturating motor events," says Michael Schriefer, a pro race-car driver from Manhattan Beach, Calif. "It's giving the young the wrong impression."

Typically, motor-sport events are festooned with banners, bunting and posters decorated with the name of the beer sponsor, and feature several beer-dispensing booths. Some events have emcees who periodically urge spectators to buy beer. Beer companies sometimes underwrite an entire event, and other times sponsor individual teams or drivers.

Randi Lynn Hubbard, one of the few female monster-truck drivers in the U.S., says there is so much beer consumed at some events that she worries about the fans getting home safely.

The report recommends that if an outright ban on beer sponsorships is politically unfeasible, other steps should be taken, such as limiting the cup size for beer sales to 12 ounces and halting sales at least one hour before an event ends.

In reply, Stephen Burrows, vice president for consumer awareness at Anheuser-Busch Cos., calls the report "pseudoscience" based on opinion, not fact.

"We've determined that racing is a good place to reach our audience, which is adults over 21," Mr. Burrows says. He adds that "to conclude that people who wear blue jeans and go to racing events are drunk drivers is an insult to people who enjoy racing events." Moreover, he notes that Anheuser-Busch has spent millions on its "Know When to Say When" ad campaign, designed to foster responsibility among the consuming public.

None of that is likely to mollify critics who argue that the industry is targeting specific segments of society that may be more vulnerable to alcohol abuse than others.

Spring break, for example, remains a controversy. At Texas's South Padre Island, a favorite gathering spot for college students, the beer companies this year "weren't handing out beer, but they had displays, and they sponsored all kinds of beach games," says Bobby Heard, a spokesman for Texans' War on Drugs, which is headquartered in Austin. "We don't want them involved in spring break at all."

Pat Taylor, director of the alcohol policies project at the Center for Science in the Public Interest, says beer companies should be banned from sponsoring rockband tours because of their potential appeal to young people. She's also concerned about malt-liquor advertising targeted to blacks and Hispanics.

Beer-industry officials say such concerns are misguided. "No studies have ever related these marketing or advertising activities to an increase or decrease in alcohol abuse," says the Beer Institute's Mr. Sanders. Rather, executives say, these marketing tools promote a specific product among consumers who already have decided to drink.

Brewers say the criticism of the industry's marketing techniques comes from a group of activists who have a paternalistic attitude toward the rest of the country. "We believe the American public is smarter than these groups give them credit for," says Anheuser-Busch's Mr. Burrows.
