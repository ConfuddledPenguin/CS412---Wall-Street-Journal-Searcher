Credit Markets: Foreign Bonds Rally 'Round the Globe --- Merged Germanys Will Test Market

  • Author: Constance Mitchell
  • Date: 05/21/90

NEW YORK -- Bond markets world-wide are starting to spring back to life after a long and painful slide. Bond prices this month have climbed around the world, due in part to a rebound in foreign currencies against the U.S. dollar and perceptions that interest rates in most countries may have peaked. Meanwhile, investors are flocking to mutual funds that buy bonds in countries from France to Japan.

The ticket to how long the rally lasts may be West Germany, where forecasts of higher interest rates remain common.

Last week's prices of British government bonds surged in the biggest rally in 2 1/2 years with prices of long-term bonds rising as much as four points, a gain of about 40 pounds for a bond with a #1,000 face amount.

"This has been the best month for international bonds since July 1989," said Victor S. Filatov, an executive vice president at J.P. Morgan & Co. So far in May, he said, the total returns on foreign bonds have outperformed U.S. bonds by an average of four percentage points.

When measured in U.S. dollars, British government bonds have produced a total return of 8.52% so far this month, according to J.P. Morgan. That compares with total returns of 7.39% for Japanese bonds, 3.16% for Australian bonds and 2.29% for French bonds and 2.01% for U.S. bonds.

These high total returns were due in part to a significant decline in the value of the U.S. dollar on foreign-exchange markets. For example, at the beginning of May when it took 159 yen to buy one U.S. dollar, a Japanese bond valued at 100,000 yen would have cost $629. But last week, after the dollar had fallen to 153 yen, the value of that same bond rose to $654 due to currency exchange. But in addition to currency gains, investors in foreign bonds also have enjoyed large capital gains as bond prices rose.

The turnaround in the global bond market is just beginning to show up in mutual fund performance statistics. According to Lipper Analytical Services, world income mutual funds -- which invest primarily in U.S. and non-U.S. fixed-income securities -- eked out a small total return of just 0.15% for the week ended May 3. That was a big change, however, from the previous week when the funds turned in a total return of negative 0.45%.

But whether the global bond rally continues depends largely upon events still developing in West Germany, which is in the process of merging with East Germany. The cost of reunification between the two countries will be enormous. Economists believe West Germany will issue record amounts of new bonds to finance the union with financially troubled East Germany. There's also inflationary potential in the currency combination.

To attract buyers for the new bonds, economists say, West Germany probably will be forced this year to boost bond yields to 9.5% from a current level of around 8.5%.

"We are a little nervous about the path of European rates," because of West Germany, said David Boardman, a portfolio manager at Rowe Price Fleming International Inc., London. If West German rates rise, "it could stop the French market from rallying further," he said. Interest rates in many European countries track West German rates.

Such concerns haven't kept investors -- both large and small -- from diving back into global bonds. Mr. Boardman said his international mutual fund, which caters primarily to U.S. investors, has been getting $1 million to $2 million in assets each day -- a sign that investors believe that international bonds offer a great deal of promise.

Mr. Filatov also notices a changing attitude among U.S. investors toward foreign bonds. "The political and economic uncertainties kept many investors on the sidelines" for most of the year, he said. "The uncertainty is going away and investors are beginning to find the fundamentals very appealing."

Mr. Boardman isn't expanding his position in most European bonds. Instead, he's moving into Australia and New Zealand. "These markets were beaten up pretty bad last year but now are benefiting from falling interest rates due to lower inflation expectations." Investors in these bonds also get high current yield, because interest rates -- while falling -- are still much higher than in many European countries. Yields on Australia's benchmark 10-year government bond, for example, have fallen 1/4 percentage point in the past few weeks but remain at a lofty 13.50%.

Thomas Prapas, managing director of fixed-income investments at Clemente Capital Corp., likes French, Spanish, Irish, Swedish and Italian bonds. "All those markets are attractive. Most of these countries have inflation that has been coming down, while West Germany's is going up," he said. Mr. Prapas agrees with other analysts that events in West Germany could hurt these markets in the short term. But he says, "we're looking at this market over the next six months. If West Germany decides to raise interest rates, that will hurt a little bit in the near term, but the long-term looks very good."

At Salomon Brothers Inc., international analysts are preaching caution. In its latest report on the global market, Salomon said the May rally "may soon enter a consolidation phase." The principal exception is Britain, where bond market sentiment could remain euphoric. Salomon said yields on Britain's 10-year notes "could test 11.50%." The yield, which was 13% at the end of April, was 12% on Friday.

Britain's bond-market rally was triggered by reports that the United Kingdom might be on the verge of joining the "exchange rate mechanism" of the European Monetary System -- an agreement designed to stabilize European Community currencies.

To become a member, some analysts believe that Britain must reduce inflation, which has been running between 7% and 10% in recent years but was running at an annual rate of 9.4% in April.

Last week's rally in the British government securities market capped a steady climb in the market that has pushed yields one percentage point lower in one month. However, some analysts are skeptical about any near-term entry of sterling into the ERM and contend the recent rally has been overdone.

The British rally also was supported by a labor report that said that U.K. unemployment rose in April for the first time since 1986.

Friday's Market Activity

Buyers flooded the junk bond market, sparking the strongest rally that market has had in weeks.

Most junk bonds, high-yielding speculative-grade securities with low credit ratings or no credit ratings at all, gained between 1/2 point to 3/4 point. That was an impressive showing considering prices of investment-grade corporate bonds and most Treasury securities fell between 1/4 and 1/2 point.

Buying interest in junk was broadbased, and included a wide variety of issues.

Among the leading winners: the pay-in-kind bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. The tobacco and food company's 14.07% convertible pay-in-kind debentures chalked up one point to end at 84 1/8.

The other widely held RJR issue, the 14.07% exchangeable debentures, due in 2007, climbed 1 3/8 to end at 71.

Also higher were recently depressed bonds of R.H. Macy & Co. Macy's 14 1/2% senior subordinated debentures, maturing in 1998, advanced 1 1/2 points to 75 1/2, and its 14 1/2% subordinated debentures, due in 2001, added 1 1/2 points to end at 65 1/2.

But investors said liquidity in the $200 billion market remains a problem.

"It's all bids and no offereds," said Dan Smith, a portfolio manager at Lisle, Ill.-based Van Kampen Merritt Inc. "You'll start out 96 1/2 bid and none offered, and prices will creep up till you get a two-sided market."

Junk-bond prices rose steadily during all of last week as prospects of declining interest rates have heartened junk-bond investors.

According to First Boston Corp.'s high-yield index, junk-bond issues have generated a 2% total return so far this month. Advancing issues outnumbered declining issues by a ratio of two to one, First Boston said.

Sam DeRosa-Farag, a high-yield analyst at First Boston, said zero-coupon bonds continued to outperform cash-paying bonds this month.

"Zero-coupon bonds tend to have higher returns in a bull market and worse returns in a bear market," said Mr. DeRosa-Farag. Strength in zero-coupon bonds is often considered a gauge of investor confidence. When investors are less skittish about the market, they are more inclined to invest in junior securities like zero-coupon and pay-in-kind bonds.

Among the zero-coupon issues that gained Friday were Hospital Corp. of America bonds, which moved up one point to end at 71, and Owens-Illinois Inc.'s zero-coupon bonds, which surged 1 1/2 points to end at 50 1/2. Bonds of both Hospital Corp. and Owens-Illinois have advanced this week on positive earnings reports.

In the Treasury market, bond prices ended as much as 1/2 point lower in quiet trading.

Friday's quiet trading stood in sharp contrast to hectic dealing a week earlier that saw the new bellwether 30-year bond surge more than 2 points.

In late trading, the benchmark bond was quoted at 100 18/32, down 1/2 point on the day to yield 8.69%. In when-issued trading activity, the new two-year notes to be auctioned Wednesday were quoted to yield 8.62%, up slightly from 8.58% on Thursday. The new five-year notes to be auctioned Thursday were quoted at a yield of 8.69%, up from 8.64%.

The price slide started late in the morning when the Federal Reserve executed weekend matched sale-purchase agreements, a technical trading operation that drains reserves out of the banking system and tends to keep interest rates up. Investors saw the move as an effort on the Fed's part to keep the closely watched federal funds rate near the Fed's widely perceived 8 1/4% target level. The rate is what banks charge each other on short-term loans.
