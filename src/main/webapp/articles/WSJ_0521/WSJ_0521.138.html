Belzberg Says First City Might Drop Risk Arbitrage

  • Author: Gary Lamphier
  • Date: 05/21/90

VANCOUVER, British Columbia -- First City Financial Corp., which is controlled by Vancouver's Belzberg family, said it is mulling a departure from the stock arbitrage business, citing shrinking takeover activity and sour investments. "We're thinking of getting out of it altogether," Samuel Belzberg, First City chairman, president, and chief executive officer, said after the company's annual meeting.

Mr. Belzberg, whose investment savvy and reputation as a corporate raider helped First City rack up big earnings during the 1980s takeover boom -- often by acquiring stakes in U.S. companies, threatening a takeover and then selling out at a profit -- said losses on risk arbitrage trading "fully account" for the sharp drop in first-quarter earnings from First City's investment and trading arm, traditionally a key profit generator.

For the quarter ended March 31, gains on investments and trading totaled just 1.7 million Canadian dollars (US$1.4 million), he said, down from C$26.6 million a year earlier. First City previously blamed falling earnings from its investment and trading business for the company's poor first-quarter earnings, released earlier last week, which fell to C$5.2 million, or 10 Canadian cents a share, from profit before special items of C$31.7 million, or 58 Canadian cents a share, a year earlier, when a gain on the sale of an investment made net income C$155.4 million, or C$2.82 a share. Revenue rose to C$243.5 million from C$241.1 million.

Mr. Belzberg didn't specify which takeover-stock plays went bad, though he distinguished between First City's risk arbitrage activity and "long-term" investments, such as its 11.5% stake in Armstrong World Industries Inc. Indeed, most of First City's securities investment profits in recent years stem not from risk arbitrage, but from the sale of its holdings in its own would-be targets, such as Armstrong.

Armstrong, a Lancaster, Pa.-based building products company that has succeeded in outmaneuvering the Belzbergs, hasn't followed the traditional script, however. First City's paper loss on its Armstrong stake is about US$30 million.

W. Gordon Lancaster, executive vice president and chief financial officer, said First City's New York arbitrage group got burned in recent months on several deals that fizzled, including Hilton Hotels Corp.'s failed attempt to auction its assets. "As the takeover world has slowed, so has arbitrage," Mr. Lancaster said.

"We've made about the equivalent of a bond yield" on arbitrage activity, he said, "which at the end of the day, isn't a very sexy return."

Mr. Lancaster said that First City's portfolio of arbitrage stocks now is about C$60 million, down from about C$200 million at its peak, and that the company will probably "unwind" the balance. Arbitrage stocks make up only a small part of First City's securities investment and trading assets, which totaled C$1.32 billion at the end of 1989.
