Politics & Policy: Thornburgh Knew Lie-Detector Tests Hurt 2 of His Aides

  • Author: Paul M. Barrett
  • Date: 05/21/90

WASHINGTON -- Attorney General Dick Thornburgh failed to disclose that his top personal aide, Robert Ross, had shown deception on part of a lie-detector test during a controversial internal Justice Department investigation, a senior department official said. Mr. Ross's flunking part of the lie-detector test was "one consideration" in his decision, announced last Monday, to resign from the department, according to the senior official.

Mr. Thornburgh has known since late last fall that Mr. Ross and another personal aide, David Runkel, had shown deception on lie-detector tests but chose not to disclose the matter "because something like that can ruin careers," said the senior official, who was designated by the attorney general's office yesterday to answer press questions on the issue. Mr. Runkel has repeatedly refused to comment.

Mr. Ross couldn't be reached for comment. Previously, he and other Justice Department officials have said his imminent departure and Mr. Runkel's reassignment to less prominent duties were part of a personnel shake-up having nothing to do with the internal Justice Department investigation.

Interviewed yesterday on ABC's "This Week with David Brinkley," Mr. Thornburgh denied a report in the Los Angeles Times that he "helped conceal" Mr. Ross's deception to protect his longtime personal assistant.

The lie-detector tests were administered as part of a departmentwide criminal probe into who had disclosed the existence of an investigation involving the office of Rep. William Gray (D., Pa.). The disclosure of the Gray investigation last May prompted accusations that it was a Republican effort to undermine the congressman's ultimately successful race to become the No. 3 official in the House Democratic leadership.

Mr. Thornburgh in December announced that the internal department review, conducted by Federal Bureau of Investigation agents and Criminal Division prosecutors, produced "insufficient evidence" for a criminal prosecution or disciplinary action against current Justice Department employees.

The senior department official who spoke yesterday said that the deception indicated by the lie-detector tests of Messrs. Ross and Runkel produced "leads" but that those leads yield "no evidence" that either man was the source of the Gray disclosure.

Mr. Thornburgh has conceded that Mr. Runkel effectively confirmed the disclosure about Mr. Gray to the CBS television reporter who broadcast the first report on the matter.

The senior department official said that at least some of the questions put to Mr. Ross as part of the lie-detector test "were relevant" directly to the Gray disclosure, as opposed to being control questions on other matters. The attorney general and his aides "were aware there would be some embarrassment" if it became known that Messrs. Ross and Runkel had shown deception, the senior official said.

Asked directly why Mr. Thornburgh didn't disclose the lie-detector test results at the same time that he exonerated his aides, the senior official responded: "That assumes an obligation to disclose {the test results}. . . . That's debatable."

In a separate review of the entire matter, the Justice Department's solicitor general, Kenneth Starr, concluded that Mr. Thornburgh had acted appropriately within his discretion.

Subsequently, Senate Judiciary Chairman Joseph Biden of Delaware, a Democratic spokesman on the Gray affair, said he was satisfied that "any further investigation of this matter would not provide any further information and therefore is not warranted."
