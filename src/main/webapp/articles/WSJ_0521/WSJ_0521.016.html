Politics & Policy: Panels Propose Defense Savings Of $1.7 Billion

  • Author: David Rogers
  • Date: 05/21/90

WASHINGTON -- House and Senate negotiators tentatively agreed to an estimated $1.7 billion in cuts and transfers from Pentagon accounts to help cover the immediate costs of increased foreign aid and domestic appropriations in the fiscal year ending Sept. 30. The reductions are part of a more than $4 billion fiscal 1990 supplemental-spending bill pending in Congress and would yield enough short-term savings to allow lawmakers to stay within this year's ceilings under the Gramm-Rudman deficit-reduction law.

Final approval of the package is contingent on agreement over a separate adjustment of appropriations within defense accounts to meet a shortfall in Pentagon personnel and medical funds. The chairmen of defense appropriations and authorizing committees are to meet early this week on the second issue. Though no details have been released, these transfers are believed to total more than $1.4 billion.

The sums involved are dwarfed by the size of the total military budget. But the exercise illustrates the vagaries of Gramm-Rudman and how the Pentagon -- and its allies in Congress -- can maneuver to blunt the increased demands now for defense cuts.

The $1.7 billion in proposed savings, for example, satisfy the budget law but don't truly match the long-term costs of the new domestic and foreign-aid expenditures, which will spill over into fiscal 1991. And by using their authority to transfer or "reprogram" funds, the defense appropriators can act more independently and help the Pentagon reduce the net impact from across-the-board cuts ordered last year under Gramm-Rudman.

For example, under reprogramming the committees can move money between slow-spending and fast-spending accounts on a dollar-for-dollar basis. This is immensely helpful in resolving an estimated $780 million shortfall in personnel accounts owing to last fall's Gramm-Rudman cuts. Under Gramm-Rudman rules, it would be necessary to cut approximately $2 billion from slower-spending defense appropriations. By using reprogramming, however, the committees can step outside Gramm-Rudman, resolving the problem and reducing the disruption to the military budget far less.

Among individual accounts, funding for a costly explosives plant backed by Sen. Bennett Johnston (D., La.) was preserved, but critics of the Louisiana project succeeded in delaying construction indefinitely. A provision requiring the Pentagon to enter into a construction contract by Aug. 1 was eliminated, and though the $238 million appropriation will remain through fiscal 1992, there is strong opposition in the House to the project's ever going forward.

The Bush administration, while frustrated by the defense cuts, would be hard-pressed to resist them, since it has a major stake in expediting $720 million in promised economic assistance for Panama and Nicaragua. The measure also includes $400 million in loan guarantees to help Israel provide housing for incoming Soviet Jews, and in a footnote to the bargaining last week, the $2.8 million fee charged the Jerusalem government up front has taken on a symbolic significance for at least some House lawmakers.

The Senate had set the fee at $1 million; the House bill would have allowed up to $4 million in charges. Though the accompanying written report doesn't refer to how the fees were set, the final number represents a $1.8 million increase from the Senate proposal and was apparently designed by House foreign operations subcommittee Chairman David Obey (D., Wis.) in response to the Israeli government's admitted role in helping finance a private organization involved in taking over the St. John's Hospice in the old Christian quarter of East Jerusalem.

The April incident provoked protests from Christian as well as Jewish leaders for provoking tensions with the Arab community, and the Israel housing ministry admitted it had covertly channeled an estimated $1.8 million to help pay for the reported $4 million purchase price.
