Vishay Intertechnology Is Set to Make A Friendly Bid for Crystalate Holdings

  • Author: Frank Allen
  • Date: 05/21/90

MALVERN, Pa. -- Vishay Intertechnology Inc. has decided to make a "white knight" offer to acquire Crystalate Holdings PLC, Felix Zandman, chairman, president and chief executive officer, said. Last month, Crystalate asked Vishay to come to its rescue after TT Group PLC made a hostile bid of about $45 million for the United Kingdom-based maker of resistors and automotive sensors. On May 3, Vishay announced that it was considering making a bid.

"The decision {to make a competing offer} has been made on our side," said Mr. Zandman, who is scheduled to begin further talks with Crystalate executives in the U.K. early this week. "We don't know yet what the response will be," he said. But he indicated that Vishay might disclose the terms of its offer by next Monday. He also implied that he considers the TT Group bid to be low.

Crystalate is the parent company of Welwyn Electric in the U.K. and two U.S. units, International Resistance Co. and RCL/Shallcross Inc. The parent had sales of about $180 million for the year ended Sept. 30, 1989. Mr. Zandman said Crystalate hasn't been profitable so far this fiscal year.

However, he indicated in an interview that acquiring Crystalate would provide a significant boost to Vishay's market positions in the U.S. and abroad. For example, Vishay already has about a 33% share of the U.S. fixed-resistor market, with $180 million in sales last year. In the same market, the two U.S. units of Crystalate have a combined share of about 10.4%.

Mr. Zandman noted that Vishay's growth rate in the world-wide market for fixed resistors is about 6% to 7% a year, compared with only about 4.5% for the industry as a whole.

Vishay considers "reasonable" and "in the ballpark" the recently increased estimates by analysts of 1990 sales and earnings, Mr. Zandman said.

For the first quarter, Vishay reported a 49% increase in net income on a 9% sales gain. The strong performance exceeded analysts' expectations, and many of them have since raised their projections for the full year.

The consensus for sales has jumped to the range of $440 million from $420 million. Fully diluted per-share earnings are now expected to be in the range of $1.92 to $2.02, up from about $1.80.

For 1989, Vishay reported net income of $17.8 million, or $1.50 a share, on sales of $415.6 million.

Mr. Zandman said Vishay expects to realize cost-reduction "synergies" of about $10 million this year through continued efforts to consolidate sales, distribution and manufacturing activities in the businesses it has recently acquired. As examples, he cited consolidations of the company's Dale and Draloric units' operations in West Germany and of Vishay's Sfernice activities in England and France that would produce combined savings of about $4.6 million.

Moreover, Vishay is beginning to benefit from lower borrowing costs and general administrative expenses as it integrates the recent acquisitions and pares debt. In the latest quarter, for example, general overhead costs fell to 17.3% of sales from 18.2% in the year-earlier period, and interest expense declined 4%.

Meanwhile, Mr. Zandman said Vishay hopes to take advantage of inexpensive manufacturing costs at its plants in Mexico, South Korea and Morrocco, where wages are low, and in Israel, where taxes are minimal and the government reimburses the company for investments in equipment.

He also indicated that Vishay might sell its large Sfernice complex in Nice, France, because real estate values there are so high. The property was appraised last year at about $12 million, he said, and the local market is "still rising."
