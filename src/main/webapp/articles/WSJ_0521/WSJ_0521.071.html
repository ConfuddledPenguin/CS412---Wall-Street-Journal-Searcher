Marketing & Media -- Advertising: Outdoor Ad Sellers Seek Return Of Packaged Goods to Billboards

  • Author: Thomas R. King
  • Date: 05/21/90

Faced with the threat of continued spending cuts by tobacco and alcohol advertisers, the outdoor ad business is stepping up efforts to lure packaged-goods marketers and other nontraditional billboard advertisers. Next month, Gannett Co.'s outdoor ad division will begin a tour through many big ad agencies pitching the value of outdoor advertising. The program, called "Creative Challenge," will target agency executives with an elaborate pitch concluding that outdoor is among the most powerful and effective ad mediums.

It won't be an easy sell. Outdoor advertising has never been the medium of choice among ad executives, in large part because of a lack of data available tracking its effectiveness. "Outdoor gets resistance from any advertiser who's very precise about measurable impact," says Joanne M. Davis, president of ad agency Omon. "And in media and creative departments, it doesn't have the glamour that television and magazines do."

But outdoor ad executives argue they can't afford to sit back and watch their major ad categories post further declines. The Institute of Outdoor Advertising is expected to report today that tobacco advertising dropped to just 12.5% of total outdoor advertising in 1989, down from 25.3% in 1988. However, the 1989 figures were calculated with a new accounting system, and the institute claims figures in previous years were overstated.

The decline, however, isn't doing much to appease tobacco industry foes. They criticize the ubiquity of outdoor cigarette ads in low income, inner-city neighborhoods, where the industry's most dedicated consumers reside. And they assert that tobacco companies will continue to heavily target those smokers, for whom billboards remain the strongest form of advertising.

The institute says overall revenue for 1989 rose to $1.6 billion from $1.4 billion in 1988, buoyed in large part by increases in the entertainment and publishing ad categories. Strong gains were also made in healthcare and apparel.

Gannett and many of its competitors, including Patrick Media Group and Naegele Outdoor Advertising, remain optimistic about signing advertisers in new categories such as insurance and food products. The companies base much of their pitch on the relative inexpensiveness of outdoor advertising.

Don Davidson, president and chief executive at Gannett Outdoor, says that his firm has already been successful in signing up a number of packaged-goods marketers, including Procter & Gamble, Clorox, Unilever and Colgate-Palmolive.

`That's where we think we're going to enjoy significant increases in business in the future," Mr. Davidson says.

But it's not clear whether those companies are committed to spend enough to offset the losses incurred at the top.

Procter & Gamble, for one, says it signed on with Gannett to advertise only one product, Tide With Bleach. "A couple of our other brands are looking at using it on a very limited basis, but we haven't really used outdoor much at all," a spokeswoman says. "It's not even a measurable percentage."

P&G says it isn't satisfied that outdoor advertising is as effective as other media in selling its brands. "It's very hard to measure the effectiveness of outdoor," the spokesman says. "There's not a lot of good research" pointing to the measurablity of outdoor advertising.

Both Patrick Media and Gannett are well aware of that sentiment. "It is our goal over the next couple of years to develop some definitive effectiveness research that will make them feel more comfortable," Mr. Davidson says.

In its presentation to agencies, Gannett plans to push its Conquest ad measurement system, which helps advertisers get a better handle on the number of people who see particular billboards. Gannett says the Conquest system allows it to target billboards so they run in neighborhoods where brands are especially hot sellers.

"We could spend a lot of time wringing our hands or we could try to understand" where to go from here, says Ron Pancratz, vice president of sales and marketing at Patrick Media. He says Patrick Media plans to take the message to advertisers that a tobacco cutback represents an opening up of some prime billboard space that was previously locked up. "We see that as an opportunity," he says.

Other major outdoor companies are looking to fill the void by signing local advertisers. Randall Swaney, who is general manager of Naegele Outdoor Advertising's Memphis market, says he plans to go after car dealerships and banks, the big clients before tobacco advertisers became the major spenders. "This really means getting back to the basics and educating these people as to why they should be buying outdoor," he says.

Lintas to Merge Agencies

Lintas said it plans to merge its recently acquired Hawley Martin Partners agency with its much larger Fahlgren & Swink ad unit.

The merged shop will be headed by David Martin as chairman. Mr. Martin, founder of Richmond's Martin Agency, is currently chairman of Hawley Martin, of Richmond, Va. Lintas said it will rename the shop Fahlgren Martin. Lintas is an Interpublic Group agency.

Smoot Fahlgren will continue in his role as chief executive of the agency, based in Atlanta. Fahlgren & Swink, which has billings of $157 million, has clients including Cincinnati Bell and Southern Co.

Ad Notes. . . .

ACQUISITION: Young & Rubicam acquired Arbeit & Co., a New York marketing consulting firm. Arbeit specializes in target marketing and individual purchase decision-making. Terms weren't disclosed.

AC&R: Saatchi & Saatchi's AC&R Advertising named Karen Amorelli president of AC&R New York. In addition, Richard Cohn and Robert Goolrick were named executive vice presidents.

COPY CATCH: Toshiba America Information Systems, Irvine, Calif., awarded its Copier Products division account to Della Femina, McNamee WCRS, Los Angeles. Toshiba America, a unit of Japan's Toshiba Corp., said the account will bill $10 million annually. The account had been handled at Ayer's Los Angeles office.

WHITNEY'S: Steuben Foods named Chiat/Day/Mojo, New York, to create ads for its Whitney's yogurt. Billings are set at $5 million.

CARNATION: Carnation Co., Los Angeles, named McCann-Erickson to handle its Buitoni brand and Dailey & Associates to create ads for a number of its Libby canned foods and Contadina canned tomato products. Billings weren't disclosed. Both agencies are Interpublic units.

Corrections & Amplifications

DAVID N. MARTIN agreed in principle to become chairman of Fahlgren & Swink, a Parkersburg, W. Va.-based ad unit of Lintas:USA. He will continue as chairman of Hawley Martin Partners, which last week agreed to be acquired by Lintas. Yesterday's advertising column incorrectly reported that Hawley Martin and Fahlgren & Swink were merging and misstated the headquarters for Fahlgren & Swink. The story also incorrectly identified Cincinnati Bell as a client of Fahlgren & Swink.
