Lotus-Novell Plan for Merger Is Called Off --- Collapse of $1.5 Billion Deal Adds to Growing Power Of Dominant Microsoft

  • Author: John R. Wilke
  • Date: 05/21/90

Lotus Development Corp.'s $1.5 billion merger with Novell Inc. collapsed at the 11th hour, eliminating a potential challenge to the growing dominance of software industry leader Microsoft Corp. The combination, announced April 6, had been hailed as redrawing the balance of power in the booming personal-computer software business. Analysts said at the time that the planned merger provided a counterweight to the power of Microsoft, Redmond, Wash. The deal's collapse leaves Microsoft more dominant than ever and gives its two most important challengers self-inflicted wounds.

Lotus's chairman and chief executive officer, Jim P. Manzi, said in an interview that negotiations with Novell fell apart late Friday, after Provo, Utah-based Novell's board unexpectedly demanded that the new company's board be made up of four directors each from Novel and Lotus, which is based in Cambridge, Mass. Lotus, which had already made major concessions to Novell to stregthen Novell's voice in management, rejected the new proposal.

Mr. Manzi seemed stung by the sudden reversal. After all, he said, Novell's chairman had first proposed the merger. "It's a shame," Mr. Manzi said. "We ended up wasting a lot of time and money talking with people who apparently didn't know what they wanted."

Novell's version of the events couldn't be determined. Raymond Noorda, Novell's chairman, president and chief executive, was unavailable yesterday, a spokeswoman for him said. She said the company's executives wouldn't have any comment until today.

Analysts said the failure of the two companies to come to terms is likely to lead to other partnerships. "This sets the stage for a realignment of the software industry," Rick Sherlund, an analyst with Goldman, Sachs & Co., said.

In a letter of intent signed April 5, Lotus and Novell spelled out a four-three board with four directors from Lotus, and designated Mr. Noorda vice chairman. Novell was to be acquired through a stock swap of 1.19131 Lotus shares for each Novell share.

But investors at first reacted negatively to the proposed deal, and Novell holders were unhappy that it didn't offer them a premium for their shares. Critics also raised questions about whether the companies' technologies and sharply different management cultures could be combined. Indeed, analysts said institutional holders of Novell may have pressured board members to alter terms of the deal.

Mr. Manzi said that in subsequent negotiations, Lotus had agreed to make Mr. Noorda chairman of the combined companies, with Mr. Manzi serving as president and chief executive, and agreed to rename the company Lotus/Novell Corp. The revised terms, meant to ensure a strong management role for Mr. Noorda, were agreed upon Wednesday, Mr. Manzi said. He was so sure the merger would go through that he left with his family Thursday for a weekend vacation at Walt Disney World in Orlando, Fla. "We thought we had a deal," he said.

But Novell's board met Friday and demanded that the composition of the board be changed, Mr. Manzi said. "They had repeatedly agreed to the four-three board, and we weren't going to give in on this," he said. "A four-four board is a prescription for deadlock."

The combined companies would have had revenue of more than $1 billion, surpassing that of Microsoft and dominating their respective markets. Lotus has long been the leader in financial spreadsheet software with its best-selling 1-2-3 line, while Novell holds an estimated 60% market share in network operating systems, the software that weaves stand-alone personal computers into networks.

Analysts said the broken deal marks a major setback for both companies, and leaves Microsoft in an even stronger position on the eve of one of the largest product announcements in its history. Microsoft tomorrow will announce Windows 3.0, a strategic enhancement of its flagship operating-system software package known as MS-DOS, the basic operating software that controls most personal computers.

In the wake of the failed merger, "Microsoft emerges as the big winner," said Mr. Sherlund of Goldman Sachs. Lotus and Novell "have cast their future," he said. "It's likely to be tougher for both of them -- and a lot easier for Microsoft."

Analysts also said the collapse leaves open the possibility that Novell will seek other partners. "They've been down that road once, maybe they are willing to try again," said David Readerman of Shearson Lehman Hutton Inc. He didn't speculate on other potential partners.

The collapse of the deal led to speculation that another suitor had emerged for Novell. But Mr. Manzi dismissed this possibility and added that there was a no-shop clause in the letter of intent, precluding Novell from seeking other partners during negotiations.

Nonetheless, some Novell investors began accumulating shares last week, betting that the deal would be broken and that a richer one might be forthcoming, said Robert Therrien, an analyst with PaineWebber Inc.

The merger's failure raises anew all of the long-term strategic concerns that brought the two companies together. With Microsoft in control of the basic software that runs personal computers, nearly every other company in the industry must tailor their products to work with those of Microsoft. The company's lengthening shadow led one industry executive to call it "the Standard Oil of the software industry."

"Those concerns aren't going away," Mr. Sherlund of Goldman Sachs said. "And it's going to be very awkward for Novell and Lotus to go back to shareholders and tell them that the compelling need they saw for this merger last month is no longer so pressing."

In national over-the-counter trading Friday, Lotus shares closed at $34.375, unchanged, and Novell shares closed at $40.125, up 12.5 cents.
