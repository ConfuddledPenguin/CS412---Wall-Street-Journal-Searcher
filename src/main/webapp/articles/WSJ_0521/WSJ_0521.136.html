International: Japan's NTT Loosens Its `Family' Ties

  • Author: Jacob M. Schlesinger
  • Date: 05/21/90

TOKYO -- Many of the big victories in the struggle to enter Japan's market don't involve knocking down formal trade barriers. They aren't even multibillion-dollar export contracts. They are foreign companies' first penetrations into the tight-knit Japanese corporate "families" that work closely together on a wide range of products.

That was the significance of Nippon Telegraph & Telephone Corp.'s announcement last week that it had selected three foreign companies -- American Telephone & Telegraph Co.'s AT&T International Inc. unit and Motorola Inc., along with Telefon AB L.M. Ericsson of Sweden -- plus seven Japanese partners, to develop its next-generation mobile-telephone system.

Analysts say the size of the contracts probably wasn't very big. (Terms of the agreement weren't disclosed). But they could ultimately give those companies a large chunk of Japan's burgeoning mobile-phone market, which, according to Michael Jeremy of Baring Securities Ltd., could grow to 6.5 million users by 1998 from an estimated 400,000 today.

While AT&T has tried to modify its U.S. equipment for Japan, "it's difficult for NTT to find something they can use," says Allan Yoshimura, managing director of AT&T Japan's network systems group. "By participating more closely in the designs, it's a lot easier for us to address the specific needs of NTT."

That's quite a change. Almost all NTT's procurement previously went to its "family" companies, suppliers like NEC Corp., Fujitsu Ltd., Hitachi Ltd. and Oki Electric Industry Co. Even when it opened bids to outsiders, the family companies had long been working with their "parent" to learn equipment plans and to help design specifications. It was nearly impossible for foreign companies to compete.

"This kind of long-term participation is traditional in Japan, and it has puzzled some foreign companies as to why they're not welcomed as a bidder on technologies where they feel they can offer a competitive project," says Michael Mintz, director of Dow Chemical Japan Ltd.'s office of technology, who is pushing to get greater foreign access to Japanese research. "The reason is that a number of `partners' have been working with the future purchaser for many years."

Indeed, in many sectors, U.S. companies, frustrated with years of low market share in Japan, are now pushing hard to get equal access to future product plans. In some cases, the access is limited because of keiretsu, formal networks between manufacturers and suppliers, as well as banks and other affiliated companies. Sometimes the exclusionary ties are less formal but just as strong.

The semiconductor industry is a good case study in trying to break into those relationships. In 1986, with the U.S. share of the Japanese market below 10% and fading, the two governments signed a five-year accord aimed at boosting American sales here. For three years, market share stayed stagnant, the U.S. imposed sanctions, and both sides traded accusations.

But in the past year, the acrimony has faded and the two sides have been working more closely together. Consumer electronics is an area where the U.S. semiconductor market share has been particularly low. For years, Japanese companies simply argued that U.S. companies didn't make the right kind of chips. But earlier this year, a bilateral consumer task force drew up a report of Japanese makers' needs and American supplier capacity, with plans on how to match the two.

Similarly, high-definition television sets will go on sale in the next couple of years, and Japanese electronics companies are scouting around for suppliers. The old approach would have been to contact informally their own semiconductor divisions, and perhaps other friendly domestic companies. But last month, top executives from all the major Japanese electronics companies flew to San Francisco and gave detailed blueprints of their HDTV sets to American semiconductor companies.

"If you want to design integrated circuits" for the HDTV system, "this tells you what it is, and where it's going, and gives you three to five years to develop the product," says David A. Hytha, president of the U.S. Semiconductor Industry Association's Japan office and managing director of AT&T Microelectronics' Japan office. "It's extraordinarily valuable."

American auto-parts companies are also clamoring for more early information. The U.S. says its share of the Japanese market is under 1% and "one of the crucial issues is access to the design phase," says a U.S. government official. In order to stave off threatened sanctions last month, Japanese industry officials met in Las Vegas to discuss ways of plugging in more American parts.

In addition, U.S. companies are pushing to get more access to the government research projects that, because all the major Japanese companies participate, help shape the future market. In fact, Japan's Ministry of International Trade and Industry recently declared all its major research projects open to foreign companies.

"I think we've got the barriers down now," says Dr. Mintz of Dow Chemical. "Now, whether American companies feel they are able to benefit by participation we won't know for years."
