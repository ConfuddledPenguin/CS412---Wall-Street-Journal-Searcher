Bank of Italy Key Rate Cut One Point to 12.5%

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/21/90

MILAN, Italy -- The discount rate at the Bank of Italy, the central bank, was reduced one full percentage point to 12.5%, effective today, in a move aimed at restoring stability to the European Monetary System, the treasury said. The treasury cited development of the Italian economy and the ad hoc budget-deficit reduction package approved by the government on Friday.

It was the first time the discount rate -- the interest rate at which the Bank of Italy lends funds to commercial banks -- was changed since March 3, 1989, when the central bank's key rate was raised to 13.5% from 12.5%. Thus, it is a pivotal indicator of the central bank's monetary policy and will help commercial banks set their lending rates to the public. Under Italian law, the treasury decides on changes in the discount rate, following a recommendation by the Bank of Italy governor.

The move didn't surprise financial markets, though many money-market operators had believed the Bank of Italy would wait until June before easing its monetary stance.

The government approved an emergency package of spending cuts and new revenue measures that will reduce the 1990 budget deficit by 11.75 trillion lire ($9.68 billion) to 136 trillion lire.

The treasury said the entry of the lira into the narrow, 2.25% band in the European Monetary System in January triggered a large inflow of foreign capital, which had "pushed the lira to its upper limit {in the EMS}, despite conspicuous" interventions by the Bank of Italy. The lira continued to strengthen, despite a cut in short-term interest rates this year, which narrowed Italy's interest-rate differential with other European countries.
