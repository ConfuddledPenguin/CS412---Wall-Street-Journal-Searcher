What's News -- Business and Finance

LOTUS'S MERGER with Novell collapsed late Friday, eliminating a potential challenge to Microsoft's dominance in computer software. The proposed $1.5 billion deal fell apart after Novell demanded an equal number of directors in the new company. Analysts said the failure of the merger is likely to lead to other partnerships in the industry. ---

Many banks have tightened lending standards for midsized and small firms amid worries about the economy and their own loan portfolios, a Fed survey says. Also, the Fed disclosed that complaints about a credit crunch contributed to its decision in March not to raise interest rates.

A bill to let banks underwrite and sell insurance was approved by Delaware's Legislature late Thursday. The measure, which the governor has pledged to sign, could make the state a springboard for national insurance-marketing efforts by banks.

---

Treasury bond investors aren't getting enough government protection, the GAO says. The agency, which cites questionable dealer sales tactics and the lack of timely price data, says the Treasury should have more control over the market.

Junk-bond prices staged the biggest rally in weeks Friday as buyers flooded the market. But investors said market liquidity remains a problem. Stocks and Treasury bonds declined, while the dollar was mixed.

---

Pan Am's Northeast shuttle was put up for sale just weeks after its only competitor in the market, the Trump Shuttle, went on the block. But financial and regulatory hurdles may prevent a quick sale of either service.

---

PacifiCorp's $1.82 billion bid for Pinnacle West ran into a new obstacle. An Arizona utility, Salt River Project, proposed to pay up to $500 million for certain assets of Pinnacle's Arizona PS unit, the state's biggest electric utility.

---

Southwest Savings of Dallas was placed under federal control Friday due to losses from real estate loans. The thrift was one of the first to join the much-criticized 1988 Southwest Plan, in which it acquired insolvent Texas S&Ls with federal aid.

---

Cable-TV operators appear less likely to face stringent new rules as efforts to re-regulate the industry lose momentum. The most onerous proposals may have been blunted by intense lobbying and by divisions among cable rivals and critics.

---

First City Financial said it is considering getting out of the stock arbitrage business. The Vancouver firm, which is controlled by Canada's Belzberg family, cited shrinking takeover activity and sour investments.

---

Pic 'N' Save put itself up for sale to try to prevent an investor group led by David Batchelder from taking control of its board. But the retailer of close-out merchandise indicated it didn't expect any bidders to emerge.

---

Chrysler expects a "decent" second quarter due to aggressive cost-cutting, Vice Chairman Greenwald said. But temporary closings at two minivan plants will likely mean a "tough" third quarter," he said.
