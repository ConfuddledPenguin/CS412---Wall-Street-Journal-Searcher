Foreign Exchange: Currency Market's Mood Isn't Friendlier Toward Dollar but Selling Pressure Cools

  • Author: Candace Cumberbatch Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/21/90

NEW YORK -- The mood in the currency market hasn't gotten much friendlier toward the dollar, though selling pressure has abated. The dollar was propped up late last week by traders buying to cover previous sales that helped push the dollar down too far too fast in recent weeks. But traders say the global mix of interest rates probably will keep it from rising very high in the near term.

Many analysts think U.S. interest rates are poised to edge down because economic growth is apparently slowing. At the same time, Japan and West Germany are expected to keep rates around current levels or raise them to combat inflation.

As long as there are expectations for lower U.S. rates, the dollar isn't expected to draw heavy buyers.

Declining interest rates generally tend to undercut a currency by lessening purchases of investments denominated in the unit. Also, yields on deposits of the unit are less attractive in the Euro-currency market when rates decline.

"I think we'll see a little more dollar selling emerge. I don't think we'll go too much higher," says Rick Porter, manager of corporate trading at Westpac Banking Corp., New York. "I'm in the sell-dollar camp" for the very short run, he says.

Late Friday here, the dollar was quoted at 153.00 yen and 1.6477 marks, compared with 152.14 yen and 1.6495 marks late Thursday. It was little changed from the week-earlier levels of 152.65 yen and 1.6478 marks.

In early trading in Tokyo Monday, the dollar was quoted at 153.33 yen, up from Friday's Tokyo close of 152.90 yen. Meanwhile, the dollar was trading at about 1.6510 marks.

Short covering rescued the dollar last week after it dropped very close to an important psychological support at 150 yen. However, the dollar is still well below month-ago levels of 158 yen and 1.67 marks. Even more short covering this week won't lift the dollar anywhere near its month-ago levels, traders say.

It has been tough for economists to get a handle on U.S. rates this year because of mixed signals about growth and inflation. But many think the Federal Reserve may get leeway to ease its monetary policy slightly if a number of sectors remain limp.

"Most people still believe the next move out of the Fed is likely to be toward lower rates. That limits the upside for the dollar," says Steven Wood, director of financial markets research at Bank of America, San Francisco.

On the other hand, lower rates aren't seen likely in Japan and West Germany soon.

The Bank of Japan may drain a substantial amount of funds from the banking system in the near term to keep rates up and prevent the yen from entering another nosedive, analysts say. Persistent inflationary pressures are also expected to cause the Japanese central bank to lean toward tightening.

Growth in Japan's broad money supply, M2 plus certificates of deposit, climbed from an annual rate of 11.6% in March to 13.6% in April, the fastest clip since December 1976.

A Bank of Japan official attributed April's high rate to a large inflow of funds from areas that aren't included in the broad money-supply measure and to tax payments to local governments.

Although the official termed those influences temporary, he said the generally quick-paced money-supply growth is worrisome. Japan has had double-digit growth in its broad money supply for five consecutive months.

In West Germany, the Bundesbank is expected to keep a tight clamp on credit before monetary union goes into effect July 2. Although economists have played down the financial markets' inflation fears, price pressures are still expected to increase in both West Germany and East Germany after the West German mark becomes the official currency of the weaker nation.

"The clear risk is that rates would move somewhat higher" in Japan and West Germany, says Mr. Wood of Bank of America.

While the dollar isn't expected to strengthen much against the mark and the yen anytime soon, a free fall isn't seen as probable either.

The dollar usually attracts flight-to-quality buying when nervousness about German monetary union flares up. Also, any weakness in Japan's stock and bond markets tends to send traders flocking to the dollar. In fact, the dollar fared well earlier this year because of those factors.

Some economists believe the dollar's softness will be a short-lived phenomenon.

"The dollar has more upside potential from these levels than downside," says David Resler, chief economist at Nomura Securities International Inc., New York.

"The dollar has declined enough against the Deutsche mark, and it is unsustainably weak at these levels," he says, adding that the dollar may strengthen in coming months. The dollar is currently almost 20% below 1989's intraday peak of 2.0475 marks, on June 15.

Mr. Resler bases his bullish forecast on expectations that U.S. inflationary pressures will let up in coming months now that weather-related food and energy price jumps from earlier this year are gone. The annualized inflation rate, he says, may fall to 3.5% to 4% from the current 4.7%.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York Friday, gold for current delivery rose $5.20 to $374.70 an ounce. The close was the highest since April 24. Estimated volume was a very heavy five million ounces.

In Hong Kong Monday, gold was quoted at $373.45 an ounce.
