Politics & Policy: House Panel Votes Subsidy Freeze For Commodities

  • Author: Bruce Ingersoll
  • Date: 05/18/90

WASHINGTON -- The House Agriculture Committee, in a sudden burst of fiscal austerity, decided to freeze subsidy rates for major agricultural commodities at current levels for the next five years. The committee, meeting behind closed doors, rescinded more than $10 billion in farm spending increases approved at the subcommittee level.

The committee members, led by Chairman Kika de la Garza (D., Texas), also agreed to give farmers a free hand to plant whatever crops they want on 20% of the land they customarily devote to cotton, rice, wheat, feed grains and oilseed crops, such as soybeans and sunflowers. But farmers who took advantage of this planting flexibility provision wouldn't be eligible for subsidy payments on that land.

At the same time, the committee agreed to inaugurate a so-called marketing loan for soybeans and other oilseed crops, but with a proviso that the new price-support program would operate at no cost to the government.

The committee's objective is to hold farm spending from fiscal 1991 through 1995 to $53 billion, roughly what would be spent if current farm law were extended for five years. With the exception of a projected $1.5 billion increase in dairy subsidies, it appears as though the farm lawmakers will succeed for the most part, according to House Agriculture Committee staff members.

Rep. Jerry Huckaby said agreement was almost unanimous on the five-year package, which will be put to a formal vote next week, probably Tuesday. "The intention of our committee is to take to the floor a bill that can be passed," the Louisiana Democrat said. "This is a budget that isn't excessive."

To save money, the committee members put aside plans to tie so-called target prices for commodities to either farm production costs or the consumer price index. Instead, they agreed to freeze the target prices for cotton and rice at the fiscal 1990 levels, 72.9 cents a pound and $10.71 a hundred pounds, respectively. Target prices for corn would be frozen at $2.75 a bushel and wheat at $4 a bushel.

Under the farm program, the government pays farmers so-called deficiency payments, or subsidies, if market prices fall short of target prices.

The committee has yet to make adjustments on oats and barley subsidies, but it isn't likely to stick with a plan to boost price supports for these feed grains by $1.5 billion over five years.
