Economy: Fed Credit Policy Is Left Unchanged At Latest Meeting, Official Indicates

  • Author: Alan Murray and Beatrice E. Garcia
  • Date: 05/18/90

Faced with conflicting economic signals, the Federal Reserve apparently decided to leave credit policy unchanged at its meeting on Tuesday. Edward G. Boehne, president of the Philadelphia Federal Reserve Bank, said yesterday that the Fed hasn't changed monetary policy since right before Christmas. Other Fed officials wouldn't comment directly, but didn't challenge Mr. Boehne's comments.

A few weeks ago, signs of rapid inflation were leading some Fed officials to talk as if they might be ready to tighten policy, and nudge up interest rates. But government statistics released over the past week suggested the economy was weaker and inflation less threatening than previously thought.

As a result, Fed officials now seem to think their best policy may be to stay put. "The risk to the economy is fairly even between a further slowdown and inflation," Mr. Boehne told the Philadelphia Association of Retired Bankers.

Mr. Boehne is currently a voting member of the Fed's key policy committee, which is made up of five district reserve bank presidents plus the members of the Fed's board of governors.

Some market analysts have in recent days begun to speculate that the Fed may soon ease credit to help boost the economy. But for his part, Mr. Boehne seems pleased with current slow-growth rates. He said the economy has achieved the hoped-for soft landing, and is now growing "at a moderate pace that is sustainable."

"I am reasonably optimistic about the whole economy as we go forward," he said.

The Fed eased monetary policy several times late last year, with the most recent move occurring shortly before Christmas. At the time, Mr. Boehne said, there was considerable concern about recession. But despite the recent weak economic numbers, he said, fears of recession have receded.

Earlier this year, the Fed was under heavy pressure from the Treasury to bring down interest rates. Treasury officials thought high U.S. rates were partly responsible for attracting foreign investors and thus driving up the value of the dollar relative to the Japanese yen. In the last few weeks, however, the dollar's value has fallen significantly against the yen, easing the Treasury's concerns.

Mr. Boehne said that when the Fed does decide to move again, it will continue to be in the small steps that have characterized the central bank's action under the chairmanship of Alan Greenspan. "Federal Reserve policy is one of prudence," he said, "steady as she goes."
