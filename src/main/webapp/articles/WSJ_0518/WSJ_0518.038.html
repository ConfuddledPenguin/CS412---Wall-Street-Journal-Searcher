Canadian Panel Offers Solution In Quebec Dispute

  • Author: John Urquhart
  • Date: 05/18/90

OTTAWA -- A Canadian parliamentary committee unanimously recommended a plan to overcome a Canadian constitutional impasse that has revived independence demands in Quebec. The impasse arose over proposed constitutional amendments that would recognize Quebec, a largely French-speaking province, as a "distinct society" within Canada and grant new powers to provinces in areas such as immigration.

Much of the public's opposition to the changes has focused on the proposed distinct society designation for Quebec, even though constitutional lawyers have questioned whether the clause would have much legal meaning.

Prime Minister Brian Mulroney said designating Quebec a distinct society doesn't mean the province "is privileged or gains any special powers. It simply confirms the obvious: that Quebec is different and that that difference is very much worth conserving."

The parliamentary committee, which held public hearings across the country, said the constitutional proposals, known as the Meech Lake Accord, should be left intact and ratified by June 23, a legal deadline. But the panel also recommended that the federal Parliament and the 10 provincial legislatures also adopt a companion resolution addressing concerns about the proposed distinct society designation for Quebec and other constitutional issues.

The parliamentary committee said that although the distinct society clause won't give Quebec any additional powers, the companion resolution, to allay public concern, should declare that the province's special designation "in no way" infringes the constitution's charter of rights.

Mr. Mulroney said that "what is in dispute over the Meech Lake Accord is very modest." But he said sentiment is so strong that "the stakes involved in our constitutional deadlock are enormous." The Meech Lake Accord, which was negotiated in 1987 at Meech Lake, Quebec, was designed to meet Quebec's "minimum" constitutional demands.

A public opinion poll published earlier this week said 60% of Quebecers now favor Quebec's becoming politically independent while maintaining "an economic association with the rest of Canada."

The parliamentary committee's proposals provide a basis for a resolution of the constitutional dispute by the federal and provincial governments, said the committee's chairman, Jean Charest. Three provinces, Manitoba, Newfoundland and New Brunswick, oppose the Meech Lake Accord.

Newfoundland Premier Clyde Wells said the committee has made some recommendations that "point in a helpful direction." New Brunswick Premier Frank McKenna also welcomed the committee's report, saying it addressed New Brunswick's concerns.

Mr. Mulroney said he will "spare no effort to develop a consensus and break the deadlock, if at all possible." But he said he would call the provincial leaders to a constitutional conference only if "there are reasonable prospects of success."

The parliamentary committee's recommendations included a new, more flexible constitutional process for revising the federal Senate, the appointed chamber of Parliament. Senate overhaul is a major goal of several provinces.
