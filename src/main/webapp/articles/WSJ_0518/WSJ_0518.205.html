SEC's Investigation Of Baxter Focusing On Bribery Charges

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/18/90

WASHINGTON -- A Securities and Exchange Commission inquiry into Baxter International Inc.'s Mideast activities is focusing on possible violations of federal anti-bribery laws. Investigators are seeking to determine whether the Deerfield, Ill., hospital supplier violated the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act in its efforts to win access to Saudi Arabia and other Arab markets, say individuals familiar with the staff inquiry. The law prohibits payments to foreign officials by U.S. firms eager to gain business overseas.

G. Marshall Abbey, Baxter's senior vice president and general counsel, said, "We have and still do emphatically deny that there was any improper payment made" by Baxter officials in the Middle East.

Both the SEC and the Commerce Department are looking into events surrounding Baxter's 1989 removal from the Arab League's blacklist of corporations that do business with Israel. The SEC inquiry also involves Baxter's Middle Eastern activities during the mid-1980s, when the blacklisting frustrated the company's efforts to enter the Mideast market. Baxter's activities in the Middle East and the government's inquiry were the subject of a May 1 article in The Wall Street Journal.

Specific matters under SEC scrutiny include allegations raised in 1985 civil litigation in federal court in New York. In that case, a former Baxter executive charged company officials with making improper payments to Saudi officials and businessmen by cutting prices on products sold to a Saudi government contractor. Baxter denied the charges by the executive and made counter-allegations against him. He also denied the charges. The case was settled and sealed with neither side admitting guilt.

The SEC inquiry hasn't been expanded into a formal investigation.
