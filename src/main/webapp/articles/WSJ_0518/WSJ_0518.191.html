Campeau Chief Must Repay $10.7 Million To Bank, Court Says

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/18/90

TORONTO -- Robert Campeau, chairman and chief executive officer of Campeau Corp., was ordered by the Supreme Court of Ontario to repay $12.6 million Canadian dollars (US$10.7 million) of personal loans owed to Bank of Nova Scotia, according to court documents. The bank sued Mr. Campeau in February, claiming that he defaulted on personal loans against which he had pledged 1,125,000 shares of his Campeau stock. The court order allows the bank to seize the shares or any other assets held in Mr. Campeau's name. Bank of Nova Scotia declined to comment.

Bank of Nova Scotia is one of three big Canadian banks that have sued Mr. Campeau over defaults on C$192.3 million of personal loans. In April, Bank of Montreal obtained a court judgment ordering Mr. Campeau to repay C$26.7 million of loans, but it hasn't seized any of his assets.

National Bank of Canada sued Mr. Campeau in February, claiming that he defaulted on about C$153 million of personal loans. But last month, National Bank agreed to settle the loans for C$80 million and to return to Mr. Campeau the Campeau shares and convertible debentures it had seized from him in January. Mr. Campeau has until May 31 to pay National Bank the first C$40 million and regain the securities, which would again make him the largest shareholder in the company that bears his name. He has until Jan. 31, 1991 to pay National Bank the remaining C$40 million.

Mr. Campeau's Toronto lawyer, John DesBrisay, said he hopes to conclude negotiations regarding the Bank of Montreal and Bank of Nova Scotia loans before the end of this month.

"We're trying to reach an accommodation like the one we reached with the National Bank," Mr. DesBrisay said.

Mr. Campeau had used funds from the bank loans to buy Campeau stock. He stopped making payments on the loans last fall when the price of Campeau stock began to plummet on news that its U.S. retailing units, Federated Department Stores Inc. and Allied Stores Corp., were in financial difficulty. In January, Federated and Allied sought protection from creditors under Chapter 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code.
