Italian Economy Gets Forecast of Growth With Lower Inflation

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/18/90

MILAN -- The Italian economy can look forward to continued brisk economic growth and easing inflation between now and the end of 1991, the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) said. But the Paris-based organization issued a strong warning that Italy's swelling government deficit and its regional imbalances could have "damaging consequences" for the economy in view of the approaching integration of European Community (EC).

The OECD predicted gross domestic product (GDP) will grow a real or inflation-adjusted 3.2% in 1990 and 3.0% in 1991, compared with 3.4% in 1989. Although the pace of growth is thus expected to ease moderately from last year, the "outlook for real economic growth between now and end 1991 remains relatively good," the OECD said in its annual report on the Italian economy.

The OECD confirmed the forecast it made for the Italian economy made in the Economic Outlook published in December.

Inflation is expected to ease to an average of 5.5% in 1990 and to 5.2% in 1991 from 6.2% at the end of 1989. But the 24-member organization noted the reduction probably won't be enough to narrow the gap between the inflation rates of its European counterparts significantly. Furthermore, uncertainties still surround price and wage developments, the report said. Although labor relations have improved, the result of wage negotiations in key sectors is still unclear and could pose a risk for inflation.

"Despite this protracted period of expansion (between 1983 and 1989 average annual GDP growth was 3.2%), traditional imbalances in the Italian economy have persisted," the report said. The OECD's foremost concern is that the government deficit has continued to grow, aggravated by the constant increase in the cost of servicing the debt burden.

In 1989 the cost of servicing the government debt was 9% of GDP, while the total public debt was estimated to be about 100% of GDP.

Although the government has set a goal of eliminating the budget deficit net of interest payments by 1991, economists have predicted that the current targets will be overshot if additional budget measures aren't taken quickly.
