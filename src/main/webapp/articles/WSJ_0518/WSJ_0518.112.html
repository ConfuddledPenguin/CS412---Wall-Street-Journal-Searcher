Law -- Legal Beat: Prosecutor Notes Link to Boesky As Mulheren's Fraud Trial Opens

  • Author: Laurie P. Cohen and Edward Felsenthal
  • Date: 05/18/90

The relationship between John A. Mulheren Jr. and Ivan F. Boesky was described as a "two-way street of mutual advantage" by the prosecution at Mr. Mulheren's fraud and conspiracy trial in federal court in Manhattan, N.Y. Assistant U.S. Attorney E. Scott Gilbert told a jury of seven women and five men that Mr. Boesky, the convicted former stock speculator, provided Mr. Mulheren with "valuable information." In return, Mr. Gilbert said, Mr. Mulheren helped Mr. Boesky commit crimes. Mr. Boesky is expected to be the government's principal witness.

In opening arguments, Thomas Puccio, Mr. Mulheren's lawyer, countered that Mr. Boesky has "manipulated people, he's manipulated the government, and he will attempt to manipulate" the jury.

Mr. Mulheren, 40 years old, is charged with 41 counts of conspiracy, mail fraud and securities fraud for crimes he allegedly committed with Mr. Boesky in 1985 and 1986. The government claims that Mr. Mulheren "parked" stock for Mr. Boesky. Parking occurs when an investor sells securities with the understanding that they will be repurchased, usually at a prearranged time and price.

Prosecutors allege that Mr. Mulheren used the tactic to conceal the true ownership of the parked securities and to help Mr. Boesky evade taxes and avoid net capital requirements for broker-dealers.

Leonard DeStefano, 34, a former trader for Jamie Securities Co., Mr. Mulheren's defunct securities firm, is charged with two counts of conspiracy and fraud. Mr. Gilbert said Mr. DeStefano was the "scorekeeper" who kept "chit sheets" that enabled Mr. Boesky and Mr. Mulheren to keep track of how much each was owed and to hide their scheme from the government.

Mr. Gilbert told jurors they would hear from witnesses including Mr. Boesky and his former chief trader, Michael Davidoff. Mr. Boesky has completed a three-year prison term and agreed to pay $100 million in fines. Mr. Davidoff pleaded guilty to one felony count. Mr. Gilbert said Edward Harvey, a former partner of Mr. Mulheren's, would testify that Mr. Mulheren held stock for Mr. Boesky.

Arguing on behalf of Mr. Mulheren, Mr. Puccio, a partner in the New York law firm of Milbank, Tweed, Hadley & McCloy, said his client never believed he was doing anything wrong or illegal in his dealings with Mr. Boesky, once a close personal friend. "It's what's in Mr. Mulheren's mind that really counts," Mr. Puccio said, as his sportily attired client looked on. Mr. Puccio told the jury "accommodations" are part of the business of Wall Street. He said evidence would show that when Mr. Mulheren did anyone a favor it was "legal and he believed it to be legal."

Thomas Fitzpatrick, the New York lawyer representing Mr. DeStefano, told jurors that his client is simply a pawn in a high-stakes battle. "In every war, innocent non-combatants get caught up in the crossfire," he said.

Mr. Fitzpatrick described Mr. DeStefano as a "clerk" who filled out trading tickets and took orders from Mr. Mulheren without asking questions.

INVENTOR WINS $102 million in California trade-secrets case.

A Superior Court jury in San Mateo, Calif., granted $83 million in compensatory damages and $19 million in punitive damages to Dr. Agnostis Zachariades, owner of Polteco Inc., a research and development laboratory in Alameda, Calif.

The jury found Smith & Nephew Richards, a unit of London-based Smith & Nephew PLC, guilty of fraud, breach of contract and theft of trade secrets. The award is believed to be among the largest ever in a trade-secrets case.

The technology developed and patented by Polteco enhances the durability of plastics used in artificial hips and knees. In 1987, Smith & Nephew Richards, of Memphis, Tenn., entered into a contract with Polteco to incorporate the strengthened plastic into its orthopedic implants. Smith & Nephew terminated the agreement in 1988, claiming Polteco's product didn't satisfy the requirements of the contract.

The jury determined Polteco proved its improved plastic worked and that Smith & Nephew breached the agreement and fraudulently sought to keep Polteco from selling the new technology elsewhere.

Marla Stark, a lawyer with the Palo Alto office of Brown & Bain of Phoenix, Ariz., which represented Dr. Zachariades, said the jury's verdict "confirms society's general inclination to reward inventors and to prevent companies from misusing their technologies."

If affirmed by a higher court, the case could open the way to larger damage awards for breach of contracts with termination clauses, according to Michael Ladra, an intellectual-property expert at the Palo Alto law firm of Wilson, Sonsini, Goodrich & Rosati. In this case, damages were calculated based on the 17-year life of the Polteco patent even though the contract contained a provision for termination by either party on 60-days notice.

A Smith & Nephew spokesman called the verdict "a miscarriage of justice" and said the company would appeal.

Judge Thomas McGinn Smith hasn't decided whether to award $25 million in additional damages for willful and malicious misappropriation of trade secrets.

FUJI PHOTO FILM unit pleads guilty to making illegal campaign donation.

Fuji Medical Systems USA Inc., the unit of Japan's Fuji Photo Film Co., pleaded guilty to the misdemeanor charge in federal court in White Plains, N.Y. The charge involved the donation of $7,400 to the unsuccessful 1988 re-election campaign of Joseph DioGaurdi, a former Republican congressman from New York.

Richard Lawler, a lawyer for Fuji Medical, said the company violated the law by reimbursing four of its executives who contributed to the campaign. Companies are barred from contributing to political campaigns.

"It was an inadvertent mistake and it won't happen again," he said.

The U.S. attorney's office in Manhattan, which prosecuted the case, said the company could face a maximum fine of $200,000. Sentencing hasn't been set.

THREE LARGE LAW FIRMS negotiate to form venture in Europe and New York.

Hogan & Hartson of Washington, D.C., Hale & Dorr of Boston and Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison of San Francisco would open offices in New York and London this summer and Brussels by the end of the year to jointly serve corporate clients. The new entity would be named Brobeck, Hale & Hogan International.

Several U.S. law firms have affiliations with European firms to expand their reach abroad. This proposal, first reported in the Recorder, a San Francisco legal trade publication, would be the first such arrangement among domestic firms.

Hale & Dorr and Brobeck Phleger have large hi-tech practices. Hogan & Hartson has expertise in international trade and government regulation. The three firms "have very complementary strengths," said John Larson, Brobeck Phleger's managing partner.

All three firms said a joint venture would offer the advantages of pooled resources without the risk and difficulty of a full-blown merger. But key details -- including compensation, management questions and potential conflicts -- are still being ironed out. The three firms belong to a national referral network of eight regional law firms, none of which has an office in New York or Europe.

If the idea plan succeeds, said Mr. Larson, combined offices may also be opened in Germany and Japan.

ARKANSAS IS TOLD to pre-clear election guidelines with Justice Department.

In the second stage of a suit alleging bias against blacks in Arkansas elections, a panel of federal judges on Wednesday ordered the state to seek Justice Department approval before implementing any changes in its rules governing majority votes in state and local elections.

Arkansas had been the only southern state that wasn't required to seek such Justice Department clearance, according to Penda Hair, staff attorney for the NAACP Legal Defense and Educational Fund. Other states were subject to the pre-clearance requirement by the federal Voting Rights Act.

The ruling marked the second win for the defense fund in the Arkansas suit. In December the panel of judges ordered the state to redraft state legislative districts in southeastern Arkansas to make it easier to elect black representatives.

Elections in the newly drawn districts are slated for later this month.

MOST CHARGES against Leona Helmsley are dropped in New York state case.

In state court in Manhattan, Justice John Bradley threw out 180 of the 188 charges against Mrs. Helmsley. Justice Bradley said she couldn't be tried on the counts because they are essentially the same as the ones on which she was convicted in federal court in August. Mrs. Helmsley, head of the Helmsley hotel empire, was sentenced to four years in prison and fined $7.2 million for tax evasion in the federal case, which she is appealing.

Nathan Dershowitz, a lawyer for Mrs. Helmsley, said Judge Bradley "eliminated the heart of the case, and we fully believe she will be vindicated on the remaining eight counts."

A spokeswoman for the New York attorney general said the remaining charges are serious and involve omissions or falsifications on tax forms. The spokeswoman said the state hasn't decided whether to appeal Justice Bradley's decision.

UPI WINS ROUND in battle with Reuters over picture-contract dispute.

The U.S. Second Circuit Court of Appeals approved a preliminary injunction sought by United Press International to prohibit Reuters Holdings PLC from breaking a contract to provide international news photos to UPI. The decision reverses a lower court judge who denied the injunction.

The appeals panel said the lower court judge was wrong to conclude that UPI, a subsidiary of Infotechnology Inc., wouldn't suffer irreparable harm if Reuters were allowed to break the contract. The appeals court cited evidence that some of UPI's customers indicated they will immediately drop subscriptions to the news service if it can't provide the photos.

Financial difficulties prompted UPI to sell the foreign news-picture service to Reuters in 1984. As part of that transaction, Reuters agreed to furnish foreign news photos to UPI, and UPI was to provide Reuters with photos from the U.S. In 1989, Reuters told UPI it was dissatisfied with the photos from the U.S.

UPI sought the injunction when Reuters sued to terminate the agreement a few weeks ago.
