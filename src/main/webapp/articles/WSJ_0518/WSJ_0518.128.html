World Markets: London Stocks Soar in Broadly Based Advance; Hong Kong Shares Tumble and Tokyo Is Mixed

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 05/18/90

London stocks closed sharply higher across the board in active trading Thursday, as a variety of influences sent them rocketing to post their biggest daily gains since November 1987. "It's very impressive, to say the least," said Tony Rebello, chief dealer for London brokerage firm Credit Suisse First Boston.

At Hong Kong, shares tumbled 1% to their lowest level in more than two months. Tokyo stocks were mixed in moderate trading.

At London, the Financial Times-Stock Exchange 100-share index finished up 63.3 points at 2284.4, its intraday high, well above the intraday low of 2227.7, posted when official dealings began.

The narrower FT 30-share index closed up 55.9 points at 1795.7. Volume was 645.4 million shares, the healthiest showing in months and markedly better than the 460.4 million that changed hands Wednesday.

In the futures market, the June FT-SE 100 index contract closed at 2329.0, up from 2258.0 a day ago.

Strength in stock-index futures contracts and unconfirmed rumors early in the session that Britain could join the European Monetary System's exchange-rate mechanism as early as this weekend sent prices soaring.

The market "really never looked back" from the opening, Mr. Rebello said.

Dealers said strong buying by bears frantic to cover short positions buoyed the market, and some institutional investors jumped on the bandwagon amid a shortage of stock, committing to fresh positions so they wouldn't miss out on the rally.

As well, U.K. traders noted that strength on Wall Street, sustained throughout the London session, provided a good backbone of support for movement on the FT-SE 100. Most agreed that fluctuations in the Dow Jones Industrial Average hold the fate of the market in London in the short term.

"If Wall Street goes higher, I think the bull will prevail," Mr. Rebello said. Conversely, he added, if the industrial average eases, it will most likely drag the FT-SE 100 with it as investors realize that Britain's gloomy economic situation hasn't fundamentally changed.

Although a slew of British economic data was released during the session, the market largely ignored the figures and paid practically no attention to U.S. trade data that showed a wider-than-expected gap for March.

Among U.K. indicators, the public sector borrowing requirement, or budget deficit, came in at #2.12 billion ($3.56 billion) for April, higher than market forecasts of #1.25 billion. Unemployment was unchanged at a seasonally adjusted 5.6% of the work force in April.

A cluster of corporate results hit the market, where bullish sentiment was running thick enough to overcome the uneven earnings news.

Royal Dutch/Shell, the Anglo-Dutch oil and gas giant, gained 11 pence (18.5 cents) to 466 pence a share, despite a drop in net income for the first quarter.

Whitbread, a brewery company, jumped 13 to 415 on the back of a 17% rise in pretax profit for the year.

Although Royal Insurance was hit with a pretax loss of #79 million for the first quarter from extreme weather claims, its shares surged 31 to 464.

One loser on the day was Coloroll, a home products and fashion group, which fell 3 1/2 to 8 1/2. Its share price tumbled after the group announced that it might be taken private as part of a refinancing plan under discussion with an unidentified bank.

Among the major blue-chip gainers that benefited from the market's strength, Reuters bolted 49 to #11.68; brewing company Bass rose 26 to #10.13; Great Universal Stores climbed 24 to 98; pharmaceutical company Reckitt & Colman rose 27 to #12.06 and shipping and transport group P&O surged 23 to 601.

At Hong Kong, stocks slumped on continued China jitters. Late buying again helped to cushion the sharp fall.

The blue-chip Hang Seng Index dropped 31.26 points, on the back of an aggregate fall of 28 points the previous two days, to close at 2917.63. The broader Hong Kong Index lost 20.68 to 1916.54.

Players attributed the hard fall to continuing worries about the possible effects on Hong Kong if the U.S. terminates China's Most Favored Nation status.

At Tokyo, stock prices closed mixed Thursday in moderate trading as strong selling pressure emerged to counter the market's buoyant mood this week.

The Nikkei index ended the day firmer, finishing above the 32000-point level for the first time in three days. More issues declined than gained, however, pushing the broader-based Topix index moderately lower.

The Nikkei index of 225 selected issues was up 93.98 points to finish Thursday's session at 32061.60. The index lost 29.42 points Wednesday.

In Tokyo Friday, the Nikkei fell 9.28 points to close the morning session at 32052.32.

On Thursday, encouraged by renewed investor confidence, participants actively hunted for attractive shares. But observers said trading was a battle between such buying and profit-taking, as was the case in the previous two sessions.

The Nikkei index, which moved narrowly in the morning, advanced 210 points in the afternoon led by gains in electrical, automobile and other stocks, before falling to finishing near the morning close. Investors' caution that the pace of the market's recovery could be too fast helped slow down trading, observers said.

Volume on the First Section was estimated at 580 million shares, down from 721 million Wednesday. Declining issues outnumbered advancing issues, 518 to 422, while 187 remained unchanged.

The Tokyo Stock Price Index (Topix) of all issues listed in the First Section fell back Thursday after posting gains for 11 consecutive trading days. The index, which gained 4.99 points Wednesday, was down 4.54 points, or 0.19%, at 2399.23. The Second Section index, which advanced 6.60 points Wednesday, was up 6.82 points, or 0.18%, to close at 3862.20. Volume in the Second Section was estimated at 16 million shares, remaining at the same level with Wednesday.

Participants said the market mood is still optimistic, despite a slowdown of price gains in the past three days.

Share prices closed higher in Paris, Zurich, Amsterdam, Stockholm and Milan; little changed in Frankfurt and mixed in Brussels. South African gold stocks closed mostly steady.

Share prices closed lower in Sydney, Taipei and Manila; higher in Wellington and Seoul, and mixed in Singapore.
