International: Trade Surplus Of Japan Fell 51.4% in April --- Effect of Consumption Tax Cited; Exports Declined 5.8% as Imports Rose 15%

  • Author: Yumiko Ono
  • Date: 05/18/90

TOKYO -- A sharp rise in imports caused Japan's April trade surplus to drop 51.4% from the year before, reversing a widening of the surplus in March. The April trade surplus, measured on a customs-cleared basis, fell to $3.54 billion from $7.29 billion a year earlier, the Finance Ministry said. Exports fell 5.8% to $21.87 billion from $23.22 billion, while imports rose 15% to $18.33 billion from $15.93 billion. In March, the trade surplus increased for the first time in nearly a year, rising 7.2% to $7.09 billion from $6.61 billion in March 1988.

Ministry officials said that both April's sharp decline and March's increase in the surplus were in large part due to distortions caused by the imposition of a 3% consumption tax in April 1989. Imports were unusually low in April last year, as consumers were discouraged to spend after the new tax made prices higher. That heightened the increase in the year-to-year comparison of imports. But a rush of last-minute purchases in March 1989 had increased imports for that month, and one result was the upturn in the surplus in March 1990.

Nevertheless, the narrowing surplus came as a relief to the Japanese government, which continues to receive U.S. pressure to correct the bilateral trade imbalance. Both countries are trying to improve their trade relationship by removing structural impediments in their economies that supposedly prevent Japan's surplus and the U.S. deficit from shrinking.

In April, the trade surplus with the U.S. declined a hefty 46.2%, to $2.45 billion from $4.54 billion a year earlier. Exports to the U.S. fell 15.1% to $6.72 billion, while imports rose 26.8% to $4.27 billion.

The drop in the trade surplus last month convinced some economists that the surplus will continue to narrow for a while longer. Although Japan's surplus had been steadily shrinking for most of the past year, many had feared that the drop in the value of the yen against the dollar until recently would encourage companies to step up exports again, since dollar-denominated prices of Japanese goods would be lower. The Ministry calculated April exports at 157.2 yen to the dollar, compared with 132.12 yen a year earlier.

The drop in the surplus "lets a little steam off the argument that the weaker yen will lead to an export boom," said Paul Summerville, an economist for Jardine Fleming Securities Ltd. Pressure from the Japanese government and slower growth in the U.S. economy will also keep exports from rising too quickly, Mr. Summerville said.

But other economists noted that by a different measure, Japan's trade surplus has been increasing gradually this year. Seasonally adjusted figures for the first four months of 1990 showed the surplus to be growing at an annualized rate of $60 billion, said Hirohiko Okumura, chief economist for Nomura Research Institute Ltd. In the final quarter of 1989, he said, the surplus grew at an annualized pace of $50 billion.

In addition, said Mr. Okumura, "the surplus is expanding despite a recent government import promotion program. That is something to watch out for."

Exports in April were especially weak to the U.S. Shipments of autos to the U.S. fell 33.7% to $1.5 billion, reflecting slow demand in the American market and increased local production by Japanese auto makers, ministry officials said. Exports of videocassette recorders to the U.S. also dropped sharply.
