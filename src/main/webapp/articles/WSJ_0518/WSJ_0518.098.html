Letters to the Editor: Regulation Cop Walks The Beat in Sunshine

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/18/90

At the outset of his April 13 Potomac Watch column, Paul A. Gigot acknowledges that the Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs (OIRA) is a job "that sounds obscure but has real power." On this point, his analysis is impeccable. Indeed, I have long believed that OIRA and more generally the Office of Management and Budget, of which OIRA is a part, hold a position of power perhaps second only to the president's. I dare say this fact is not fully appreciated "outside the Beltway." But Mr. Gigot significantly errs in the remainder of his piece. He asserts that I am "pushing legislation that would strip {OIRA} of much of its power." The OIRA bill, authored by Sen. Jeff Bingaman (D., N.M.), is currently before the Committee on Governmental Affairs, which I chair, and would provide for the funding of OIRA through the reauthorization of the Paperwork Reduction Act of 1980.

The bill, according to Mr. Gigot, "would in effect cancel {OIRA's} current power to review paperwork demands every three years." Nothing could be further from the truth. The bill contains language that provides for OIRA to cancel automatically a federal agency's information-collection authority at the end of three years, unless the agency seeks renewal for that authority from OIRA. I made changes to the bill that would make this "sunset" provision more explicit, including a clarification that it covers all such information-collection authority, i.e., whether engendered by a regulation or not.

I have sought to keep OIRA intact, indeed to make it more effective. But at the same time, have I attempted to make sure that this "regulation cop" walks his beat out in the open? You bet I have. I strongly believe there is a need for a single entity to coordinate and manage the executive branch's making of regulatory policy in line with statutes as they are passed by Congress and signed into law by the president. Like other areas of government, regulatory initiatives often overlap with or work at cross purposes to one another, with the result being diminished American international economic competitiveness, endangered public health and safety, or other consequences.

But in exchange for bestowing on OIRA this type of powerful franchise, OIRA's actions, like those of a utility, must be publicly accountable. Accordingly, the bill provides for greater "sunshine" over OIRA's activities. Importantly, by the same token, I have made changes to the bill that will cast greater "sunshine" over regulatory agencies' activities as well.

Apparently Mr. Gigot, and possibly others, believes that greater public accountability will "gut" OIRA. I believe that not only is public accountability required to make OIRA more effective, but also that it is the proper system of governance.

Sen. John Glenn (D., Ohio)

Washington
