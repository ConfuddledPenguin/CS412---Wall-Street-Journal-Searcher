Heard on the Street: Rockwell, One of the Stocks Gorbachev Sent To the Doghouse, Flexes Muscles for Comeback

  • Author: John R. Dorfman
  • Date: 05/18/90

When is a defense stock not a defense stock? When it is Rockwell International. Rockwell today is a mixed bag, getting about 40% of its revenue from electronics, 32% from aerospace, 19% from automotive operations, mainly axles and brakes for trucks, and 9% from graphics, chiefly big printing presses.

Many people still view Rockwell mainly as a military contractor, and defense stocks are pariahs in the prevailing cut-the-budget-and-deal-with-Gorbachev climate. A few years ago, Rockwell was dependent on defense; the B-1 bomber was its most important product. Today, however, military business accounts for less than 25% of sales.

With defense stocks in the doghouse and conglomerates not in fashion on Wall Street either, shares of the Pittsburgh-based company are trading for a very modest 10 times the past 12 months' earnings. The stock, which reached 30 7/8 in 1987, has traded in the low to mid-20s for most of this year. Yesterday, in composite trading on the New York Stock Exchange, it closed at 25 3/8.

Yet the company is solidly profitable, has shown increased earnings in most years recently and sports a muscular balance sheet: Its total debt is less than 20% of total capital.

Proponents say the out-of-favor stock could provide handsome capital gains to long-term investors. Rockwell "has managed pretty well through adversity," says Hanspeter Giger of First Union Corp. in Charlotte, N.C., which recently bought more than 100,000 shares. When Rockwell's big B-1 bomber program was cut way back in 1988 and 1989, "everybody in the world thought they would lose a substantial amount of their sales," Mr. Giger says. But the company found sufficient new business so that sales fell only slightly.

Rockwell's nonmilitary divisions "are really doing quite well," says Eric Miller, chief investment officer at Donaldson, Lufkin & Jenrette. "They're not well known. And the company is continuing with a major share-repurchase program."

Donaldson analyst David Moore likes Rockwell's position as the largest supplier to the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, or NASA. Long term, he thinks Rockwell "will continue to dominate the space industry," including the National Aerospace Plane project and the lunar and Mars programs.

Even though defense spending is being cut, space spending is growing. A 24% increase in NASA budget authority to $15.2 billion is proposed for 1991. Congress may chip away at that, Mr. Moore says, but probably will let NASA's budget grow at a 10%-to-15% annual clip for the next five years.

Not everything in the outlook for Rockwell is rosy. Paul Nisbet, a Prudential-Bache analyst who rates the stock a hold, notes that a big chunk of sales are to the auto industry, which is "not extremely robust." As for printing presses, he says, "we don't see much growth in that. They've pretty well saturated the world with these big multicolor presses for newspapers."

Mr. Nisbet thinks Rockwell's sales will creep up only slowly during the next few years, to $13 billion in 1994 from about $12.5 billion in fiscal 1989. Even though he likes the way chief executive Donald R. Beall is steering the company into high-technology businesses and selling off some smaller and lower-tech operations, Mr. Nisbet doesn't think earnings will rise rapidly. Most analysts look for flat earnings this year and next.

But Wolfgang Demisch, director of research for UBS Philips & Drew, and for many years a highly regarded Wall Street defense analyst, thinks Rockwell shares will reach 32 by year end, which would be a 28% gain from recent levels.

Mr. Demisch likes that unglamorous truck-part business. "I would say they are clearly the world leaders" in truck axles and brakes, he says, adding that Rockwell is building its truck-parts business in Europe, where big trucks are becoming more common. "Europe didn't use to have Godzilla trucks, but nowadays they do."

At Grantham, Mayo, Van Otterloo & Co., a big Boston money-management firm with a strong stock-picking record, a complex computer model spat out advice to buy Rockwell late last year. The firm bought about 200,000 shares then and early this year but isn't buying any right now. Co-founder Jeremy Grantham says that Rockwell looks good on the model's value and momentum measures, but that investor interest in the stock is higher than Grantham Mayo prefers. He won't say what measure of investor interest is used.

It certainly couldn't be Wall Street recommendations. Most of Wall Street couldn't care less about Rockwell. Of 14 brokerage firms whose opinions were tallied recently by Zacks Investment Research, only two rated the stock a buy.

That's just fine with Mr. Giger of First Union, who says: "We're attracted to names that are not today's hot idea."
