Economy: Trade Deficit In March Grew By $2 Billion --- Exports Surged to a Record, But 10% Jump in Imports Put Gap at $8.45 Billion

  • Author: Hilary Stout
  • Date: 05/18/90

WASHINGTON -- After narrowing in February to its smallest point in more than six years, the U.S. merchandise trade deficit reversed course in March, widening by more than $2 billion to $8.45 billion, the Commerce Department reported. The trade balance deteriorated despite record exports of $33.28 billion, up 4.6% from February.

A 10% jump in imports eclipsed that gain. Increases in shipments of foreign-made cars, electrical machinery and telecommunications equipment helped push imports to $41.72 billion, the department said.

The March deficit was still narrower than the $9 billion average monthly trade gap of last year. In fact, the first quarter deficit, $23.87 billion, was the smallest in six years.

Analysts for the most part were heartened by the growth in exports, and many dismissed the rise in imports, predicting the slowing U.S. economy would diminish the American appetite for foreign goods.

"It looks pretty good to me. I think the most encouraging thing -- and the thing the economy needs very badly -- is the rise in exports," said Sam Nakagama, chairman and chief economist of Nakagama & Wallace Inc., a Wall Street advisory firm.

Nearly every major category of exports posted increases in March, including cars, scientific instruments, industrial machinery, chemicals and agricultural commodities. Airplane exports, which have been a big source of U.S. strength in foreign markets recently, declined.

In contrast, February's big improvement in the trade deficit, to a revised $6.10 billion, reflected a steep fall in imports and a slip in exports. Aircraft exports were one of the few categories that showed marked gains.

Overall, during the past two years, exports have grown significantly, helped by a general decline in the value of the dollar against foreign currencies, which made U.S. goods cheaper abroad.

More recently, the sharp gains in exports have slowed. Some economists believe they have been hurt by a climb in the dollar, although the U.S. currency remains lower than in the mid-1980s when the trade deficit ballooned.

Sung Won Sohn, chief economist of Norwest Corp. in Minneapolis, predicted that exports will continue to improve this year because of the vigorous economies of the country's most important trading partners. "I think economic expansion in Japan and Germany is pretty strong, so I think our exports will continue to be reasonably strong," he said. "Imports might slacken because we are seeing economic weakness here."

Expanding exports are particularly important now, given signs of softness in other sectors of the economy. If exports are strong, the country's sluggish manufacturing sector will stay busy, despite weak demand at home. "Overall the trade sector at this moment is perhaps the most encouraging sector in the whole U.S. economy," Mr. Nakagama said.

Commerce Secretary Robert Mosbacher predicted the deficit this year will register below $100 billion for the first time since 1983. Last year it was $109 billion, down considerably from a record $152 billion in 1987. But Willard Workman, director of international policy and programs at the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, said the latest trade figures "confirm that there will be only marginal improvement in the U.S. merchandise trade deficit for 1990."

"Discounting February's low figure as an aberration," he explained, "the January and March figures indicate a 1990 deficit of about $104 billion, only a 4.6% improvement over 1980."
