Ex-Northrop Aide Sentenced to Prison In Falsifying Tests

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/18/90

LOS ANGELES -- The former manager of Northrop Corp.'s now-defunct Precision Products division was sentenced in federal court here to three years in prison and fined $10,000 for his role in falsifying tests on the Air Force's air-launched cruise missile. Clarence Gonsalves, 62 years old, pleaded guilty in February to one count of conspiracy and seven counts of making false statements. Most of the counts related to his ordering an underling to make it appear that certain tests on the missile were successful when they weren't.

Los Angeles Assistant U.S. Attorney William Fahey said Mr. Gonsalves's guilty plea helped force Northrop to reach a plea agreement with the government in February. Just as opening arguments in the criminal fraud trial against the aerospace company were to begin, Northrop pleaded guilty to 34 counts of making false statements to the government in connection with the testing of parts for the cruise missile and the Navy's Harrier jet. The company also agreed to pay $17 million in fines.

As part of that settlement, the Justice Department agreed to drop 141 additional counts. It also dismissed all charges against the two highest-ranking corporate officials implicated in the case, divisional executives Joseph Yamron and Leopold Engler, who were reinstated by Northrop after the settlement. As part of the agreement, federal authorities also closed their investigations into alleged improprieties related to Northrop's making of MX missile parts and the B-2 bomber.

Corrections & Amplifications

CLARENCE GONSALVES is a former manager of the now-defunct Western Services Department, Pomona, Calif., of Northrop Corp.'s Precision Products division, and Howard Hyde is a former senior engineer of the department. Recent editions mistakenly said that the Precision Products division, Norwood, Mass., was the unit that closed.
