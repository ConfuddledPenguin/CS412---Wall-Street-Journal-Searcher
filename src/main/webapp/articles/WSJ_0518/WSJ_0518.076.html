Brazil Pressures Auto Industry With New Plan

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/18/90

SAO PAULO, Brazil -- Foreign-owned automobile plants, leading players in Brazil's industrial development, are under fire from a government that wants to open up the market. Brazil recently announced plans to allow the installation of new automobile plants. The country's big four -- Volkswagen AG, Ford Motor Co., General Motors Corp. and Fiat S.p.A. -- suddenly have found themselves at a crossroads, independent automobile industry analysts say.

Complicating the situation is a drastic anti-inflation program that has dried up consumers' money and forced existing auto makers to slash production and send workers home.

Brazil's automobile industry, the world's 11th largest, "will have to choose between producing better and cheaper vehicles that can compete internationally or be squeezed out of the market, most likely by Japanese automobile makers," said Amedeo Scabbia, of the Sao Paulo office of Technomic Consultants International, a consulting firm based in Evanston, Ill.

The four automobile companies "have a kind of gentlemen's agreement to not rock the boat," Mr. Scabbia said. "Since there is no real competition among them, they don't have to bother with developing more modern or fuel-efficient cars."

Before taking office in March, President Fernando Collor de Mello described Brazilian-made cars as "horse carts" compared with those in other countries. On May 1, he ordered a study on the feasibility of having other automobile manufacturers operate in Latin America's largest country.

"We have already been informally advised of the interest of some industries, namely Japanese, to set up operations in Brazil," said Marcos Caramuru, press spokesman for the Economy Ministry.

Mr. Caramaru said the entry of new automobile manufacturers would break the cartel-like grip Volkswagen, Ford, GM and Fiat have on the market.

But industry leaders, such as Jacy Mendonca, president of the National Automakers Association, say an invasion of foreign auto makers is unlikely in the short run.

"When we sold more than one million vehicles in 1979 no one showed any interest in coming to Brazil," said Mr. Mendonca, the local auto makers' main spokesman. "Why would outsiders want to come now, when we expect to sell less than 700,000 units?"

Mr. Mendonca said Japanese auto makers he consulted "showed no interest in investing in Brazil. They are more interested in the U.S., Asia, Mexico and Western and Eastern Europe."

Tadatsugu Tanaka, administrative director of the local subsidiary of Toyota Motor Corp., said that "although we hope to one day produce passenger cars in Brazil, that day won't come for quite a while."

Since starting operations in Brazil in 1958, Toyota has produced only jeeps and light-duty trucks here.

Mr. Tanaka said Brazil's local market was too small to warrant massive investments needed to set up automobile plants similar to others around the world.

"For the moment, the investment priorities of Toyota, Japan are in Europe, the U.S. and Asia," Mr. Tanaka said.

To produce cheaper and more competitive cars the government needs to lift a ban on imports of high-tech components, and lower taxes, which currently represent more than 40% of the final cost of a vehicle, Mr. Mendonca said.

Brazil exports approximately 30% of its production, but to enter First World markets, the Brazilian vehicle must include several high-tech components that can only be imported if the automobile is made for export.
