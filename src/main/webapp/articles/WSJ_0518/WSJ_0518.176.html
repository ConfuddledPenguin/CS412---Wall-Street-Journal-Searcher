What's News -- World-Wide

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/18/90

GORBACHEV MET with Lithuania's premier to discuss Vilnius's secession bid. In what was considered a major breakthrough in a two-month-old confrontation over the Baltic republic's proclamation of independence, the Soviet leader held two hours of talks in the Kremlin with Lithuania's Prime Minister Prunskiene, who traveled to Moscow with an offer of new concessions. The official Tass news agency reported that Gorbachev reaffirmed that the republic must rescind its declaration, saying Lithuania's proposal to suspend its secession drive and remain part of the Soviet security system didn't address the issue.

Secretary of State Baker, reportedly deadlocked in arms-control talks in Moscow with Shevardnadze, said he would meet today with Prunskiene to discuss the situation in the breakaway state.

---

Bush and West Germany's Kohl held talks in Washington on German unification and other East-West issues. The president, preparing for this month's superpower summit, expressed support for a united Germany with "full control over all its territory," and said reunification should end the allies' postwar rights in Germany.

---

The Pentagon announced that the U.S. isn't asking NATO allies to increase defense budgets by 3% a year, a reversal of longstanding policy. The decision, which was to be unveiled at a meeting of defense ministers next week, came amid an extensive review by the alliance of its military needs amid the political changes in East Europe.

---

Bush said that while he won't tolerate discrimination in hiring, he would reject any legislation that imposes quotas on employers. Following the president's White House comments, congressional Democrats and Republicans unveiled a compromise amendment to an anti-bias measure that is intended to deal with Bush's objections.

---

About 150,000 state employees returned to work in Nicaragua, ending a weeklong strike that crippled services in Managua and strained the nation's already troubled economy. As part of the settlement, President Chamorro's government agreed to double the salaries of the public workers.

---

The Dominican Republic's Bosch accused conservative President Balaguer of fraud in Wednesday's presidential election, and he called for street demonstrations. Partial returns from the nationwide balloting showed the leftist former president, who was toppled in a 1963 coup, trailing Balaguer.

---

A jury in Brooklyn convicted a white teen-ager of second-degree murder in the racial slaying of 16-year-old black Yusuf Hawkins in August 1989. Joseph Fama, 19, accused of being the triggerman in the killing in the New York City borough, faces 25 years to life in prison. A second jury is deliberating the case against a co-defendant.

---

Romanian politicians concluded campaigning for Sunday's presidential and parliamentary balloting, the country's first free elections in 53 years. While about 70,000 backers of interim President Iliescu rallied in Bucharest, tens of thousands of demonstrators branded him a "neo-Communist."

---

The republic of Slovenia appeared headed for a clash with Yugoslavia's federal authorities, saying it would resist any emergency measures to restrict its autonomy. Belgrade's eight-member presidency announced Wednesday that it would take "urgent steps" to prevent separatist moves.

---

A special London investigator said some members of the British army have passed intelligence documents to loyalist Protestant terrorists in Northern Ireland. The data reportedly was about Irish Republic Army members. The official said 94 people had been arrested as a result of the inquiry.

---

Police in South Korea fought stone-throwing students in Kwangju and a dozen other cities, part of nationwide protests called by a dissident coalition to mark today's 10th anniversary of a crushed anti-government rebellion in the southern city. In Ulsan, 27,000 workers at the nation's largest car plant continued a strike for a third day.

---

Negotiators in Manila attempted to break a deadlock over the future of U.S. military bases in the Philippines, failing to resolve a dispute over whether Washington has made full payments for the installations. The round of talks is scheduled to end today.

---

Israel's President Herzog granted Prime Minister Shamir a 21-day extension on his mandate to form a new government. Shamir had cited disputes with potential partners over the distribution of cabinet posts. The country's Labor-Likud coalition collapsed March 15 over Shamir's refusal to accept U.S. proposals for Israeli-Palestinian talks.

---

South African police guarded a gold mine in Welkom, about 180 miles south of Johannesburg, after two whites were killed Wednesday in a clash with black workers. In Brussels, President de Klerk, who is on a nine-nation tour, said he wouldn't let extremists of any type interfere with his plans to ease South Africa's apartheid system.
