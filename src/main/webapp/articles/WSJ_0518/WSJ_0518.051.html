Commercial Banks Curb Lending to Third World

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/18/90

BASEL, Switzerland -- The developed world's commercial banks continued to restrict lending to Third World countries, according to a new report on banking activity covering the fourth quarter of last year. The banks' claims on Third World countries that aren't members of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries declined by $4.7 billion, with $3.2 billion of that reduction in Latin America alone.

At the same time, developing countries substantially increased their deposits in the banks, said the report by the Bank for International Settlements. Asian countries paced these new deposits, which rose by $14.6 billion in the fourth quarter from growth of $9.6 billion in the third quarter. Deposits totaled $58.2 billion at the end of 1989.

While new net lending of $100 million in the fourth quarter doubled from the year-earlier period and rose by $10 billion from the third quarter, it occurred mostly within the industrial area covered by the BIS report. The U.S. and Japan were the largest borrowers.

The good borrowing demand in the developed area stemmed from "strong economic growth, merger and takeover activity and tightening credit conditions in the domestic markets," according to the report.

Total net international bank lending for all of 1989 rose a record $330 billion, after a rise of $225 billion in 1988. At the end of 1989, net international bank lending totaled $2.64 trillion.
