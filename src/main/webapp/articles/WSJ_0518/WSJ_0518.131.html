Technology: New Unisys Hardware, Software Moves Fill Gaps in Its Unix-System Strategy

  • Author: Paul B. Carroll
  • Date: 05/18/90

NEW YORK -- Unisys Corp. made a series of hardware and software announcements that fill gaps in its plan to bet its future on computers that use the Unix operating system. Consultants said the moves don't provide breakthroughs but will help Unisys emphasize to customers how serious it is about Unix -- which has become popular because customers are looking for software that runs on numerous types of computers and doesn't lock them into buying hardware from a single supplier.

"This positions them as a solid supplier of Unix products," said Tom Willmott, a consultant with Aberdeen Group, Boston.

The main part of the computer and defense-electronics company's announcements addresses two software problems related to the fact that there are dozens and dozens of different varieties of the Unix operating system. Cyril Yansouni, a Unisys executive vice president, calls the problems "the dark side of Unix."

Unisys said it had worked with 30 software companies to ensure that 50 of the major packages of Unix software can work together. At the moment, software that runs on the Unix operating system has a disturbing tendency to break other software packages running on Unix -- a problem exacerbated by the fact that there are so many flavors of Unix on the market.

Unisys, based in Blue Bell, Pa., will sell customers any combination of these packages and handle any problems that crop up. At the moment, customers complain that any company with hardware or software that is part of a Unix system gives customers the runaround by insisting that somebody else caused the problem.

Unisys also said it did considerable work to insure that the same version of Unix that runs on its entry-level workstations can run on its biggest superminicomputers -- not a trivial task, given that the underlying hardware is so different. In principle, customers like not having to monkey around with variations in operating systems. However, in practice, consultants weren't sure that many customers had software that would need to run on such a broad spectrum of machines.

On the hardware side, Unisys introduced some workstations, including some based on Intel Corp.'s new 80486 processor. It also unveiled revised midsized and high-end systems using slightly faster versions of the original Intel processors.

In addition, Unisys unveiled what it called a workstation, whose list price has been kept to $2,395 largely by omitting a hard-disk drive. The design is slick: The system consists of just a keyboard and a monitor, because a "Personality Pack" containing the computer attaches to the back of the monitor. Disk-less workstations, however, have generally met with just middling success.

Unisys has acknowledged that it has a long way to go with its Unix strategy. While the Unix business has grown rapidly, it lost money last year, and many companies are finding profits hard to come by in an area where competition among computer makers is so fierce. But Mr. Yansouni was optimistic, pointing to some large contracts Unisys won recently and to opportunities for cost-cutting.
