Second-Oldest Fund For Stocks Is Ready For New Money --- Industry Eyebrows Raised By State Street's Move After 45-Year Closure

  • Author: Michael Siconolfi
  • Date: 05/18/90

Is this a sign of a stock-market top? After 45 years of shutting out new investors, the nation's second-oldest stock fund has finally opened its doors again.

But the move by the $575 million State Street Investment Trust, which was launched in 1924 and has been closed to new money since 1945, raised eyebrows among some mutual-fund executives. That's because it comes just as the stock market has climbed a wall of worry to record highs.

Met Life-State Street Investment Services Inc., the fund's distributor, plays down the timing; officials say investors simply were clamoring for them to reopen the ancient fund.

"It's not immune to market cycles, and we recognize that it can't time the market short-term with precision," said portfolio manager Peter C. Bennett. But he added, "This fund has been through thick and thin."

Mr. Bennett, who has run the Boston-based fund for two years, said investors who plunked down $10,000 in the fund in 1924 would have $28 million today. Last year, the fund rose 32.1%, compared with the 31.6% gain in the Standard & Poor's 500 stock index. But in the 10 years ending March 31, the fund rose 289%, far less than the 410% gain in the S&P index, according to Lipper Analytical Services Inc.

When State Street Investment Trust closed, the nation was still in World War II, and the fund's assets stood at a then-bloated $55 million. With the New York Stock Exchange trading just one million shares a day, the fund had the equivalent of a $6 billion portfolio today, fund executives said. The 45-year hiatus is the longest period a fund has been closed to new investors; many stock funds haven't been around for that long.

The fund's 10 largest holdings make up 45% of assets; the fund holds just 50 stocks. Its biggest holding, Bristol-Myers Squibb Co., represents 7% of the fund. Overall, Met Life-State Street manages a total of $16 billion, mostly in pension funds; it manages about $3 billion in mutual funds spread among 25 portfolios.

The fund's reopening has stirred a debate as to which fund actually is the nation's oldest. Massachusetts Investors Trust, incorporated in May 1924, is widely considered the first. But the founders of the State Street fund, which incorporated in July 1924, were managing money in 1923, State Street officials say.

"We actually had the money first; our three founders were managing an investment club that later became the fund," said William P. Rich, Met Life-State Street president.

"It doesn't matter when you started managing money," countered a spokesman for Massachusetts Financial Services, adviser to Massachusetts Investors Trust. "What matters is when you started an open-end mutual fund -- and Massachusetts Investors Trust was the first."
