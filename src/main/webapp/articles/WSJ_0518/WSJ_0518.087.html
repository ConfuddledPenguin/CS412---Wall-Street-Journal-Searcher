Columbia S&L Deposits Drop $243.3 Million --- Thrift to Omit a Payment On Subordinated Debt But Sees No Cash Bind

  • Author: Richard B. Schmitt
  • Date: 05/18/90

BEVERLY HILLS, Calif. -- Columbia Savings & Loan Association said that $243.3 million in retail deposits have been withdrawn since it reported a huge 1989 loss last month and that it is omitting a semiannual payment due this week on $38.8 million in subordinated debt. In addition, the S&L said that the Office of Thrift Supervision has joined the list of regulatory bodies investigating its junk-bond ties to Drexel Burnham Lambert Inc. and that the probe includes a look at "certain expenses and activities of former association officers." All the disclosures were made in the company's first-quarter report to the OTS.

The April deposit outflow, combined with total first-quarter withdrawals of $200 million, means the thrift has lost about 16% of its $2.8 billion retail-deposit base at year end.

Columbia experienced an additional $733.4 million in withdrawals through April because of maturing brokered deposits and faces a further $1.2 billion in withdrawals of such deposits in the rest of the year. Retail deposits are considered a more crucial indicator of investor confidence, however, and are also a generally cheaper and more stable source of funds.

Nonetheless, Columbia officials said the thrift is highly liquid and does not foresee a cash squeeze.

"Columbia and its subsidiaries have liquidity which is nearly two times the legal requirement," said a spokesman. "We continue to be encouraged by the confidence shown by our depositors at this time. We believe we have sufficient liquidity to meet all foreseeable liquidity requirements."

Columbia had about $1.1 billion in cash or cash equivalents at March 31 to cushion further withdrawals. The company is also raising cash by selling assets. It is also believed to be examining other strategies for raising funds in light of a number of new restrictions placed on the institution because of its financial troubles. Under federal law, Columbia, insolvent since Feb. 28, is prohibited from taking or rolling over brokered deposits, which have historically accounted for about 60% of its deposits. In addition, the Federal Home Loan Bank of San Francisco recently put restrictions on its ability to obtain further advances from it.

The payment omission on its 11.5% subordinated debt could be more ominous for the company's common shareholders than the debt holders themselves, some of which have already marked down the debt about 90% of face value.

Some analysts believe Columbia will likely attempt to swap the debt for equity in an attempt to help boost capital levels. In the OTS filing, for instance, the thrift confirmed previous reports that it plans to offer to swap some $72.9 million in other subordinated debt outstanding for new preferred stock, a plan that could further gut the interests of common shareholders. The spokesman declined to comment on any plans for the 11.5% debt.

The company also declined to comment on the OTS investigation, which according to the filing, was initiated late last year but only recently came to Columbia's attention. The Securities and Exchange Commission and the Manhattan U.S. attorney's office have been inquiring into the possible existence of an illegal "stock-parking" arrangement between Drexel and Columbia. Parking is essentially a sham sale of a security that is designed to hide the true owner for tax or disclosure purposes. Columbia was one of Drexel's biggest junk-bond clients.

The company also declined to comment on the OTS expense probe. According to proxy materials for the company's June 5 annual meeting, Columbia paid its top executives $4 million in bonuses last year, the huge operating loss notwithstanding. Former Chief Executive Thomas Spiegel's pay as a consultant to the company has been reduced to $1 a year -- from a $500,000 annual rate -- because of regulators' objections.

For 1989, Columbia reported a net loss of $591.1 million, or $30.11 a share. It reported a net loss for the first quarter of $293.3 million, or $14.62 a share. The company hopes to stem the flow of red ink by selling its $2.9 billion junk-bond portfolio, which has eroded in value by about $1 billion since last year, triggering all the losses.
