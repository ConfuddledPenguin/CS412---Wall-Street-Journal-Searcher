REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Blaming the Voters First

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/18/90

It has become fashionable to complain that less than half of Americans now vote in national elections. We share that concern, but we can't subscribe to the spreading theory that national apathy is primarily the fault of the voters. Rather it's a natural reaction to a political system that has increasingly isolated itself from the electorate. The latest example of the "blame-the-voters-first" syndrome is a report recently issued by the Markle Commission on the Media and the Electorate. It claims that American democracy is threatened by voters who are so indifferent and apathetic that we face a "citizen abdication of the electoral process."

The commission members have their own ideas on how to fix the system, such as establishing an American Citizens Foundation (to "monitor American political conduct") and having Congress "direct the FCC to call upon the networks" to educate the electorate on the democratic process. Candidates should also "pledge to conduct clean campaigns." (The commission members conclude that the Bush campaign advertising set a tone voters found "distasteful.")

But the commission's report is more interesting for its tone than for its analysis or recommendations. "American voters today do not seem to understand their rightful place in the operation of American democracy," the commission says. "When the nation was founded, the looming question was whether a ruling class would be perpetually in control of the new government, not whether the people would some day abandon it."

Well, an increasing number of Americans believe a separate class has indeed developed in this country. A political complex has grown up around Washington for the purpose of transferring money from the taxpayers to targeted interest groups. As a Wall Street Journal reader wrote us recently in defense of this new system, "We are now a nation of pressure groups." She may be right.

Geoffrey Garin, a Democratic pollster, says, "People don't feel any sense of ownership over the federal government. It isn't them and it isn't theirs."

A new book, "Politics By Other Means" by Cornell University political scientists Benjamin Ginsberg and Martin Shefter, says there is a reason why voters no longer feel connected to the political process. They argue that the U.S. is entering a "post-electoral" political order as electoral competition declines. Rather than engage the voters directly, competing interests have taken political combat inside the Beltway, using weapons such as congressional investigations, media revelations and judicial proceedings. The authors term the most popular tactic RIP: revelation, investigation and prosecution.

These new arenas of political conflict have little room for the average voter. The American people have responded to being sidelined from the real political struggles in predictable fashion: They stay home on election day.

At the same time that fewer voters are bothering to involve themselves in national politics, one party has controlled the House of Representatives for 36 straight years, a dominance rivaled only by Mexico, South Africa and the Soviet Union. The average House incumbent won 73.5% of the vote in 1988, and one in five was not opposed by a major-party candidate.

However, none of this is to say that people are no longer interested in their community or country. Citizen activity and interest may have declined in national politics, but it is vibrant and growing at the local level, where volunteerism, charitable giving and neighborhood associations are thriving. The American people are fulfilling their civic duty in areas where they believe they can have an impact. Washington, on the other hand, might as well be Mars.

But there are also hopeful signs that voters may start to insist that political power once again be decided by elections and not by special prosecutors, federal lawsuits or abstruse regulatory proceedings. A bumper crop of initiatives will appear on state ballots this year on everything from cutting taxes to allowing voter confirmation of judges. The developing campaign to limit terms of Members of Congress is supported in polls by two-to-one, with backing from liberals and conservatives, blacks and whites, the rich and poor.

The American people aren't naturally apathetic and indifferent to civic concerns. They no doubt would very much like to have more control of their government. The more relevant question is whether the Beltway ruling class will let them.
