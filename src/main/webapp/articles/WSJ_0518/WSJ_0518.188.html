Soviets, United Technologies' Pratt Link Could Lead to Joint Plane-Engine Output

  • Author: Charles W. Stevens
  • Date: 05/18/90

The Pratt & Whitney division of United Technologies Corp., Hartford, Conn., said it signed a cooperation agreement with a Soviet agency that could lead to joint production of aircraft engines. The agreement, also signed by the aircraft engine unit of Daimler-Benz AG of West Germany, follows the establishment of several new business relationships between Western and Soviet aerospace concerns recently. These new links are driven in large part by Soviet demand for Western technology and an eagerness in the West to gain access to the huge Soviet aviation market that is opening up as East-West relations defrost.

Despite the West's shifting political posture toward Moscow, the new links between the Western and Soviet aerospace concerns face questions about possible technology transfers of potential military significance. "This is all so new to everyone, we're writing this book as we go along," said a Pratt & Whitney spokesman in East Hartford, Conn.

He said Pratt & Whitney's agreement with the Soviet Ministry of Aviation Industry and Daimler-Benz's Motoren-und Turbinen-Union, known as MTU, is aimed at the eventual development and co-production of jet engines for two new Soviet passenger planes. The aircraft are the Ilyushin Il-96, a long-range, four-engine airliner with about 270 seats, and the Tupolev Tu-204, a twin-engine plane with about 170 seats, the spokesman said. The accord formalizes and advances earlier discussions about supplying Pratt & Whitney engines for these planes, he said.

Under the cooperation agreement, Pratt & Whitney will consider joint ventures with the Soviets on all but the biggest commercial jet engines, those producing 50,000 or more pounds of thrust to power wide-body planes, the spokesman said. In addition to jet-power plants, the Soviets want to talk about co-production of turboprop engines, he said.

The cooperation pact is Pratt & Whitney's latest move in its hunt for Soviet business. Earlier this year, when the Soviet airline Aeroflot announced its first purchase of Western aircraft from Airbus Industrie, a European aircraft consortium based in France, Pratt & Whitney pressed its case to the Soviets for fitting the wide-body planes with engines from East Hartford. But in March, Aeroflot said it would buy the engines from Fairfield, Conn.-based General Electric Co. instead.

"They have a very large potential (engine) market," the Pratt & Whitney spokesman said of the Soviet Union. The cooperation pact "opens the door for us for further discussions," he said. He added that the agreement "is more a market-driven decision than a technology-driven decision, on our part, in any case."

Besides the Aeroflot purchase of Airbus planes and GE engines, the recently launched Soviet-Western aerospace industry relationships include an agreement between London-based Rolls-Royce PLC, a maker of aircraft engines, and the Sukhoi and Syulka design bureaus to develop an engine for a supersonic executive jet that is under joint development by Sukhoi and Gulfstream Aerospace Corp. of Savannah, Ga.

Shares of United Technologies, which also has businesses in building systems and automotive products, closed at $57.375, up 25 cents, in New York Stock Exchange composite trading yesterday.
