Securities Giants In Japan Report Higher Earnings --- Huge Trading Profits in 1989 More Than Offset Drop In Market in Early 1990

  • Author: Marcus W. Brauchli
  • Date: 05/18/90

Bolstered by huge trading profits in 1989, Japan's giant securities companies managed to report sharply higher earnings for the 12 months ended last March 31 despite the Tokyo market's steep decline this year. Analysts said the Big Four, Nomura Securities Co., Daiwa Securities Co., Nikko Securities Co. and Yamaichi Securities Co., relied heavily on trading profits in Japanese equities warrants in London last year. More than $70 billion in new warrants were issued last year, nearly all of them underwritten by Japanese securities companies. The Japanese securities companies would simply hold onto the new warrants until they could sell them at a big profit.

A warrant is issued in conjunction with a bond and, like an option, allows its holder to buy a certain amount of stock at a set price within a set period. As share prices go higher -- and Japanese prices soared to a record at the close of 1989 -- warrant prices zoom.

Since the end of last year, though, the stock and warrant markets have shriveled. The Tokyo stock market dropped nearly a third in highly volatile trading, spurred on by the weakening yen and higher interest rates. Turnover dried up as investors fled the market.

"The first quarter should have killed them, but they'd already made their killing in warrants," a Tokyo-based financial analyst at one U.S. securities company said.

Nonetheless, some analysts have warned that Japan's securities companies could be in for a rockier road this year, even though the Nikkei average of 225 stocks has regained about a third of its losses of the first three months. That's largely because turnover has remained at a paltry half-billion shares a day recently, down from twice that level a year ago.

Under a 1988 Japanese law, the four securities firms changed their fiscal year from one ending Sept. 30 to one ending March 31. Consequently, the following results for the fiscal year that ended last March are compared with a doubling of the results for the six-month transition fiscal period that ended March 31, 1989.

Nomura's pretax profit slipped 1% to 488.87 billion yen ($3.22 billion). Operating revenue fell 2% to 986.19 billion yen. Net income totaled 218.68 billion yen, up 5%.

Daiwa Securities became the second-largest Japanese brokerage in terms of operating revenue, posting a 4.7% gain to 660.14 billion yen.

Pretax profit for Daiwa rose 0.3% to 313.15 billion yen, while net income rose 11.4% to 146.03 billion yen.

Third-place Nikko Securities posted a 1.6% gain in pretax profit to 260.45 billion yen. Operating revenue rose 6.5% to 600.99 billion yen.

Nikko's net income jumped 16% to 120.14 billion yen.

Yamaichi's pretax profit rose 7.9% to 233.66 billion yen. Operating revenue was also up 7.9% to 573.5 billion yen. Net income increased 6.5% to 98.05 billion yen.
