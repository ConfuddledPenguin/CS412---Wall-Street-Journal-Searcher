Soviets Fail to Pay on Steel From Japan; Orders Held

  • Author: null
  • Date: 05/18/90

TOKYO -- Four Japanese steel companies have halted production of steel pipes ordered by the Soviet Union, a further sign of the foreign-exchange difficulties shackling the Soviet economy. Nippon Steel Corp., Sumitomo Metal Industries Ltd., NKK Corp. and Kawasaki Steel Corp. temporarily stopped the output at the beginning of May, because they hadn't received any payment since the beginning of the year. They are holding orders for delivery between April and September of 130,000 tons of pipes for drilling and shipping oil.

Though the companies will probably continue to do business with Soviet organizations, they won't resume production until payment is guaranteed, a Nippon Steel spokesman said. This is the first time that the Soviet Union has delayed payments this long, he said. The companies have sent a group of trading company officials to the U.S.S.R. to request a guarantee for the shipments.

Japan recently doubled the rate on export insurance for steel shipments to the Soviet Union. Japanese trading houses also have been notifying the government they hadn't received payment for textiles and chemical products. Moscow owes as much as an estimated $200 million to Japanese companies. The Soviet Union also has been delaying payment on orders to other countries, even for necessities such as grain.

After the decentralization of the Soviet economy last year, autonomous trading organizations sprouted nationwide, and some have been unable to secure enough currency to pay their bills. But major Soviet procurement agencies were faulted in the steel pipes case.
