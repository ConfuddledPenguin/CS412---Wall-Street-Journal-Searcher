Latest U.S. Demands for Market Access In Japan May Spur Strong Opposition

  • Author: Christopher B. Wilcox Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/18/90

TOKYO -- The U.S. government intends to push for far broader moves by Japan to open its financial markets, and the tack could spur greater political opposition here than previous discussions. In the past, the U.S. has primarily sought specific openings for U.S. banks and securities firms into Japan's financial markets on a case-by-case basis. But this time around, the U.S. is seeking broad deregulation of deposit rates, widespread direct dealings in the currency markets and greater opportunity for foreign financial institutions to manage Japanese cash.

Washington's wider approach may have the potential for big payoffs to U.S. financial institutions, but there is also greater risk of the U.S.-Japanese negotiations getting bogged down in special-interest politics in Tokyo.

The U.S. negotiating team will be headed by David Mulford, the Treasury's undersecretary for international affairs, while Japan's delegation will be headed by Makoto Utsumi, the Finance Ministry's vice minister for international affairs. The talks are scheduled for Monday and Tuesday; informal discussions have been going on for some time.

Washington's latest demands are viewed as threats by Japan's smaller banks, its intimate network of money dealers and its investment management industry. These institutions have many allies in parliament, the Bank of Japan and the Finance Ministry.

"Japanese small- and medium-sized financial institutions are strongly opposed to quick deregulation of interest rates," says a bank regulator at the Finance Ministry, noting that many feel they'll be unable to compete.

He says the smaller Japanese banks "are very much afraid that they will follow the path of U.S. savings and loan institutions," many of which have collapsed in recent months.

Then there is the question of how Japan's government-run postal savings system would set rates if they were aggressively deregulated, officials say.

If the system chose to compete with the big banks by jacking up rates, the move would hurt smaller financial institutions, especially those outside the cities. If the system set its rates lower, as the less competitive markets outside the big cities allow, then it would lose market share in urban centers to big banks.

Furthermore, the prospect of higher yields in the postal savings system isn't comforting to the government. If the postal savings system has to pay higher yields, it conceivably would have to collect more from its investments, including the money it lends to the government.

The potential cost to Japan's government of higher yields in the postal savings system is just one example of the risk of deregulating interest rates. Rising yields throughout the financial system would threaten to push up the government's cost of borrowing.

Even though there is talk of a three-year timetable to allow Japanese institutions to adapt to broad deregulation of interest rates, that may not be long enough given the number of Japanese institutions affected and the wariness of regulators.

In addition, the alarm of Japanese regulators about the impact of futures and program trading on the stock market makes them leery of aggressively pursuing another foreign initiative. Stock-index futures were lobbied for by U.S. and other foreign firms and governments, and foreign companies so far have dominated use of the instrument.
