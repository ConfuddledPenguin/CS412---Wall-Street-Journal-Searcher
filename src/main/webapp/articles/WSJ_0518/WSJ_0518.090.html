Oerlikon-Buehrle to Post Profit in '90, Now Fears Possible Takeover Attempts

  • Author: David Plott Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/18/90

ZURICH -- Just when Oerlikon-Buehrle Holding AG appears on the verge of posting a slight profit for the first time in over four years, company management has begun worrying about the possibility of a takeover attempt, managing director Michael Funk said yesterday. "Several different raiders, some of them known to us and some of them hidden, have expressed an interest over the past six months in taking us over," Mr. Funk said. "As a result, we'll have to devote more time to these takeover questions."

Mr. Funk didn't specify the possible raiders, but said they include foreigners as well as Swiss, and they appear interested in buying parts of the diversified arms, industrial and consumer products group.

Mr. Funk, at a press conference, insisted, however, that the company has no plans to sell off any units, adding that such a move would only take place "at our own initiative."

Analysts reacted with skepticism that the company, or any of its units, might become the target of a hostile takeover attempt, given the fact that about 45% of the company's capital is in the hands of the Buehrle family.

"I really don't think there's a threat of an unfriendly takeover, because of those family shares," said Heinz Waech, head of investment research at Zuercher Kantonalbank.

Mr. Funk, in commenting on the outlook for 1990, said that "thanks to substantial reductions in losses in the military sector, and an anticipated increase in profitability at {the consumer goods division} Bally, the whole group has budgeted for a slight profit."

Although company officials stressed that the profit would likely be small, it would mark the first time since 1985 that Oerlikon-Buehrle has operated in the black.

In 1989, the group lost about 42 million Swiss francs ($29.9 million) compared to a loss of 35.5 million francs in 1988. The loss posted in 1989, however, was sharply narrower than the forecast of a loss of 50 million to 100 million francs, made by the company at a press conference last October.

Analysts said the company forecast for a slight profit in 1990 is within expectations, although at least one analyst said he wouldn't be surprised if the company merely broke even.

At Thursday's press conference, Mr. Funk said that the military sector would continue to operate in the red in 1990, but didn't provide a figure. The sector lost about 100 million francs in 1989, and has lost a total of 1.1 billion francs over the past 10 years, Mr. Funk said.

The company said the performance of its military division continues to be hampered by costs associated with the development of its anti-aircraft weapons system, ADATS, which it is developing jointly with Martin Marietta Corp. of the U.S.

In October, Oerlikon-Buehrle restructured its military and industrial divisions in an effort to streamline operations, increase competitiveness and make joint ventures in both areas easier. The military activities are now housed under the name Gruppe Oerlikon-Contraves, while the industrial operations, including electronics, engineering, computers and system technologies, are housed under the name Gruppe Oerlikon Industrien.

Mr. Funk said that Gruppe Oerlikon Industrien would likely post a loss in 1990, but didn't provide a figure.

Board Chairman Dietrich Buehrle, in the company's 1990 annual report, said the company's new structure would focus on resorting more to joint ventures and partnerships in the development of products, rather than going it alone.

"The traditional approach of developing complete systems entirely at our own risk can no longer be maintained," he said. "The development of major systems can only be achieved through collaboration with partners, or through common projects within a joint venture."

Oerlikon-Buehrle announced Thursday that its Pilatus Aircraft unit, Switzerland's largest aerospace company, and Beech Aircraft Corp. of the U.S. have agreed to jointly compete for a U.S. Air Force and Navy contract for a trainer jet. If the contract is awarded, Pilatus "will produce a major portion of the Beech-Pilatus Trainer for Beech, with final assembly occurring at Beech's Kansas facilities," according to a prepared statement released here.
