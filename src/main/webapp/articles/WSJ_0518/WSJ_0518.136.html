Lever Bros. Testing Recyclable Package For Snuggle, Wisk --- Firm Joind the `Green' Chorus Of Advertising Claims For Laundry Products

  • Author: Kathleen Deveny
  • Date: 05/18/90

NEW YORK -- Joining the noisy parade of green claims, a unit of Unilever Group says it's testing recyclable packaging in the U.S. for its Snuggle liquid fabric softener and Wisk liquid laundry detergent. Procter & Gamble Co. already has announced plans to package its Tide, Cheer and Era liquid laundry detergents and liquid Downy fabric softener in bottles made partly from recycled plastic, starting this fall. Colgate-Palmolive Co. is also beginning to use recycled plastics in its bottles of Palmolive dishwashing liquid.

Lever Brothers, a U.S. subsidiary of the Anglo-Dutch consumer-products maker, said it is looking at packaging one-gallon sizes of the liquid laundry products in a corrugated paper box lined with a plastic bag. The new design uses 75% less plastic than the traditional heavy plastic bottle. Once the container is empty, the plastic liner can be separated from the carton, so the pieces can be recycled.

Lever has been shipping the new boxes since February to retailers in eight states in the upper Midwest and Northwest. If the test is a success, the company will introduce them nationally. Lever has no results from its tests yet. "We're still waiting to hear what the consumer has to say about it," said Greg Creed, environmental marketing category manager.

Despite the interest in protecting the environment, green packaging isn't risk-free. U.S. consumers have been loath to sacrifice convenience for environmental benefits. When Wendy's International Inc. attempted to replace foam plates with flimsier paper plates, for example, consumers balked. Consumers also resisted PepsiCo Inc.'s plan to sell some soda in three-liter plastic bottles. The jumbo bottles, which cut down on trash, didn't fit easily into refrigerators.

There are some signs of change. Late last year, P&G cautiously began testing refill pouches for liquid household products in Washington, D.C., and Baltimore. P&G officials expected consumer resistance, even though some refills require 75% less packaging. But sales have been surprisingly strong, and now the company is expanding its test of pouches of liquid Downy concentrate to six states in the Midwest.

Lever officials said they weren't expecting an adverse consumer reaction to the "bag-in-box" packaging, which has been used in the past by the beverage industry. The packaging is not refillable, and is relatively simple to use. To pursuade skeptical consumers, Lever rolled out the new boxes on a carpet of coupons, offering $1 off on Wisk and $1.50 off on Snuggle. "We believe these brand names, with their inherent strength, will reassure consumers who are worried about the change," said Mr. Creed.
