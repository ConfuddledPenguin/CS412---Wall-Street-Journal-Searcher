Unemployment Rose In Britain in April; Rate Policy Is a Factor

  • Author: Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/18/90

LONDON -- Britain's seasonally adjusted jobless total rose in April, the first monthly increase in nearly four years and further evidence that the government's policy of high interest rates is damping the country's economic growth. However, other data show that the slowdown isn't yet having any impact on high wage pressures. Along with other recent figures showing relatively firm retail sales and rises in factory gate prices, the latest batch of reports tended to confirm that inflation pressures remain strong. "These inflation problems are going to be with us through this year," said David Owen, an economist at Kleinwort Benson & Co.

The unemployment data showed a 1,200 increase in the number of unemployed to approximately 1.6 million, the first rise recorded since the jobless total started to fall from a high of more than 3.1 million in July 1986.

Mr. Owen said most of the losses came in the manufacturing sector, which is being hardest hit by the slowdown. Elsewhere in the service sector, he noted, the labor market remains "quite tight."

Michael Howard, the Secretary of State for Employment, pointed out that the rise wasn't unexpected given the lower economic growth forecast this year. Nevertheless, Mr. Howard added, "the interruption in the downward trend in unemployment need only be short term, but much depends on the behavior of wage negotiators."

So far, though, there is little sign that wages are anywhere near starting to fall in line with the leaner economic outlook, economists said.

The underlying annual rate of average earnings for March held firm at 9 1/2% in March as it had in February. The underlying rate remained high although several large wage boosts announced in March have yet to be included in the figures.

Several economists, including Mr. Owen, say the strong trend for high wage settlements, both in the service and manufacturing sectors, will probably push the underlying rate up to 10% in the next few months and keep it up there for some time to come.

"We will need to see a larger shakeout in the job market" before wages settlements start falling, he said, forecasting that unemployment could rise to as much as 1,750,000 by the end of this year and head for two million next year.
