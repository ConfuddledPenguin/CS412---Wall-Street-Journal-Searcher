Brussels Exchange Tangles With Media Over Flow of Facts --- News Groups Criticize Plan By Bourse to Distribute Information on Market

  • Author: Peter R. Greiff Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 05/18/90

BRUSSELS -- The usually unaggressive Brussels stock exchange and a handful of Belgian and international news groups have clashed in recent weeks over just whose job it is to get the facts out. News groups, including Reuters Ltd., say the little Bourse de Bruxelles is seeking to act as a monopoly, violating European Community competition rules and principles of press freedom. Reuters is a unit of London-based Reuters Holdings PLC.

The Brussels exchange, meanwhile, says it is protecting the integrity of the Belgian market, ensuring a fair and orderly playing field for all participants.

In the latest development in the controversy, the Belgian Finance Ministry said yesterday that the government is reviewing the proposals in response to complaints by the media. The government plans to issue a report in two weeks.

The squabble in Belgium, not traditionally a leader in EC financial markets, could be a trial run for a much bigger battle as 12 Community stock exchanges move to form an EC-wide trading system.

At stake is who controls the information investors see and when they see it.

If the Belgian bourse has its way, it will become a clearinghouse for news about companies, receiving market-affecting corporate announcements before the news groups, and then distributing them to market participants through a bourse-operated information system.

News groups, like other market watchers, would have access to the announcements by subscribing to a bourse computer data service, for what exchange officials say will be a reasonable fee.

The news groups are crying foul. In an 18-page letter sent last week to the Belgian government, Reuters and several Belgian news organizations complained that giving the bourse such a role would go against the spirit and letter of EC laws on transparency and competition.

Moreover, many don't trust the Brussels bourse to get the company information to the public quickly and without risk of insider trading.

"This could institutionalize insider trading," said Herman Smedts, manager of electronic information at the electronic publishing arm of Flemish language financial daily De Financieel Ekonomische de Tijd.

Even if the bourse could process the information smoothly and quickly, the disclosure first to subscribers of the stock market data service would discriminate against foreigners or institutions who aren't regular market participants, Mr. Smedts and others said.

Bourse officials, however, say their way would prevent insider trading by giving the bourse a chance to suspend trading in a share before the information is released.

Also, they say, by timing the release of news to after-bourse hours, the bourse could assure small investors will see reports in newspapers the next day, and be able to act with the same information as market professionals.

"We are mainly concerned that information gets to all people at the same time," said Jean Peterbroek, chairman of the Commission of the Bourse and of a leading Brussels brokerage firm,, Peterbroek van Campenhout et Cie.

Money, for the news groups and the bourse, is also at stake. The bourse, a nonprofit corporation, recently set up a company called SA Bourse-Data-Beurs NV (BDB) to sell data. The news groups say by channeling information to its fully owned subsidiary, the bourse is giving BDB a monopoly.

The real purpose of BDB, they say, is to pay for the computer trading system the Brussels bourse bought in 1988.

Bourse officials don't deny they intend to offset their costs through BDB, but say they are not out to gouge subscribers either.
