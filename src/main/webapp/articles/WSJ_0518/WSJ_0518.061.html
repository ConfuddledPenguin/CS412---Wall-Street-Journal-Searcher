Sweden Central Bank Cuts Its Discount Rate to 11%

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 05/18/90

STOCKHOLM -- Sweden's Riksbank, or central bank, cut its discount rate to 11% from 12%, signaling a stabilization of monetary policy which had tightened substantially amid a political crisis here in mid-February. At that time -- with capital markets closed by a bank strike, pressure mounting on the krona, and the minority government paralyzed by repudiation of its economic policy -- the Riksbank took emergency action and drove up money-market rates by 2.25 percentage points to a lofty 15.25%. In following weeks, it kept liquidity of financial markets under tight control with a steady stream of so-called repurchase agreements.

The pressure finally began to ease early last month after a bipartisan accord on economic policy rescued the government. Riksbank Gov. Bengt Dennis, noting that benchmark money-market interest rates are back to levels that prevailed before the February flap, said the discount rate had been reduced "to show a new lower interest-rate level has been established."

But he warned Swedish rates would remain unusually high as a result of continued economic imbalances -- in particular inflation and wage increases that remain far above rates prevailing in other industrial countries.
