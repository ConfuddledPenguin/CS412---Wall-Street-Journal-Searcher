International: U.S. Ignoring Colombian Aid, Official Charges

  • Author: Thomas Kamm
  • Date: 05/18/90

BOGOTA, Colombia -- A senior official of Colombia's beleaguered government charged that restrictive U.S. trade policies and failure to deliver on aid is impeding Colombia's ability to fight the drug war. In an interview here, the official, who requested anonymity, disclosed that Colombia sent the Bush administration a "package of trade, technical and financial" assistance requests more than two months ago and has received no reply from the U.S.

The plan, the official said, was sent in the wake of an anti-drug summit in the Colombian coastal city of Cartagena in February that brought together President Bush and the leaders of the three Andean nations, Colombia, Peru and Bolivia. At the summit, the U.S. agreed to place more emphasis on trade and aid to help wean the Andean countries from their economic dependence on cocaine, rather than provide mostly a military and police response. But, the official said, the U.S. isn't living up to its promise.

"We were very hopeful, especially after Cartagena," the official said. But now, Colombia feels a "negative attitude" from the U.S., he said, adding, "It looks like they're not interested." Alluding to recent U.S. decision to start a $35 million military aid program in Peru, the official said: "The response of the U.S. is the traditional response to these problems -- more military, more U.S. troops, more aircraft carriers, not practical solutions on the ground."

In Washington, the White House disputed claims that the administration hasn't followed through on commitments made in Cartagena. "We have been communicating with the Colombians and doing things for them, so to imply that we haven't been responsive is incorrect," said a White House spokesman.

He said the requests alluded to by the Colombian official are a "very broadbased list of things, most of which would require special legislation to give special advantages to the Colombians." The suggestion that these requests, many of them trade-related, can be accomplished in a matter of months "shows a lack of understanding of the U.S. political system."
