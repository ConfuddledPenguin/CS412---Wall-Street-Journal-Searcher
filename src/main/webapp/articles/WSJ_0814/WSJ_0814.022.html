Housing and Taxes Are Problems, But We've Got Bigger Fish to Fry

  • Author: Kevin Goldman
  • Date: 08/14/90

New Yorkers are mad as hell and they aren't going to take it anymore. Takeout menus, that is.

It comes with living in New York: Chinese restaurants relentlessly shove takeout menus under doors in apartments and brownstones. Of course, many urban dwellers take full advantage and order by telephone. But how many menus does one household need?

To combat the growing paper assault, "No Menus" posters are popping up in lobbies all over Manhattan. Sometimes the posters have a Chinese menu with the international "no" symbol -- a circle with slash mark -- superimposed on it.

The signs can be quite elaborate: some are written in Chinese. Others threaten a full-scale boycott of the offending establishment if the menus don't stop coming.

"I call these restaurants myself and say, `You've got to stop doing this, it's annoying the tenants,'" says Rick Delacruz, the building manager of Checkers, a rental apartment house on Manhattan's Upper West Side. "They say they're sorry and a week later, they're back."

What infuriates some New Yorkers is how delivery persons, after dropping off food at one apartment, can then wander the building, leaving menus upon menus in their wake.

In a random check, a dozen Chinese restaurants around town deny that they are the culprits. "We even have a list of apartment buildings where we're not welcome," says James Shau, manager of the Empire Szechuan Columbus on Columbus Avenue. "I notice the signs. We instruct our employees not to do it. But every day we have a change in personnel and so it's done sporadically."

"If people have signs, we don't put them in the building," says the manager of another restaurant. "But we still put them in where there are no signs."

Apartment managers compare the menus to another big city annoyance: roaches. They may disappear for a while, but it's only a matter of time before they return. Mr. Delacruz says it is embarrassing, when showing a vacant apartment to a prospective tenant, to find a slew of menus under the door. "It shows our security is in question," he says.

Tom Goodman, a spokesman for CBS News, says his Upper East Side apartment building has a partial solution. "The doormen tell offending restaurants they won't be allowed to deliver to the building anymore," he says.
