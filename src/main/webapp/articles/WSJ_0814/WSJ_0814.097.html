Abreast of the Market: Industrials Rise 30.20, Rebounding From Reaction to Nikkei Decline

  • Author: David Wilson
  • Date: 08/14/90

NEW YORK -- The stock market rebounded from an early bout of selling in slow trading, curtailed by an afternoon power outage in the city's financial district. The Dow Jones Industrial Average dropped about 32 points just after the opening in the wake of steep losses on overseas stock markets, including Tokyo, where the Nikkei index of 225 Japanese issues recorded one of its sharpest declines ever.

But on Wall Street, prices gradually rebounded after the initial wave of selling subsided, as a combination of bargain-hunting and futures-related program buying lifted the market. The Dow Jones industrials finished up 30.20 at 2746.78.

Broader market measures couldn't keep pace with the industrial average, which benefited from renewed buying interest in both oil stocks and blue-chip, recession-resistant consumer issues.

Standard & Poor's 500-Stock Index recorded the equivalent of a 25-point advance in the Dow Jones industrials by rising 0.21 to 335.73, while the New York Stock Exchange Composite Index went up 1.48 to 185.63.

Declining issues outpaced advancers on the Big Board by 766 to 726. Volume was low, with 122,820,000 shares changing hands, compared with 145,340,000 shares Friday. The latest total was the lowest in five weeks. Traders attributed the industrial average's early slide to nervousness about how the market would react to the latest developments in the Middle East. Mideast concern continued to take its toll on the Tokyo market; the Nikkei Index of 225 issues plunged 1153.12, or 4.2%, to 26176.43. The drop was its fourth-largest point loss and seventh-largest percentage drop on record.

But while New York stock prices tumbled at the start of trading, "There was not an avalanche of sell orders, by any means," said Sid Dorr, head of institutional trading at Charles Schwab & Co., San Francisco. By midday, the industrial average had erased its opening losses, and a round of buying in the final hour of trading sent it up sharply.

The power outage struck lower Manhattan as the result of a substation fire about noon EDT at a Consolidated Edison Co. of New York plant.

The American Stock Exchange shut down its trading at 1:13 p.m. EDT after losing power to its systems, and outages curtailed operations at a number of New York-based brokerage firms. The New York Stock Exchange said the power problem didn't close down its operations.

Eastman Kodak surged 2 1/2 to 43 1/2 in composite trading of 1.5 million shares, and other blue-chip consumer stocks attracted buyers as well. Merck climbed 2 1/8 to 86 7/8, Procter & Gamble rose 1 7/8 to 81 3/4, Coca-Cola gained 3/4 to 44 1/2 and Philip Morris added 5/8 to 46 1/2 on volume of 1.4 million shares.

Energy stocks rallied along with the price of oil; September crude-oil futures were up 54 cents a barrel, to $26.77; dealings on the New York Mercantile Exchange were halted because of power problems.

Exxon gained 3/4 to 52 3/8, Texaco added 3/8 to 63 1/2, Mobil climbed 1 1/4 to 67 1/8, Amoco rose 3/4 to 56 1/2, Unocal added 5/8 to 32 5/8 and Atlantic Richfield rallied 1 3/4 to 136 3/4. Du Pont, which owns Conoco, advanced 1 1/8 to 38 5/8.

The precious-metals sector, another strong performer lately as a result of the turmoil in the Middle East, also attracted market players as the price of gold rose above $400 an ounce. Even American Barrick Resources, which said it planned to sell five million common shares for 24.50 Canadian dollars each, added 1/2 to 22 3/8.

Among other gold stocks, Placer Dome rose 1 3/8 to 19 1/4 on 1.1 million shares, ASA Ltd. jumped 2 3/8 to 54 3/8, Homestake Mining gained 1 1/8 to 23, Hecla Mining went up 1/2 to 12 3/4 and Battle Mountain Gold rose 3/8 to 13.

Stock in Midway Airlines, a Chicago-based carrier, rose 2 to 9 5/8 amid reports that AMR Corp.'s American Airlines unit has hired the investment banking firm of Goldman Sachs & Co. to study the possible purchase of part or all of Midway.

Multiple rounds of buy programs related to stock-index arbitrage boosted the market in the final hour, traders said. The arbitrage buying went into effect after index futures rallied to sharp premiums to the cash indexes; the lack of activity aside from the programs magnified the market impact of the buying, traders said.

International Business Machines climbed 1 3/4 to 103 3/4 on 1.5 million shares as technology issues benefited from bargain-hunting. Compaq Computer added 2 3/8 to 56 1/4, Hewlett-Packard went up 1/2 to 38 1/4, Motorola gained 1 3/8 to 76 1/4 and Texas Instruments rose 3/4 to 28 7/8.

Japan OTC Equity Fund fell 5/8 to 9 7/8 in Big Board trading following Tokyo's sell-off, and put warrants on the Nikkei index -- a security which permits its holders to profit when the measure drops -- rallied in the abbreviated session on the American Stock Exchange. Salomon Inc.'s put warrants gained 7/8 to 13 3/4 and led the American exchange's most actives.

The American Stock Exchange Market Value Index fell 1.32 to 338.00. Volume at the 1 p.m. closing totaled 9,480,000 shares.
