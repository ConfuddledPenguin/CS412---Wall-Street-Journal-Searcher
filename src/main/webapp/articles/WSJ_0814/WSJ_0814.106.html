Credit Markets: Power Outage Curbs Trading; 30-Year Treasury Falls 1/4 Point as Short-Term Securities Advance

  • Author: Constance Mitchell and Kevin Donovan
  • Date: 08/14/90

NEW YORK -- Trading activity in the bond market was brought to a virtual standstill as a power outage in lower Manhattan, which includes Wall Street, left some dealers without equipment with which to buy or sell securities. "We didn't see any trading" during most of the afternoon, said James Capra, a senior vice president at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc.

Securities dealers such as Shearson were unable to trade because their electronic broker screens were inoperable and telephone lines were dead. The power outage occurred in the early afternoon and was caused by a fire at a Consolidated Edison substation.

Even firms that weren't affected by the outage said they saw very little business. "Nothing was going on," said Joel Kazis, manager of government trading at Smith Barney, Harris Upham. He said the market was moderately active during morning trading, but "once the power failure happened downtown, things really quieted down."

Meanwhile, bond prices ended mixed. The benchmark 30-year Treasury bond ended 1/4 point lower, a loss of $2.50 for a bond with a $1,000 face amount. The issue's yield, which moves in the opposite direction from its price, rose to 8.80%.

Dealers said prices of long-term securities continued to suffer from perceptions that inflation is accelerating. Yesterday's rise in the price of oil and gold merely heightened investors fears of inflation.

Short-term securities, however, ended modestly higher because of perceptions that the Federal Reserve will soon ease monetary policy by lowering the federal funds rate from 8%. The rate is what banks charge each other on overnight loans.

"Investors believe that the Fed will ease soon," said Lawrence Leuzzi, a trading manager at S.G. Warburg. Mr. Leuzzi believes the Fed could vote to ease credit conditions after its next Federal Open Market Committee meeting, scheduled for next week.

Until then, Mr. Leuzzi believes that prices of short-term securities will continue to rise. The latest two-year notes ended about 1/8 point higher, while the yield on the notes fell to 7.89%.

Treasury Securities

The benchmark 30-year bond ended at a price of 99 13/32 to yield 8.80%, compared with Friday's price of 99 21/32 to yield 8.771%.

The latest 10-year notes ended at a price of 100 13/32 to yield 8.67%, compared with Friday's price of 100 17/32 to yield 8.65%.

The Treasury sold $18.4 billion of shortterm bills yesterday at its regular weekly auction. Although some primary dealers had trouble placing bids at the auction because of the power outage, a spokesman for the Fed said the auction proceeded smoothly.

The average discount rate was 7.41% for three-month bills and 7.31% for six-month bills. Those rates were higher than at last week's auction, when they were 7.23% and 7.19%, respectively.

Corporate Issues

The junk bond market edged lower on thin volume after the power outage in lower Manhattan.

"The afternoon was a non-event," said one high-yield trader. "Of the five brokers I talk to, only two were fully staffed."

Most junk bonds, or speculative-grade securities with low or no credit ratings, ended 1/8 to 1/4 point lower.

Among the high-yield securities that traded lower were junk bonds of RJR Nabisco Inc. RJR's 17% exchangeable pay-in-kind debentures ended 3/8 point lower at 90 1/8, while the food and tobacco giant's 13.5% subordinated debentures finished down 1/4 point at 97 1/2.

The junk bonds of Fort Howard Corp. were also lower after the company reported that its second-quarter loss widened to $26.8 million from $19.3 million a year earlier.

Fort Howard's 12 3/8% senior subordinated notes ended 3/8 point lower at 95 3/4.

Meanwhile, among the key gainers were junk bonds of Bond Brewing, which skyrocketed on unconfirmed rumors that a higher tender offer for the bonds is in the works. Bond Brewing's 12.625% subordinated debentures, due in 2006, closed up 10 points at 62.

Foreign Bonds

The plunge in the Japanese stock market caught up with the already wobbly Japanese government bond market, sending bond prices sharply lower.

Driven largely by arbitrage trading, the Nikkei stock average ended 4% lower, the fourth-largest drop in percentage terms. In response, the price of Japan's benchmark No. 119 10-year bond ended at 83.26, down 0.28 point, to yield 8.085%.

The selling in the stock and bond markets had a greater impact than might otherwise have been the case as participation is thin with the Japanese August holidays in full swing. On the other hand, the absence of investors during another rout in the Japanese markets may have relieved them of another source of selling.

West German and British government bonds ended lower in quiet trading.

Municipals

Municipal bond prices were virtually unaffected by the power problems, traders said, with bonds stable at early levels of 1/8 to 3/8 point easier.

"If bonds were going to be volatile, this has probably taken it out of them," one trader said.

The New Jersey Turnpike Authority's 7.20% issue of 2018 was off 3/8 point, at 98 bid to yield 7.35%.

Mortgage-Backed Securities

Mortgage securities trading ground to a standstill during the power outage in New York.

One trader quoted Government National Mortgage Association 9 1/2% pass-through securities for August delivery at 99 1/4, up 1/8 on the day, Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corp. 9 1/2% securities up 5/32 at 98 26/32, and Federal National Mortgage Association 9 1/2% securities up 1/8 at 99 10/32.

Prior to the trading interruption, pricing details for a $500 million Freddie Mac real estate mortgage investment conduit were announced.
