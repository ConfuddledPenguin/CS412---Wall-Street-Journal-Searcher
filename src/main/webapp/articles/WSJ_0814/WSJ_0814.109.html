Commodities: Several Wheat Prices Fall to Contract Lows on Sign Soviet Purchases of U.S. Wheat Are Shriveling

  • Author: Scott Kilman
  • Date: 08/14/90

CHICAGO -- Prices of several wheat futures contracts sank to life-of-contract lows amid signs that Soviet purchases of U.S. wheat are shriveling. Prices of some corn futures contracts tumbled to their lowest levels since February as good growing weather in the Midwest continued to improve the prospects for an eight-billion bushel corn harvest this fall.

"It was a big down day," said Victor Lespinasse, an assistant vice president at Dean Witter Reynolds, Chicago.

In trading at the Chicago Board of Trade, the wheat contract for September delivery fell to a new low of $2.7375 a bushel before settling the day at $2.7525 a bushel, down 5.25 cents.

The price of the September wheat contract has fallen about 30% over the past year amid the combination of forecasts for a big harvest in the U.S. this year as well as slumping demand from the Soviet Union.

The Agriculture Department reported last week that the U.S. wheat harvest should increase 33% from last year's crop, which was damaged by drought.

At the same time, good growing weather in the Soviet Union is increasing crop prospects there, and cutting its need for U.S. wheat.

According to Daniel W. Basse, director of market research at AgResource Co., Chicago, the pace of U.S. wheat exports has dropped 32% since June 30, compared with the year-earlier period.

Because of the Soviet crop prospects, most analysts expect the Soviet Union to reduce its grain imports from all sources by roughly 20% through next June. It isn't clear how U.S. trade will be affected; however, the U.S. is one of the largest shippers of grain to the Soviet Union.

Crop prices were also depressed yesterday by reports that the Soviet Union's plan to sell roughly $5 billion in diamonds is running into internal opposition. U.S. grain traders had been speculating that the cash-strapped Soviet Union would use some of the proceeds from any diamond sales to buy U.S. crops.

In July, the Soviet Union agreed to sell diamonds to the Swiss branch of De Beers Consolidated Mines Ltd. and to borrow $1 billion from the company.

According to traders, the Russian Federation is apparently opposing the move by the central government. It isn't clear whether that will block the pact, but the Russian republic is a source of Soviet diamonds.

U.S. grain exports also seem to be slipping at least temporarily under the weight of higher fuel costs, which increases shipping costs, traders said.

After the close of trading yesterday, the weekly crop condition reports by Midwest states indicated that the corn crop is maturing slowly. The unusually wet spring delayed the planting season.

However, the crop generally is in good shape even though rainfall in some states such as Iowa has been the heaviest in 118 years.

In that state, 91% of the corn crop is in excellent or good condition, compared with 89% a week earlier. While the rainfall has washed out some fields, it has helped restore subsoil moisture that was exhausted during the 1988 drought.

Soybean prices dropped yesterday in the wake of beneficial rains in the Mississippi Delta region, where conditions have been dry.

In other commodity markets yesterday:

ENERGY: Persian Gulf activities again overshadowed all other factors in influencing petroleum prices and futures trading yesterday. Spot prices for North Sea crudes were 35 cents to 55 cents a barrel higher. September crude on the New York Mercantile Exchange was 54 cents higher, at $26.77 a barrel, when trading was halted because of a power outage in lower Manhattan. Prices were bolstered by reports that an Iraqi pipeline across Saudi Arabia was closed and an Iraqi tanker had been turned away from a Saudi terminal.

But events outside the Middle East helped buoy prices, as well. For the fourth time in 11 days, North Sea oil workers stopped work in a bid for union recognition and better safety rules. News reports said some 7,000 workers walked out in the 24-hour wildcat strike, but added that the companies involved put the number much lower and said production wasn't being affected. Separately, the Houston Ship Channel was hit by yet another oil spill. A barge buckled, spilling 20,000 gallons of heating oil into the channel, according to Coast Guard officials. The waterway was obstructed, but not closed.

NEW YORK MARKETS: A major power failure struck New York City's downtown financial district early yesterday afternoon, shutting down electricity and disrupting communications.

A spokeswoman at the Commodity Exchange, which is in the World Trade Center, said the clocks on the exchange froze at 1:13 p.m. EDT and trading was suspended shortly afterward.

"There are no closing or settling prices because the exchange floor is pitch dark and the prices on the boards on the wall aren't visible," the spokeswoman said. The Commodity Exchange is part of a five-exchange complex housed in the Commodity Exchange Center. All the exchanges share the same trading floor.

At around 2 p.m., the personnel of the exchanges were evacuated and sent home.

Precious metals, with trading spurred by the volatile Middle Eastern situation, were strong at the time of their suspension. The August gold contract, which will cease trading Aug. 29, was up $8.30 an ounce based on the last posted trade, at $405.20. The most-active September contract advanced $8.10 to $414.00. September silver surged, outdoing even gold as it last traded at $5.1520 an ounce, for a gain of 16.10 cents. October platinum rose $9.70 an ounce to $506.00.

James Kneafsey, president of Cambridge Financial Management Inc. of Cambridge, Mass., said that in the gold sector, "The market's ability to rise above $400 and hold the gains was very significant."

Ian MacDonald, vice president, Credit Suisse, New York, said "the majority of the buying, especially for bullion, is coming out of the Middle East," adding, "There seems to be a major shift among investors toward gold because of war and inflation." He noted that silver was pulled up by gold.

London dealers said investors in the Far East, Europe and the U.S. also joined in the buying that sent gold hurtling higher.

The gold price could rise as high as $423, analysts said. But the market is very volatile, and any setback, such as a slump in the global stock market, could be negative for gold because it would point to a recession and slower jewelry demand, they said.

Jeffrey Nichols, president of American Metal Trading Advisors in Toronto, said a number of factors accounted for the exceptional strength of the gold market. "There is a tightness in the gold supply," he said. This triggered short-covering, or the buying back of contracts by traders who had previously sold in anticipation that prices would decline, Mr. Nichols said.

Mr. Nichols said producers had been heavy sellers recently. Yesterday, however, "apparently they didn't have any more gold to sell, and this helped prices rise more than had been expected."

Finally, Mr. Nichols noted, gold was due to go up because "it had some catching up with the rise in the price of oil and to compensate for the drop in the dollar."

Last-trade prices were mixed in the "soft" commodity markets. September delivery cocoa was down $44 per metric ton to $1,097, while October sugar was unchanged at 10.49 cents a pound. September coffee was up 2.10 cents a pound at 95.60 cents, mostly on reports that a dock strike may be erupting in Brazil, the world's major coffee producer.

September copper's last trade was at $1.2010, for a decline of 1.90 cents a pound. There was strong options-related buying early in the day in London, but this petered out later. By the time copper opened for trading in New York on the Commodity Exchange prices in London had already turned downward, and the decline was reflected in the New York market in what one analyst described as "dull trading." Cash markets for copper were quiet. Japan has recently been a strong buyer of physical copper but no interest was in evidence from that quarter yesterday, an analyst said. Another analyst noted that Thursday's intraday high of $1.3430 in the New York September contract, which was a new life-of-contract high, was the highest price for that contract since October 1989. October cotton was off 1.55 cents a pound to 73.35 cents. A trader said contracts were sold in the wake of a U.S. Department of Agriculture report. "It was mildly bullish," the trader added, "but that had been expected and was already in the market. Traders then started selling."
