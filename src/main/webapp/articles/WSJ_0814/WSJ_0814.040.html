Foreign Exchange: New York Power Outage Shortens Session That Found Investors Selling Dollar, Yen

  • Author: Thomas L. Waite Special to The Wall Street Journal
  • Date: 08/14/90

NEW YORK -- A power outage in lower Manhattan all but ended foreign-exchange dealings by 2 p.m. EDT, truncating a session investors had been using to sell dollars and yen for European currencies. The electrical shutdown, caused by a transformer fire near the Brooklyn Bridge, left many New York trading rooms without computers or telephones. Many offices able to switch to emergency power were left without the services of electronic dealing and quoting systems.

Electricity hadn't been restored to lower Manhattan by the end of the working day.

Ian Foakes, foreign-exchange manager for PaineWebber International in New York, said the only parallel for Monday that he remembers occurred in 1985 when New York currency trading was halted by an approaching tornado.

New York trading rooms that were unaffected technically by the outage, and those elsewhere in North America, were generally unable to continue working without their counterparts or the services of the New York broker network.

"When they remove the other team from the field, you have no one to bat against," Mr. Foakes said. He said that even though the PaineWebber offices in midtown Manhattan never lost their dealing capabilities, his traders generally refrained from entering the extremely thin late-afternoon market.

"If you have no counterparties, you have no way to get out" if, for example, fighting started in the smoldering Middle East, Mr. Foakes said.

Even before the strange end to the North American trading session, a lack of noise from the armies facing each other on the Kuwait-Saudi Arabia frontier had sapped foreign-exchange activity. U.S. dealers outside of New York quipped that dealings had been so sparse they didn't notice the difference at first when New York went black.

In fact, most signs from the Mideast suggested the military showdown is cooling down or is at least not worsening.

Dollar dealers were greeted in the morning by a picture on the front page of the Financial Times in which President Bush was directing the crisis from the seat of his golf cart at his Maine vacation home.

The weekend offer by Iraqi leader Saddam Hussein to pull out of Kuwait if Israel leaves its occupied territories, though rejected by the U.S. and other nations, was seen as an indication that the leader would like a peaceful, face-saving solution to the standoff.

As a result, after a 1 1/2 weeks of trading the dollar to exhaustion, currency dealers were free to concentrate on non-dollar cross-trading strategies. "Traders have gotten burned out trying to follow the dollar," said Judy Rubenstein, a Los Angeles vice president with Bank of America.

The day's most interesting activity involved selling Japanese yen for European currencies led by the Swiss franc, she said.

The cross-dealings were a natural outgrowth of a market determined to stay square, neither oversold nor overbought, on the dollar until the Persian Gulf crisis is resolved, added Martin L. McNulty, a vice president in New York for Lloyds Bank.

The dollar was hurt severely by the interest in its European counterparts and even failed to benefit from the yen's weakness. About the time trading was forced to stop in New York, the dollar was quoted at 1.5765 marks, down from 1.5905 marks late Friday in New York. Sterling was trading at $1.8830, up from $1.8740 late Friday.

The U.S. currency was also changing hands at 150.40 yen, down from 150.45 yen in New York late Friday.

Before trading shut down, the dollar was trading at 1.3165 Swiss francs, down from 1.3375 francs late Friday.

In early trading in Tokyo Tuesday, the dollar was quoted at 149.95 yen, down from Monday's Tokyo close of 150.65 yen. Meanwhile, the U.S. currency was trading at about 1.5775 marks; in Tokyo Monday, it closed at 1.5781 marks.

Traders said that while the dollar may still be a safe haven during times of extreme global disruption, the nervous buying caused by the present extended period of tension is flowing to gold and the Swiss franc, both age-old harbors.

On the Commodity Exchange in New York, in late trading before the power outage gold for current delivery was $405.20 an ounce, up $8.20.

In early trading in Hong Kong Tuesday, gold was quoted at $402.75 an ounce.
