Wall Street Limps Through a Blackout Monday

  • Author: A Wall Street Journal News Roundup
  • Date: 08/14/90

NEW YORK -- A power blackout in lower Manhattan forced an early-afternoon closing of New York's two biggest commodity exchanges and the American Stock Exchange, disrupting trading in some financial markets. Futures trading was halted in oil and gold, two of the world's most important commodities whose prices have jumped in reaction to turmoil in the Middle East. The blackout also affected a wide range of financial instruments, from stock options and currencies to the Federal Reserve's weekly auction of Treasury bills. Trading in bond markets was especially hard hit.

It was an eerie scene. Hundreds of traders stood in darkened commodity trading pits in the World Trade Center, below clocks that stood frozen at 1:13. Now-useless trading cards littered the floor.

The New York Mercantile Exchange, which trades the world's benchmark oil futures contract, and the Commodity Exchange, which trades the major gold contract, both shut down trading. Three smaller commodity exchanges and the nearby American Stock Exchange, a major stock-options market, joined them.

But several blocks away on Broad Street, the New York Stock Exchange, the nation's biggest stock market, remained open and finished up in a final-hour rally.

The blackout was caused by a fire that broke out around noon EDT in a Consolidated Edison electrical substation and knocked out power to several blocks in the financial district here. As of late yesterday, Con Ed officials said most of the power had been restored, but not before eight firefighters out of 100 on the scene were injured.

The exchanges plan to open today, barring any further blackouts. But the disruption couldn't have come at a worse time for New York's commodity markets, which have been roiled in the past week by volatile oil and precious metals trading. For the first time in more than a decade, traders and investors had no closing price for oil and gold and other important commodities.

However, these and other commodities continued to trade in ports and other trading centers -- the spot markets -- in the U.S. and abroad. But that didn't help some futures traders who were stuck in the middle of trading positions.

Blair Karasin, an independent stock-index futures trader, said that he had bet that stock prices would rise, but that he didn't complete his trade because he couldn't reach brokers in Chicago, where index futures are traded. Now he doesn't know the price at which he will be able to complete the transaction.

"I hope nothing terrible happens overnight," he said after walking down eight flights of stairs at the World Trade Center. The New York Merc and Comex said they would establish settlement prices for commodities traded there at today's close.

Yesterday's problems demonstrated once again how concentrated the nation's financial machinery is, and how quickly the markets can be disrupted. Major brokerage firms and the stock exchanges -- including the Big Board -- have spent millions of dollars in recent years to build better backup systems to handle emergencies. Many of those systems were used yesterday to keep some semblance of order.

At the darkened Merc and Comex, prices stood frozen on wallboards above traders' heads: The current crude oil contract stood at $26.77 a barrel, up 54 cents; gold was $405.20 an ounce, up $8.20. The Merc plans to open by 11 today; the Comex hadn't yet set an opening time.

Although the American Stock Exchange shut down, trading in some of its stocks continued on regional exchanges, traders said. The Amex composite index closed at 338.14, down 1.18.

The blackout occurred shortly before the bidding deadline for the government's regular weekly Treasury bill auction. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York, which conducts the auction on behalf of the Treasury Department, said the sale of $18.4 billion of three-month and six-month bills proceeded smoothly. That's because the Fed's trading and auction equipment was running on a backup power system.

"As far as we are concerned, the dealers were all able to make bids and therefore the auction took place normally," said a Fed spokesman.

But several dealers had trouble communicating with their own employees who were waiting at the Fed to receive bidding instructions from their trading desks. Some employees couldn't get through to find out at what price to bid.

A manager at Shearson Lehman Government Securities Inc. said his firm was forced to bid "blind" at the auction, meaning that it participated without knowing exactly how the market was trading. Dealers typically base auction bid prices on current market prices, which change rapidly in the bond market.

It's not the first time that trading has been disrupted at the exchanges here. Two weeks ago, the New York commodity exchanges closed for 50 minutes when the ticker feed that transmits commodity prices broke. But traders and exchange officials couldn't recall the last time the exchanges didn't provide final settlement prices.

The power outage, which struck Con Ed's South Street Seaport substation, also wasn't the most disruptive that Wall Street has seen. That's because the Big Board didn't have to close. An early-morning electrical fire last November shut down both the Big Board and the American Stock Exchange for an hour, temporarily dislocating stock-index futures trading on the Chicago commodity exchanges.

That fire broke out in the lower-Manhattan building that houses the Securities Industry Automation Corp., which processes stock quotations from the nation's major exchanges.

The U.S. foreign-exchange market, which trades about $175 billion a day, also was thrown into some confusion yesterday. Initially, only a few institutions were affected. But as the day went on, the blackout spread, knocking out phone lines and trading screens at banks, securities firms and currency brokers.

Some dealers alleged that Chicago traders tried to take advantage of the situation. With many big commercial and investment banks here unable to trade, they said, traders on the Chicago Mercantile Exchange tried to push the dollar higher.

By so doing, they were squeezing those traders and speculators who had earlier sold dollars short, betting that the currency would keep falling. Often, traders, big investors and corporations leave so-called stop-loss orders with banks to automatically buy or sell currencies at a specific price. These act as a safety valve to either lock in a profit or stem a loss.

But unable to trade, some New York banks couldn't fulfill these stop-losses and some dealers got caught in a shortsqueeze, said a trader at Chase Manhattan Bank here. "It was difficult to call out and get prices," said Leonard Dendunnen, a trader at Union Bank of Switzerland here.

The Federal Reserve Bank of New York switched immediately to backup power, a spokesman said. Although the computerized system that settles billions of dollars in daily payments among banks continued to operate during the power outage, some institutions were having trouble linking up with the system by computer. But the spokesman said that meant only that the institutions affected would have to settle transactions by hand or by messenger.

"Some transactions may be stuck in a computer and have to be done manually," he said, "but the capacity exists to settle normally."

At the two big commercial banks with headquarters in lower Manhattan -- J.P. Morgan and Bank of New York -- the power outage caused only marginal problems. "There was only a brief interruption of trading until the emergency power kicked on," said a spokesman for J.P. Morgan. At Bank of New York, a spokesman said that while there was no interruption of customer transactions, the building was without lights. "We're literally in the dark," the spokesman said.
