Top Japanese Banks' Ratings on Debt Lowered by Moody's

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/14/90

TOKYO -- Moody's Investors Service Inc. downgraded credit ratings of two of Japan's biggest banks, citing their high exposure to Japan's real estate market and vulnerability to rising interest rates. The New York-based rating concern lowered the long-term debt of Dai-Ichi Kangyo Bank Ltd. and Fuji Bank Ltd. to double-A-1 from triple-A. Moody's retained the Prime-1 rating on the two banks' short-term debt. Moody's earlier announced it was considering a downgrading of the two banks' debt.

In taking its action, Moody's focused on the high overheads and slow pace of adaptation to changing market conditions at the two banks. Dai-Ichi Kangyo is the world's largest bank, with $421 billion in assets, and Fuji, with $392 billion, is the fourth biggest.

Interest rates in Japan have been moving up rapidly this year, raising the cost of funds at the banks, and both companies have large amounts of real estate loans outstanding in Japan. Land prices in Japan, which have doubled in the past five years, are considered inflated by many analysts.

"The issue is core profitability," said Roger B. Arner, Moody's representative director in Japan. "This is significant in that it's a further differentiation among the large commercial banks. These banks are less able to compete."

The banks objected to Moody's credit-rating revision. "We highly regret this decision, which we believe does not accurately reflect the bank's state of affairs," a spokeswoman for Dai-Ichi Kangyo said.

A spokesman for Fuji Bank said the bank wasn't happy with the revision. He said Fuji would issue a statement later.

Moody's Mr. Arner played down the importance of the lower ratings to the fund-raising abilities of Dai-Ichi Kangyo and Fuji. "These are still very high ratings," he said.
