Study Reveals Brokerages' Selection Process of Stocks

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/14/90

NEW YORK -- Within reason, brokerage houses in the Journal-Zacks study of stock-picking prowess may choose what list of stocks they will be judged on. Of course, they have to specify in advance. Merrill Lynch, PaineWebber and Raymond James are judged by the stocks their analysts rate "1" on a five-point scale. Kidder Peabody is judged by stocks rated "1"or "2."

A.G. Edwards is judged by stocks analysts say to "buy" or "accumulate." Prudential-Bache is judged on a each analyst's "single best idea," not counting sell recommendations.

At four firms -- Dean Witter, Goldman Sachs, Shearson Lehman and Smith Barney, a stock-selection committee reviews analysts' recommendations and selects a subgroup as the firm's official recommended list. Smith Barney goes one step further, and winnows the committee list down to a short "retail recommended list."

The study assumes that an investor buys the recommended stocks, and sells all others, even if they're designated as "holds" or "weak buys." Equal dollar amounts are put into each stock, and portfolios are adjusted monthly to keep the amounts equal. All buying and selling is on the last trading day of each month. Commissions and taxes are disregarded, mostly to make the results comparable to market indexes. Dividends are included.

Brokerage houses are given a chance to check the data for accuracy. Nine firms did so this quarter; Merrill Lynch said it didn't have a person available to do the checking.
