REVIEW & OUTLOOK (Editorial): Clouds Over Pakistan

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/14/90

The news from Pakistan in the wake of Benazir Bhutto's ousting is disturbing. Citing "corruption and nepotism in the federal government," military-backed President Ghulam Ishaq Khan dissolved Ms. Bhutto's government last week, appointed an interim prime minister and set new elections for October 24. Though the claims of "corruption" and "nepotism" on Ms. Bhutto's watch are certainly well-founded, the recent activities undertaken by the Pakistani security forces have themselves advanced beyond legitimacy, or so it would appear. The result is harassment of not only those associated with Ms. Bhutto, but a crackdown on any dissent in the country.

The police have started to round up many of Ms. Bhutto's key associates and supporters. Ms. Bhutto's mother was harassed by security forces when she tried to leave the country on a plane to London. A newspaper executive and four senior journalists were arrested last Saturday. By some lights, the country is in a virtual state of martial law.

Interim Prime Minister Ghulam Mustafa Jatoi has denied that the arrests are political. But one can't help feeling unease about the current weeding out of "corruption" and the imposition of "order" in Pakistan. The Pakistani military, despite its denials of involvement, seems to be the invisible force behind Ms. Bhutto's ouster and the subsequent crackdown on her supporters.

Under the rubric of ridding the government of corruption, the military had taken over Pakistan's government five times before the dissolution of the Bhutto government. Ms. Bhutto said right after the announcement of this recent dissolution, "They have done it again."

The military is miffed over Ms. Bhutto's meddling in its "internal affairs," her handling of the ethnic strife in Sind, and her search for a political solution in Afghanistan. But while there can be legitimate complaints about her policies and her management of government, Pakistan's voters are entitled to decide for themselves whether she continues or, if not, who her replacement will be. It is going to be hard for anyone to regard the coming election as a fair one if the contenders continue to be subject to arrest and intimidation by the security forces. Indeed, the arrests and proposed trials of prominent Bhutto backers will themselves be suspect if they come to be regarded as political arrests rather than actions based on genuine evidence of corruption. That will hardly serve either the cause of democracy or justice.

This ouster seems to have been done "by the book," or pursuant to the Pakistani constitution, though there might be grounds for challenging President Ghulam Ishaq Khan's actions in court -- a course of action Ms. Bhutto reportedly will take. The Pakistani courts and the political arena -- when new elections are finally held -- are the forums in which Pakistan's problems should be worked out. The laws of the land and the voice of the Pakistani people -- not the imposition of the military's will -- are the only legitimate means to the ultimate end, which is the salvation of Pakistan's fragile democracy.

Pakistan's problems are many, to be sure, involving not only bad government management but dangers of serious conflicts pitting Islamic fundamentalists in rural areas against more sophisticated urban dwellers. But those problems can only be solved through democratic processes, not by strong-arm actions.
