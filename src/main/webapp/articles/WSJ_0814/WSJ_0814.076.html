More Soviet Businessmen Are Allowed to Live in U.S.

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/14/90

WASHINGTON -- President Bush, seeking what he calls "more normal" economic ties with the Soviet Union, raised a longstanding ceiling on the total number of Soviet businessmen permitted to live and work in the U.S. That limit has remained for many years at 69. Mr. Bush, who didn't say how many more Soviet businessmen will be allowed into the U.S., linked his step to the need to "establish overall equality of opportunity for American business people" who live in the Soviet Union.

Mr. Bush said this move is in line with U.S. commitments to assist the Soviet Union "institutionalize lasting, market-oriented economic reforms." However, he cautioned that he could reconsider his decision "should there be evidence that this opportunity is being misused for intelligence-gathering or other inappropriate activities."

U.S. companies have been pressing for the right to post more resident representatives in the Soviet Union and to have wider contacts in Soviet society. But such privileges are limited by reciprocity rules. U.S. companies with Soviet joint ventures also are seeking easier entry to the U.S. for their Soviet partners.
