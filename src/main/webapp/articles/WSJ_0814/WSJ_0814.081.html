Politics & Policy: Cheney Plans to Cut Back Purchases By Navy of Submarines, Destroyers

  • Author: Andy Pasztor
  • Date: 08/14/90

WASHINGTON -- Signaling that the Navy must bear its share of painful budget cuts, Defense Secretary Dick Cheney proposed scaling back plans to purchase superquiet Seawolf submarines and the most advanced class of destroyers. The announcement, intended to cut about $7 billion from Navy procurement plans through the mid-1990s, reflects the harsh, long-term budget constraints facing each of the services despite the current U.S. military buildup in Saudi Arabia. The Seawolf attack subs and the Arleigh Burkeclass guided-missile destroyers covered by Mr. Cheney's announcement are among the most expensive and prized Navy programs.

After a four-month review of major ship-construction plans, Secretary Cheney proposed to cut annual Seawolf purchases roughly in half and reduce to 16 from 25 the number of destroyers slated to be built by 1995.

Mr. Cheney also is expected to announce that he wants to redesign and scale back the Army's controversial, $42 billion plan to purchase a new generation of light helicopters, Pentagon officials said. The results of the helicopter review were expected to be announced along with the ship proposals, but officials said Mr. Cheney is likely to release those details as early as today.

There already has been strong pressure from Congress to postpone purchases of the fast attack subs, which cost more than $1.7 billion each and are to be built by General Dynamics Corp.'s Electric Boat Division and Tenneco Inc.'s Newport News Shipbuilding & Drydock Co. unit. Under the Cheney proposals, some Pentagon officials said there still would be enough Seawolf business to support two contractors.

By contrast, the destroyers so far have escaped serious congressional budget-cutting efforts. The ships are built by a unit of Litton Industries Inc. and closely held Bath Iron Works.

While emphasizing that "current events remind us of how fragile world peace can be," Mr. Cheney said he nevertheless concluded that the Navy's original production plans should be shelved in light of dramatic improvements in U.S.-Soviet relations. Specifically, Mr. Cheney wants to reduce Seawolf procurement to three vessels every two years from 10 vessels every three years.

By embracing congressional proposals to eliminate at least one of the two Seawolfs requested by the Navy in fiscal 1991, which begins Oct. 1, Mr. Cheney hopes to save $1.8 billion in that year alone.

However, the Middle East crisis is expected to help the Pentagon's budget in other ways. The Bush administration apparently hopes to capitalize on the Iraqi invasion of Kuwait to deflect hefty troop cuts proposed for next year by the Senate and the House Armed Services Committee. Senior Army and Marine Corps officers, for instance, already are privately warning congressional committees that eliminating 100,000 to 130,000 active-duty troops in 1991 could make it difficult for the services to maintain the U.S. commitment to the Saudis.

Even if lawmakers reject that argument, Pentagon officials predict that the full House will restore some of the $5.9 billion the armed services panel slashed last month from President Bush's request for maintenance, training and other "military readiness" accounts.
