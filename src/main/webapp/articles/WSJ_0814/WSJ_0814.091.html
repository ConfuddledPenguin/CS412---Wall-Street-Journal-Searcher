Souter Nomination Shows Pogo Was Right

  • Author: Jerry Della Femina
  • Date: 08/14/90

It was, according to my friend Vinnie the Altar Boy, "hot" in Our Lady of Grace Church on Avenue W in Brooklyn on that Sunday in August 1949. The Friendly Frost fans were blowing hot air at the congregation and it was standing room only because this was the "10 o'clock" and that was Father Cafaro's Mass. We all knew he didn't handle the heat too well and would have everybody out in 40 minutes. Father Cafaro crossed us up. He stood at the pulpit, perspiration dripped down his face, and gave a long sermon on communism. He told us how godless communism was the greatest threat to our freedom. Then he told us we must all pray for the conversion of Russia.

I was 13 and my friend and I giggled at the word "conversion," because the only conversion we knew came after a touchdown was scored by the football Giants and very often a conversion was the difference between our fathers and uncles beating the point spread.

So, for the next 40 years, Father Cafaro and those priests who followed prayed for the conversion of Russia. They were war years -- most times it was a cold war, occasionally it got hot. There was Korea, the A-bomb, Castro, the Missile Crisis, Vietnam, Joe McCarthy ripping the country apart, Nikita Khrushchev screaming "We will bury you," bomb shelters, Sputnik ("My God, they're winning space") . . .

We never had a moment when our politicians weren't telling us that our way of life, our freedom, was being threatened by communism. This was the generation that lived under the gun, and if it consumed too much aspirin, Valium, grass, cocaine, Haagen-Dazs ice cream, acid, Perrier . . . it had to be forgiven, because we all grew up being taught to duck and cover under our desks if we saw a flash of light. This would save us, they said, from the hydrogen bomb.

And then one day the headlines in the papers and the local news told us we'd won. The wall was down. The other guys were on the ropes. Father Cafaro's prayers of 1949 had been answered in 1989. And we were all so numb and so weary that we walked around saying, "Hey, how about that Berlin Wall?" in the same voice we've said "Hey, how about those Mets?" But we won. And nothing could hurt us now.

It lasted about eight months. The headlines one Saturday read "Brennan Resigns from Court" and by Monday they read "Bush to Move Swiftly to Select a Successor."

That day, a timid looking little man who bears a striking resemblance to Mad magazine's ("What? Me Worry?") Alfred E. Newman was being introduced to the U.S. as the latest prospective member of the court. Nowhere in the introduction was there any mention that this is the man who will tip the balance of power in the court for the next 30 years.

Those who watched the C-SPAN coverage of that press conference saw George Bush whining his latest version of "Read My Lips": "No, I didn't ask him his views on abortion. Let's not make abortion the litmus test of a Supreme Court justice." None bothered to ask the president, "If the future freedom of our children is not a litmus test of a Supreme Court Justice, what is?"

As I watched the president turn away question after question from the press and heard his excuse for why the country couldn't hear Judge Souter's views on abortion until the Senate heard them first, my mind kept going back to that great Walt Kelly "Pogo" cartoon where the swamp animals finally come to the conclusion that "We Have Met The Enemy and They Is Us."

And what does this new court mean? To me, it means that my four-year-old daughter no longer has -- and might not have in her lifetime -- the right to her own body . . . her right to free choice. And if, in the future, she should ever become pregnant, she must have an unwanted baby because the state says she must, and that will be the law of the land. It means my 15-month-old son will grow up in a world where if he is a homosexual, he will have to stay in the closet for life because the state will recognize only "real" men and real "child-bearing" women just like the Russians would have done to us if they'd had the chance.

We will live to see many of the freedoms that were won in our lifetimes taken away by the Bushes, the Scalias, the Anthony Kennedys, and now, the Souters. Roe v. Wade? Forget it. We'll be lucky if Miranda and Brown v. Board of Education are around when it's over.

I'm a Republican/Libertarian who has never voted for a Democrat in his life. But I'm also a Republican who has always believed you can't have too conservative a president, nor too liberal a Supreme Court -- and that makes me a republican in the true sense of the word.

And so, this Sunday, a 54-year-old Republican/Libertarian will go back to that little church on Avenue W in Brooklyn, and he will kneel and say his first prayer in 40 years. I will pray for the conversion of George Bush and his Supreme Court.
