AT&T Credit Card Now Ranks Eighth In the Country

  • Author: John J. Keller
  • Date: 08/14/90

NEW YORK -- American Telephone & Telegraph Co. said its new credit card ranks eighth in the country, with revenue of $750 million after 140 days in operation. In addition, the company and its partner, Universal Bank, a unit of Columbus, Ga.-based Synovus Financial Corp., said they have begun offering a gold MasterCard version of the AT&T Universal card, which is available as either a Visa card or MasterCard.

Since its introduction on March 26, the Universal card has garnered 2.7 million accounts and 4.6 million cardholders, according to AT&T Universal Card Services Corp., the AT&T unit that issues the card.

Moreover, AT&T said more than 11 million long-distance calls and purchases have been made using the card, for a total of $750 million in transactions.

AT&T announced its millionth account on June 12, 78 days after launching the card. Since then it has signed another 1.7 million accounts.

That comes to about 27,000 cards per day. If this rate holds, AT&T by this time next year could have more than 12 million accounts, making it the second-largest credit card issuer in the country. Moreover, annual revenue could top $3 billion. An AT&T spokesman declined to make a prediction about the card's future prospects.

"Our continuing success in the marketplace is a sign that consumers are delighted with the advantages offered by the AT&T Universal Card," said Paul G. Kahn, president of AT&T Universal, in a statement.

His competitors aren't delighted, however. Several major banks, including Citicorp and Chase Manhattan Corp., have filed lawsuits, challenging the legality of AT&T's card.

AT&T is offering the Universal card free for life to consumers who sign up in 1990 and use it at least once a year. It is also offering discounts on long-distance calls made with the card and assorted warranties and service guarantees.

AT&T said it derived its ranking from figures provided by Credit Card News, a Chicago-based newsletter.
