GAO Says Treasury Underpriced Bonds That It Sold to Mexico

  • Author: Author Unknown
  • Date: 08/14/90

WASHINGTON -- The General Accounting Office said the Treasury's sale of zero-coupon bonds to Mexico involved "an effective subsidy of approximately $192 million." The GAO, in findings to be presented at a House Banking Committee hearing today, said the subsidy resulted from the Treasury's decision to price the zero-coupon bonds based on the coupon bond rate rather than the market rate for comparable zero-coupon securities.

Allan Mendelowitz, director of international trade, energy and finance issues for GAO, said in a statement prepared for delivery to the House panel that the pricing method was within Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady's legal authority. But he added that the GAO, Congress's investigative arm, believes the pricing decision "was neither appropriate nor good public policy."

Mr. Mendelowitz said that if the Treasury wanted to help Mexico, "the correct way would have been to obtain congressional approval through the authorization and appropriations process, rather than with an effective subsidy provided through the underpricing of the zeros."

The Treasury this year sold Mexico about $30 billion of zero-coupon bonds as part of debt restructuring under the so-called Brady Plan. Zero-coupon bonds are sold at a deep discount to their face value and pay no interest until maturity. The Treasury priced the bonds in relation to conventional 30-year bonds rather than Strips, the zero-coupon securities created in the marketplace when dealers strip Treasury securities.

The pricing method was attacked by various lawmakers and others as a backdoor subsidy, but was strongly defended by the Treasury as appropriate.

"In the long run, this decision could set a precedent that will cost the United States many times more than $192 million," Mr. Mendelowitz said. "The Mexico deal was the first of many agreements anticipated under the Brady Plan."
