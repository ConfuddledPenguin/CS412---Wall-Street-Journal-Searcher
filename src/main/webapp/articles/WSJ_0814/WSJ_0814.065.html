Letters to the Editor: Are Porn Prosecutions a Raw Deal?

  • Author: null
  • Date: 08/14/90

I find myself compelled to express my considerable dissatisfaction and concern regarding your July 27 page-one article on pornography defendants. You imply that pornographers are being treated unfairly by the judicial system by being subject to multiple prosecutions. You suggest that merely because Philip Harvey's local community has not found his pornographic materials objectionable, that Mr. Harvey should be allowed to distribute these materials anywhere he pleases without regard to community values. I submit to you that Mr. Harvey in choosing to peddle his materials throughout the country should be responsible to the values of communities in which he expects to do business. You further suggest that the Justice Department and Attorney General Richard Thornburgh have, as a result of implied political ambitions, begun prosecuting pornographers to gain conservative political advantage.

I believe rather that his putting child pornographers behind bars should bring little protest inasmuch as Mr. Thornburgh and the Justice Department are enforcing the laws established by the judicial systems within this country. Furthermore, enforcement of current obscenity and pornography laws should not be weakened by past inaction to enforce such laws. I expect the Justice Department to enforce these laws and commend Mr. Thornburgh and former Attorney General Edwin Meese for their courageous actions to uphold the law and defend the child victims of such pornographic materials.

Glen Berryhill

Long Beach, Calif.

---

The questions you raised illustrate the problem with trying to squelch free expression, for whatever motive, in a free society.

Even if one can find a standard to prove which material "appeals to prurient interests, depicts sexual conduct in a patently offensive way and lacks serious literary, artistic, political or scientific value" (a task that is daunting enough), one question still remains: Should that material be illegal? If obscenity can be made illegal, why not blasphemy, atheism, communism or any number of things that most people find offensive?

The bottom line is this: Either we believe in free speech or we don't. The Constitution doesn't simply say that "the majority rules." On the contrary, it is an anti-majoritarian document that in many cases is there to protect the minority from the majority. In my opinion, that should include the minority that gets its jollies from watching "Prisoner of Paradise."

So long as pornographers don't engage in child or animal abuse, and so long as everyone involved, including the viewer, is a consenting adult, then I think the Justice Department has bigger fish to fry.

James Melton

Arlington, Mass.

---

Your article on obscenity prosecutions and the perils of double jeopardy lacked a certain historical perspective. Venue shopping on the part of overzealous prosecutors is nothing new: I was the victim of one such case of judicial fraud when a federal indictment was handed down against me in 1974, in Wichita, Kan.

My newspaper specializes in sexual satire, and we found we had sold four subscriptions to the whole of Kansas -- all four to U.S. postal inspectors. Even though the bulk of my sales were to New York City and environs, I had to undergo the egregious expense of defending myself in a courtroom a thousand miles distant from my home base.

In general, the current hysteria over censorship of art and music holds a savage amusement for those of us who've been on the front lines of First Amendment battles. The censorship of pornography creates a climate in which the guardians of social purity can go on to bigger bonfires.

The First Amendment must be inclusive, plenary and immune for it to have any meaning at all. I have long decried the media's silence on the suppression of adult entertainment. To paraphrase that old quote about the Holocaust, no one in the media objected when they came for the pornographers, because no one in the media published porn. No one objected when they came for the rappers, because they weren't rappers. No one objected to the silencing of the performance artists, because, after all, they didn't do performance art. By the time the censors come for The Wall Street Journal, the New York Times, Time or Newsweek, there just may be no one left to object.

Al Goldstein

Publisher

New York
