JAPAN'S IMPORT OMBUDSMAN

Japan's Office of Trade and Investment Ombudsman will broaden its work to process claims concerning market openness and smooth entry of imports, an agency official said. The office also decided to add up to three non-Japanese to its now eight-member advisory council.

In the Structural Impediment Initiative bilateral trade talks that ended in June, Tokyo promised the U.S. it would broaden the function of the agency. The talks were aimed at breaking down Japanese barriers to imports and correcting U.S. investment imbalances.

Members of the council decided to expand its scope to handle "concrete" complaints by foreign companies concerning the smooth entry of imports, the official said. This will allow the agency, among other things, to process claims concerning direct investment in Japan, according to the official.

The agency was established in 1982 to receive and process claims regarding Japan's import standards and certification system. Yesterday's decision will probably be presented at a follow-up meeting of the bilateral talks to be held this fall.

EAST GERMAN CHEMICALS

Western chemical companies have so far invested only modestly in East Germany companies, according to a report by the Center for Information and Documentation in East Berlin. Western reticence is due to the large number of plants with high pollution emissions and the enormous costs associated with cleaning up already polluted land and rivers in East Germany. The West German Chemical Industry Association has said East German chemical firms should cover the costs of environmental cleanup themselves.

EUROPEAN TV SATELLITE

Societe Europeennee des Satellites, operator of Europe's first privately owned satellite network, said a number of international aerospace companies are bidding to supply the company with a third satellite. The Luxembourg-based company's first satellite, Astra 1a, was launched by the European Ariane rocket in December 1988. It began transmitting to television stations across Europe in February 1989. Among the users of Astra 1a are Sky Channel, the pop-music channel MTV and Eurosport. A second satellite, Astra 1b, is due to be launched at the end of this year.

CHINESE EXPORT QUALITY

Chinese authorities are waging a sometimes losing battle in their effort to improve the quality of goods made for export. While some improvements have been recorded since new quality-control and inspection laws were enacted in August 1989, the track record of many Chinese exporters is still dismal, official reports show. In one case, nearly 75% of the cloth produced by Taiyuan Cotton Textile Mill, in northern Shanxi province, was found to be of poor quality.

TURKISH POWER PLANT

Eight Japanese companies will set up a joint venture with the Turkish government to build and run an electric power plant in Turkey. Under the arrangement, the Turkish government will be able to build the plant, learn how to run and maintain the system and eventually take full ownership without having to pay more than 30% of the costs of construction, according to a spokesman for Electric Power Development, one of the Japanese companies involved in the project. Under the arrangement, the eight companies will provide 70% of the capital for the 18 billion-yen ($119.6 million) joint venture; staterun TEK, a Turkish power company, will contribute the rest.

BUSINESS AS USUAL

Saddam Hussein hasn't yet scared tourists from Egypt, Turkey and Jordan.

Despite ominous headlines, a military buildup in Saudi Arabia and Iraq's threats of a holy war against the U.S., hotels, airlines and travel agencies serving the Eastern Mediterranean have reported few cancellations from Western travelers.

"Up until now, business is better than we expected, said Rico de Schepper, general manager of the Nile Hilton in Cairo. "So far, we haven't received any cancellations." Pan Am and TWA said flights to Cairo, Istanbul and Ankara are running normally.

A U.S. travel agent said Egyptair flights out of the U.S. are wait-listed for the rest of the summer. The London office of Royal Jordanian Airlines said it just received bookings for a 14-person group to travel to Amman next month, and a 90-person group next May.

European charter carriers said it's more or less business as usual.
